Energy—Turbine Power per Unit Mass of Flow

GIVEN A steam turbine generator unit used to produce electricity is shown in Fig. E5.21a. Assume the steam enters a turbine with a velocity of 30 m/s and enthalpy, \(h_{1}\), of 3348 kJ/kg (see Fig. E5.21b). The steam leaves the turbine as a mixture of vapor and liquid having a velocity of 60 m/s and an enthalpy of 2550 kJ/kg.

The flow through the turbine is adiabatic, and changes in elevation are negligible.

FIND Determine the work output involved per unit mass of steam through-flow.

Admin
Solution

We use a control volume that includes the steam in the turbine from the entrance to the exit as shown in Fig. E5.21b. Applying Eq. 5.69 to the steam in this control volume we get

 

\(\dot{m}\left[\check{u}_{2}-\check{u}_{1}+\left(\frac{p}{\rho}\right)_{2}-\left(\frac{p}{\rho}\right)_{1}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}-V_{1}^{2}}{2}+ \cancel{g\left(z_{2}-z_{1}\right)}^{0(\text{no elevation change})}\right]=\cancel{\dot{Q}_{\text {net in }}}^{0(\text{adiabatic flow})} +\dot{W}_{\text {shaft net in }}\) (1)

 

\(\dot{m}\left[\breve{h}_{\text {out }}-\breve{h}_{\text {in }}+\frac{V_{\text {out }}^{2}-V_{\text {in }}^{2}}{2}+g\left(z_{\text {out }}-z_{\text {in }}\right)\right]={\dot{Q}_{\text {net in}}}+\dot{W}_{\text {shaft in}}\) (5.69)

 

The work output per unit mass of steam through-flow \(W_{\text {shaft net in }}\), wshaft net in, can be obtained by dividing Eq. 1 by the mass flow rate, , to obtain

 

\(w_{\text {shaft net in }}=\frac{\dot{W}_{\text {shaft net in}}}{\dot{m}}=\check{h}_{2}-\check{h}_{1}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}-V_{1}^{2}}{2}\) (2)

 

\(\text { Since } w_{\text {shaft net out }}=-w_{\text {shaft net in }} \text {, We obtain }\)

 

\(w_{\substack{\text { shaft } \\ \text { net out }}}=\check{h}_{1}-\check{h}_{2}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}-V_{2}^{2}}{2}\)

 

or

 

\(\begin{aligned}w_{\substack{\text { shaft } \\\text { net out }}} &=3348 kJ / kg -2550 kJ / kg \\&+\frac{\left[(30 m / s )^{2}-(60 m / s )^{2}\right][1 J /( N \cdot m )]}{2\left[1( kg \cdot m ) /\left( N \cdot s ^{2}\right)\right](1000 J / kJ )}\end{aligned}\)

 

Thus,

 

\(\begin{array}{l}w_{\text {shaft }}=3348 kJ / kg -2550 kJ / kg -1.35 kJ / kg\\=797 kJ / kg\end{array}\)

 

COMMENT Note that in this particular example, the change in kinetic energy is small in comparison to the difference in enthalpy involved. This is often true in applications involving steam turbines. To determine the power output, \(W_{\text {shaft }}\), we must know the mass flowrate, \(\dot{m}\).
