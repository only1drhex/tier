The Conical Pendulum

A small ball of mass m is suspended from a string of length L. The ball revolves with constant speed v in a horizontal circle of radius r as shown in Figure 6.3. (Because the string sweeps out the surface of a cone, the system is known as a conical pendulum.) Find an expression for v in terms of the geometry in Figure 6.3.

Admin
Solution

Conceptualize Imagine the motion of the ball in Figure 6.3a and convince yourself that the string sweeps out a cone and that the ball moves in a horizontal circle.

Categorize The ball in Figure 6.3 does not accelerate vertically. Therefore, we model it as a particle in equilibrium in the vertical direction. It experiences a centripetal acceleration in the horizontal direction, so it is modeled as a particle in uniform circular motion in this direction.

Analyze Let θ represent the angle between the string and the vertical. In the diagram of forces acting on the ball in Figure 6.3b, the force \(\overrightarrow{T}\) xerted by the string on the ball is resolved into a vertical component \(T \cos \theta\) and a horizontal component \(T \sin \theta\) acting toward the center of the circular path.

Apply the particle in equilibrium model in the vertical direction:

\(\sum F_{y}=T \cos \theta-m g=0\)

\(\text { (1) } T \cos \theta=m g\)

Use Equation 6.1 from the particle in uniform circular motion model in the horizontal direction:

\(\sum F=m a_{c}=m \frac{v^{2}}{r}\) (6.1)

(2) \(\sum F_{x}=T \sin \theta=m a_{c}=\frac{m v^{2}}{r}\)

Divide Equation (2) by Equation (1) and use \(\sin \theta / \cos \theta=\tan \theta \):

\(\tan \theta=\frac{v^{2}}{r g}\)

Solve for v:

\(v=\sqrt{r g \tan \theta}\)

Incorporate \(r=L \sin \theta\) from the geometry in Figure 6.3a:

\( v=\sqrt{L g \sin \theta \tan \theta}\)

Finalize Notice that the speed is independent of the mass of the ball. Consider what happens when θ goes to 908 so that the string is horizontal. Because the tangent of 908 is infinite, the speed v is infinite, which tells us the string cannot possibly be horizontal. If it were, there would be no vertical component of the force \(\overrightarrow{T}\) to balance the gravitational force on the ball. That is why we mentioned in regard to Figure 6.1 that the puck’s weight in the figure is supported by a frictionless table.
