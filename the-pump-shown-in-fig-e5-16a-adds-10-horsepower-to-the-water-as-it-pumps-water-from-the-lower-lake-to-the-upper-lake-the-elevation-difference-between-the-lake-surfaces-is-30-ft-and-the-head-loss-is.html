Guest Question.

The pump shown in Fig. E5.16a adds 10 horsepower to the water as it pumps water from the lower lake to the upper lake. The elevation difference between the lake surfaces is 30 ft and the head loss is 15 ft.
FIND Determine
(a) the flowrate and
(b) the power loss associated with this flow.

Admin
Solution

(a) The energy equation (\(\frac{p_{out}}{\gamma } +\frac{V^{2}_{out} }{2g}+z_{out}= \frac{p_{in}}{\gamma } +\frac{V^{2}_{in} }{2g}+z_{in}+h_{s}-h_{L} \)) for this flow is

\( \frac{p_{2}}{\gamma } +\frac{V^{2}_{2} }{2g}+z_{2}= \frac{p_{1}}{\gamma } +\frac{V^{2}_{1} }{2g}+z_{1}+h_{s}-h_{L}\) (1)

where points 1 and 2 (corresponding to “in” and “out” in \(\frac{p_{out}}{\gamma } +\frac{V^{2}_{out} }{2g}+z_{out}= \frac{p_{in}}{\gamma } +\frac{V^{2}_{in} }{2g}+z_{in}+h_{s}-h_{L} \)) are located on the lake surfaces. Thus, \(p_{1}=p_{2}=0\) and \(V_{1}=V_{2}=0\) so that Eq. 1 becomes

\( h_{s}=h_{L}+z_{2}-z_{1}\) (2)

where \(z_{2}=30 ft\), \(z_{1}=0\), and \(h_{L}=15 ft\), The pump head is obtained from \(h_{s}={w_{shaft\quad net\quad in}}/{g}=\frac{\dot{W}_{shaft\quad net\quad in} }{\dot{mg} } =\frac{\dot{W}_{shaft\quad net\quad in}}{\gamma Q} \) as

\( h_{s}={\dot{W}_{shaft\quad net\quad in}}/{\gamma Q}= {(10 hp)({{550ft\cdot Ib}/{s}}/{hp})}/{(62.4{Ib}/{ft^{3}})}Q={88.1}/{Q}\)

where \( h_{s}\) is in ft when Q is in \({ft^{3}}/{s}\).

Hence, from Eq. 2,

\({88.1}/{Q}=15 ft+30 f\)

or

\(Q=1.96{ft^{3}}/{s}\)

COMMENT Note that in this example the purpose of the pump is to lift the water (a 30-ft head) and overcome the head loss (a 15-ft head); it does not, overall, alter the water’s pressure or velocity.

(b) The power lost due to friction can be obtained from \(h_{s}={\dot{W}_{shaft\quad net\quad in}}/{g}=\frac{\dot{W}_{shaft\quad net\quad in} }{\dot{mg} } =\frac{\dot{W}_{shaft\quad net\quad in}}{\gamma Q} \) as

\( \dot{W}_{loss}=\gamma Qh_{L} =(62.4{Ib}/{ft^{3}})(1.96{{ft^{3}}/{s}})(15 ft)=1830{ft\cdot Ib}/{s}({1hp}/{550{ft\cdot Ib}/{s}})=3.33 hp\)

COMMENTS The remaining 10 hp – 3.33 hp = 6.67 hp that the pump adds to the water is used to lift the water from the lower to the upper lake. This energy is not “lost,” but it is stored as potential energy.

By repeating the calculations for various head losses, \(h_{L}\), the results shown in Fig. E5.16b are obtained. Note that as the head loss increases, the flowrate decreases because an increasing portion of the 10 hp supplied by the pump is lost and, therefore, not available to lift the fluid to the higher elevation.
