Guest Question.

An idea1 fluid flows through the divergent section of pipe in Fig. 4-7a such that it enters with a velocity \(V_1\). If the flow is steady, determine the velocity \( V_2 \)at which it exits.

Admin
Solution

Fluid Description. This is a case of one-dimensional, steady, nonuniform flow. It is nonuniform because the velocities are diffierental each location. Because the fluid is assumed to be ideal. its density is constant and the viscosity is zero. For this reason. the velocity profile. will be uniform over each cross section.

 

Analysis I. To analyze the flow, we will consider a fixed control volume to represent the fluid system within the divergent section, Fig. 4- 7a. At time\( r + \Delta t \) this system will move to the position shown in Fig. 4-7b. Therefore, the amount of mass moving out into the pipe of diameter\( d_2 \)during the time\( \Delta t\) is \( m_{out} =\rho \Delta V_{out} = \rho(V_2 \Delta t)\left(\frac{1}{4}\pi d^2_2 \right) , \)and the amount of mass lost at the entrance is equivalent to an amount of mass that would move through the pipe of diameter \(d_1. It is m_{in} = \rho \Delta V_{in} = p(V_1 \Delta t) (\frac{1}{4} \pi d_1^2). \)

Due to steady flow,, tbe. mass of fluid within the control volume remains constant. Therefore, because the total amount of the system’s mass is constant, we require

\(m_{out} – m_{in} =0 \)

 

\(\rho (V_2 \Delta t)\left(\frac{1}{4}\pi d^2_2 \right) – \rho (V_1 \Delta t)\left\langle \frac{1}{4} \pi d_1^2\right\rangle = 0\)

 

\(V_2 = V_1(\frac{d_1}{d_2} )^2 \)

The result indicates the velocity decreases,\( V_2 < V_1,\) something to be expected, since the flow is out of a larger area.

Analysis II .Now let’s see how we can obtain this same. result by applying the Reynolds transport theorem . Here the extensive. property is the mass N = m, so \(\eta = m /m – I.\) Applying Eq. 4-4. we bave

\(\left\langle\frac{Dm}{Dt} \right\rangle _{syst} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int_{cv} \rho d\forall + int_{cv} \rho V_{f/cv} \cdot dA\)

The term on the left is zero because the system mass does not change with time. Also, the first term on the right is zero because the flow is steady, that is, there is no local change of the mass within the control volume, Fig. 4-7c. Because both the density and the velocity at each open control surface are constant, they can be factored out of the integral in the last term, and integration then yields the in and out control surface areas, \(A_{in} and A_{out}\)Â· These areas have positive outward directions, as shown in Fig. 4-7c, and so the dot product evaluation of the last term in the above equation reduces to

\(0 = 0 + \rho V_2A_{out} – \rho V_1 A_{in}\)

 

\(0 = 0 + V_2(\frac{1}{4}\pi d_2^2 ) – V_1(\frac{1}{4}\pi d_1^2 )\)

 

\(V_2 = V_1(\frac{d_1}{d_2} )^2\)

we will extend this application of tbe R1eynoids transport theorem further in. Sec. 4.4.
