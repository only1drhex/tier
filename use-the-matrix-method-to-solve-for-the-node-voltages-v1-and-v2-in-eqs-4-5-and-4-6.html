Guest Question.

Use the matrix method to solve for the node voltages \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) in Eqs. 4.5 and 4.6.

\(\frac{v_1-10}{1}+\frac{v_1}{5}+\frac{v_1-v_2}{2} =0. \) (4.5)

\(\frac{v_2-v_1}{2}+\frac{v_2}{10}-2 =0. \) (4.6)

Admin
Solution

The first step is to rewrite Eqs. 4.5 and 4.6 in matrix notation. Collecting the coefficients of \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) and at the same time shifting the constant terms to the right-hand side of the equations gives us

\(1.7v-1 – 0.5v-2 = 10,\)

\(-0.5v-1 + 0.6v-2 = 2. \) (A.57)

It follows that in matrix notation, Eq.A.57 becomes

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} 1.7 & -0.5 \\ -0.5 & 0.6 \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} v_{1} \\ v_{2} \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix} 10 \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ] ,\)

or

AV = I, (A.59)

where

\(A=\left [ \begin{matrix} 1.7 & -0.5 \\ -0.5 & 0.6 \end{matrix} \right ] ,\)
\(V=\left [ \begin{matrix} v_{1} \\ v_{2} \end{matrix} \right ],\)
\( I=\left [ \begin{matrix} 10 \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ] .\)

To find the elements of the V matrix, we premultiply both sides of Eq. A.59 by the inverse of A; thus

\(A^{-1}AV = A^{-1}I. \) (A.60)

Equation A.60 reduces to

\(UV = A^{-1}I, \) (A.61)

or

\(V = A^{-1}I. \) (A.62)

It follows from Eq. A.62 that the solutions for \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) are obtained by solving for the matrix product
\(A^{-1}I.\)

To find the inverse of A, we first find the cofactors of A. Thus

\(Δ_{11}=(-1)^2(0.6) = 0.6,\)

\(Δ_{12} = (-1)^3(-0.5) = 0.5,\) (A.63)

\(Δ_{21}=(-1)^3(0.5) = 0.5,\)

\(Δ_{22}=(-1)^3(1.7) = 1.7.\)

The matrix of cofactors is

\(B=\left [ \begin{matrix} 0.6 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1.7 \end{matrix} \right ] ,\) (A.64)

and the adjoint of A is

\(adj \ A = B^T=\left [ \begin{matrix} 0.6 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1.7 \end{matrix} \right ] ,\)

The determinant of A is

\(det \ A=\left | \begin{matrix} 1.7 & -0.5 \\ -0.5 & 0.6 \end{matrix} \right | (1.7)(0.6) – (0.25) = 0.77.\)

(A.66)

From Eqs. A.65 and A.66, we can write the inverse of the coefficient matrix, that is,

\(A^{-1}=\frac{1}{0.77} \left [ \begin{matrix} 0.6 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1.7 \end{matrix} \right ] .\) (A.67)

Now the product \(A^{-1}I\) is found:

\(A^{-1}I=\frac{100}{77} \left [ \begin{matrix} 0.6 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1.7 \end{matrix} \right ]\left [ \begin{matrix} 10 \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ] .\)

\(=\frac{100}{77}\left [ \begin{matrix} 7 \\ 8.4 \end{matrix} \right ]=\left [ \begin{matrix} 9.09 \\ 10.91 \end{matrix} \right ].\) (A.68)

It follows directly that

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} v_{1} \\ v_{2} \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix} 9.09\\ 10.91 \end{matrix} \right ] ,\) (A.69)

or \(v_1 = 9.09\) V and \(v_2 = 10.91\) V.
