Guest Question.

Smith and Jones are the only competing suppliers of communication services in their community. At present, they each have a 50% share of the market. However, Smith has recently upgraded his service, and a survey indicates that from one month to the next, 90% of Smith’s customers remain loyal, while 10% switch to Jones. On the other hand, 70% of Jones’s customers remain loyal and 30% switch to Smith. If this goes on for six months, how large are their market shares? If this goes on for a long time, how big will Smith’s share become?

Admin
Solution

Let \(S_m\) be Smith’s market share (as a decimal) at the end of the m-th month and let \(J_m\) be Jones’s share. Then \(S_m + J_m = 1\), since between them they have 100% of the market. At the end of the (m + 1)-st month, Smith has 90% of his previous customers and 30% of Jones’s previous customers, so

\(S_{m+1} = 0.9S_m + 0.3J_m\)

Similarly,

\(J_{m+1} = 0.1S_m + 0.7J_m\)

We can rewrite these equations in matrix-vector form:

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} S_{m+1}\\ J_{m+1} \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} 0.9 & 0.3 \\ 0.1 & 0.7 \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} S_m \\ J_m \end{matrix} \right ]\)

The matrix \(T = \left [ \begin{matrix} 0.9 & 0.3 \\ 0.1 & 0.7 \end{matrix} \right ]\) is called the transition matrix for this problem: it describes the transition (change) from the state \(\left [ \begin{matrix} S_m \\ J_m \end{matrix} \right ]\) at time m to the state \(\left [ \begin{matrix} S_{m+1}\\ J_{m+1} \end{matrix} \right ]\) at time m + 1.

To answer the questions, we need to determine \(T^6 \left [ \begin{matrix} 0.5 \\ 0.5 \end{matrix} \right ]\) and \(T^m \left [ \begin{matrix} 0.5 \\ 0.5 \end{matrix} \right ]\) for m large.

We could compute \(T^6\) directly, but this approach is not reasonable for calculating \(T^m\) for large values of m. So, we diagonalize T. We find that \(\lambda _1 = 1\) is an eigenvalue of T with eigenvector \(\vec{v_1} = \left [ \begin{matrix} 3\\ 1\end{matrix} \right ]\), and \(\lambda_2 = 0.6\) is the other eigenvalue, with eigenvector \(\vec{v_2} = \left [ \begin{matrix} 1\\ -1\end{matrix} \right ]\). Thus,

\(P = \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{matrix} \right ]\) and \(P^{−1} = \frac{1}{4} \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -3 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

It follows that

\(T^m = PD^{m}P^{−1} = \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} 1^m & 0 \\ 0 & (0.6)^m \end{matrix} \right ] \frac{1}{4} \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -3 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

We could now answer our question directly, but we get a simpler calculation if we observe that the eigenvectors form a basis, so we can write

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} S_0 \\ J_0 \end{matrix} \right ] = c_1 \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] + c_2 \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right ] = P \left [ \begin{matrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Then,

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \end{matrix} \right ] = P^{-1} \left [ \begin{matrix} S_0 \\ J_0 \end{matrix} \right ] = \frac{1}{4} \left [ \begin{matrix} S_0 + J_0 \\ S_0 – 3J_0 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Hence,

\(T^m \left [ \begin{matrix} S_0 \\ J_0 \end{matrix} \right ] = T^m \left( c_1 \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] + c_2 \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right ] \right) \)

\(= c_{1}T^m \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] + c_{2} T^m\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

\(= c_{1} \lambda^{m}_{1} \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] + c_{2} \lambda^{m}_{2}\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right ]\) by Section 6.1 Problem C9 (a)

\(= \frac{1}{4} (S_0 + J_0) \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] + \frac{1}{4} (S_0 – 3J_0) (0.6)^m \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Now \(S_0 = J_0 = 0.5\). When m = 6,

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} S_6 \\ J_6 \end{matrix} \right ] = \frac{1}{4} \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] – \frac{1}{4} (0.6)^6 \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

\(\approx \frac{1}{4} \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 – 0.0467 \\ 1 + 0.0467 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

\(\approx \left [ \begin{matrix} 0.738\\ 0.262 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Thus, after six months, Smith has approximately 73.8% of the market. When m is very large, \((0.6)^m\) is nearly zero, so for m large enough \((m \rightarrow \infty )\), we have \(S_{\infty} = 0.75\) and \(J_{\infty} = 0.25\). Thus, in this problem, Smith’s share approaches 75% as m gets large, but it never gets larger than 75%. Now look carefully: we get the same answer in the long run, no matter what the initial value of \(S_{0}\) and \(J_{0}\) are because \((0.6)^m \rightarrow 0\) and \(S_0 + J_0 = 1\).
