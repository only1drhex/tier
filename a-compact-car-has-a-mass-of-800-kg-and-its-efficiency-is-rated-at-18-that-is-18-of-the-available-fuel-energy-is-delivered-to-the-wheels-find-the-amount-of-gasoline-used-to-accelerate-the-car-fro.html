Guest Question.

A compact car has a mass of 800 kg, and its efficiency is rated at 18%. (That is, 18% of the available fuel energy is delivered to the wheels.) Find the amount of gasoline used to accelerate the car from rest to 27 m/s (60 mi/h). Use the fact that the energy equivalent of 1 gal of gasoline is \( 1.3 \times 10^{8} J \) J.

Admin
Solution

The energy required to accelerate the car from rest to a speed v is equal to its final kinetic energy, \( \frac{1}{2} m v^{2}: \)

\( K=\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}=\frac{1}{2}(800 kg )(27 m / s )^{2}=2.9 \times 10^{5} J \).

If the engine were 100% efficient, each gallon of gasoline would supply \( 1.3 \times 10^{8} J \) of energy. Because the engine is only 18% efficient, each gallon delivers an energy of only \( (0.18)\left(1.3 \times 10^{8} J \right)=2.3 \times 10^{7} J \). Hence, the number of gallons used to accelerate the car is

\( \text { Number of gal }=\frac{2.9 \times 10^{5} J }{2.3 \times 10^{7} J / gal }=0.013 gal \).

Let us estimate that it takes 10 s to achieve the indicated speed. The distance traveled during this acceleration is

\( \Delta x=\bar{v} \Delta t=\frac{v_{x f}+v_{x i}}{2}(\Delta t)=\frac{27 m / s +0}{2}(10 s ) \)

\( =135 m \approx 0.08 mi \).

At a constant cruising speed, 0.013 gal of gasoline is sufficient to propel the car nearly 0.5 mi, over six times farther.
This demonstrates the extreme energy requirements of stopand- start driving.
