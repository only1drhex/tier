Guest Question.

Two identical particles of spin \(\frac{1}{2}\) are enclosed in a one-dimensional box potential of length L with walls at x = 0 and x = L.

(a) Find the energies of the three lowest states.

(b) Then, subjecting the particles to a perturbation

\(\hat{H}_{p}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right)=-V_{0} L^{2} \delta\left(x_{1}-\frac{L}{2}\right) \delta\left(x_{2}-\frac{L}{3}\right)\),

calculate its ground state energy using first-order time-independent perturbation theory.

Admin
Solution

Since the two particles have the same spin, the spin wave function of the system, \(\chi_{s}\left(s_{1}, s_{2}\right)\), must be symmetric, so \(\chi_{s}\) is any one of the triplet states:

\(\chi_{s}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}|1,1\rangle=\left|\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{1}\left|\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{2} \\|1,0\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\left|\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{1}\left|\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{2}+\left|\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{1}\left|\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{2}\right] \\|1,-1\rangle=\left|\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{1}\left|\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{2}\end{array}\right.\) (9.450)

In addition, since this two-particle system is a system of identical fermions, its wave function must be antisymmetric. Since the spin part is symmetric, the spatial part of the wave function has to be antisymmetric:

\(\Psi\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right)=\psi_{A}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right) \chi_{s}\left(s_{1}, s_{2}\right)\); (9.451)

that is,

\(\psi_{A}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\phi_{n_{1}}\left(x_{1}\right) \phi_{n_{2}}\left(x_{2}\right)-\phi_{n_{2}}\left(x_{1}\right) \phi_{n_{1}}\left(x_{2}\right)\right]\)

 

\(=\frac{1}{L}\left[\sin \left(\frac{n_{1} \pi x_{1}}{L}\right) \sin \left(\frac{n_{2} \pi x_{2}}{L}\right)-\sin \left(\frac{n_{2} \hbar x_{1}}{L}\right) \sin \left(\frac{n_{1} \pi x_{2}}{L}\right)\right]\). (9.452)

The energy levels of this two-particle system are

\(E=\frac{\pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{2 m L^{2}}\left(n_{1}^{2}+n_{2}^{2}\right)=E_{0}\left(n_{1}^{2}+n_{2}^{2}\right)\), (9.453)

where \(E_{0}=\pi^{2} \hbar^{2} /\left(2 m L^{2}\right)\). Note that these energy levels are threefold degenerate because of the spin part of the wave function; that is, there are three different spin states that correspond to the same energy level \(\pi^{2} \hbar^{2}\left(n_{1}^{2}+n_{2}^{2}\right) /\left(2 m L^{2}\right)\).

(a) Having written the general expressions for the energies and the wave functions, it is now easy to infer the energy levels and wave functions of the three lowest states. First, we should note that the ground state cannot correspond to \(n_{1}=n_{2}=1\), for the spatial wave function would be zero. The ground state corresponds then to \(n_{1}=1, n_{2}=2\); its energy follows from (9.453),

\(E^{(0)}=E_{0}\left(1^{2}+2^{2}\right)=5 E_{0}=\frac{5 \pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{2 m L^{2}}\), (9.454)

and the wave function \(\psi_{0}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right)\) follows from (9.452).

The first excited state corresponds to \(n_{1}=1, n_{2}=3\). So the wave function \(\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right)\) can be inferred from (9.452) and the energy from (9.453):

\(E^{(1)}=E_{0}\left(1^{2}+3^{2}\right)=10 E_{0}=\frac{5 \pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{m L^{2}}\). (9.455)

The second excited state corresponds to \(n_{1}=2, n_{2}=3\); hence the energy is given by

\(E^{(2)}=13 E_{0}=\frac{13 \pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{2 m L^{2}} \text {. }\) (9.456)

(b) Introducing the perturbation \(\hat{H}_{p}=-V_{0} L^{2} \delta\left(x_{1}-L / 2\right) \delta\left(x_{2}-L / 3\right)\), and since \(\hat{H}_{p}\) is diagonal in the spin space, the ground state energy to first-order perturbation theory is given by

\(E=\frac{5 \pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{2 m L^{2}}+\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right|\psi_{0}\right\rangle\) (9.457)

where

\(\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right| \psi_{0}\right\rangle=\int_{0}^{L} d x_{1} \int_{0}^{L} d x_{2} \psi_{0}^{*}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right) \hat{H}_{p}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right) \psi_{0}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right)\). (9.458)

Since

\(\psi_{0}^{*}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right)=\psi_{0}\left(x_{1}, x_{2}\right)=\frac{1}{L}\left[\sin \left(\frac{\pi x_{1}}{L}\right) \sin \left(\frac{2 \pi x_{2}}{L}\right)-\sin \left(\frac{2 \pi x_{1}}{L}\right) \sin \left(\frac{\pi x_{2}}{L}\right)\right]\), (9.459)

we have

\(\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right| \psi_{0}\right\rangle=-\frac{V_{0} L^{2}}{L^{2}} \int_{0}^{L} d x_{1} \delta\left(x_{1}-\frac{L}{2}\right) \int_{0}^{L} d x_{2} \delta\left(x_{2}-\frac{L}{3}\right)\)

 

\(\times\left[\sin \left(\frac{\pi x_{1}}{L}\right) \sin \left(\frac{2 \pi x_{2}}{L}\right)-\sin \left(\frac{2 \pi x_{1}}{L}\right) \sin \left(\frac{\pi x_{2}}{L}\right)\right]^{2}\)

 

\(=-V_{0}\left[\sin \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \sin \left(\frac{2 \pi}{3}\right)-\sin (\pi) \sin \left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right)\right]^{2}\)

 

\(=-\frac{3}{4} V_{0}\); (9.460)

hence

\(E=\frac{5 \pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{2 m L^{2}}-\frac{3}{4} V_{0}\). (9.461)
