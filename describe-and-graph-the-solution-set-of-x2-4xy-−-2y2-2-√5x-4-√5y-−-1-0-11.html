Guest Question.

Describe and graph the solution set of
\(x^{2}+4 x y-2 y^{2}+2 \sqrt{5} x+4 \sqrt{5} y-1=0\). (11)

Admin
Solution

The equation has the form \(x ^{T} A x + a ^{T} x +f=0\), where
\(x =\left[\begin{array}{l}x \\y\end{array}\right], \quad A=\left[\begin{array}{rr}1 & 2 \\2 & -2\end{array}\right], \quad a =\left[\begin{array}{c}2 \sqrt{5} \\4 \sqrt{5}\end{array}\right], \quad \text { and } \quad f=-1\).
From , we know that \(Q^{T} A Q=D\), where

\(Q=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\left[\begin{array}{rr}1 & 2 \\-2 & 1\end{array}\right] \quad \text { and } \quad D=\left[\begin{array}{rr}-3 & 0 \\0 & 2\end{array}\right]\).

For \(y =[u, v]^{T}\), we make the substitution x = Qy in Eq. (11), obtaining

\(2 v^{2}-3 u^{2}-6 u+8 v-1=0\).

Completing the square, we can express the previous equation as

\(2\left(v^{2}+4 v+4\right)-3\left(u^{2}+2 u+1\right)=6\).

or

\(\frac{(v+2)^{2}}{3}-\frac{(u+1)^{2}}{2}=1\). (12)

From analytic geometry, Eq. (12) defines a hyperbola in the uv-plane, where the center of the hyperbola has coordinates (−1, −2); Fig. 7.1 shows the hyperbola. (For reference, the vertices of the hyperbola have coordinates \((-1,-2 \pm \sqrt{3})\) and the foci have coordinates \((-1,-2 \pm \sqrt{13})\).)

Finally, Fig. 7.2 shows the solution set of Eq. (11), shown in the xy-plane. The hyperbola shown in Fig. 7.2 is a rotation of hyperbola (12) shown in Fig. 7.1.
