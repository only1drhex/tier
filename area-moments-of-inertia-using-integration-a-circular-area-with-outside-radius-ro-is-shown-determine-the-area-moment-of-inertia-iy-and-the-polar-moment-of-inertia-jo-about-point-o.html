Area Moments of Inertia Using Integration

A circular area with outside radius \(r_o\) is shown. Determine the area moment of inertia \(I_y\) and the polar moment of inertia \(J_O\) about point O.

Admin
Solution

Road Map We will use Eq. (\(I_y = \int{\widetilde{x}^2 dA}\)) on p. 534 to determine \(I_y\) and Eq. (\(J_O = \int{\widetilde{r}^2 dA} = I_x + I_y\)

where

\(\widetilde{x}, \widetilde{y}\), and \(\widetilde{r}\) are defined in Fig. 10.3. Note that the definitions of these are identical to those used in Chapter 7. For example, \(\widetilde{x}\) is the distance (i.e., moment arm) from the y axis to the centroid of area element dA.

\(I_x\) is the area moment of inertia about the x axis.

\(I_y\) is the area moment of inertia about the y axis.

\(J_O\) is the polar moment of inertia of the area about point O.

\(I_{xy}\) is the product of inertia of the area about point O.) to determine \(J_O\).

\(I_y\)

Governing Equations & Computation To determine \(I_y\) using a single integration, expressions for dA and \(\widetilde{x}\) are needed, and these must be developed using an area element parallel to the y axis, as shown in Fig. 2:

\(dA = hdx\) and \(\widetilde{x} = x\). (1)

The height h of the area element is related to x and radius \(r_o\) by the Pythagorean theorem \(r^2_o = x^2 + (h/2)^2\), hence

\(h = 2\sqrt{r^2_o – x^2}\). (2)

Substituting Eqs. (1) and (2) into Eq. (10.2), and using a table of integrals or computer software to carry out the integration, the moment of inertia about the y axis is

\(I_y = \int{\widetilde{x}^2 dA} =\int_{-r_o}^{r_o}{x^2 2\sqrt{r^2_o – x^2} dx} \)

 

\(= [\frac{-x}{2}(r^2_o – x^2)^{3/2} + \frac{r^2_o x}{4}\sqrt{r^2_o – x^2} +\frac{r^2_o }{4} \sin^{-1}(\frac{x}{r_o})] |^{r_o}_{-r_o}\)

 

\(= \frac{\pi r^4_o}{4}\). (3)

\(J_O\)

Governing Equations & Computation To determine \(J_O\) using a single integration, an area element having a uniform value of \(\widetilde{r}\) is needed, and this necessitates the use of a thin circular area element as shown in Fig. 3. With r being a radial coordinate, expressions for dA and \(\widetilde{r}\) are obtained from Fig. 3 as

\(dA = 2\pi r dr\) and \(\widetilde{r} = r\). (4)

Substituting Eq. (4) into Eq. (10.3), the polar moment of inertia about point O is

\(J_O = \int{\widetilde{r}^2 dA} = \int_{0}^{r_o}{r^2 2\pi r dr} = \frac{\pi r^2_o}{2}\). (5)

Discussion & Verification

• Because of symmetry of the circular shape in Fig. 1, it is obvious that \(I_x = I_y\).
• Noting from Eq. (10.3) that \(J_O = I_x + I_y\), and that \(I_x = I_y\) as discussed above, we could have obtained Eq. (5) by inspection from the result in Eq. (3), or vice versa.
