Guest Question.

The circuit in Fig. 8.57 is called a General Impedance Converter, or GIC. We wish to develop an expression for the impedance \(Z_\text{eq}\) in terms of \(Z_1, Z_2, Z_3, Z_4\), and \(Z_5\), and then using resistors of equal value and a 1-μF capacitor, create a 1-H equivalent inductance.

Admin
Solution

We simply employ the ideal op-amp assumptions; that is, there is no current entering any op-amp input, and the voltage across any op-amp’s input terminals is zero. As a result, the voltage at both nodes A and C are \(\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}\). Next, we apply KCL at each op-amp input. At node A

 

 

\(\frac{\mathbf{V}_{2}-\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}}{\mathbf{Z}_{2}}=\frac{\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}}{\mathbf{Z}_{1}}\)

 

which yields

 

\(\mathbf{V}_{2}=\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}\left[1+\frac{\mathbf{Z}_{2}}{\mathbf{Z}_{1}}\right]\) 8.47

 

At node C, we find

 

\(\frac{\mathbf{V}_{3}-\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}}{\mathbf{Z}_{4}}=\frac{\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}-\mathbf{V}_{2}}{\mathbf{Z}_{3}}\)

 

Solving for \(\mathbf{V}_{3}\) yields

 

\(\mathbf{V}_{3}=\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}\left[1+\frac{\mathbf{Z}_{4}}{\mathbf{Z}_{3}}\right]-\mathbf{V}_{2}\left[\frac{\mathbf{Z}_{4}}{\mathbf{Z}_{3}}\right]\)

 

Substituting our results from Eq. 8.47, we can express \(\mathbf{V}_{3}\) as

 

\(\mathbf{V}_{3}=\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{in}}\left[1-\frac{\mathbf{Z}_{2} \mathbf{Z}_{4}}{\mathbf{Z}_{1} \mathbf{Z}_{3}}\right]\) 8.48

 

At node E, we write

 

\(\mathbf{I}_{\text {in }}=\frac{\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}-\mathbf{V}_{3}}{\mathbf{Z}_{5}}\)

 

Then using our expression for \(\mathbf{V}_{3}\) in Eq. (8.48), we find that

 

\(\mathbf{I}_{\mathrm{in}}=\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{in}}\left[\frac{\mathbf{Z}_{2} \mathbf{Z}_{4}}{\mathbf{Z}_{1} \mathbf{Z}_{3} \mathbf{Z}_{5}}\right]\)

 

Finally, the impedance of interest is

 

\(\mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{eq}}=\frac{\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{in}}}{\mathbf{I}_{\mathrm{in}}}=\left[\frac{\mathbf{Z}_{1} \mathbf{Z}_{3} \mathbf{Z}_{5}}{\mathbf{Z}_{2} \mathbf{Z}_{4}}\right]\) 8.49

 

Now, if

 

\(\mathbf{Z}_{1}=\mathbf{Z}_{3}=\mathbf{Z}_{5}=\mathbf{Z}_{2}=R\) and \(\mathbf{Z}_{4}=1 / j \omega C\), then \(\mathbf{Z}_{\text {eq }}\) becomes

 

\(\mathbf{Z}_{\mathrm{eq}}=j \omega C R^{2}=j \omega L_{\mathrm{eq}}\)

 

Hence, the value of R necessary to yield a 1 -H inductance is 1000 Ω. At this point, we must address the question: why go to all this trouble just to make inductance? The answer is size and weight. A 1-H inductor would be very large and heavy. The GIC is easy to construct with integrated circuit components, requires very little space, and weighs only a few grams!
