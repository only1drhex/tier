Guest Question.

Determine the deflection equation and the deflection at the middle of the simply supported beam shown in Fig. 15.37(a).

Admin
Solution

We replace the partial downward load of 10 kN/m from x=1 to x=3m by a downward load of 10 kN/m
from x=1 to x=4m and an upward load of 10 kN/m from x=3 to x=4m The replacement loads
added together give the original load. The loads and reactions are shown in Fig. 15.37(b).
We use three singularity functions from Table 15.2. From (b) of the table, the singularity function for
the moment of the reaction R=8.5 kN is \(M_x=P_0 〈x-a〉^1 =8.5 〈x〉^1=8.5x ,\) and for the couple
M= 6 kN.m from (a) of the table is \(M_x=6 〈x-a〉^0 =6 〈x-1〉^0.\) The singularity functions for the bending
moment of the uniform loads at x=1 m and 3 m of -10 kN/m and + 10 kN/m from (c) of the table are \(M_x=-w_0 〈x-a〉^2/2 =-10 〈x-1〉^2/2 and +10 〈x-3〉^2/2.\)
Therefore, for the bending-moment equation in terms of singularity functions, we have

\(M_x= 8.5x + 6 〈x-1〉^0 -\frac{10 〈x-1〉^2}{2} +\frac{10 〈x-3〉^3}{2} \)

Substituting the moment equation into Eq. (15.12), we write

\(EI\frac{d^2 \delta }{dx^2} = 8.5x + 6 〈x-1〉^0 -\frac{10 〈x-1〉^2}{2} +\frac{10 〈x-3〉^2}{2} \)

Multiplying by dx and integrating with respect to x, we have

\(EI \frac{d \delta }{dx} = \frac{8.5x^2}{2} + \frac{6 〈x-1〉^1}{1} -\frac{10 〈x-1〉^3}{6} +\frac{10 〈x-3〉^3}{6} +C_1 \) (a)

 

\(EI\delta= \frac{8.5x^3}{6} + \frac{6 〈x-1〉^2}{2} -\frac{10 〈x-1〉^4}{24} +\frac{10 〈x-3〉^4}{24} +C_1x +C_2 \) (b)

When x=0 , \(\delta =0\) ; therefore, from Eq. (b), o= 0 – 0 – 0 + 0 + \(C_2\) or \(C_2\) = 0.When x=4m , \(\delta\)=0, and from Eq. (b), we write

\(0=\frac{8.5(4)^3}{6} +\frac{6(4 – 1)^2}{2} -\frac{10(4 – 1)^4}{24} -\frac{10(4 – 3)^4}{24} +4C_1 +0 \)

or \(C_1 = -21.08 kN.m^2\)

Dividing by EI and substituting the values of \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) into Eq. (b), we have the following deflection equation.

\(\delta =\frac{1}{EI} [\frac{8.5 x^3}{6} +\frac{6 〈x-1〉^2}{2} -\frac{10 〈x-1〉^4}{24} +\frac{10 〈x-3〉^4}{24} -21.08x ]\)

The maximum deflection occurs where the slope of the deflection curve is zero. The slope equation is a cubic equation and can be solved most easily by trial and error. A trial-and-error solution to find the location of a zero slope yields a value of x= 1.986 m.† The deflection at the middle of the beam at x=2m differs from the maximum deflection by less than 0.01 percent. The deflection at the middle of the beam is as follows:

\(\delta_M =\frac{1}{EI} [\frac{8.5 (2)^3}{6} +\frac{6 〈2-1〉^2}{2} -\frac{10 〈2-1〉^3}{24} – \cancel{\frac{10 〈2-2〉^3}{24}} -21.08(2) ]\)

or \(\delta_M =- \frac{28.2}{EI} kN.m^3\)
