Guest Question.

A mechanical accelerometer7 is an instrument attached to a structure in order to obtain its acceleration. In this
example, the accelerometer is attached to a rocket. The schematic of Figure 4.2 is not to scale and shows the
accelerometer as a structure (with mass, damping, and stiffness properties) that is attached to the rocket. Evaluate
how quickly the accelerometer will provide accurate data on a rocket accelerating at a high rate.

Admin
Solution

Here, a rocket of (assumed constant) mass \(m_{s}\)
is accelerating at a rate of \(\ddot{x}(t)\) due to the thrust force
\(T(t)\). In general, there is a lag in the response time of
the accelerometer such that \(\ddot{y}(t) \neq \ddot{x}(t)\), where \(y(t)\) is the displacement of mass \(m\) measured relative to the rocket.

To derive the equations of motion for the accelerometer and the rocket, free-body diagrams can be drawn for each, assuming that the accelerometer is fixed rigidly to the rocket. Applying Newton’s second law, the equation

of motion for the accelerometer mass \(m\) is

\(m \frac{d^{2}}{d t^{2}}(y+x)+c y+k y=0\),

where the only forces on the mass are due to the accelerometer
stiffness and damping, and the inertia force of the accelerating rocket.
It is assumed that the only force acting on the rocket is the thrust \(T(t)\). The forces ignored are those due to aerodynamics
and the elastic vibration of the structure itself.
The thrust-driven equation of motion for the rocket of
mass \(m_{s}\) is then

\(m_{s} \ddot{x}=T(t)\).

Substituting \(\ddot{x}\) from Equation 4.9 into Equation 4.8 results in a single equation for the accelerometer response,

\(m \ddot{y}+c \dot{y}+k y=-\frac{m}{m_{s}} T(t)\).

Dividing by \(m\), Equation 4.10 becomes

\(\ddot{y}+2 \zeta \omega_{n} \dot{y}+\omega_{n}^{2} y=-\frac{T(t)}{m_{s}}\),

with the initial conditions given by \(y(0)=y_{0}\) and \(\dot{y}(0)=\) \(v_{0}\). Taking the Laplace transform of each side, we find

\(\begin{gathered}{\left[s^{2} Y(s)-s y_{0}-v_{0}\right]+2 \zeta \omega_{n}\left[s Y(s)-y_{0}\right]} \\+\omega_{n}^{2} Y(s)=-\frac{1}{m_{s}} T (s)\end{gathered}\)

To continue, the thrust needs to be specified. Assuming that the thrust acts nearly instantaneously, it can be approximated by a step function of magnitude \(P\) ,

\(\begin{aligned}&T(t)=P u(t) \\&T(s)=P \frac{1}{s}\end{aligned}\)

where the unit step function \(u(t)=1 \text { for } t \geq 0\) and zero for \(t<0\) Then, combining like terms in Equation
4.11 leads to the expression for the transform of the accelerometer response,

\(Y(s)=\frac{-P / m_{s}+s\left(s+2 \zeta \omega_{n}\right) y_{0}+s v_{0}}{s\left(s^{2}+2 \zeta \omega_{n} s+\omega_{n}^{2}\right)}\)

As a numerical example, assume that the initial conditions are zero, that is, \(y_{0}\) = 0 and \(v_{0}\) = 0, and that \(2 \zeta \omega_{n}=8 rad / s\) and \(\omega_{n}^{2}=15( rad / s )^{2}\). With these values,
the system is slightly overdamped, with ζ = 1.03,
putting it just above critical damping. Equation 4.12
becomes

\(\begin{aligned}Y(s) &=\frac{-P / m_{s}}{s\left(s^{2}+8 s+15\right)} \\&=\frac{-P / m_{s}}{\varepsilon(s+3)(s+5)}\end{aligned}\)

To invert \(Y(s)\) via transform tables, we expand the above
using partial fractions,

\(Y(s)=\frac{p_{1}}{s}+\frac{p_{2}}{s+5}+\frac{p_{3}}{s+3}\)

and express this equation in the form of Equation 4.13
with the numerator set equal to \(P / m_{s}\). The\(p_{i}\) are found, yielding

\(Y(s)=-\frac{1}{m_{s}}\left[\frac{P / 15}{s}+\frac{P / 10}{s+5}+\frac{-P / 6}{s+3}\right]\)

From the Laplace transform tables in Appendix A, the
inverse transform of both sides yields

\(y(t)=-\frac{1}{m_{s}}\left[\frac{P}{15} u(t)+\frac{P}{10} e^{-5 t}-\frac{P}{6} e^{-3 t}\right]\)

This equation tells us that as \(t\) increases, the second and third terms representing the transient response decay and \(y(t) \rightarrow-P / 15 m_{o}\). As expected, for an overdamped
system there are no oscillations, just a decay to zero. The first term is the steady-state response to the loading. Once \(y(t)\) is known, the steady-state acceleration of the rocket is found to be \(\ddot{y}(t)\).
