LAWN SPRINKLER: ANALYSIS USING FIXED CONTROL VOLUME

A small lawn sprinkler is shown in the sketch at right. At an inlet gage pressure of 20 kPa, the total volume flow rate of water through the sprinkler is 7.5 liters per minute and it rotates at 30 rpm. The diameter of each jet is 4 mm. Calculate the jet speed relative to each sprinkler nozzle. Evaluate the friction torque at the sprinkler pivot.

Admin
Solution

Given: Small lawn sprinkler as shown.


Find:

(a) Jet speed relative to each nozzle.
(b) Friction torque at pivot.
Solution: Apply continuity and angular momentum equations using fixed control volume enclosing sprinkler arms.
Governing equations:

\(\cancel{\frac{\partial}{ \partial t}}^{=0(1)} \int_{\mathrm{CV}} \rho d \forall+\int_{CS} \rho \vec V. d\vec A=0\)

 

\(\vec{r} \times \vec{F}_{s}+\int_{\mathrm{CV}} \vec{r} \times \vec{g} \rho d \forall+\vec{T}_{\text {shaft }}=\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int_{\mathrm{CV}} \vec{r} \times \vec{V} \rho d \forall+\int_{\mathrm{CS}} \vec{r} \times \vec{V} \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}\) (1)

where all velocities are measured relative to the inertial coordinates \(X Y Z\).
Assumptions:

(1) Incompressible flow.
(2) Uniform flow at each section.
(3) \(\vec{\omega}=\) constant.
From continuity, the jet speed relative to the nozzle is given by
\(\begin{aligned}V_{\mathrm{rel}} &=\frac{Q}{2 A_{\mathrm{jet}}}=\frac{Q}{2} \frac{4}{\pi D_{\mathrm{jet}}^{2}} \\&=\frac{1}{2} \times 7.5 \frac{L}{\mathrm{~min}} \times \frac{4}{\pi} \frac{1}{(4)^{2} \mathrm{~mm}^{2}} \times \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{1000 \mathrm{~L}} \times 10^{6} \frac{\mathrm{mm}^{2}}{\mathrm{~m}^{2}} \times \frac{\mathrm{min}}{60 \mathrm{~s}} \end{aligned}\)

 

\(V_{\mathrm{rel}} =4.97 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\) \(\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad V_{rel}}\)

Consider terms in the angular momentum equation separately. Since atmospheric pressure acts on the entire control surface, and the pressure force at the inlet causes no moment about \(O, \vec{r} \times \vec{F}_{s}=0\). The moments of the body (i.e., gravity) forces in the two arms are equal and opposite and hence the second term on the left side of the equation is zero. The only external torque acting on the CV is friction in the pivot. It opposes the motion, so

\(\vec{T}_{\text {shaft }}=-T_{f} \hat{K}\) (2)

Our next task is to determine the two angular momentum terms on the right side of Eq. 1. Consider the unsteady term: This is the rate of change of angular momentum in the control volume. It is clear that although the position \(\vec{r}\) and velocity \(\vec{V}\) of fluid particles are functions of time in \(X Y Z\) coordinates, because the sprinkler rotates at constant speed the control volume angular momentum is constant in \(X Y Z\) coordinates, so this term is zero; however, as an exercise in manipulating vector quantities, let us derive this result. Before we can evaluate the control volume integral, we need to develop expressions for the instantaneous position vector, \(\vec{r}\), and velocity vector, \(\vec{V}\) (measured relative to the fixed coordinate system \(X Y Z\) ) of each element of fluid in the control volume. \(O A\) lies in the \(X Y\) plane; \(A B\) is inclined at angle \(\alpha\) to the \(X Y\) plane; point \(B^{\prime}\) is the projection of point \(B\) on the \(X Y\) plane.

We assume that the length, \(L\), of the tip \(A B\) is small compared with the length, \(R\), of the horizontal arm \(O A\). Consequently we neglect the angular momentum of the fluid in the tips compared with the angular momentum in the horizontal arms.

 

Consider flow in the horizontal tube \(O A\) of length \(R\). Denote the radial distance from \(O\) by \(r\). At any point in the tube the fluid velocity relative to fixed coordinates \(X Y Z\) is the sum of the velocity relative to the tube \(\vec{V}_{t}\) and the tangential velocity \(\vec{\omega} \times \vec{r}\). Thus

\(\vec{V}=\hat{I}\left(V_{t} \cos \theta-r \omega \sin \theta\right)+\hat{J}\left(V_{t} \sin \theta+r \omega \cos \theta\right)\)

(Note that \(\theta\) is a function of time.) The position vector is

\(\vec{r}=\hat{I} r \cos \theta+\hat{J} r \sin \theta\)

and

\(\vec{r} \times \vec{V}=\hat{K}\left(r^{2} \omega \cos ^{2} \theta+r^{2} \omega \sin ^{2} \theta\right)=\hat{K} r^{2} \omega\)

Then

\(\int_{\forall_{O A}} \vec{r} \times \vec{V} \rho d \forall=\int_{O}^{R} \hat{K} r^{2} \omega \rho A d r=\hat{K} \frac{R^{3} \omega}{3} \rho A\)

and

\(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int_{\forall \circ A} \vec{r} \times \vec{V} \rho d \forall=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\hat{K} \frac{R^{3} \omega}{3} \rho A\right]=0\) (3)

 

where \(A\) is the cross-sectional area of the horizontal tube. Identical results are obtained for the other horizontal tube in the control volume. We have confirmed our insight that the angular momentum within the control volume does not change with time.

Now we need to evaluate the second term on the right, the flux of momentum across the control surface. There are three surfaces through which we have mass and therefore momentum flux: the supply line (for which \(\vec{r} \times \vec{V}=0\) ) because \(\vec{r}=0\) and the two nozzles. Consider the nozzle at the end of branch \(O A B\). For \(L \ll R\), we have

\(\vec{r}_{\mathrm{jet}}=\left.\vec{r}_{B} \approx \vec{r}\right|_{r=R}=\left.(\hat{I} r \cos \theta+\hat{J} r \sin \theta)\right|_{r=R}=\hat{I} R \cos \theta+\hat{J} R \sin \theta\)

and for the instantaneous jet velocity \(\vec{V}_{j}\) we have

\(\begin{aligned}\vec{V}_{j} &=\vec{V}_{\mathrm{rel}}+\vec{V}_{\mathrm{tip}}=\hat{I} V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha \sin \theta-\hat{J} V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha \cos \theta+\hat{K} V_{\mathrm{rel}} \sin \alpha-\hat{I} \omega R \sin \theta+\hat{J} \omega R \cos \theta \\\vec{V}_{j} &=\hat{I}\left(V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha-\omega R\right) \sin \theta-\hat{J}\left(V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha-\omega R\right) \cos \theta+\hat{K} V_{\mathrm{rel}} \sin \alpha \\\vec{r}_{B} \times \vec{V}_{j} &=\hat{I} R V_{\mathrm{rel}} \sin \alpha \sin \theta-\vec{J} R V_{\mathrm{rel}} \sin \alpha \cos \theta-\hat{K} R\left(V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha-\omega R\right)\left(\sin ^{2} \theta+\cos ^{2} \theta\right) \\\vec{r}_{B} \times \vec{V}_{j} &=\hat{I} R V_{\mathrm{rel}} \sin \alpha \sin \theta-\vec{J} R V_{\mathrm{rel}} \sin \alpha \cos \theta-\hat{K} R\left(V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha-\omega R\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

The flux integral evaluated for flow crossing the control surface at location \(B\) is then

\(\int_{\mathrm{CS}} \vec{r} \times \vec{V}_{j} \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}=\left[\hat{I} R V_{\mathrm{rel}} \sin \alpha \sin \theta-\hat{J} R V_{\mathrm{rel}} \sin \alpha \cos \theta-\hat{K} R\left(V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha-\omega R\right)\right] \rho \frac{Q}{2}\)

The velocity and radius vectors for flow in the left arm must be described in terms of the same unit vectors used for the right arm. In the left arm the \(\hat{I}\) and \(\hat{J}\) components of the cross product are of opposite sign, \(\operatorname{since} \sin (\theta+\pi)=-\sin (\theta)\) and \(\cos (\theta+\pi)=-\cos (\theta)\). Thus for the complete CV,

\(\int_{\mathrm{CS}} \vec{r} \times \vec{V}_{j} \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}=-\hat{K} R\left(V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha-\omega R\right) \rho Q\) (4)

Substituting terms (2), (3), and (4) into Eq. 1, we obtain

\(-T_{f} \hat{K}=-\hat{K} R\left(V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha-\omega R\right) \rho Q\)

or

\(T_{f}=R\left(V_{\mathrm{rel}} \cos \alpha-\omega R\right) \rho Q\)

This expression indicates that when the sprinkler runs at constant speed the friction torque at the sprinkler pivot just balances the torque generated by the angular momentum of the two jets.
From the data given,

\(\omega R=30 \frac{\mathrm{rev}}{\mathrm{min}} \times 150 \mathrm{~mm} \times 2 \pi \frac{\mathrm{rad}}{\mathrm{rev}} \times \frac{\min }{60 \mathrm{~s}} \times \frac{\mathrm{m}}{1000 \mathrm{~mm}}=0.471 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

Substituting gives

\(\begin{gathered}T_{f}=150 \mathrm{~mm} \times\left(4.97 \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}} \times \cos 30^{\circ}-0.471 \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}}\right) 999 \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^{3}} \times 7.5 \frac{\mathrm{L}}{\mathrm{min}} \\\times \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{1000 \mathrm{~L}} \times \frac{\mathrm{min}}{60 \mathrm{~s}} \times \frac{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{3}}{\mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}} \times \frac{\mathrm{m}}{1000 \mathrm{~mm}}\end{gathered} \)

\(T_{f}=0.0718 \mathrm{~N} \cdot \mathrm{m}\) \(\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad T_{f}}\)

 

This problem illustrates use of the angular momentum principle for an inertial control volume. Note that in this example the fluid particle position vector \(\vec r\) and velocity vector \(\vec V\) are timedependent (through Î¸) in XYZ coordinates. This problem will be solved again using a noninertial (rotating) xyz coordinate system in Example 4.15 (on the Web).
