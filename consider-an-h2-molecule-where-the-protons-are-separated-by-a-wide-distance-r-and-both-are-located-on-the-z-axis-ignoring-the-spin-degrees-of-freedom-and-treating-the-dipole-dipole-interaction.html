Guest Question.

Consider an \(H _{2}\) molecule where the protons are separated by a wide distance R and both are located on the z-axis. Ignoring the spin degrees of freedom and treating the dipole–dipole interaction as a perturbation, use perturbation theory to estimate an upper limit for the ground state energy of this molecule.

Admin
Solution

Assuming the protons are fixed in space and separated by a distance R, we can write the Hamiltonian of this molecule as follows:

\(\hat{H}=\hat{H}_{0}+\hat{H}_{p}=\hat{H}_{0}^{A}+\hat{H}_{0}^{B}+\hat{H}_{p}\), (9.430)

where \(\hat{H}_{0}^{A}\) and \(\hat{H}_{0}^{B}\) are the unperturbed Hamiltonians of atoms A and B,and \(\hat{H}_{p}\) is

\(\hat{H}_{p}=\frac{e^{2}}{R}+\frac{e^{2}}{\left|\vec{R}+\vec{r}_{A}-\vec{r}_{B}\right|}-\frac{e^{2}}{\left|\vec{R}+\vec{r}_{A}\right|}-\frac{e^{2}}{\left|\vec{R}-\vec{r}_{B}\right|}\), (9.431)

where \(\vec{r}_{A}\) and \(\vec{r}_{B}\) are the position vectors of the electrons of atoms A and B as measured from the protons. If \(R \gg a_{0}\), where \(a_{0}=\hbar^{2} /\left(\mu e^{2}\right)\) is the Bohr radius, an expansion of (9.431) in powers of \(\vec{r}_{A} / R\) and \(\vec{r}_{A} / R\) yields, to first nonvanishing terms, an expression of the order of \(1 / R^{3}\) :

\(\hat{H}_{p}=\frac{e^{2}}{R^{3}}\left[\vec{r}_{A} \cdot \vec{r}_{B}-3\frac{\left(\vec{r}_{A}\cdot\vec{R}\right)\left(\vec{r}_{B} \cdot \vec{R}\right)}{R^{2}}\right]\). (9.432)

This is the dipole–dipole interaction energy between the dipole moments of the two atoms. Since \(\vec{R}=R \hat{z}\) we can write (9.432) as

\(\hat{H}_{p}=\frac{e^{2}}{R^{3}}\left(\hat{X}_{A} \hat{X}_{B}+\hat{Y}_{A}\hat{Y}_{B}-2\hat{Z}_{A}\hat{Z}_{B}\right)\). (9.433)

The ground state energy and wave function of the (unperturbed) molecule are

\(E_{0}=E_{0}^{A}+E_{0}^{B}=2 E_{100}=-\frac{e^{2}}{a_{0}}\), \(\left|\phi_{0}\right\rangle=\left|\phi_{0}^{A}\right\rangle\left|\phi_{0}^{B}\right\rangle=|100\rangle_{A}|100\rangle_{B}\). (9.434)

The first-order correction to the molecule’s energy,\(E^{(1)}=\left\langle\phi_{0}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right|\phi_{0}\right\rangle\), is given by

\(E^{(1)}=\frac{e^{2}}{R^{3}}\left(\left\langle\phi_{0}^{A}\left|\hat{X}_{A}\right| \phi_{0}^{A}\right\rangle\left\langle\phi_{0}^{B}\left|\hat{X}_{B}\right|\phi_{0}^{B}\right\rangle+\left\langle\phi_{0}^{A}\left|\hat{Y}_{A}\right|\phi_{0}^{A}\right\rangle\left\langle\phi_{0}^{B}\left|\hat{Y}_{B}\right| \phi_{0}^{B}\right\rangle\right.\)

 

\(\left.-2\left\langle\phi_{0}^{A}\left|\hat{Z}_{A}\right| \phi_{0}^{A}\right\rangle\left\langle\phi_{0}^{B}\left|\hat{Z}_{B}\right| \phi_{0}^{B}\right\rangle\right)\). (9.435)

Since the operators \(\hat{X}, \hat{Y}\), and \(\hat{Z}\) are odd and the states \(\left|\phi_{0}^{A}\right\rangle\)and\(\left|\phi_{0}^{B}\right\rangle\) are spherically symmetric, then all the terms in (9.435) are zero; hence \(E^{(1)}=0\).

\(E^{(2)}=\sum_{n, l, m ; n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime} \neq 1,0,0} \frac{\left|\left\langle n, l, m ; n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right|\phi_{0}\right\rangle\right|^{2}}{2 E_{100}-E_{n}-E_{n^{\prime}}}\), (9.436)

where

\(\left\langle n, l, m ; n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right|\phi_{0}\right\rangle=\frac{e^{2}}{R^{3}}\left(\left\langle n, l, m\left|X_{A}\right| 1,0,0\right\rangle_{A}\left\langle n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime}\left|X_{B}\right| 1,0,0\right\rangle_{B}\right.\)

 

\(+\left\langle n, l, m\left|Y_{A}\right| 1,0,0\right\rangle_{A}\left\langle n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime}\left|Y_{B}\right| 1,0,0\right\rangle_{B}\)

 

\(\left.+-2\left\langle n, l, m\left|Z_{A}\right| 1,0,0\right\rangle_{A}\left\langle n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime}\left|Z_{B}\right| 1,0,0\right\rangle_{B}\right)\). (9.437)

The terms of this expression are nonzero only if \(l=l^{\prime}=1\), since the \(\hat{X}, \hat{Y}\), and \(\hat{Z}\) operators are proportional to \(Y_{1 m}\). We can evaluate \(E^{(2)}\) using a crude approximation where we assume the denominator of (9.436) is constant and we take \(E_{n} \simeq E_{n^{\prime}}\). Note that, for \(n \geq 2\), we have \(E_{n l m} \geq E_{200}\). In this case we can rewrite (9.436) as

\(E^{(2)} \leq \frac{1}{2\left(E_{100}-E_{200}\right)} \sum_{n, l, m ; n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime} \neq 1,0,0}\left|\left\langle n, l, m ; n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right| \phi_{0}\right\rangle\right|^{2}\); (9.438)

since the diagonal term is zero \(\text { (i.e., }\left\langle 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right| 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\right\rangle=0 \text { ), }\) we have

\(\sum_{n,l,m;n^{\prime},l^{\prime},m^{\prime}}\left|\left\langle n, l, m;n^{\prime},l^{\prime},m^{\prime}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right| \phi_{0}\right\rangle\right|^{2}\)

 

\(=\sum_{n, l, m;n^{\prime},l^{\prime},m^{\prime}}\left\langle 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right| n, l, m ; n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime}\right\rangle\left\langle n, l, m ; n^{\prime}, l^{\prime}, m^{\prime}\left|\hat{H}_{p}\right| 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\right\rangle\)

 

\(=\left\langle 1,0,0;1,0,0\left|\left(\hat{H}_{p}\right)^{2}\right| 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\right\rangle\)

 

\(=\frac{e^{4}}{R^{6}}\left\langle 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\left|\left(X_{A} X_{B}+Y_{A} Y_{B}-2 Z_{A} Z_{B}\right)^{2}\right| 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\right\rangle\). (9.439)

The calculation of \(\left\langle 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\left|\left(X_{A} X_{B}+Y_{A} Y_{B}-2 Z_{A} Z_{B}\right)^{2}\right| 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\right\rangle\) can be madeeasier by the use of symmetry. Due to spherical symmetry, the cross terms are zero:

\(\left\langle X_{A} Y_{A}\right\rangle_{A}=\left\langle X_{A} Z_{A}\right\rangle_{A}=\left\langle Y_{A} Z_{A}\right\rangle_{A}=\left\langle X_{B} Y_{B}\right\rangle_{B}=\cdots=\left\langle Y_{B} Z_{B}\right\rangle_{B}=0\), (9.440)

while the others are given as follows (see (9.45)):

\(\left\langle100\left|\hat{Z}^{2}\right|100\right\rangle=\frac{1}{4 \pi} \int_{0}^{\infty} r^{4} R_{10}^{2}(r) d r \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin \theta \cos ^{2} \theta d \theta \int_{0}^{2 \pi} d \varphi=a_{0}^{2}\). (9.45)

 

\(\left\langle X_{A}^{2}\right\rangle_{A}=\left\langle Y_{A}^{2}\right\rangle_{A}=\left\langle Z_{A}^{2}\right\rangle_{A}=\left\langle X_{B}^{2}\right\rangle_{B}=\left\langle Y_{B}^{2}\right\rangle_{B}=\left\langle Z_{B}^{2}\right\rangle_{B}=a_{0}^{2},\), (9.441)

where \(\langle C\rangle_{A}=\left\langle\phi_{0}^{A}|C| \phi_{0}^{A}\right\rangle\) and \(\langle D\rangle_{B}=\left\langle\phi_{0}^{B}|D|\phi_{0}^{B}\right\rangle\). We can thus obtain

\(\left\langle 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\left|\left(X_{A} X_{B}+Y_{A} Y_{B}-2 Z_{A} Z_{B}\right)^{2}\right| 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\right\rangle=6 a_{0}^{4}\). (9.442)

Inserting (9.442) into (9.439) and then the resultant expression into (9.438), we get

\(E^{(2)}\leq\frac{\left\langle1,0,0;1,0,0\left|\left(\hat{H}_{p}\right)^{2}\right| 1,0,0 ; 1,0,0\right\rangle}{2\left(E_{100}-E_{200}\right)}=\frac{3 e^{4} a_{0}^{4}}{R^{6}} \frac{1}{E_{100}-E_{200}}\), (9.443)

or

\(E^{(2)} \leq-\frac{8 e^{2} a_{0}^{5}}{R^{6}}\), (9.444)

because \(E_{100}=-e^{2} / 2 a_{0}\) and \(E_{200}=-e^{2} / 8 a_{0}\). Finally, the upper limit for the ground state
energy of this molecule to second-order perturbation theory is given by

\(E_{2} \leq 2 E_{100}-\frac{8 e^{2} a_{0}^{5}}{R^{6}} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad E_{2} \leq-\frac{e^{2}}{a_{0}}\left(1+8 \frac{a_{0}^{6}}{R^{6}}\right)\). (9.445)
