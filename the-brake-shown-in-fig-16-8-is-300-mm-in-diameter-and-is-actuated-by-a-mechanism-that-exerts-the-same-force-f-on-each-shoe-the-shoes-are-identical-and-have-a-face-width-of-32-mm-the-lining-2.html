Guest Question.

The brake shown in Fig. 16–8 is 300 mm in diameter and is actuated by a mechanism that exerts the same force F on each shoe. The shoes are identical and have a face width of 32 mm. The lining is a molded asbestos having a coefficient of friction of 0.32 and a pressure limitation of 1000 kPa. Estimate the maximum
(a) Actuating force F.
(b) Braking capacity.
(c) Hinge-pin reactions.

Admin
Solution

(a) The right-hand shoe is self-energizing, and so the force F is found on the basis that the maximum pressure will occur on this shoe. Here \(θ_{1} = 0°, θ_{2} = 126°, θ_{a} = 90°, and sin θ_{a} = 1\). Also,

\(a =\sqrt{(112)^{2} + (50)^{2}} = 122.7\) mm

Integrating Eq. (16–2) from 0 to\( θ_{2}\) yields

\(M_{f} =\int{f dN(r − a cos θ) }=\frac{f p_{a}br}{sin θ_{a}} \int _{θ_{1}}^{θ_{2}}{sin θ(r − a cos θ) dθ}\) (16–2)

\(M_{f} =\frac{f p_{a}br}{sin θ_{a}} \left[ \left(−r cos θ\right)^{θ_{2}}_{0} − a \left(\frac{1}{2} sin^{2} θ\right)^{ θ_{2}}_{0}\right]\)

\(=\frac{f p_{a}br}{sin θ_{a}} \left(r − r cos θ_{2} −\frac{a}{2} sin^{2} θ_{2}\right)\)

Changing all lengths to meters, we have

\(M_{f} = (0.32)[1000(10)^{3}](0.032)(0.150)×\left[ 0.150 − 0.150 cos 126°−\left(\frac{0.1227}{2}\right) sin^{2} 126°\right]\)

= 304 N · m

The moment of the normal forces is obtained from Eq. (16–3). Integrating from 0 to \(θ_{2}\) gives

\(M_{N} =\int{dN(a sin θ) }=\frac{p_{a}bra}{sin θ_{a}} \int_{θ_{1}}^{θ_{2}}{sin^{2} θ dθ}\) (16–3)

\(M_{N} =\frac{p_{a}bra}{sin θ_{a}} \left(\frac{θ}{2} −\frac{1}{4} sin 2θ\right)^{θ_{2}}_{0}\)
\(=\frac{p_{a}bra}{sin θ_{a}} \left(\frac{θ_{2}}{2} −\frac{1}{4} sin 2θ_{2}\right)\)

\(= [1000(10)^{3}](0.032)(0.150)(0.1227)\left\{ \frac{π}{2} \frac{126}{180}−\frac{1}{4} sin [(2)(126°)]\right\}\)
‘
= 788 N · m

From Eq. (16–4), the actuating force is

\(F =\frac{M_{N} − M_{f}}{c}\) (16–4)

\(F =\frac{M_{N} − M_{f}}{c} =\frac{788 − 304}{100 + 112} = 2.28\) kN

(b) From Eq. (16–6), the torque applied by the right-hand shoe is

\(T =\int_{f r dN} =\frac{f p_{a}br^{2}}{sin θ_{a}} \int_{θ_{1}}^{θ_{2}}{sin θ dθ}\)
\(=\frac{f p_{a}br^{2}(cos θ_{1} − cos θ_{2})}{sin θ_{a}}\) (16–6)

\(T_{R} =\frac{f p_{a}br^{2}(cos θ_{1} − cos θ_{2})}{sin θ_{a}}\)

\(=\frac{0.32[1000(10)^{3}](0.032)(0.150)^{2}(cos 0° − cos 126°)}{sin 90°} = 366\) N · m

The torque contributed by the left-hand shoe cannot be obtained until we learn its maximum operating pressure. Equations (16–2) and (16–3) indicate that the frictional and normal moments are proportional to this pressure. Thus, for the left-hand shoe,

\(M_{N} =\int{dN(a sin θ) }=\frac{p_{a}bra}{sin θ_{a}} \int_{θ_{1}}^{θ_{2}}{sin^{2} θ dθ}\) (16–3)

\(M_{N} =\frac{788p_{a}}{1000} M_{f} =\frac{304p_{a}}{1000}\)

Then, from Eq. (16–7),

\(F =\frac{M_{N} + M_{f}}{c}\) (16–7)

\(F =\frac{M_{N} + M_{f}}{c}\)

or

\(2.28 =\frac{(788/1000)p_{a} + (304/1000)p_{a}}{100 + 112}\)

Solving gives \(p_{a}\) = 443 kPa. Then, from Eq. (16–6), the torque on the left-hand shoe is

\(T_{L} =\frac{f p_{a}br^{2}(cos θ_{1} − cos θ_{2})}{sin θ_{a}}\)

Since \(sin θ_{a} = sin 90°= 1\), we have

\(T_{L} = 0.32[443(10)^{3}](0.032)(0.150)^{2}(cos 0° − cos 126°) = 162\) N · m

The braking capacity is the total torque:

\(T = T_{R} + T_{L} = 366 + 162 = 528\) N · m

(c) In order to find the hinge-pin reactions, we note that \(sin θ_{a}\) = 1 and \(θ_{1}\) = 0. Then Eq. (16–8) gives

\(A =\sum\limits_{θ_{1}}^{θ_{2}}{θ_{1} sin θ cos θ dθ}=(\frac{1}{2} sin^{2} θ)^{θ_{2}}{θ_{1}}\)

\(A =\sum\limits_{θ_{1}}^{θ_{2}}{sin^{2} θ dθ}=(\frac{θ}{2}-\frac{1}{4}sin 2θ)^{θ_{2}}{θ_{1}}\) (16-8)

\(A =\frac{1}{2} sin^{2} θ_{2} =\frac{1}{2} sin^{2} 126° = 0.3273\)
\(B =\frac{θ_{2}}{2} − \frac{1}{4} sin 2θ_{2} =\frac{π(126)}{2(180)} −\frac{1}{4} sin [(2)(126° )] = 1.3373\)

Also, let

\(D =\frac{p_{a}br}{sin θ_{a}} =\frac{1000(0.032)(0.150)}{1} = 4.8\) kN

where \(p_{a}\) = 1000 kPa for the right-hand shoe. Then, using Eq. (16–9), we have

\(R_{x} =\frac{p_{a}br}{sin θ_{a}}(A − f B) − F_{x}\)
\(R_{y} =\frac{p_{a}br}{sin θ_{a}} (B + f A) − F_{y}\) (16–9)

\(R_{x} = D(A − f B) − F_{x} = 4.8[0.3273 − 0.32(1.3373)] − 2.28 sin 24°\)
= −1.410 kN
\(R_{y} = D(B + f A) − F_{y} = 4.8[1.3373 + 0.32(0.3273)] − 2.28 cos 24°\)
= 4.839 kN

The resultant on this hinge pin is

\(R =\sqrt{(−1.410)^{2} + (4.839)^{2}} = 5.04\) kN

The reactions at the hinge pin of the left-hand shoe are found using Eqs. (16–10) for a pressure of 443 kPa. They are found to be \(R_{x}\) = 0.678 kN and \(R_{y}\) = 0.538 kN.

\(R_{x} =\frac{p_{a}br}{sin θ_{a}}(A + f B) − F_{x}\)
\(R_{y} =\frac{p_{a}br}{sin θ_{a}} (B – f A) − F_{y}\) (16–10)

\(R =\sqrt{(0.678)^{2} + (0.538)^{2}} = 0.866\) kN
The reactions for both hinge pins, together with their directions, are shown in Fig. 16–9.

This example dramatically shows the benefit to be gained by arranging the shoes to be self-energizing. If the left-hand shoe were turned over so as to place the hinge pin at the top, it could apply the same torque as the right-hand shoe. This would make the capacity of the brake (2)(366) = 732 N · m instead of the present 528 N · m, a 30 percent improvement. In addition, some of the friction material at the heel could be eliminated without seriously affecting the capacity, because of the low pressure in this area.
This change might actually improve the overall design because the additional rim exposure would improve the heat-dissipation capacity.
