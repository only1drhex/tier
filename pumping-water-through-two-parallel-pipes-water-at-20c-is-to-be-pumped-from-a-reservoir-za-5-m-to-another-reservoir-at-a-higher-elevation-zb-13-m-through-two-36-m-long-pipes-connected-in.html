Pumping Water through Two Parallel Pipes

Water at 20¬∞C is to be pumped from a reservoir (\(Z_{A}\) = 5 m) to another reservoir at a higher elevation (\(Z_{B}\) = 13 m) through two 36-m-long pipes connected in parallel, as shown in Fig. 8‚Äì51. The pipes are made of commercial steel, and the diameters of the two pipes are 4 and 8 cm. Water is to be pumped by a 70 percent efficient motor‚Äìpump combination that draws 8 kW of electric power during operation. The minor losses and the head loss in pipes that connect the parallel pipes to the two reservoirs are considered to be negligible. Determine the total flow rate between the reservoirs and the flow rate through each of the parallel pipes.

Solution

SOLUTION The pumping power input to a piping system with two parallel pipes is given. The flow rates are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 The flow is steady (since the reservoirs are large) and incompressible. 2 The entrance effects are negligible, and thus the flow is fully developed. 3 The elevations of the reservoirs remain constant. 4 The minor losses and the head loss in pipes other than the parallel pipes are negligible. 5 Flows through both pipes are turbulent (to be verified).

Analysis This problem cannot be solved directly since the velocities (or flow rates) in the pipes are not known. Therefore, we would normally use a trial-and-error approach here. However, equation solvers are widely available, and thus, we simply set up the equations to be solved by an equation solver. The useful head supplied by the pump to the fluid is determined from

 

\(\dot{W}_{\text {elect }}=\frac{\rho \dot{V} g h_{\text {pump, } u}}{\eta_{\text {pump-motor }}} \rightarrow 8000 W =\frac{\left(998 kg / m ^{3}\right) \dot{V}\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right) h_{\text {pump, } u}}{0.70}\) (1)

 

We choose points A and B at the free surfaces of the two reservoirs. Noting that the fluid at both points is open to the atmosphere (and thus \(P_{A}=P_{B}=P_{atm}\)) and that the fluid velocities at both points are nearly zero \(\left(V_{A} \approx V_{B} \approx 0\right)\) since the reservoirs are large, the energy equation for a control volume between these two points simplifies to

 

\(\frac{P_{A}}{p g}+\alpha_{A} \frac{V_{A}^{2}}{2 g}^{0} + z_{A}+h_{\text {pump }, u}=\frac{P_{B}}{p g}+\alpha_{B} \frac{V_{B}^{2}}{2 g}^{0}+z_{B}+h_{L} \)

 

or

 

\(h_{\text {pump, u }}=\left(z_{B}-z_{A}\right)+h_{L}\)

 

or

 

\(h_{\text {pump }, u}=(13 m -5 m )+h_{L}\) (2)

 

where

 

\(h_{L}=h_{L, 1}=h_{L, 2}\) (3) (4)

 

We designate the 4-cm-diameter pipe by 1 and the 8-cm-diameter pipe by 2. Equations for the average velocity, the Reynolds number, the friction factor, and the head loss in each pipe are

 

\(V_{1}=\frac{\dot{V}_{1}}{A_{c, 1}}=\frac{\dot{V}_{1}}{\pi D_{1}^{2} / 4} \quad \rightarrow \quad V_{1}=\frac{\dot{V}_{1}}{\pi(0.04 m )^{2} / 4}\) (5)

 

\(V_{2}=\frac{\dot{V}_{2}}{A_{c, 2}}=\frac{\dot{V}_{2}}{\pi D_{2}^{2} / 4} \quad \rightarrow \quad V_{2}=\frac{\dot{V}_{2}}{\pi(0.08 m )^{2} / 4}\) (6)

 

\(\operatorname{Re}_{1}=\frac{\rho V_{1} D_{1}}{\mu} \quad \rightarrow \quad \operatorname{Re}_{1}=\frac{\left(998 kg / m ^{3}\right) V_{1}(0.04 m )}{1.002 \times 10^{-3} kg / m \cdot s }\) (7)

 

\(\operatorname{Re}_{2}=\frac{\rho V_{2} D_{2}}{\mu} \quad \rightarrow \quad \operatorname{Re}_{2}=\frac{\left(998 kg / m ^{3}\right) V_{2}(0.08 m )}{1.002 \times 10^{-3} kg / m \cdot s }\) (8)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{1}{\sqrt{f_{1}}} &=-2.0 \log \left(\frac{\varepsilon / D_{1}}{3.7}+\frac{2.51}{\operatorname{Re}_{1} \sqrt{f_{1}}}\right) \\& \rightarrow \frac{1}{\sqrt{f_{1}}}=-2.0 \log \left(\frac{0.000045}{3.7 \times 0.04}+\frac{2.51}{\operatorname{Re}_{1} \sqrt{f_{1}}}\right)\end{aligned}\) (9)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{1}{\sqrt{f}_{2}} &=-2.0 \log \left(\frac{\varepsilon / D_{2}}{3.7}+\frac{2.51}{\operatorname{Re}_{2} \sqrt{f_{2}}}\right) \\& \rightarrow \frac{1}{\sqrt{f_{2}}}=-2.0 \log \left(\frac{0.000045}{3.7 \times 0.08}+\frac{2.51}{\operatorname{Re}_{2} \sqrt{f_{2}}}\right)\end{aligned}\) (10)

 

\(h_{L, 1}=f_{1} \frac{L_{1}}{D_{1}} \frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 g} \quad \rightarrow \quad h_{L, 1}=f_{1} \frac{36 m }{0.04 m } \frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)} \) (11)

 

\(h_{L, 2}=f_{2} \frac{L_{2}}{D_{2}} \frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2 g} \quad \rightarrow \quad h_{L, 2}=f_{2} \frac{36 m }{0.08 m } \frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)}\) (12)

 

\(\dot{V}=\dot{V}_{1}+\dot{V}_{2}\) (13)

 

This is a system of 13 equations in 13 unknowns, and their simultaneous solution by an equation solver gives

 

\(\dot{V}=0.0300 m ^{3} / s , \quad \dot{V}_{1}=0.00415 m ^{3} / s , \quad \dot{V}_{2}=0.0259 m ^{3} / s\)

 

\(V_{1}=3.30 m / s , \quad V_{2}=5.15 m / s , \quad h_{L}=h_{L, 1}=h_{L, 2}=11.1 m , h_{\text {pump }}=19.1 m\)

 

\(\operatorname{Re}_{1}=131,600, \quad \operatorname{Re}_{2}=410,000, \quad f_{1}=0.0221, \quad f_{2}=0.0182\)

 

Note that Re > 4000 for both pipes, and thus the assumption of turbulent flow is verified.

Discussion The two parallel pipes have the same length and roughness, but the diameter of the first pipe is half the diameter of the second one. Yet only 14 percent of the water flows through the first pipe. This shows the strong dependence of the flow rate on diameter. Also, it can be shown that if the free surfaces of the two reservoirs were at the same elevation (and thus \(z_{A}=z_{B}\)), the flow rate would increase by 20 percent from 0.0300 to 0.0361 m¬≥/s. Alternately, if the reservoirs were as given but the irreversible head losses were negligible, the flow rate would become 0.0715 m¬≥/s (an increase of 138 percent).
