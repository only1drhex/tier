Guest Question.

Effect of Efficiency on Nozzle Exit Velocity

Air at 200 kPa and 950 K enters an adiabatic nozzle at low velocity and is discharged at a pressure of 110 kPa. If the isentropic efficiency of the nozzle is 92 percent, determine (a) the maximum possible exit velocity, (b) the exit temperature, and (c) the actual exit velocity of the air. Assume constant specific heats for air.

Admin
Solution

The acceleration of air in a nozzle is considered. For specified exit pressure and isentropic efficiency, the maximum and actual exit velocities and the exit temperature are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 Steady operating conditions exist. 2 Air is an ideal gas. 3 The inlet kinetic energy is negligible.

Analysis A sketch of the system and the T-s diagram of the process are given in Fig. 7–54.

The temperature of air will drop during this acceleration process because some of its internal energy is converted to kinetic energy. This problem can be solved accurately by using property data from the air table. But we will assume constant specific heats (thus sacrifice some accuracy) to demonstrate their use. Let us guess the average temperature of the air to be about \(850 \mathrm{~K}\). Then, the average values of \(c_{p}\) and \(k\) at this anticipated average temperature are determined from to be \(c_{p}=\) \(1.11 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\) and \(k=1.349\).

(a) The exit velocity of the air will be a maximum when the process in the nozzle involves no irreversibilities. The exit velocity in this case is determined from the steady-flow energy equation. However, first we need to determine the exit temperature. For the isentropic process of an ideal gas we have:

\(\frac{T_{2 s}}{T_{1}}=\left(\frac{P_{2 s}}{P_{1}}\right)^{(k-1) / k}\)

or

\(T_{2 s}=T_{1}\left(\frac{P_{2 s}}{P_{1}}\right)^{(k-1) / k}=(950 \mathrm{~K})\left(\frac{110 \mathrm{kPa}}{200 \mathrm{kPa}}\right)^{0.349 / 1.349}=814 \mathrm{~K}\)

This gives an average temperature of \(882 \mathrm{~K}\), which is somewhat higher than the assumed average temperature \((850 \mathrm{~K})\). This result could be refined by reevaluating the \(k\) value at \(882 \mathrm{~K}\) and repeating the calculations, but it is not warranted since the two average temperatures are sufficiently close (doing so would change the temperature by only \(0.6 \mathrm{~K}\), which is not significant).

Now we can determine the isentropic exit velocity of the air from the energy balance for this isentropic steady-flow process:

\(\begin{array}{c}e_{\mathrm{in}}=e_{\mathrm{out}} \\h_{1}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2}=h_{2 s}+\frac{V_{2 s}^{2}}{2}\end{array}\)

or

\(\begin{aligned}V_{2 s} &=\sqrt{2\left(h_{1}-h_{2 s}\right)}=\sqrt{2 c_{p, \operatorname{avg}}\left(T_{1}-T_{2 s}\right)} \\&=\sqrt{2(1.11 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K})[(950-814) \mathrm{K}]\left(\frac{1000 \mathrm{~m}^{2} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}{1 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}}\right)} \\&=549 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\end{aligned}\)

(b) The actual exit temperature of the air is higher than the isentropic exit temperature evaluated above and is determined from

\(\eta_{N} \cong \frac{h_{1}-h_{2 a}}{h_{1}-h_{2 s}}=\frac{c_{p, \operatorname{avg}}\left(T_{1}-T_{2 a}\right)}{c_{p, \text { avg }}\left(T_{1}-T_{2 s}\right)}\)

or

\(0.92=\frac{950-T_{2 a}}{950-814} \rightarrow T_{2 a}=825 \mathrm{~K}\)

That is, the temperature is \(11 \mathrm{~K}\) higher at the exit of the actual nozzle as a result of irreversibilities such as friction. It represents a loss since this rise in temperature comes at the expense of kinetic energy (Fig. 7-55).

(c) The actual exit velocity of air can be determined from the definition of isentropic efficiency of a nozzle,

\(\eta_{N}=\frac{V_{2 a}^{2}}{V_{2 s}^{2}} \rightarrow V_{2 a}=\sqrt{\eta_{N} V_{2 s}^{2}}=\sqrt{0.92(549 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})^{2}}=527 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)
