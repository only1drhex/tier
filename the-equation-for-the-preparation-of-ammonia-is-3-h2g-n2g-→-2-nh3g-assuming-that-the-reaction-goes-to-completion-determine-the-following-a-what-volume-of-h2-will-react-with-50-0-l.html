Guest Question.

The equation for the preparation of ammonia is

\(3 H_2(g) + N_2(g) \xrightarrow[]{400^\circ C} 2 NH_3(g)\)

Assuming that the reaction goes to completion, determine the following:

(a) What volume of \(H_2\) will react with 50.0 L of \(N_2\)?

(b) What volume of \(NH_3\) will be formed from 50.0 L of \(N_2\)

(c) What volume of \(N_2\) will react with 100. mL of \(H_2\)

(d) What volume of \(NH_3\) will be produced from 100. mL of \(H_2\)

(e) If 600. mL of \(H_2\) and 400. mL of \(N_2\) are sealed in a flask and allowed to react, what amounts of \(H_2 , N_2 , and NH_3\) are in the flask at the end of the reaction?

Admin
Solution

The answers to parts (a)–(d) are shown in the boxes and can be determined from the equation by inspection, using the principle of reacting volumes:

3 \(H_2\) (g) + \(N_2\) (g) → 2\(NH_3\)(g)

3 volumes 1volume 2 volum..es

(a) 150.L 50.0 L

(b) 50.0 L 100. L

(c) 100. mL 33.3 mL

(d) 100. mL 66.7 mL

(e) Volume ratio from the equation = \(\frac{3 volumes H_2}{1 volume N_2} \)

Volume ratio used = \(\frac{600. mL H_2}{400. mL N_2} = \frac{3 volumes H_2}{2 volumes N_2}\)

Comparing these two ratios, we see that an excess of \(N_2\) is present in the gas mixture. Therefore, the reactant limiting the amount of \(NH_3\) that can be formed is \(H_2\):

3 \(H_2(g) + N_2(g) \rightarrow 2 NH_3(g)\)

600. mL 200. mL 400. mL

To have a 3 : 1 ratio of volumes reacting, 600. mL of \(H_2\) will react with 200. mL of \(N_2\) to produce 400. mL of \(NH_3\) leaving 200. mL of \(N_2\) unreacted. At the end of the reaction, the flask will contain 400. mL of \(NH_3\) and 200. mL of \(N_2\).
