Guest Question.

Given:

The dam with the same data as in (shown in Fig. Ex. C.1a).

Solution

a. Divide the flow system into fragments. The critical points chosen are the bottoms of the sheet piles. Refer to Table C.1 and review the fragments. The heavy lines represent impervious boundaries, which can be in vertical or horizontal directions. You choose the fragments to match the particular boundary conditions of your problem. Notice how the definitions of s and T are used. Their values are shown in Fig. Ex. C.1a. The vertical, dashed (equipotential) lines separate the flow regime into three fragments, as shown circled in the figure. Clearly, the flow q through each fragment must be the same and is stated by Eq. (7.19e). However, in the method of fragments this equation is changed slightly to

 

\(q=\Delta q N_{f}=k h_{L}\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)\left(\frac{N_{f}}{N_{d}}\right)\) (7.19e)

 

\(q=k h_{L} \frac{N_{f}}{N_{d}}=\frac{k h_{m}}{\Phi_{m}}\) (c.1)

 

where \(h_{m}\) is the head loss in the m th fragment, where m = 1,2,3, \(\ldots\), n, and \(\Phi_{m}\) is the dimensionless form factor for the m th fragment. The form factor is equal to \(N_{d} / N_{f}\).

In this example \(h_{L}=12 m =\sum h_{m}\), that is, the sum of the head loss in each fragment. Also, since the flow is equal in each fragment and is equal to the total flow, we have

 

\(\frac{q}{k}=h_{1} \frac{N_{f}}{N_{d}}=h_{2} \frac{N_{f}}{N_{d}}=h_{3} \frac{N_{f}}{N_{d}}\) (c.2)

 

or

 

\(\frac{q}{k}=\frac{h_{1}}{\Phi_{1}}=\frac{h_{2}}{\Phi_{2}}=\frac{h_{3}}{\Phi_{3}}=\frac{h}{\sum \Phi}\) (c.3)

 

and, finally, the flow is

 

\(q=\frac{k h}{\sum_{m=1}^{n} \Phi}\) (c.4)

 

The next step is to define the types of fragments for our problem and to determine the value of the form factors \(\Phi\) for each fragment. Nine general types of fragments are shown in Table C.1, where the heavy lines represent impervious boundaries. Other fragments are also available in the literature. Also given are the values of \(\Phi\) in terms of the geometry of each problem. If you study Fig. Ex. C.1a, you can see that fragments 1 and 3 are type II but fragment 2 is a type V fragment. Had the sheet piles been of different lengths, then fragment 2 would be a type VI instead of a type V fragment.

Next, we have to determine the form factors for our two types of fragments. For type II fragments, we see from Table C.1 that \(\Phi=K / K^{\prime}\). Both K and \(K^{\prime}\) are functions of m, which is defined as

 

\(m=\sin \frac{\pi \cdot s}{2 T}\) (c.5)

 

where s = depth of the sheet pile, and
T = thickness of the soil layer.

 

Substituting the values for s and T of our example into Eq. (C.5), we find that

 

\(m=\sin \frac{\pi \cdot s}{2 T}=\sin \frac{\pi}{2} \frac{12}{30}=0.588\)

 

The value of \(K / K^{\prime}\) can be found from Table C.2. For \(m=0.588, m^{2}=0.345\), so \(K / K^{\prime}\) is equal to about 0.865 (by interpolation), which equals \(\Phi_{1}\). By inspection, \(\Phi_{1}\) is also equal to \(\Phi_{3}\). These values are tabulated in Table Ex. C.1.

For fragment 2 , which is a type V, we need to compare L and 2s to obtain \(\Phi\). For our example, L = 40 m and 2s = 20 m. Since L > 2s, \(\Phi\) is given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Phi_{2} &=2 \ln \left(1+\frac{s}{a}\right)+\frac{L-2 s}{T} \\&=2 \ln \left(1+\frac{10}{18}\right)+\frac{40-2 \times 10}{28} \\&=0.884+0.714=1.598\end{aligned}\)

 

Note that the distance a = 18 m is the distance from the bottom impervious boundary to the bottom end of the sheet pile.

The quantity of flow is found from Eq. (C.4),

 

\(\begin{aligned}q &=\frac{k h}{\sum_{m=1}^{n} \Phi}=\frac{20 \times 10^{-4} \frac{ cm }{ s }\left(\frac{ m }{100 cm }\right)(12 m )^{2}}{0.865+1.598+0.865} \text { per metre } \\&=7.21 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s \text { per metre of dam } \\&=8.65 \times 10^{-3} m ^{3} / s \text { for a dam } 120 m \text { long }\end{aligned}\)

 

This compares satisfactorily with the value of \(8.31 \times 10^{-3} m ^{3} / s\) obtained in .

An alternative way to determine the form factor is to use Fig. C.1. Since s/T= 12 / 28=0.4, find \(1 /(2 \Phi)\) equal to 0.575 for b/T = 0. Solving for \(\Phi\), we obtain 0.87, which is close to our previously determined value of 0.865.

 

b. Computation of the exit gradient \(i_{E}\) at point E is easy. From Table C.1, fragment type II, we find the formula for the exit gradient is

 

\(i_{E}=\frac{h \pi}{2 K T m}\) (c.6)

 

where the value of m is from Eq. (C.5) and equals 0.588; the value of h is the head loss in the third (exit) fragment. The value of K is found in Table C.2 for \(m^{2}=0.345\); interpolating, K = 1.741. The value of h to use in Eq. (C.6) is the head loss in the third fragment, where the water exits, and it is from Eq. (C.3).

 

\(h_{3}=\frac{\Phi_{3} h}{\sum_{m=1}^{n} \Phi}=\frac{0.865 \times 12 m }{3.328}=3.12 m\) (c.7)

Substituting these values and \(m_{3}=0.588\) into Eq. (C.6), we obtain

 

\(i_{E}=\frac{3.12 m (\pi)}{2 \times 1.741 \times 30 m \times 0.588}=0.16\)

 

This result compares well with the value of 0.14 found in .

An alternative procedure to find the exit gradient at point E is to use Fig. C.2. For this example, \(s / T=12 / 30=0.40\); enter the graph and find \(\left(i_{E} \cdot s\right) / h_{m}=0.6\). Solving for \(i_{E}\), we find that

 

\(i_{E}=\frac{0.6 h_{m}}{s}=\frac{0.6 \times 3.12 m }{12 m }=0.16\)

 

c. To compute the pressure distribution under the dam, we assume that the head loss varies linearly from fragment 1 to fragment 3.

Compute the head loss per fragment:

 

\(\begin{aligned}\sum \Phi &=\Phi_{1}+\Phi_{2}+\Phi_{3} \\&=0.865+1.598+0.865=3.328\end{aligned}\)

 

The head loss per fragment is given by Eq. (C.7),

 

\(\begin{array}{l}h_{1}=\frac{\Phi_{1} h}{\sum \Phi}=\frac{0.865 \times 12 m }{3.328}=3.12 m \\h_{2}=\frac{\Phi_{2} h}{\sum \Phi}=\frac{1.598 \times 12 m }{3.328}=5.76 m \\h_{3}=h_{1} \text { due to symmetry }\end{array}\)

 

Redraw the dam to scale and place the values of head at selected points (Fig. Ex. C.1b) At equipotential line \(A ^{\prime}\), the head loss is \(h_{1}=3.12 m\); the head loss is therefore

 

\(h-h_{1}=12 m -3.12 m =8.88 m\). Similarly, at equipotential line \(F ^{\prime}, h_{2}=5.76 m\). Therefore the head at \(F^{\prime}\) is

 

\(h-h_{1}-h_{2}=12-3.12-5.76=3.12 m\)

 

Assuming that the head loss varies linearly from points \(A^{\prime}-A-F-F^{\prime}\), which is equal to the total distance of \(10 m +40 m +10 m =60 m\), then the head loss per metre is \(h_{2} / 60 m\), or \(5.76 m / 60 m =0.096 m / m\). Thus the head at point A = the head at \(A^{\prime}-10 m \times 0.096 m\) per metre, or \(8.88-10 \times 0.096=7.92 m\). Likewise, the head at F is 4.08 m.

 

To this head we add the tailwater head of 2 m. The vertical uplift pressures may now be computed, as shown in Fig. Ex. C.1c. These values compare almost exactly with those shown in Fig. Ex. 7.15.

 

d. Comparison of values of flow, exit gradient, and uplift pressures as determined by the two different procedures are summarized in Table Ex. C.1b. If you look closely and round off, you will see that the two methods give about the same results. Analyzing with the MOF takes much less time than drawing a flow net or setting up and running a computational software solution!
