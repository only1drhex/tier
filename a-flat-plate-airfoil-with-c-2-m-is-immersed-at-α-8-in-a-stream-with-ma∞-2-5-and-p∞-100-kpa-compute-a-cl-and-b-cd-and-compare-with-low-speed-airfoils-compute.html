Guest Question.

A flat-plate airfoil with C = 2 m is immersed at α = 8° in a stream with \(Ma_∞\) = 2.5 and \(p_∞\) = 100 kPa. Compute (a) \(C_L and (b) C_D\), and compare with low-speed airfoils. Compute (c) lift and (d) drag in newtons per unit span width.

Admin
Solution

Instead of using a lot of space outlining the detailed oblique shock and Prandtl-Meyer expansion computations, we list all pertinent results in Fig. E9.19 on the upper and lower surfaces. Using the theories of Secs. 9.9 and 9.10, you should verify every single one of the calculations in Fig. E9.19 to make sure that all details of shock expansion theory are well understood.

The important final results are \(p_2 and p_3\), from which the total force per unit width on the plate is

\(F = (p_2 – p_3)bC = (165.7 – 56.85)(kPa)(1 m)(2 m) = 218 kN\)

The lift and drag per meter width are thus

L = F cos 8° = 216 kN

D = F sin 8° = 30 kN

These are very large forces for only 2 \(m^2\) of wing area.

From Eq. (9.102) the lift coefficient is

\(C_L = \frac{L}{\frac{1}{2}kp_{\infty} Ma_{\infty}^2 bC} C_D = \frac{D}{\frac{1}{2} kp_{\infty} Ma_{\infty}^2 bC}\) (9.102)

\(C_L = \frac{216 kN}{\frac{1}{2} (1.4)(100 kPa)(2.5)^2(2 m^2)} = 0.246\)

The comparable low-speed coefficient from Eq. (8.67) is \(C_L\) = 2π sin 8° = 0.874, which is 3.5 times larger.

\(C_L = 2 \int^1_0 {\frac{\gamma}{U} d \left(\frac{x}{C}\right)} = 4 \sin \alpha \int^1_0 {\left(\frac{C}{x} – 1\right)^{1/2} d \left(\frac{x}{C}\right)} = 2 \pi \sin \alpha \approx 2\pi \alpha\) (8.67)

From Eq. (9.102) the drag coefficient is

\(C_D = \frac{30 kN}{\frac{1}{2} (1.4)(100 kPa)(2.5)^2(2 m^2)} = 0.035\)

From Fig. 7.25 for the NACA 0009 airfoil \(C_D\) at α = 8° is about 0.009, or about 4 times smaller.

Notice that this supersonic theory predicts a finite drag in spite of assuming frictionless flow with infinite wing aspect ratio. This is called wave drag, and we see that the d’Alembert paradox of zero body drag does not occur in supersonic flow.
