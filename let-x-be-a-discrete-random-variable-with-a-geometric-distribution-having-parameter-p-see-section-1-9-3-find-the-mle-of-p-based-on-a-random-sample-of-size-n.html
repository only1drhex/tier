Guest Question.

Let X be a discrete random variable with a geometric distribution having parameter p (see Section 1.9.3). Find the MLE of p based on a random sample of size n.

Admin
Solution

If we have a random sample of size n, then we have observed independently \(x =\left\{x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}\right\}\). We now want a MLE of p. The probability of observing \(x_{i}\) comes from the geometric distribution (see Section 1.9.3),

 

\(P \left[X_{i}=x_{i}\right]=p q^{x_{i}-1}, \quad i=1,2, \ldots n\) (5.14)

 

where q = 1 âˆ’ p. The likelihood function is thus

 

\(\begin{aligned}L(p \mid x ) &= P \left[X_{1}=x_{1} \cap X_{2}=x_{2} \cap \cdots \cap X_{n}=x_{n}\right] \\&=\prod_{i=1}^{n} p q^{x_{i}-1}\end{aligned}\)

 

\(=p^{n} \prod_{i=1}^{n}(1-p)^{x_{i}-1}\) (5.15)

 

while its logarithm is

 

\(\begin{aligned}\ln (L) &=n \ln p+\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-1\right) \ln (1-p) \\&=n \ln p+\ln (1-p) \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-1\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

We now set the derivative of ln(L) to zero:

 

\(\frac{\partial \ln L}{\partial p}=\frac{n}{p}-\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i}-1\right)}{1-p}=0\)

 

Solving this for p, which we will now call \(\hat{p}\), gives us

 

\(\hat{p}=\frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i}}=\frac{1}{\bar{x}}\)

 

where \(\bar{x}\) is just the average of our observations.
