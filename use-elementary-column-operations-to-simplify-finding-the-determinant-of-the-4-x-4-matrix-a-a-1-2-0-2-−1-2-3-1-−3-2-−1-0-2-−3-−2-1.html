Guest Question.

Use elementary column operations to simplify finding the determinant of the (4 × 4) matrix A:

\(A=\left[\begin{array}{rrrr}1 & 2 & 0 & 2 \\-1 & 2 & 3 & 1 \\-3 & 2 & -1 & 0 \\2 & -3 & -2 & 1\end{array}\right]\).

Admin
Solution

In of Section 6.2, a laborious cofactor expansion showed that det(A) = −63. In column form, \(A=\left[ A _{1}, A _{2}, A _{3}, A _{4}\right]\), and clearly we can introduce a zero into the (1, 2) position by replacing \(A _{2} \text { by } A _{2}-2 A _{1}\). Similarly, replacing \(A _{4} \text { by } A _{4}-2 A _{1}\) creates a zero in the (1, 4) entry. Moreover, by Theorem 6, the determinant is unchanged. The details are

\(\operatorname{det}(A)=\left|\begin{array}{rrrr}1 & 2 & 0 & 2 \\-1 & 2 & 3 & 1 \\-3 & 2 & -1 & 0 \\2 & -3 & -2 & 1\end{array}\right|=\left|\begin{array}{rrrr}1 & 0 & 0 & 2 \\-1 & 4 & 3 & 1 \\-3 & 8 & -1 & 0 \\2 & -7 & -2 & 1\end{array}\right|\)
\(=\left|\begin{array}{rrrr}1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\-1 & 4 & 3 & 3 \\-3 & 8 & -1 & 6 \\2 & -7 & -2 & -3\end{array}\right|\).

Thus it follows that det(A) is given by

\(\operatorname{det}(A)=\left|\begin{array}{rrr}4 & 3 & 3 \\8 & -1 & 6 \\-7 & -2 & -3\end{array}\right|\).

We now wish to create zeros in the (1, 2) and (1, 3) positions of this (3 × 3) determinant. To avoid using fractions, we multiply the second and third columns by 4 (using Theorem 3), and then add a multiple of −3 times column 1 to columns 2 and 3:

\(\operatorname{det}(A)=\left|\begin{array}{rrr}4 & 3 & 3 \\8 & -1 & 6 \\-7 & -2 & -3\end{array}\right|=\frac{1}{16}\left|\begin{array}{rrr}4 & 12 & 12 \\8 & -4 & 24 \\-7 & -8 & -12\end{array}\right|=\frac{1}{16}\left|\begin{array}{rrr}4 & 0 & 0 \\8 & -28 & 0 \\-7 & 13 & 9\end{array}\right|\).

Thus we again find det(A) = −63.
