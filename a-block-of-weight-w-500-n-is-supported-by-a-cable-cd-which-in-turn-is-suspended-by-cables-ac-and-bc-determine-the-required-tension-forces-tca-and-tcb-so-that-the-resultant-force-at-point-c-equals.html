Guest Question.

A block of weight \(W\) = 500 \(N\) is supported by a cable \(CD\), which in turn is suspended by cables \(AC\) and \(BC\). Determine the required tension forces \(T_{CA} \) and \(T_{CB} \) so that the resultant force at point \(C\) equals zero.

Admin
Solution

Step 1: Because all cables are concurrent at point \(C\), isolate point \(C\) and show all forces.

Directions for forces \(T_{CD} \), \(T_{CA} \), and \(T_{CB} \) are known, but their magnitudes are unknown. \(T_{CD} \) is equal to the weight \(W\), which acts vertically downward.

Step 2: Resolve each force into its x and y components.

\(T_{CA_{x} } =\frac{3}{5} T_{CA }\)

 

\(T_{CA_{y} } =\frac{4}{5} T_{CA }\)

 

\(T_{CB_{x} } = T_{CB } \cos 30^{\circ } =0.866 \space T_{CB }\)

 

\(R_{CB_{y} } = T_{CB } \sin 30^{\circ } =0.5 \space T_{CB }\)

 

\( T_{CD}=W=500 \space N\)

Step 3: Write the resultant component force equations.

\(R_{x} =\sum{F_{x}} =T_{CA_{x} }+T_{CB_{x} }=0\)

We set \(R_{x}\) = 0 to comply with the condition that no resultant force develops at point \(C\); that is, all forces are balanced at this point.

\(\therefore R_{x}=-\frac{3}{5} T_{CA }+0.866T_{CB }=0\)

 

\( \frac{3}{5} T_{CA }=0.866T_{CB }\) (1)

then,

\(R_{y} =\sum{F_{y}} =+T_{CA_{y} }+T_{CB_{y} }-500 \space N=0\)

 

\(\therefore R_{y} =\frac{4}{5} T_{CA }+ .5 T_{CB }-500 \space N=0\)

 

\( \frac{4}{5} T_{CA }+0.5 T_{CB }=500 \space N \) (2)

From writing the resultant equations \(T_{CA }\) and \(T_{CB }\), we obtain two equations, (1) \( \frac{3}{5} T_{CA }=0.866T_{CB }\) and (2) \( \frac{4}{5} T_{CA }+0.5 T_{CB }=500 \space N \) , containing two unknowns. Solving the two equations simultaneously,

\( \frac{3}{5} T_{CA }=0.866T_{CB }\) (1)

 

\( \frac{4}{5} T_{CA }+0.5 T_{CB }=500 \space N \) (2)

From equation (1) \( \frac{3}{5} T_{CA }=0.866T_{CB }\) ,

\( T_{CA }= \frac{5}{3} (0.866\space T_{CB }) =1.44 \space T_{CB }\)

Substituting into equation (2) \( \frac{4}{5} T_{CA }+0.5 T_{CB }=500 \space N \) ,

\( \frac{4}{5}( 1.44 \space T_{CB})+0.5 \space T_{CB }=500 \space N\)

 

\( \therefore 1.15 \space T_{CB}+0.5 \space T_{CB }=500 \space N\)

Solving for \(T_{CB}\),

\(1.65\space T_{CB }=500 \space N\)

 

\( T_{CB }=\frac{500 \space N}{1.65} = 303 \space N\)

Substituting the value of \( T_{CB }\) back into equation (1) \( \frac{3}{5} T_{CA }=0.866T_{CB }\) or (2) \( \frac{4}{5} T_{CA }+0.5 T_{CB }=500 \space N \) ,

\(T_{CA }= 436.4 \space N \)

Graphical Check:

Because \(T_{CD }\) has a known magnitude and direction, it will be used as the starting (or base) force

Step 1: Draw the force polygon using the tip-to-tail method.

Step 2: Draw force \(T_{CD }\) first, to scale.

Step 3: Draw the lines of action for \(T_{CA }\) and \(T_{CB }\); the order does not matter.

Forces \(T_{CA }\) and \(T_{CB }\) have known directions but unknown magnitudes; therefore, only their lines of action are drawn initially. We know that because \(R\) = 0, the tip of the last force must end at the tail (the origin) of the first forceâ€”in this case, \(T_{CD }\).

Step 4: The intersection of the two lines of action determines the limits for\(T_{CB }\) and \(T_{CA }\).

Step 5: Scale off the magnitudes for \(T_{CB }\) and \(T_{CA }\).

Step 6:

\( T_{CA }= 436.4 \space N\)
