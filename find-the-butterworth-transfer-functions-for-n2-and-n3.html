Guest Question.

Find the Butterworth transfer functions for \( n=2\) and \( n=3\) .

Admin
Solution

For \( n=2\), we find the roots of the polynomial

\(1+\left(-1\right) ^{2} s^{4} =0\).

Rearranging terms, we find

\(s^{4}=-1=1\angle 180°\).

Therefore, the four roots are

\(s_{1}=1\angle 45° =1/\sqrt{2} +j/\sqrt{2} \),

 

\(s_{2}=1\angle 135° =-1/\sqrt{2} +j/\sqrt{2} \),

 

\(s_{3}=1\angle 225° =-1/\sqrt{2} +-j/\sqrt{2} \),

 

\(s_{4}=1\angle 315° =1/\sqrt{2} +-j/\sqrt{2} \).

Roots \(s_{2}\) and \(s_{3}\) are in the left-half plane. Thus,

\(H\left(s\right) =\frac{1}{\left(s+1/\sqrt{2}-j/\sqrt{2}\right) \left(s+1/\sqrt{2}+j/\sqrt{2}\right) }\)

 

\(=\frac{1}{\left(s^{2}+\sqrt{2} s+1\right) } \).

For \( n=3\), we find the roots of the polynomial

\(1+\left(-1\right) ^{3} s^{6} =0\).

Rearranging terms,

\(s^{6}=1\angle 0°=1 \angle 360°\).

Therefore, the six roots are

\(s_{1}=1\angle 0° =1\),

 

\(s_{2}=1\angle 60° =1/2+j\sqrt{3}/2\),

 

\(s_{3}=1\angle 120° =-1/2+j\sqrt{3}/2\),

 

\(s_{4}=1\angle 180° =-1+j0\),

 

\(s_{5}=1\angle 240° =-1/2+ -j\sqrt{3}/2\),

 

\(s_{6}=1\angle 300° =1/2+ -j\sqrt{3}/2\).

Roots \(s_{3}\), \(s_{4}\), and \(s_{5}\) are in the left-half plane. Thus,

\(H\left(s\right) =\frac{1}{\left(s+1\right) \left(s+1/2-j/\sqrt{3}/2\right)\left(s+1/2+j/\sqrt{3}/2\right) }\)

 

\(=\frac{1}{\left(s+1\right)\left(s^{2}+s+1 \right) } \).

We note in passing that the roots of the Butterworth polynomial are always equally spaced around the unit circle in the \(s \) plane. To assist in the design of Butterworth filters, Table 15.1 lists the Butterworth polynomials up to \(n = 8 \).
