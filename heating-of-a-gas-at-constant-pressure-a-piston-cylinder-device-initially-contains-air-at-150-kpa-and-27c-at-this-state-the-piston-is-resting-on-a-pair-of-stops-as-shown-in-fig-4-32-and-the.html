Heating of a Gas at Constant Pressure

A piston-cylinder device initially contains air at 150 kPa and 27째C. At this state, the piston is resting on a pair of stops, as shown in Fig. 4-32, and the enclosed volume is 400 L. The mass of the piston is such that a 350-kPa pressure is required to move it. The air is now heated until its volume has doubled. Determine (a) the final temperature, (b) the work done by the air, and (c) the total heat transferred to the air.

Admin
Solution

Air in a piston-cylinder device with a set of stops is heated until its volume is doubled. The final temperature, work done, and the total heat transfer are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 Air is an ideal gas since it is at a high temperature and low pressure relative to its critical-point values. 2 The system is stationary and thus the kinetic and potential energy changes are zero, \(\Delta \mathrm{KE}=\Delta \mathrm{PE}=0\) and \(\Delta E=\Delta U\). 3 The volume remains constant until the piston starts moving, and the pressure remains constant afterwards. 4 There are no electrical, shaft, or other forms of work involved.

Analysis We take the contents of the cylinder as the system (Fig. 4-32). This is a closed system since no mass crosses the system boundary during the process. We observe that a piston-cylinder device typically involves a moving boundary and thus boundary work, \(W_{b}\). Also, the boundary work is done by the system, and heat is transferred to the system.
(a) The final temperature can be determined easily by using the ideal-gas relation between states 1 and 3 in the following form:

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{P_{1} V_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{P_{3} V_{3}}{T_{3}} \longrightarrow \frac{(150 \mathrm{kPa})\left(V_{1}\right)}{300 \mathrm{~K}} &=\frac{(350 \mathrm{kPa})\left(2 V_{1}\right)}{T_{3}} \\T_{3} &=1400 \mathrm{~K}\end{aligned}\)

(b) The work done could be determined by integration, but for this case it is much easier to find it from the area under the process curve on a \(P-V\) diagram, shown in Fig. 4 -32:

\(A=\left(V_{2}-V_{1}\right) P_{2}=\left(0.4 \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)(350 \mathrm{kPa})=140 \mathrm{~m}^{3} \cdot \mathrm{kPa}\)

Therefore,

\(W_{13}=140 \mathrm{~kJ}\)

The work is done by the system (to raise the piston and to push the atmospheric air out of the way), and thus it is work output.
(c) Under the stated assumptions and observations, the energy balance on the system between the initial and final states (process 1-3) can be expressed as

\(\begin{aligned}\underbrace{E_{\text {in }}-E_{\text {out}}}_{\begin{array}{c}\text { Net energy transfer } \\\text { by heat, work, and mass }\end{array}} &=\underbrace{\Delta E_{\text {system }}}_{\begin{array}{c}\text { Change in internal, kinetic, } \\\text { potential, etc., energies }\end{array}} \\\quad\\Q_{\text {in }}-W_{b, \text{out}} &=\Delta U=m\left(u_{3}-u_{1}\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

The mass of the system can be determined from the ideal-gas relation:

\(m=\frac{P_{1} V_{1}}{R T_{1}}=\frac{(150 \mathrm{kPa})\left(0.4 \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)}{\left(0.287 \mathrm{kPa} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\right)(300 \mathrm{~K})}=0.697 \mathrm{~kg}\)

The internal energies are determined from the air table () to be

\(\begin{aligned}&u_{1}=u_{@ 300 \mathrm{~K}}=214.07 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \\&u_{3}=u_{@ 1400 \mathrm{~K}}=1113.52 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\end{aligned}\)

Thus,

\(\begin{aligned}Q_{\text {in }}-140 \mathrm{~kJ} &=(0.697 \mathrm{~kg})[(1113.52-214.07) \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{kg}] \\Q_{\text {in }} &=767 \mathrm{~kJ}\end{aligned}\)

Discussion The positive sign verifies that heat is transferred to the system.
