Guest Question.

Net Positive Suction Head

GIVEN A centrifugal pump is to be placed above a large, open water tank, as shown in Fig. 12.13, and is to pump water at a rate of 0.5 \(ft ^{3} / s\). At this flowrate the required net positive suction head, \(NPSH _{ R }\), is 15 ft, as specified by the pump manufacturer. The water temperature is 80 °F and atmospheric pressure is 14.7 psi. Assume that the major head loss between the tank and the pump inlet is due to filter at the pipe inlet having a minor loss coefficient \(K_{L}=20\). Other losses can be neglected. The pipe on the suction side of the pump has a diameter of 4 in.

FIND Determine the maximum height, \(z_{1}\), that the pump can be located above the water surface without cavitation. If you were required to place a valve in the flow path would you place it upstream or downstream of the pump? Why?

Admin
Solution

From Eq. 12.25 the available net positive suction head, \(NPSH _{A}\), is given by the equation

 

\(NPSH _{ A }=\frac{p_{ atm }}{\gamma}-z_{1}-\sum h_{L}-\frac{p_{v}}{\gamma}\) (12.25)

 

and the maximum value for \(z_{1}\) will occur when \(NPSH _{ A }= NPSH _{ R }\). Thus,

 

\(\left(z_{1}\right)_{\max }=\frac{p_{ atm }}{\gamma}-\sum h_{L}-\frac{p_{v}}{\gamma}- NPSH _{ R }\) (1)

 

Since the only head loss to be considered is the loss

 

\(\sum h_{L}=K_{L} \frac{V^{2}}{2 g}\)

 

with

 

\(V=\frac{Q}{A}=\frac{0.5 ft ^{3} / s }{(\pi / 4)(4 / 12 ft )^{2}}=5.73 ft / s\)

 

it follows that

 

\(\sum h_{L}=\frac{(20)(5.73 ft / s )^{2}}{2\left(32.2 ft / s ^{2}\right)}=10.2 ft\)

 

From Table B.1 the water vapor pressure at 80 °F is 0.5069 psia and \(\gamma=62.22 lb / ft ^{3}\). Equation (1) can now be written as

 

\(\begin{aligned}\left(z_{1}\right)_{\max }=& \frac{\left(14.7 lb / in ^{2}\right)\left(144 in ^{2} / ft ^{2}\right)}{62.22 lb / ft ^{3}}-10.2 ft \\&-\frac{\left(0.5069 lb / in ^{2}\right)\left(144 in ^{2} / ft ^{2}\right)}{62.22 lb / ft ^{3}}-15 ft \\=& 7.65 ft\end{aligned}\)

 

Thus, to prevent cavitation, with its accompanying poor pump performance, the pump should not be located higher than 7.65 ft above the water surface.

COMMENT If the valve is placed upstream of the pump, not only would the pump have to operate with an additional loss in the system, it would now operate with a lower inlet pressure because of this additional upstream loss and could now suffer cavitation with its usually negative consequences. If the valve is placed downstream of the pump, the pump would need to operate with more loss in the system and with higher back pressure than without the valve. Depending on the stability of the pump at higher back pressures, this could be inconsequential or important. Usually, pumps are stable even with higher back pressures. So, placing the valve on the downstream side of the pump is normally the better choice.
