Guest Question.

A 1.5 \(m^{3}\) tank contains 500 kg of liquid water in equilibrium with pure water vapor, which fills the remainder of the tank. The temperature and pressure are \(100^{\circ }C\) and 101.33 kPa. From a water line at a constant temperature of \(70^{\circ }C\) and a constant pres-sure somewhat above 101.33 kPa, 750 kg of liquid is bled into the tank. If the temper-ature and pressure in the tank are not to change as a result of the process, how much energy as heat must be transferred to the tank?

Admin
Solution

Choose the tank as the control volume. There is no work, and kinetic- and potential-energy changes are assumed negligible. Equation (2.28) therefore is written

\( \frac{d(mU)_{cv}}{dt}+\Delta (H\dot{m} )_{fs}=\dot{Q}+\dot{W}\) (2.28)

 

\( \frac{d(mU)_{tank}}{dt}-H^{\prime }\dot{m}^{\prime }=\dot{Q}\)

where the prime denotes the state of the inlet stream. The mass balance, \(\dot{m}^{\prime }=dm_{tank}/dt \), may be combined with the energy balance to yield

\( \frac{d(mU)_{tank}}{dt}-H^{\prime }\frac{dm_{tank}}{dt}=\dot{Q}\)

Multiplication by dt and integration over time (with \(H^{\prime }\) constant) give

\( Q=\Delta (mU)_{tank}-H^{\prime }\Delta m_{tank}\)

The definition of enthalpy may be applied to the entire contents of the tank to give

\( \Delta (mU)_{tank}=\Delta (mH)_{tank}-\Delta (PmV)_{tank}\)

Because total tank volume mV and P are constant, \(\Delta (PmV)_{tank} = 0.\) Then, with \(\Delta (mH)_{tank} = ( m_{2}H_{2})_{tank} − ( m_{1}H_{1} )_{tank}\) , the two preceding equations combine to yield

\( Q=(m_{2}H_{2})_{tank}-(m_{1}H_{1})_{tank}-H^{\prime }\Delta m_{tank}\) (A)

where \(\Delta m_{tank}\) is the 750 kg of water bled into the tank, and subscripts 1 and 2 refer to conditions in the tank at the beginning and end of the process. At the end of the process the tank still contains saturated liquid and saturated vapor in equilibrium at \(100^{\circ }C\) and 101.33 kPa. Hence, \(m_{1}H_{1}\) and \(m_{2}H_{2}\) each consist of two terms, one for the liquid phase and one for the vapor phase.
The numerical solution makes use of the following enthalpies taken from the steam tables:

\( H^{\prime }=293.0 kJ\cdot kg^{-1};\) saturated liquid at \(70^{\circ }C\)

\( H_{tank}^{l}=419.1 kJ\cdot kg^{-1};\) saturated liquid at \(100^{\circ }C\)

\( H^{\nu }_{tank}=2676.0 kJ\cdot kg^{-1};\) saturated vapor at \(100^{\circ }C\)

The volume of vapor in the tank initially is 1.5 \(m^{3}\) minus the volume occupied by the 500 kg of liquid water. Thus,

\( m^{\nu }_{1}=\frac{1.5-(500)(0.001044)}{1.673}=0.772 kg\)

where 0.001044 and \(1.673 m^{3}\cdot kg^{−1}\) are the specific volumes of saturated liquid and saturated vapor at \(100^{\circ }C\) from the steam tables. Then,

\( (m_{1}H_{1})_{tank}=m_{1}^{l}H_{1}^{l}+m_{1}^{\nu }H_{1}^{\nu }=500(419.1)+0.722(2676.0)=211,616 kJ\)

At the end of the process, the masses of liquid and vapor are determined by a mass balance and by the fact that the tank volume is still 1.5 \(m^{3}:\)

\( m_{2}=500+0.722+750=m_{2}^{\nu }+m_{2}^{l}\)

 

\( 1.5=1.673m_{2}^{\nu }+0.001044m_{2}^{l}\)

Solution gives:

\( m_{2}^{l}=1250.65 kg \) and \( m_{2}^{\nu }=0.116 kg\)

Then, with \(H_{2}^{l} = H_{1}^{l}\) and \(H_{2}^{V}=H_{1}^{V},\)

\( (m_{2}H_{2})_{tank}=(1250.65)(419.1)+(0.116)(2676.0)=524,458 kJ\)

Substitution of appropriate values into Eq. (A) gives:

\( Q=524,458-221,616-(750)(293.0)=93,092 kJ\)

Before conducting the rigorous analysis demonstrated here, one might make a reasonable estimate of the heat requirement by assuming that it is equal to the enthalpy change for heating 750 kg of water from \(70^{\circ }C\) to \(100^{\circ }C\). This approach would give 94,575 kJ, which is slightly higher than the rigorous result, because it neglects the heat effect of condensing water vapor to accommodate the added liquid.
