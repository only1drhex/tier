Solution

Ranking departments according to highest work flow and locations according to highest inter-location distances helps in making assigmnets.

Trip Distance (meters) Department Pair Work Flow
A–B 20 1–3 170
B–C 30 2–3 100
A–C 40 1–2 30

From these listings, you can see that departments 1 and 3 have the highest interdepartmental work flow, and that locations A and B are the closest. Thus, it seems reasonable to consider assigning 1 and 3 to locations A and B, although it is not yet obvious which department should be assigned to which location. Further inspection of the work flow list reveals that 2 and 3 have higher work flow than 1 and 2, so 2 and 3 should probably be located more closely than 1 and 2. Hence, it would seem reasonable to place 3 between 1 and 2, or at least centrailize that deparment with repect to the other two. The resulting assigmnents might appear as illustarted in Figure 6.11 .

If the cost per meter to move any load is $1, you can compute the total dail transportation cost for this assigmment by multiplying each department’s number of loads by the trip distance, and summing those quantiies:

Department Number of Loads Between Location Distance To: Loads × Distance
1 2.3 A C: 40 30 × 40 =1,200
3: 170 B: 20 170 × 30 =3,400
2 3: 100 100 × 30 =\(\underline{3,000}\)
3 B C: 30 7,600

At $1 per load meter, the cost for this plan is $7,600 per day. Even though it might appear that this arrangement yields the lowest transportion cost, you cannot be absolutey positive of that without aclutually computing the total cost for every alternative and comparing it to this one. Instead , rely on the choice of reasonable heuristic rules such as those demonstrated above to ar ve at a satisfactory, if not optimal, solution.
