Guest Question.

(a) Calculate the resistance per unit length of a 22-gauge Nichrome wire, which has a radius of 0.321 mm.

Admin
Solution

The cross-sectional area of this wire is

\(A=\pi r^{2}=\pi\left(0.321 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~m}\right)^{2}=3.24 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~m}^{2}\)

The resistivity of Nichrome is \(1.5 \times 10^{-6} \Omega \cdot \mathrm{m}\) (see Table 27.1). Thus, we can use Equation 27.11 to find the resistance per unit length:

\(\frac{R}{\ell}=\frac{\rho}{A}=\frac{1.5 \times 10^{-6} \Omega \cdot \mathrm{m}}{3.24 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~m}^{2}}=4.6 \Omega / \mathrm{m}\)

(b) If a potential difference of \(10 \mathrm{~V}\) is maintained across a 1.0-m length of the Nichrome wire, what is the current in the wire?

Because a 1.0 -m length of this wire has a resistance of \(4.6 \Omega\), Equation 27.8 gives

\(I=\frac{\Delta V}{R}=\frac{10 \mathrm{~V}}{4.6 \Omega}=2.2 \mathrm{~A}\)

Note from Table 27.1 that the resistivity of Nichrome wire is about 100 times that of copper. A copper wire of the same radius would have a resistance per unit length of only \(0.052 \Omega / \mathrm{m}A 1.0-\mathrm{m}\) length of copper wire of the same radius would carry the same current \( (2.2 \mathrm{~A})\) with an applied potential difference of only \(0.11 \mathrm{~V}\)Because of its high resistivity and its resistance to oxidation, Nichrome is often used for heating elements in toasters, irons, and electric heaters.
