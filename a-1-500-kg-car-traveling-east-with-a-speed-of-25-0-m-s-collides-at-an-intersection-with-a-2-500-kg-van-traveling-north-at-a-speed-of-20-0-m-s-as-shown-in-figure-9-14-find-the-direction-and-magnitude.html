Guest Question.

A 1 500-kg car traveling east with a speed of 25.0 m/s collides at an intersection with a 2 500-kg van traveling north at a speed of 20.0 m/s, as shown in Figure 9.14. Find the direction and magnitude of the velocity of the wreckage after the collision, assuming that the vehicles undergo a perfectly inelastic collision (that is, they stick together).

Admin
Solution

Let us choose east to be along the positive x direction and north to be along the positive y direction. Before the collision, the only object having momentum in the x direction is the car. Thus, the magnitude of the total initial momentum of the system (car plus van) in the x direction is

\( \sum p_{x i}=(1500 kg )(25.0 m / s )=3.75 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s \).

Let us assume that the wreckage moves at an angle Î¸ and speed \( v_{f} \) after the collision. The magnitude of the total momentum in the x direction after the collision is

\( \sum p_{x f}=(4000 kg ) v_{f} \cos \theta \).

Because the total momentum in the x direction is conserved, we can equate these two equations to obtain

(1) \( 3.75 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s =(4000 kg ) v_{f} \cos \theta \).

Similarly, the total initial momentum of the system in the y direction is that of the van, and the magnitude of this momentum is \( (2500 kg )(20.0 m / s )=5.00 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s \) Applying conservation of momentum to the y direction, we have

\( \sum p_{y i}=\sum p_{y f} \).

(2) \( 5.00 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s =(4000 kg ) v_{f} \sin \theta \) .

If we divide Equation (2) by Equation (1), we obtain

\( \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}=\tan \theta=\frac{5.00 \times 10^{4}}{3.75 \times 10^{4}}=1.33 \).

\( \theta=53.1^{\circ} \).

When this angle is substituted into Equation (2), the value of \( v_{f} \text { is } \)

\( v_{f}=\frac{5.00 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s }{(4000 kg ) \sin 53.1^{\circ}}=15.6 m / s \).

It might be instructive for you to draw the momentum vectors of each vehicle before the collision and the two vehicles together after the collision.
