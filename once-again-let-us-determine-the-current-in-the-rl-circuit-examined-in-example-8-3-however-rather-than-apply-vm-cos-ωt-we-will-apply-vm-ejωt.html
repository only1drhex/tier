Guest Question.

Once again, let us determine the current in the RL circuit examined in . However, rather than apply \(V_{M}\) cos ωt, we will apply \(V_{M} e^{jωt}\).

Admin
Solution

The forced response will be of the form

i(t) = \(I_{M} e^{j(ωt+Φ)}\)

where only \(I_{M}\) and Φ are unknown. Substituting v(t) and i(t) into the differential equation for the circuit, we obtain

\(RI_{M} e^{j(ωt+Φ)} + L\frac{d}{dt} (I_{M} e^{j(ωt+Φ)}) = V_{M} e^{jωt}\)

Taking the indicated derivative, we obtain

\(RI_{M} e^{j(ωt+Φ)} + jωLI_{M}e^{j(ωt+Φ)} = V_{M} e^{jωt}\)

Dividing each term of the equation by the common factor \(e^{jωt}\) yields

\(RI_{M}e^{jΦ}+ jωLI_{M} e^{jΦ}= V_{M}\)

which is an algebraic equation with complex coefficients. This equation can be written as

\(I_{M}e^{jΦ} = \frac{V_{M}}{R + jωL}\)

Converting the right-hand side of the equation to exponential or polar form produces the equation

\(I_{M}e^{jΦ} = \frac{V_{M}}{\sqrt{R^{2} + ω^{2}L^{2}}} e^{j[-tan^{-1}(ωL/R)]}\)

(A quick refresher on complex numbers is given in the Appendix for readers who need to sharpen their skills in this area.) The preceding form clearly indicates that the magnitude and phase of the resulting current are

\(I_{M} =\frac{V_{M}}{\sqrt{R^{2} + ω^{2}L^{2}}}\)

and

Φ = \(-tan^{-1} \frac{ωL}{R}\)

However, since our actual forcing function was \(V_{M}\) cos ωt rather than \(V_{M} e^{jωt}\), our actual response is the real part of the complex response:

i(t) = \(I_{M}\) cos (ωt + Φ)
= \(\frac{V_{M}}{\sqrt{R^{2} + ω^{2}L^{2}}} cos \Big(ωt – tan^{-1} \frac{ωL}{ R} \Big) \)

Note that this is identical to the response obtained in the previous example by solving the differential equation for the current i(t).
