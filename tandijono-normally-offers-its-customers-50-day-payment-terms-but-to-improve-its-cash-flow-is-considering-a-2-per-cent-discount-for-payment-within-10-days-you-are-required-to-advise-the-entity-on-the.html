Guest Question.

Tandijono normally offers its customers 50-day payment terms, but to improve its cash flow is considering a 2 per cent discount for payment within 10 days. You are required to advise the entity on the cost of this proposed action. Assume a 365-day year and an invoice for $100.

Admin
Solution

The entity would receive $98 on day 10 instead of $100 on day 50. Tandijono would then be able to invest the $98 for 40 days (50 – 10).
If the prevailing interest rate is r per cent per annum, the entity will benefit as follows:

$98 × r % × \( \frac{40}{365} \)

However, to achieve this the entity has to grant $2 in discount. To break even on this scheme, we must have:

$98 × r % × \( \frac{40}{365} \) ≥ $2

Rearranging:

r % ≥ \( \frac{2}{98} \) × \( \frac{40}{365} \) =18.6%

The 18.6 per cent would then be compared with the entity cost of capital to establish if this is an efficient method of financing the shorter credit period.
Note: A more accurate method can be used that deals with the compound interest effect. The compound interest formula states that the value S attained by a single sum X, after n periods at r per cent, is:

\( S=X(1+r)^{n}\)

Using this formula to quantify the cost of offering the cash discount:

\( 100 = 98 (1 + r )^{1}\)

where
r = periodic rate (in this case the rate is for a 40-day period)
1+ r = 1.0204
r = 2.04%

This periodic rate must then be converted into an annual rate using the formula:
\( (1 + annualised rate) = (1 + periodic rate)^{n}\)

where

n = number of compounding periods in the year (in this example \( (\frac{365}{40} =9.125 ) \)

(1 + annualised rate) =\( (1.0204)^{9.125}\) = 1.202
annualised rate = 20.2%

This compounding process can be short cut as shown below:

\( 1 + r = (\frac{100}{98})^{9.125}\)

1 + r = 1.202
