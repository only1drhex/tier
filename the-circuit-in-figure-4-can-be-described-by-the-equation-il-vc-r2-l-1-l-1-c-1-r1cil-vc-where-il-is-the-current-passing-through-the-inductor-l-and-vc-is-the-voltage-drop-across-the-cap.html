Guest Question.

The circuit in Figure 4 can be described by the equation

\( \left [ \begin{matrix} \acute{i}_{L} \\ \acute{v}_{C} \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} -R_{2}/L & -1/L \\ 1/C & -1/(R_{1}C) \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} i_{L} \\ v_{C} \end{matrix} \right ] \)

where \( i_{L} \) is the current passing through the inductor L and \( v_{C} \) is the voltage drop across the capacitor C. Suppose \( R_{1} \) is 5 ohms, \( R_{2} \) is .8 ohm, C is .1 farad, and L is .4 henry. Find formulas for \( i_{L} \) and \( v_{C} \) , if the initial current through the inductor is 3 amperes and the initial voltage across the capacitor is 3 volts.

Admin
Solution

For the data given, \( A = \left [ \begin{matrix} -2 & -2.5 \\ 10 & -2 \end{matrix} \right ] \text{and} x_{0} = \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 3 \end{matrix} \right ] \) . The method discussed in Section 5.5 produces the eigenvalue \( \lambda = -2 + 5i \) and the corresponding eigenvector \( v_{1} = \left [ \begin{matrix} i \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ] \) . The complex solutions of \( \acute{x} = Ax \) are complex linear combinations of

\( x_{1}(t) = \left [ \begin{matrix} i \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ] e^{(-2+5i)t} \text{and} x_{2}(t) = \left [ \begin{matrix} -i \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ]e^{(-2-5i)t} \)

Next, use equation (7) to write

\( e^{(a+bi)t} = e^{at} \cdot e^{ibt} = e^{at}(\cos bt + i \sin bt) \) (7)

\( x_{1}(t) = \left [ \begin{matrix} i \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ] e^{-2t}(\cos 5t + i \sin 5t) \)

The real and imaginary parts of \( x_{1} \) provide real solutions:

\( y_{1}(t) = \left [ \begin{matrix} -\sin 5t \\ 2 \cos 5t \end{matrix} \right ] e^{-2t} , y_{2}(t) = \left [ \begin{matrix} \cos 5t \\ 2 \sin 5t \end{matrix} \right ] e^{-2t} \)

Since \( y_{1} \) and \( y_{2} \) are linearly independent functions, they form a basis for the two-dimensional real vector space of solutions of \( \acute{x} = Ax \). Thus the general solution is

\( x(t) = c_{1}\left [ \begin{matrix} -\sin 5t \\ 2 \cos 5t \end{matrix} \right ] e^{-2t} + c_{2}\left [ \begin{matrix} \cos 5t \\ 2 \sin 5t \end{matrix} \right ] e^{-2t} \)

To satisfy \( x(0) = \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 3 \end{matrix} \right ] \) , we need \( c_{1}\left [ \begin{matrix} 0 \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ] + c_{2}\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 \\ 3 \end{matrix} \right ] \) , which leads to \( c_{1} = 1.5 \text{and} c_{2} = 3 \) . Thus

\( x(t) = 1.5\left [ \begin{matrix} -\sin 5t \\ 2 \cos 5t \end{matrix} \right ] e^{-2t} + 3\left [ \begin{matrix} \cos 5t \\ 2 \sin 5t \end{matrix} \right ] e^{-2t} \)

or

\( \left [ \begin{matrix} i_{L}(t) \\ v_{C}(t) \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} -1.5 \sin 5t + 3 \cos 5t \\ 3 \cos 5t + 6 \sin 5t \end{matrix} \right ]e^{-2t} \)

See Figure 5.
