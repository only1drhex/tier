Guest Question.

The network in Fig. 8.55 models an unfortunate situation that is all too common. Node A, which is the voltage \(v_\text{in}(t)\) at the output of a temperature sensor, has “picked-up” a high frequency voltage, \(v_\text{noise}(t)\), caused by a nearby AM radio station. The noise frequency is 700 kHz. In this particular scenario, the sensor voltage, like temperature, tends to vary slowly. Our task then is to modify the circuit to reduce the noise at the output without disturbing the desired signal, \(v_{\text {in }}(t)\).

Admin
Solution

Consider the network in Fig. 8.56a. If component X has a high impedance, that is, much greater than R at 700 kHz, and an impedance of zero at dc, we should be able to alleviate the problem. Using voltage division to obtain \(\mathbf{V}_{o}\) in Fig. 8.56b, we find

 

\(\mathbf{V}_{o}=\left[\frac{R}{R+j \omega L}\right] \mathbf{V}_{1}\)

 

where \(V_{1}\) and ω are either \(\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}\) and 0, or \(\mathbf{V}_{\text {noise }}\) and \(2 \pi\left(700 \times 10^{3}\right)\). At dc, ω = 0, the inductor’s impedance is zero, the voltage division ratio is unity, \(\mathbf{V}_{1}\) is \(\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}\) and \(\mathbf{V}_{o}\) equals \(\mathbf{V}_{\text {in }}\). But at 700 kHz, \(\mathrm{V}_{1}\) is \(\mathbf{V}_{\text {noise }}\) and the desired voltage division ratio should be very small; that is, the inductor impedance must be much greater than R, so that \(\mathbf{V}_{o}\) becomes nearly zero. If we choose to reduce the noise at the output by 90 %, we find

 

\(\left|\frac{R}{R+j \omega L}\right|=\frac{1}{10} \quad \text { at } f=700 \mathrm{kHz}\)

 

Solving this equation yields L = 22.6 mH, which is close to a standard inductor value.
