Guest Question.

A ball of mass m is dropped from a height h above the ground, as shown in Figure 8.6.

(A) Neglecting air resistance, determine the speed of the ball when it is at a height y above the ground.

(B) Determine the speed of the ball at y if at the instant of release it already has an initial upward speed \( v_{i} \) at the initial altitude h.

Admin
Solution

Figure 8.6 and our everyday experience with falling objects allow us to conceptualize the situation. While we can readily solve this problem with the techniques of Chapter 2.

let us take an energy approach and categorize this as an energy problem for practice. To analyze the problem, we identify the system as the ball and the Earth. Because there is no air resistance and the system is isolated, we apply the principle of conservation of mechanical energy to the ball–Earth system.
At the instant the ball is released, its kinetic energy is \( K_{i}=0 \text { and the potential energy of the system is } U_{i}=m g h \text {. } \)

When the ball is at a distance y above the ground, its kinetic \( \text { energy is } K_{f}=\frac{1}{2} m v_{f}{ }^{2} \text { and the potential energy relative to } \) \( \text { the ground is } U_{f}=m g y \text {. Applying Equation } 8.9 \text {, we obtain } \)

\( K_{f}+U_{f}=K_{i}+U_{i} \) (8.9).

\( K_{f}+U_{f}=K_{i}+U_{i} \).

\( \frac{1}{2} m v_{f}^{2}+m g y=0+m g h \).

\( v_{f}^{2}=2 g(h-y) \).

\( v_{f}=\sqrt{2 g(h-y)} \).

The speed is always positive. If we had been asked to find the ball’s velocity, we would use the negative value of the square root as the y component to indicate the downward motion.

(B) Determine the speed of the ball at y if at the instant of release it already has an initial upward speed \( v_{i} \) at the initial altitude h.

In this case, the initial energy includes kinetic energy equal to \( \frac{1}{2} m v_{i}^{2} \text { and Equation } 8.9 \text { gives } \)

\( K_{f}+U_{f}=K_{i}+U_{i} \) (8.9).

\( \frac{1}{2} m v_{f}^{2}+m g y=\frac{1}{2} m v_{i}^{2}+m g h \).

\( v_{f}^{2}=v_{i}^{2}+2 g(h-y) \).

\( v_{f}=\sqrt{v_{i}{ }^{2}+2 g(h-y)} \).

Note that this result is consistent with the expression \( v_{y f}^{2}=v_{y i}^{2}-2 g\left(y_{f}-y_{i}\right) \text { from kinematics, where } y_{i}=h \).

Furthermore, this result is valid even if the initial velocity is at an angle to the horizontal (Quick Quiz 8.6) for two reasons: (1) energy is a scalar, and the kinetic energy depends only on the magnitude of the velocity; and (2) the change in the gravitational potential energy depends only on the change in position in the vertical direction.
