Guest Question.

A certain refrigerator has a COP of 5.00. When the refrigerator is running, its power input is 500 W. A sample of water of mass 500 g and temperature 20.0°C is placed in the freezer compartment. How long does it take to freeze the water to ice at 0°C? Assume that all other parts of the refrigerator stay at the same temperature and there is no leakage of energy from the exterior, so that the operation of the refrigerator results only in energy being extracted from the water.

Admin
Solution

Conceptualize this problem by realizing that energy leaves the water, reducing its temperature and then freezing it into ice. The time interval required for this entire process is related to the rate at which energy is withdrawn from the water, which, in turn is related to the power input of the refrigerator. We categorize this problem as one in which we will need to combine our understanding of temperature changes and phase changes from Chapter 20 with our understanding of heat pumps from the current chapter.
To analyze the problem, we first find the amount of energy that we must extract from 500 g of water at 20°C to turn it into ice at 0°C. Using Equations 20.4 and 20.6,

\( Q=m c \Delta T \) (20.4).

\( Q=\pm m L \) (20.6).

\( \left|Q_{c}\right|=\left|m c \Delta T+m L_{f}\right|=m\left|c \Delta T+L_{f}\right| \).

\( =(0.500 kg )\left[\left(4186 J / kg \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(20.0^{\circ} C \right)+3.33 \times 10^{5} J / kg \right] \).

\( =2.08 \times 10^{5} J \).

Now we use Equation 22.4 to find out how much energy we need to provide to the refrigerator to extract this much energy from the water:

\( \operatorname{COP}(\text { cooling mode })=\frac{\left|Q_{c}\right|}{W} \) (22.4).

\( COP =\frac{\left|Q_{c}\right|}{W} \longrightarrow W=\frac{\left|Q_{c}\right|}{ COP }=\frac{2.08 \times 10^{5} J }{5.00} \).

\( W=4.17 \times 10^{4} J \).

Using the power rating of the refrigerator, we find out the time interval required for the freezing process to occur:

\( P =\frac{W}{\Delta t} \longrightarrow \Delta t=\frac{W}{ P }=\frac{4.17 \times 10^{4} J }{500 W }=83.3 s \).

To finalize this problem, note that this time interval is very different from that of our everyday experience; this suggests the difficulties with our assumptions. Only a small part of the energy extracted from the refrigerator interior in a given time interval will come from the water. Energy must also be extracted from the container in which the water is placed, and energy that continuously leaks into the interior from the exterior must be continuously extracted.
In reality, the time interval for the water to freeze is much longer than 83.3 s.
