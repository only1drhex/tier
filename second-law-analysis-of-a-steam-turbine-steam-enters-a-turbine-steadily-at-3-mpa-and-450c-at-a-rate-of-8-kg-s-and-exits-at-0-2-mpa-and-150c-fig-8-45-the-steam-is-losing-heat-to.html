Second-Law Analysis of a Steam Turbine

Steam enters a turbine steadily at 3 MPa and 450°C at a rate of 8 kg/s and exits at 0.2 MPa and 150°C, (Fig. 8–45). The steam is losing heat to the surrounding air at 100 kPa and 25°C at a rate of 300 kW, and the kinetic and potential energy changes are negligible. Determine (a) the actual power output, (b) the maximum possible power output, (c) the second-law efficiency, (d) the exergy destroyed, and (e) the exergy of the steam at the inlet conditions.

Admin
Solution

A steam turbine operating steadily between specified inlet and exit states is considered. The actual and maximum power outputs, the second-law efficiency, the exergy destroyed, and the inlet exergy are to be determined.

1 This is a steady-flow process since there is no change with time at any point and thus \( \Delta m_{ CV }=0, \Delta E_{ CV }=0, \text { and } \Delta X_{ CV }=0 \). 2 The kinetic and potential energies are negligible.
Analysis We take the turbine as the system. This is a control volume since mass crosses the system boundary during the process. We note that there is only one inlet and one exit and thus \( \dot{m}_{1}=\dot{m}_{2}=\dot{m} \). Also, heat is lost to the surrounding air and work is done by the system.
The properties of the steam at the inlet and exit states and the state of the environment are

Inlet state: \( \left.\begin{array}{c} P_{1}=3 MPa \\ T_{1}=450^{\circ} C \end{array}\right\} \begin{aligned} h_{1} &=3344.9 kJ / kg \\ s_{1} &=7.0856 kJ / kg \cdot K \end{aligned} \) (Table A–6)

 

Exit state: \( \left.\begin{array}{rl} P_{2} & =0.2 MPa \\ T_{2} & =150^{\circ} C \end{array}\right\} \begin{aligned} h_{2} &=2769.1 kJ / kg \\ s_{2} &=7.2810 kJ / kg \cdot K \end{aligned} \) (Table A–6)

 

Dead state: \( \left.\begin{array}{rl} P_{0} & =100 kPa \\ T_{0} & =25^{\circ} C \end{array}\right\} \begin{aligned} h_{0} \cong h_{f @ 25^{\circ} C } &=104.83 kJ / kg \\ s_{0} \cong s_{f @ 25^{\circ} C } &=0.3672 kJ / kg \cdot K \end{aligned} \) (Table A–4)

(a) The actual power output of the turbine is determined from the rate form of the energy balance,

\( \underbrace{\dot{E}_{\text {in }}-\dot{E}_{\text {out }}}_{\begin{array}{c} \text { Rate of net energy transfer } \\ \text { by heat, work, and mass } \end{array}}=\underbrace{d E_{\text {system }} {d t}^{\nearrow ^{0 (steady)}} }_{\begin{array}{c} \text { Rate of change e in internal, kinetic, } \\ \text { potential, etc., energies } \end{array}}=0 \)

 

\( \begin{aligned} \dot{E}_{\text {in }} &=\dot{E}_{\text {out }} \\ \dot{m} h_{1} &=\dot{W}_{\text {out }}+\dot{Q}_{\text {out }}+\dot{m} h_{2} \quad(\text { since ke } \cong \text { pe } \cong 0) \\ \dot{W}_{\text {out }} &=\dot{m}\left(h_{1}-h_{2}\right)-\dot{Q}_{\text {out }} \\ &=(8 kg / s )[(3344.9-2769.1) kJ / kg ]-300 kW \\ &=4306 kW \end{aligned} \)

(b) The maximum power output (reversible power) is determined from the rate form of the exergy balance applied on the extended system (system + immediate surroundings), whose boundary is at the environment temperature of \( T_{0} \), and by setting the exergy destruction term equal to zero,

\( \underbrace{\dot{X}_{\text {in }}-\dot{X}_{\text {out }}}_{\begin{array}{c} \text { Rate of net exergy transfer } \\ \text { by heat, work, and mass } \end{array}}-\underbrace{\dot{X}_{\text {destroyed }^{\nearrow ^{0 (reversible)}}} }_{\begin{array}{c} \text { Rate of exergy } \\ \text { destruction } \end{array}}=\underbrace{d X_{\text {system }} / d t^{\nearrow {0 (steady)} }}_{\begin{array}{c} \text { Rate of change } \\ \text { in exergy } \end{array}}=0 \)

 

\( \begin{aligned} \dot{X}_{\text {in }} &=\dot{X}_{\text {out }} \\ \dot{m} \psi_{1} &=\dot{W}_{\text {rev,out }}+\dot{X}_{\text {heat }}^{\nearrow 0}+\dot{m} \psi_{2} \\ \dot{W}_{\text {rev,out }} &=\dot{m}\left(\psi_{1}-\psi_{2}\right) \\ &=\dot{m}\left[\left(h_{1}-h_{2}\right)-T_{0}\left(s_{1}-s_{2}\right)-\Delta ke ^{\nearrow 0}-\Delta pe ^{\nearrow 0}\right] \end{aligned} \)

Note that exergy transfer with heat is zero when the temperature at the point of transfer is the environment temperature \( T_{0} \). Substituting,

\( \begin{aligned} \dot{W}_{\text {rev }, \text { out }}=&(8 kg / s )[(3344.9-2769.1) kJ / kg \\ &-(298 K )(7.0856-7.2810) kJ / kg \cdot K ] \\ =& 4665 kW \end{aligned} \)

(c) The second-law efficiency of a turbine is the ratio of the actual work delivered to the reversible work,

\( \eta_{\text {II }}=\frac{\dot{W}_{\text {out }}}{\dot{W}_{\text {rev,out }}}=\frac{4306 kW }{4665 kW }=0.923 \text { or } 92.3 \% \)

That is, 7.7 percent of the work potential is wasted during this process.
(d) The difference between the reversible work and the actual useful work is the exergy destroyed, which is determined to be

\( \dot{X}_{\text {destroyed }}=\dot{W}_{\text {rev,out }}-\dot{W}_{\text {out }}=4665-4306=359 kW \)

That is, the potential to produce useful work is wasted at a rate of 359 kW during this process. The exergy destroyed could also be determined by first calculating the rate of entropy generation \( \dot{S}_{\text {gen }} \) during the process.
(e) The exergy (maximum work potential) of the steam at the inlet conditions is simply the stream exergy, and is determined from

\( \begin{aligned} \psi_{1} &=\left(h_{1}-h_{0}\right)-T_{0}\left(s_{1}-s_{0}\right)+\frac{V_{1^{\nearrow^ 0}}^{2} }{2}+{g z_{1}}^{\nearrow ^0} \\ &=\left(h_{1}-h_{0}\right)-T_{0}\left(s_{1}-s_{0}\right) \\ &=(3344.9-104.83) kJ / kg -(298 K )(7.0856-0.3672) kJ / kg \cdot K \\ &=1238 kJ / kg \end{aligned} \)

That is, not counting the kinetic and potential energies, every kilogram of the steam entering the turbine has a work potential of 1238 kJ. This corresponds to a power potential of (8 kg/s)(1238 kJ/kg) = 9904 kW. Obviously, the turbine is converting 4306/9904 = 43.5 percent of the available work potential of the steam to work.
