Guest Question.

For the slider crank mechanism shown in Figure 8.21(a), the link lengths are

\(r_{O_{2}B} = r_{2} =3.5 in; r_{BD} = r_{3} = 9.0 in; r_{BG_{3}} = 4.5 in\)

and the constant input rotational speed is

\( \dot{θ}_{2} = 500 rpm CC = 52.4 rad/sec CCW\)

An outline of the acceleration polygon is given in Figure 8.21(b). The coupler, link 3, has a uniform cross section and the following properties:

\(m^{*}_{3} = 3.0 lbm ; I^{*}_{G_{3}} = 47.0 lbm-in^{2}\)

For the position shown, determine the forces on link 3 at the turning pairs due to the inertia of link 3.

Admin
Solution

Since the coupler has a uniform cross section, we locate the position of its center of mass midway between B and D on the acceleration polygon, as shown in Figure 8.21(b). The mag-nitude of the acceleration of the center of mass is

\(\left|\bar{a}_{G_{3}} \right| = 6350 in / sec^{2}\)

and the tangential acceleration component between points B and D is

\( a^{T}_{DB} = 7330 in / sec^{2}\)

The angular acceleration of link 3 is then

\(\ddot{\theta }_{3} = \frac{a^{T}_{DB}}{r_{DB}} = \frac{7330 }{9.0} = 814 rad/ sec^{2} CCW\)

The units of the quantities employed in this example correspond to the third set listed in Table 2.3. In the calculation of the inertia force, we substitute the corresponding value of the gravitational constant, gc, in Equation (2.14-15) \(\bar{F}_{IN} = -m \bar{a}_{G} = – \frac{m^{*}}{g_{c}} \bar{a}_{G} \) to calculate the magnitude of the inertia force:

\( \left|\bar{F}_{IN} \right|_{3} = m_{3} \left|\bar{a}_{G_{3}} \right| = \frac{m^{*}_{3}}{g_{c}} \left|\bar{a}_{G_{3}} \right| \)

 

\( = \frac{3.0 lbm}{\left\lgroup386 \frac{lbm-in}{lb-sec^{2}} \right\rgroup } × 6350 in/sec^{2} = 49.4 lb\)

From Equation (2.14-18)\( M_{IN} = – I_{G} \ddot{\theta } = – \frac{I_{G}^{*}}{g_{c}} \ddot{\theta } \), the magnitude of the inertia moment is

\( \left|M_{IN} \right|_{3} = I_{G_{3}} \left| \ddot{\theta }_{3}\right| = \frac{I_{G_{3}}^{*}}{g_{c}} \left| \ddot{\theta }_{3}\right| \)

 

\( = \frac{47.0 lbm-in^{2}}{\left\lgroup386 \frac{lbm-in}{lb-sec^{2}} \right\rgroup } × 814 rad/sec^{2} = 99.1 in- lb\)

Using Equation (8.8-4) \(ρ =\frac{\left|M_{G} \right|}{\left|\bar{F} \right|} = \frac{ I_{G} \left|\ddot{\theta } \right| }{m \left|\bar{a}_{G} \right| } = \frac{\left|M_{IN} \right|}{\left|\bar{F}_{IN} \right|} \), the radius of the inertia circle is

\(ρ_{3} =\frac{\left|M_{IN} \right|_{3}}{\left|\bar{F}_{IN} \right|_{3}} = \frac{99.1 in- lb}{49.4 lb} = 2.0 in \)

The inertia force, \(\bar{F}_{IN}\), is added to the drawing of the mechanism, offset by the radius of the inertia circle. The direction of \(\bar{F}_{IN}\) is opposite to that of \(\bar{a}_{G_{3}}\) . In this example, the link has an angular acceleration in the counterclockwise direction. Thus, the forces at the turning pairs are providing a moment in the counterclockwise direction. Therefore, we place the inertia force as shown in Figure 8.21(c) so that it applies an inertia moment in the clockwise direction, opposite to that of the actual moment. The sum of the moments is then zero. Since the mass of the slider is not considered in the analysis, the slider (link 4) is a two-force member. The direction of the force transmitted from the slider onto link 3 is perpendicular to the direction of the slide. The lines of action of the inertia force and that from the slider intersect at K. The third line of action, through the turning pair at B, must pass through the same intersection point. Using the inertia force and the other two lines of action, the force polygon is completed, as shown in Figure 8.21(d). The magnitudes of the forces located at B and D as a result of the inertia in link 3 are

\(\left|\bar{F}_{43} \right| = 13.1 lb ; \left|\bar{F}_{23} \right| = 42.3 lb\)

The directions of these forces are indicated in the force polygon.
