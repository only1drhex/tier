Guest Question.

Use the Fourier transform to find \( i_{o} \left(t\right)\) in the circuit shown in Fig. 17.9. The current source is \( i_{g} \left(t\right)\) the signum function 20 sgn\(\left(t\right) A\).

Admin
Solution

The Fourier transform of the driving source is

\( I_{g} \left(\omega \right)=\mathscr{F} \left\{20 sgn\left(t\right) \right\}\)

 

\( =20\left(\frac{2}{j\omega } \right)\)

 

\( =\frac{40}{j\omega }\).

The transfer function of the circuit is the ratio of \( I_{0}\) to \( I_{g}\); so

\(H \left(\omega \right)=\frac{I_{0}}{I_{g} } =\frac{1}{4+j\omega }\).

The Fourier transform of \( i_{0}\left(t \right)\) is

\( I_{0}\left(\omega \right)=I_{g} \left(\omega \right)H \left(\omega \right)\)

 

\(=\frac{40}{j\omega \left(4+j\omega \right)}\).

Expanding \( I_{0}\left(\omega \right)\) into a sum of partial fractions yields

\( I_{0}\left(\omega \right)=\frac{K_{1}}{j\omega }+\frac{K_{2}}{4+j\omega}\).

Evaluating \(K_{1}\) and \(K_{2}\) gives

\(K_{1}=\frac{40}{4}=10\),

 

\(K_{2}=\frac{40}{-4}=-10\).

Therefore

\( I_{0}\left(\omega \right)=\frac{10}{j\omega}-\frac{10}{4+j\omega}\).

The response is

\( i_{0}\left(\omega \right)=\mathscr{F} ^{-1} \left[I_{0}\left(\omega \right) \right] \)

 

\(=5 sgn\left(t\right) -10e^{-4t}u\left(t\right)\).

Figure 17.10 shows the response. Does the solution make sense in terms of known circuit behavior? The answer is yes, for the following reasons. The current source delivers \(-20 A\) to the circuit between \(-\infty\) and \(0\). The resistance in each branch governs how \(-20 A\) the divides between the two branches. In particular, one fourth of the \(-20 A\) appears in the \( i_{0}\) branch; therefore \( i_{0}\) is \(-5\) for \( t\lt 0\). When the current source jumps from \(-20 A\) to \(+20 A\) at \( t=0\), \( i_{0}\) approaches its final value of \(+5 A\) exponentially with a time constant of \(\frac{1}{4} s\).

An important characteristic of the Fourier transform is that it directly yields the steady-state response to a sinusoidal driving function. The reason is that the Fourier transform of \(\cos \omega _{0}t\) is based on the assumption that the function exists over all time. illustrates this feature.
