Guest Question.

A cable-supported roof carries a uniform load w = 0.6 kip/ft (Figure 6.7a). If the cable sag at midspan is set at 10 ft, what is the maximum tension in the cable (a) between points B and D and (b) between points A and B?

Admin
Solution

(a) Apply Equation 6.6 at midspan to analyze the cable between points B and D. Apply the uniform load to a simply supported beam and compute the moment\( M_{z} \) at midspan (Figure 6.7c). Since the moment curve is a parabola, the cable is also a parabola between points B and D.

 

\( Hh_{z}=M_{z}\) (6.6)

 

\( Hh=M_{z}=\frac{wL^{2}}{8}\)

 

\( H\left ( 10 \right )=\frac{0.6\left ( 120 \right )^{2}}{8}\)

 

\( H=108 \) kips

 

The maximum cable tension in span BD occurs at the supports where the slope is maximum. To establish the slope at the supports, we differentiate the equation of the cable\( y=4\frac{hx^{2}}{L^{2}}\) (Figure 6.7b).

 

\( \tan \Theta = \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{8hx}{L^{2}}\)

 

at\( x= 60 \) ft, \( \tan \Theta =8\left ( 10 \right )\left ( 60 \right )/\left ( 120 \right )^{2}=\frac{1}{3}\) , and \( \Theta = 18.43^{\circ}\)

 

\( \cos \Theta = 0.949\)

 

Substituting into

 

\( T = \frac{H}{\cos \Theta } \) (6.1)

 

\( T = \frac{108}{0.949 } =113.8 \) kips

 

(b) If we neglect the weight of the cable between points A and B, the cable can be treated as a straight member. Since the cable slope θ is 45°, the cable tension equals

 

\( T = \frac{H}{\cos \Theta }=\frac{108}{0.707}=152.76 \) kips
