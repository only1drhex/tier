Guest Question.

As shown in Fig. E3.17a, a pipe bend is supported at point A and connected to a flow system by flexible couplings at sections 1 and 2. The fluid is incompressible, and ambient pressure \(p_{a}\) is zero. (a) Find an expression for the torque T that must be resisted by the support at A, in terms of the flow properties at sections 1 and 2 and the distances \(h_{1}\) and \(h_{2}\) . (b) Compute this torque if \(D_{1} = D_{2}= 3 in, p_{1} = 100 lbf/in^{2} gage\) , \(p_{2} = 80 lbf/in^{2} gage\) , \(v_{1}=40 ft/s\), \(h_{1}= 2 in, h_{2} = 10 in, and \rho =1.94 slugs/ft^3\).

Admin
Solution

Part (a): The control volume chosen in Fig. E3.17b cuts through sections 1 and 2 and through the support at A, where the torque \(T_{A}\) is desired. The flexible couplings description specifies that there is no torque at either section 1 or 2, and so the cuts there expose no moments. For the angular momentum terms \(r \times V, r\) should be taken from point A to sections 1 and 2. Note that the gage pressure forces \(p_{1} A_{1}\) and \(p_{2} A_{2}\)both have moments about A. Equation (3.59)

\(\sum{M_{0}}=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\int_{CV}^{}{(r\times V)\rho dv} \right] +\int_{CS}^{}{(r\times v)\rho (V\cdot n)dA}\)

with one-dimensional flux terms becomes
\(\sum{M_{A}}=T_{1}+r_{1}\times (-p_{1}A_{1}n_{1} ) +r_{2}\times (-p_{2}A_{2}n_{2} )\)

\(=(r_{2}\times V_{2})(+\dot{m}_{out})+(r_{1}\times V_{1})(-\dot{m}_{in})\)

Figure E3.17c shows that all the cross products are associated with either \(r_{1} \sin \theta_{1}= h_{1}\) or
\(r_{2} \sin \theta_{2} = h_{2}\), the perpendicular distances from point A to the pipe axes at 1 and 2. Remember that \(\dot{m}_{out}=\dot{m}_{in}\) from the steady flow continuity relation. In terms of counterclockwis moments, Eq. (1) then becomes
\(T_{A}+p_{1}A_{1}h_{1}-p_{2}A_{2}h_{2}=\dot{m}(h_{2} V_{2}-h_{1} V_{1} )\) (2)
Rewriting this, we find the desired torque to be
\(T_{A}=h_{2}(p_{2}A_{2}+\dot{m}V_{2})-h_{1}(p_{1}A_{1}+\dot{m}V_{1})\)
counterclockwise. The quantities \(p_{1}\) and \(p_{2}\) are gage pressures. Note that this result is independent of the shape of the pipe bend and varies only with the properties at sections 1 and 2 and the distances \(h_{1}\) and \(h_{2} .^{15}\)
Part (b): For the numerical example, convert all data to BG units:
\(D_{1}=D_{2}= 3 in= 0.25 ft\)

\(p_{1}= 100\frac{lbf}{in^2}=14.400\frac{lbf}{ft^2}\) \(p_{2}=80\frac{lbf}{in^2}=11.520\frac{lbf}{ft^2}\)

\(h_{1}= 2 in=\frac{2}{12}ft\), \(h_{2}=10 in =\frac{10}{12}ft\)
, \(\rho =1.94\frac{slug}{ft^3}\)
The inlet and exit areas are the same, \(A_{1} = A_{2} = (\pi/4)(0.25 ft)^2 = 0.0491 ft^2\). Since the density is constant, we conclude from mass conservation, \(\rho A_{1}V_{1} = \rho A_{2}V_{2}\), that \(V_{1} =V_{2} = 40 ft/s\). The mass flow is \(\dot{m}=\rho A_{1}V_{1}=(1.94\frac{slug}{ft^3})(0.0491 ft^2)(40\frac{ft}{s})=3.81\frac{slug}{s}\)
• Evaluation of the torque: The data can now be substituted into Eq. (3):
\(T_{A}=(\frac{10}{12}ft)\left[\left(11,520 \frac{lbf}{ft^2} \right)(0.0491 ft^2)+\left(3.81\frac{slug}{s} \right)\left(40\frac{ft}{s} \right)\right]-\left(\frac{2}{10} \right)\left[\left(14.400\frac{lbf}{ft^2} \right)(0.0491 ft^2)+\left(3.81\frac{slug}{s} \right) \left(40\frac{ft}{s} \right) \right]\)

\(=598 ft\cdot lbf -143 ft\cdot lbf= 455 ft\cdot lbf \) counterclockwise
• Comments: The use of standard BG units is crucial when combining dissimilar terms,
such as pressure times area and mass flow times velocity, into proper additive units for a numerical solution
