Guest Question.

The Hamiltonian due to the interaction of a particle of mass m, charge q, and spin \(\vec{S}\) with a magnetic field pointing along the z-axis is \(\hat{H}=-(q B / m c) \hat{S}_{z}\) . Write the Heisenberg equations of motion for the time-dependent spin operators \(\hat{S}_{x}(t), \hat{S}_{y}(t)\), and \(\hat{S}_{z}(t)\), and solve them to obtain the operators as functions of time.

Admin
Solution

Let us write \(\hat{H}\) in a lighter form \(\hat{H}=\omega \hat{S}_{z}\) where \(\omega=-q B / m c\). The commutation of \(\hat{H}\) with the components of the spin operator can be inferred at once from \(\left[\hat{S}_{x}, \hat{S}_{z}\right]=-i \hbar \hat{S}_{y}\) and \(\left[\hat{S}_{y}, \hat{S}_{z}\right]=i \hbar \hat{S}_{x}\):

\(\left[\hat{S}_{x}, \hat{H}\right]=-i \hbar \omega \hat{S}_{y}, \quad\left[\hat{S}_{y}, \hat{H}\right]=i \hbar \omega \hat{S}_{x}, \quad\left[\hat{S}_{z}, \hat{H}\right]=0\). (10.174)

The Heisenberg equations of motion for \(\hat{S}_{x}(t), \hat{S}_{y}(t)\), and \(\hat{S}_{z}(t)\) can be obtained from \(d \hat{A}_{H}(t) / d t=(1 / i \hbar)\left[\hat{A}_{H}(t), \hat{H}\right]=(1 / i \hbar) e^{i t \hat{H} / \hbar}[\hat{A}(0), \hat{H}] e^{-i t \hat{H} / \hbar}\) which, using (10.174), leads to

\(\frac{d \hat{S}_{x}(t)}{d t}=\frac{1}{i \hbar}\left[\hat{S}_{x}(t), \hat{H}\right]=\frac{1}{i \hbar} e^{i t \hat{H} / \hbar}\left[\hat{S}_{x}(0), \hat{H}\right] e^{-i t \hat{H} / \hbar}\)

 

\(=\frac{-i \hbar \omega}{i \hbar} e^{i t \hat{H} / \hbar} \hat{S}_{y}(0) e^{-i t \hat{H} / \hbar}=-\omega \hat{S}_{y}(t)\). (10.175)

Similarly, we have

\(\frac{d \hat{S}_{y}(t)}{d t}=\frac{1}{i \hbar} e^{i t \hat{H} / \hbar}\left[\hat{S}_{y}(0), \hat{H}\right] e^{-i t \hat{H} / \hbar}=\frac{i \hbar \omega}{i \hbar} e^{i t \hat{H} / \hbar} \hat{S}_{x}(0) e^{-i t \hat{H} / \hbar}=\omega \hat{S}_{x}(t)\), (10.176)

 

\(\frac{d \hat{S}_{z}(t)}{d t}=\frac{1}{i \hbar} e^{i t \hat{H} / \hbar}\left[\hat{S}_{z}(0), \hat{H}\right] e^{-i t \hat{H} / \hbar}=0\). (10.177)

To solve (10.175) and (10.176), we may combine them into two more conducive equations:

\(\frac{d \hat{S}_{\pm}(t)}{d t}=\pm i \omega \hat{S}_{\pm}(t)\), (10178)

where \(\hat{S}_{\pm}(t)=\hat{S}_{x}(t) \pm i \hat{S}_{y}(t)\). The solutions of (10.178) are \(\hat{S}_{\pm}(t)=\hat{S}_{\pm}(0) e^{\pm i \omega t}\) which, when
combined with \(\hat{S}_{x}(t)=\frac{1}{2}\left[\hat{S}_{+}(t)+\hat{S}_{-}(t)\right]\) and \(\hat{S}_{y}(t)=\frac{1}{2 i}\left[\hat{S}_{+}(t)-\hat{S}_{-}(t)\right]\), lead to

\(\hat{S}_{x}(t)=\hat{S}_{x}(0) \cos (\omega t)-\hat{S}_{y}(0) \sin (\omega t)\), (10.179_

 

\(\hat{S}_{y}(t)=\hat{S}_{y}(0) \cos (\omega t)+\hat{S}_{x}(0) \sin (\omega t)\). (10.180)

The solution of (10.177) is obvious:

\(\frac{d \hat{S}_{z}(t)}{d t}=0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \hat{S}_{z}(t)=\hat{S}_{z}(0)\). (10.181)
