Guest Question.

The stiffness of a helical compression spring (see Figure 15.12) can be calculated from:

\( k = \frac{d^{4}G}{8D^{3}n} \)

where d is wire diameter; G, modulus of rigidity; D, coil diameter; n, number of coils; k, spring stiffness.

The mean and standard deviation for each variable are known:

\( \mu _{d} = 2.34 mm, \mu _{D} = 16.71 mm, \)
\( \mu _{G} = 79.29 \times 10^{3} N/mm^{2},\)
\( \mu _{n} = 14 coils, \)
\( \sigma _{d} = 0.010 mm, \sigma _{D} = 0.097 mm, \)
\( \sigma _{G} = 1.585 \times 10^{3} N/mm^{2} \)
\( \sigma _{n} = 0.0833 coils. \)

Calculate the sure-fit extreme tolerance limits and the statistical basic normal tolerance limits for the spring stiffness. Assume \( \pm 3\sigma \) natural tolerance limits: i.e. 99.73 per cent of measurements lie within ± 3 standard deviations.

Admin
Solution

Substituting values into the equation for k to live the average value of the spring constant:

\( \overset{-}{k} = \frac{2.34^{4} \times 79.29 \times 10^{3}}{8 \times 16.71^{3} \times 14} \)
\( = 4.549 N/mm \).
\( \sigma _{d} = 0.01 mm, \)
\( \Delta d = 6 \times \sigma _{d} = 0.06 mm, \)
\( \sigma _{G} = 1.585 \times 10^{3}, \)
\( \Delta G = 6 \times \sigma _{G} = 9510 N/mm^{2}, \)
\( \sigma _{D} =0.097, \)
\( \Delta D = 6 \times \sigma _{D} = 0.582 mm \)
\( \sigma _{n} =0.0833 coils, \)
\( \Delta n = 6 \times \sigma _{n} = 0.5 \)
\( \frac{k}{d} = \frac{4d^{3}G}{8D^{3}n} = \frac{2.34^{3} \times 79.29 \times 10^{3}}{2 \times 16.71^{3} \times 14} \)
\( = 7.776. \)
\( \frac{k}{G} = \frac{d^{4}}{8D^{3}n} = 5.7374 \times 10^{-5}. \)
\( \frac{k}{D} = – \frac{3d^{4}G}{8D^{4}n} = – 8.1673 \times 10^{-1}. \)
\( \frac{k}{n} = – \frac{d^{4}G}{8D^{3}n^{2}} = -3.2494 \times 10^{-1} \)
\( \Delta k_{sure-fit} = \left|\frac{k}{d} \right| \Delta d + \left|\frac{k}{G} \right| \Delta G + \left|\frac{k}{D} \right| \Delta D + \left|\frac{k}{n} \right| \Delta n \)
\( = 7.776 \times 0.06 + 5.7374 \times 10^{-5} \times 9510 + 0.81673 \times 0.582 + 0.32494 \times 0.5 \)
\( =0.46656 + 0.5456 + 0. 4753 + 0.16247 \)
\( = 1.65N/mm.\)

This figure (\( (\Delta k_{ sure-fit }) \)) gives the overall worst case variability of the spring stiffness (i.e. the total tolerance). The bilateral tolerance can be obtained by dividing Δk by two (i.e.1.65/2 = 0.825).

So the spring stiffness can be stated as k = 4.549 ± 0.825N/mm
Calculation of the basic normal variability:

\( \Delta k_{basic normal}^{2} = \left|\frac{k}{d} \right|^{2} \Delta d^{2} + \left|\frac{k}{G} \right|^{2} \Delta G^{2} + \left|\frac{k}{D} \right|^{2} \Delta D^{2} + \left|\frac{k}{n} \right|^{2} \Delta n^{2} \)
\( = 0.21768 + 0.297708 + 0.22594 + 0.026397 \)
\( = 0.7677. \)
\(\Delta k_{basic normal} = 0.8762 N/mm. \)

The overall variability in the spring stiffness is calculated as 0.8762 N/mm using the basic normal method. The bilateral tolerance can be calculated by dividing this value in two, i.e. the spring stiffness k = 4.549 ± 0.4381 N/mm.

The basic normal variation model implies a narrower band width of variation k = 4.549 ± 0.438N/mm versus k = 4.549 ± 0.825N/mm for sure-fit. This is more attractive from a sales perspective and accounts for 99.73 per cent (i.e.nearly all) of all items.

If we wanted to decrease the variation in k, we would need to tighten the individual tolerances on d, G, D and n with particular attention to d and D as these are controllable and influential on the overall variability.

The standard deviation of the spring stiffness can be calculated, if required, by σk = Δk/6 = 0.146.
