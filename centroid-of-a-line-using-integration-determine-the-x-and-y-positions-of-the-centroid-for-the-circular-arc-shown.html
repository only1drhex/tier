Centroid of a Line Using Integration

Determine the x and y positions of the centroid for the circular arc shown.

Admin
Solution

Road Map Because the arc is symmetric about the x axis, the centroid must lie on the x axis. Therefore, by inspection, the y position of the centroid is \(\overline{y}= 0\). To determine the x position of the centroid, we will evaluate the first expression in Eq. (7.11) on p. 398, and for developing expressions for dL and \(\widetilde{x}\), polar coordinates will be very convenient.

Governing Equations & Computation Expressions for dL and \(\widetilde{x}\) will be written using the line element shown in Fig. 2 as follows:

\(dL = r d\theta\) , (1)
\(\widetilde{x} = r \cos \theta\) . (2)

Substituting Eqs. (1) and (2) into the first expression of Eq. (\(\overline{x} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dL} }{\int{dL} }\) and \(\overline{y} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{y} dL} }{\int{dL} }\)

where \(\widetilde{x}\) and \(\widetilde{y}\) are the locations of the centroid of length element dL measured from the origin of the coordinate system.), the x position of the centroid is given by

\(\overline{x} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dL} }{\int{dL} } = \frac{\int\limits_{-\alpha }^{\alpha }{r \cos \theta r d\theta} }{\int\limits_{-\alpha }^{\alpha }{r d\theta} } = \frac{r^2(\sin \theta) |^{\alpha}_{-\alpha}}{r(\theta)|^{\alpha}_{-\alpha}} = \frac{2r^2 \sin \alpha}{2 r \alpha}\) (3)

\(=\frac{r \sin \alpha}{\alpha}\) . (4)

Discussion & Verification

â€¢ For a circular arc, r = constant, and the integrals in Eq. (3) are simplified, as follows. Because r is not a function of \(\theta\) , the \(r^2\) term in the numerator and the r term in the denominator of Eq. (3) may be brought outside their integrals, leaving simple functions of \(\theta\) to be integrated. For a line that is not a circular arc, r is a function of \(\theta\), giving rise to generally more difficult integrals to evaluate. For such cases, the approach used in , where integrations are carried out with respect to x (or y), may be more convenient.

â€¢ The validity of our results can be partially checked by considering the location of N x for particular values of \(\alpha\). When \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\), the arc approaches a point, and the centroid should approach \(\overline{x} = r\), which Eq. (4) does.* When \(\alpha = \pi \), the arc becomes a full circle, and the centroid should become \(\overline{x} = 0\), which Eq. (4) does.

where \(\widetilde{x}\) and \(\widetilde{y}\) are the locations of the centroid of length element dL measured from the origin of the coordinate system.) on p. 398 by using \(\widetilde{y} = r \sin \theta\) to confirm that \(\overline{y} = 0\).
