Guest Question.

Consider the following circuit involving two capacitors and three inductors that are connected to a time-harmonic voltage source.

Find \(v_{C}(t)\) in steady state.

Admin
Solution

In order to solve this problem, we need to convert the time-domain circuit into a phasor-domain circuit. Considering that \(\omega=1000 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}\), we have the following conversions:

  • \(4 \cos \left(1000 t+45^{\circ}\right) \mathrm{V} \longrightarrow \frac{4}{\sqrt{2}} \underline{/\pi/4} \mathrm{~V},\)
  • \(0.1 \mathrm{H} \longrightarrow j \times 1000 \times 0.1=j 100 \Omega\),
  • \(0.2 \mathrm{H} \longrightarrow j \times 1000 \times 0.2=j 200 \Omega\),
  • \(0.4 \mathrm{H} \longrightarrow j \times 1000 \times 0.4=j 400 \Omega\),
  • \(1.25 \mu \mathrm{F} \longrightarrow 1 /\left(j \times 1000 \times 1.25 \times 10^{-6}\right)=-j 800 \Omega\),
  • \(5 \mu \mathrm{F} \longrightarrow 1 /\left(j \times 1000 \times 5 \times 10^{-6}\right)=-j 200 \Omega\).

Consequently, in the phasor domain, we have the following circuit.

Now the circuit can be solved via any method: nodal analysis, mesh analysis, or by directly using KCL and KVL. Since the circuit is quite simple, one can also use series and parallel connections of impedances to find the overall input impedance seen by the voltage source:

\(\begin{aligned}Z_{\text {in }} &=j 100+(j 400-j 200) \|(j 200-j 800)=j 100+\frac{j 200 \times(-j 600)}{j 200-j 600} \\&=j 100+\frac{j 200 \times(-j 600)}{-j 400}=j 100+j 300=j 400 \Omega .\end{aligned}\)

We note that the input impedance is purely reactive and does not have any real part due to lack of resistance and power dissipation. The current flowing through the voltage source can be found to be

\(\underline{i}_{\text {in }}=\frac{\frac{4}{\sqrt{2}} \underline{/\pi/4}}{j 400} \mathrm{~A} .\)

Since the numerator and denominator of the fraction above have different forms, one needs to manipulate one of them in order to evaluate the expression. For example, modifying the numerator, we have

\(\begin{aligned}\underline{i}_{\text {in }} &=\frac{\frac{4}{\sqrt{2}}(\cos (\pi / 4)+j \sin (\pi / 4))}{j 400} \\&=\frac{2+j 2}{j 400}=\frac{1+j}{j 200}=\frac{1-j}{200}=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{200} \underline{/(-\pi/4)} \mathrm{A} .\end{aligned}\)

Alternatively, one can find

\(\underline{i}_{\text {in }}=\frac{\frac{4}{\sqrt{2}} \underline{/\pi/4}}{400 \underline{/\pi/2}}=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{200} \underline{/\pi/4 -\pi / 2}=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{200} \underline{/(-\pi/4)} \mathrm{A} .\)

In order to find the current through the \(1.25 \mu \mathrm{F}\) capacitor, we can use the rule of current division:

\(\underline{i}_{C}=\frac{j 200}{j 200-j 600} \times \underline{i}_{in}=-\frac{1}{2} \underline{i}_{in}=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{400} \underline{/ (-\pi / 4+\pi)}=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{400} \underline{/3\pi/4} \mathrm{~A} .\)

Finally, the voltage across the capacitor can be found to be

\(\begin{aligned}\underline{v}_{C} &=Z_{C} \underline{i}_{C}=-j 800 \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{400} \underline{/3\pi/4} \mathrm{~A}=2 \sqrt{2} \underline{/3\pi/4-\pi / 2 }\\&=2 \sqrt{2} \underline{/\pi/4} \mathrm{~V} .\end{aligned}\)

Converting the final expression into the time domain, we have

\(\begin{aligned}v_{C}(t) &=\sqrt{2} \operatorname{Re}\left\{\underline{v}_{C} \exp (j \omega t)\right\}=4 \cos (1000 t+\pi / 4) \\&=4 \cos \left(1000 t+45^{\circ}\right) \mathrm{V} .\end{aligned}\)

We note that, as a coincidence, the voltage of the capacitor is in phase with the voltage source, while this could not be understood directly without analyzing the circuit.
