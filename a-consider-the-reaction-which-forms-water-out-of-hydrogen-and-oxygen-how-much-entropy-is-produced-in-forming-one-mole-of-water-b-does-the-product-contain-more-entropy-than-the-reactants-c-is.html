Guest Question.

(a) Consider the reaction which forms water out of hydrogen and oxygen. How much entropy is produced in forming one mole of water?

(b) Does the product contain more entropy than the reactants?

(c) Is the reaction exothermic or endothermic? How much entropy and energy are exchanged in heating or cooling? Remember that the reaction is assumed to proceed at constant temperature and pressure.

Admin
Solution

(a) The amount of entropy produced is equal to the energy dissipated, divided by the temperature of the fluid. We have

\(\begin{aligned} \bar{s}_{\text {prod }} &=-\frac{1}{T}\left[\mu_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}-\left(\mu_{\mathrm{H}_{2}}+0.5 \mu_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}\right)\right] \\ &=-\frac{1}{298}\left[-237.2 \cdot 10^{3}-(1 \cdot 0+0.5 \cdot 0)\right] \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \mathrm{mole}}=796 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \mathrm{mole}} \end{aligned}\)

 

(b) We need to know the molar entropies of the species involved. The change of entropy of the substances calculated for the reaction is

\(\begin{aligned} \Delta \bar{s} &=\bar{s}_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}-\left(\bar{s}_{\mathrm{H}_{2}}+0.5 \bar{s}_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}\right) \\ &=69.9 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \mathrm{mole}}-(1 \cdot 130.6+0.5 \cdot 205) \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \mathrm{mole}}=-163.2 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \mathrm{mole}} \end{aligned}\)

 

This means that water contains less entropy than the elemental substances out of which it is formed.

(c) Considering that entropy has been produced and that the entropy content has decreased, it is clear that the fluids must be cooled. The amount of entropy exchanged is given by

\(\bar{s}_{e}=\Delta \bar{s}-\bar{s}_{\text {prod }}=-163.2 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \mathrm{mole}}-796 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \mathrm{mole}}=-959 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \mathrm{mole}}\)

 

This value, multiplied by the temperature of the fluid, is equal to the energy emitted \(\left(\bar{e}_{t h}\right):\)

\(\bar{e}_{t h}=T \bar{s}_{e}=298 \mathrm{~K} \cdot\left(-959 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{K} \cdot \text { mole }}\right)=-285.8 \cdot 10^{3} \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\text { mole }}\)

 

This should be equal to the heating value reported in tables of fuels (see Table 6.5). We have calculated the energy exchanged in cooling of the system for one mole of \(H_{2}\) gas. To obtain the same number for a kilogram of \(H_{2}\), we divide by the molar mass of the gas and obtain a value of \(285.8 Â· 10^{3} kJ/mole / 0.0020 kg/mole = 143 MJ/kg\).
