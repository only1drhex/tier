Guest Question.

Given:

Data from Fig. Ex. 10.10a. The value of \(c_{v}\), the coefficient of consolidation, is 1 \(m ^{2} / yr\).

Required:

Compute and plot the time rate of consolidation settlement.

Admin
Solution

From Fig. Ex. 10.10b you probably noticed that the increase in \(\Delta \sigma\) from the loaded tank on the surface is almost linear. Such a linear variation of \(\Delta u=\Delta \sigma\) allows us to use Eqs. (9.5),(10.9) and (9.12) to prepare our plot.

 

\(T=c_{v} \frac{t}{H_{ dr }^{2}}\) (9.5)

 

\(U_{ avg }=\frac{s(t)}{s_{c}}\) (9.12)

 

\(\sigma_{z}=q_{o} I\) (10.9)

 

A look at Fig. Ex. 10.10a will show that both the top and bottom layers are pervious and will drain the compressible clay layer during consolidation. Therefore, we have a case of double drainage, and 2H=30 m, where H= the drainage distance.

Using Eq.(9.5), we find

 

\(T=c_{v} \frac{t}{H_{ dr }^{2}}=1 \frac{ m ^{2}}{ yr } \frac{t^{2}}{(15 m )^{2}}\)

 

Solving for the real time, t,

 

\(t=\frac{T(15 m )^{2}}{c_{v}}=T \frac{225 m ^{2}}{1 \frac{ m ^{2}}{ yr }}=T \times 225 yr\)

 

Now, make a copy of Table 9.1 and add two more columns – one for \(\Delta h\) and the other for time t. For a given value of \(U_{ avg }\), multiply the corresponding value of T in Column (2) of Table Ex. $10.11$ in the above equation and solve for t (or, 225 Ã— 0.008=1.8 yr) and this goes in Column (3). Fill in the rest of Column (3) the same way.

The fourth column is nothing more than \(U_{ avg } \times s_{c} ; \text { for } U_{ avg }=0.1, \text { the } \Delta h \text { is } 0.1 \times 68 cm =6.8 cm\). Fill in the rest of Column (4).

A plot of expected settlement versus time that would be sent to the client is shown in Fig. Ex. 10.11 . If the life of the expected structure is only 50 yr, then the most settlement that would occur by that time would be only about 40 cm. Note that the rate of settlement depends on many factors. Just as in , for important structures, geotechnical engineers would try to take settlement measurements with time and compare them with the predicted values. If the field data deviates, then one could back-calculate the coefficient of consolidation, \(c_{v}\), for example, and recalculate the time-settlement. After all, you have a full-scale field loading test with the prototype structure!
