Quadratic Interpolation

Fit a second-order polynomial to the three points used in :

\(x_0 = 1 f (x_0) = 0\)

 

\(x_1 = 4 f (x_1) = 1.386294\)

 

\(x_2 = 6 f (x_2) = 1.791759\)

Use the polynomial to evaluate ln 2.

Admin
Solution

Applying Eq. (18.4)

\(b_0 = f (x_0)\) (18.4)

yields

\(b_0 = 0\)

Equation (18.5)

\(b_1 = \frac{f (x_1) – f (x_0)}{x_1 – x_0}\) (18.5)

yields

\(b_1 = \frac{1.386294 – 0}{4 – 1} = 0.4620981\)

and Eq. (18.6)

\(b_2 =\frac{\frac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1} -\frac{f(x_1)-f(x_0)}{x_1-x_0}}{x_2-x_0} \) (18.6)

\(b_2 = \frac{\frac{1.791759 – 1.386294}{6 – 4}- 0.4620981}{6 – 1} = – 0.0518731\)

Substituting these values into Eq. (18.3)

\(f_2(x) = b_0 + b_1(x – x_0) + b_2(x – x_0)(x – x_1)\) (18.3)

yields the quadratic formula

\(f_2(x) = 0 + 0.4620981(x – 1) – 0.0518731(x – 1)(x – 4)\)

which can be evaluated at x = 2 for

\(f_2(2) = 0.5658444\)

which represents a relative error of \(Îµ_t = 18.4 \%\). Thus, the curvature introduced by the quadratic formula (Fig. 18.4) improves the interpolation compared with the result obtained using straight lines in and Fig. 18.3.
