Guest Question.

For the two degree-of-freedom mass-spring-damper system governed by Equation 6.113, compare the responses for the (a) undamped, (b) classically damped, and (c) non-classically damped systems.

 

\([M]\{\ddot{q}\}+[C]\{\dot{q}\}+[K]\{q\}=\{0\}\)
Admin
Solution

This system has the following mass and stiffness property matrices,

 

\([M]=m\left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\0 & 1\end{array}\right], \quad[K]=k\left[\begin{array}{cc}2 & -1 \\-1 & 2\end{array}\right]\)

 

where for convenience we assume \(m\) = 1 and \(k\) = 1 with appropriate units, and the damping matrix is

 

\([C]=\left[\begin{array}{cc}c_{1}+c_{2} & -c_{2} \\-c_{2} & c_{2}+c_{3}\end{array}\right]\)

 

The initial conditions are given by the vectors

 

\(\{q(0)\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}1 \\2\end{array}\right\}, \quad\{\dot{q}(0)\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}-1 \\1\end{array}\right\}\)

 

For purposes of comparison, we provide the results for an undamped system, a classically damped system, and a non-classically damped system. The natural frequencies are obtained by solving \(\left([M] \lambda^{2}+[K]\right)\{v\}=\) \(\{0\}\) while the damped frequencies are obtained from \(\left([M] \lambda^{2}+[C] \lambda+[K]\right)\{v\}=\{0\}\). The damped frequencies are slightly smaller than the natural frequencies, and so we retain four digits after the decimal point in order to highlight the slight differences.

(a) For an undamped system, \(c_{1}=c_{2}=c_{3}=0\), with the solution of the eigenvalue problem, and subsequent normalization with respect to the mass matrix, yielding the following natural frequencies and modal ratios,

 

\(\begin{aligned}&{[\Omega]=\operatorname{diag}[1,3]} \\&{[U]=\left[\begin{array}{ll}\{u\}_{1} & \{u\}_{2}\end{array}\right]=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & 1 \\1 & -1\end{array}\right]}\end{aligned}\)

 

The complete solution is the superposition of the natural modes,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\{q(t)\} &=\sum_{j=1}^{2}\{s(t)\}_{j} \\&=C_{1} \cos \left(t-\theta_{1}\right)\{u\}_{1} \\&+C_{2} \cos \left(\sqrt{3} t-\theta_{2}\right)\{u\}_{2} .\end{aligned}\)

 

The constants \(C_{1}\), \(C_{2}\), \(\theta_{1}\), and \(\theta_{2}\) are determined by satisfying the initial conditions. From [\(U\) ] we see that the modes are either in phase or out of phase.

(b) For a damped system, we assume that \(c_{1}\) = \(c_{2}\) =\(c_{3}\) =0.1. Since \(C\) = 0.1\(K\), the system is proportionately damped, with the general solution

 

\(\begin{aligned}\{q(t)\} &=\sum_{j=1}^{2}\{s(t)\}_{j} \\&=C_{1} e^{-0.05 t} \cos \left(0.9987 t-\theta_{1}\right)\{u\}_{1} \\&+C_{2} e^{-0.15 t} \cos \left(1.7255 t-\theta_{2}\right)\{u\}_{2} .\end{aligned}\)

 

In this case there is a decaying oscillation. The coefficients in the decaying exponentials, −0.05 and −0.15, are the real parts of the complex eigenvalues.
(c) For the non-classically damped system, we assume that \(c_{1}\) = 0.6, and \(c_{2}\) = \(c_{3}\) = 0.1. Solving the quadratic eigenvalue problem Equation 6.115 yields

 

\(\left([M] \lambda^{2}+[C] \lambda+[K]\right)\{v\}=\{0\}\)

 

\(\lambda_{1}=\lambda_{3}^{*}=-0.1792+1.0008 i\)

 

\(\{v\}_{1}=\{v\}_{3}^{*}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}0.7389 e^{-i 0.1288} \\-0.7242 e^{-i 3.0108}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

\(=\left\{\begin{array}{c}-0.7389 e^{-i 7.83^{\circ}} \\-0.7242 e^{-i 172.51^{\circ}}\end{array}\right\},\)

 

and

 

\(\lambda_{2}=\lambda_{4}^{*}=-0.2708+1.6819 i\)

 

\(\{v\}_{2}=\{v\}_{4}^{*}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}-0.7337 e^{-i 2.9169} \\-0.7296 e^{-i 0.2213}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

\(=\left\{\begin{array}{c}-0.7337 e^{-i 167.13^{\circ}} \\-0.7296 e^{-i 12.68^{\circ}}\end{array}\right\},\)

 

where the eigenvectors were normalized in accordance with Equation 6.118,

 

\(2 \lambda_{j}\{v\}_{j}^{T}[M]\{v\}_{j}+\{v\}_{j}^{T}[C]\{v\}_{j}=2 i \omega_{j}\)

 

From Equation 6.116, the two non-classically damped modes of vibration are given by

 

\(\{s(t)\}_{j}=C_{j} e^{\alpha_{j} t}\left\{\begin{array}{c}r_{j 1} \cos \left(\omega_{j} t-\theta_{j}-\phi_{j 1}\right) \\r_{j 2} \cos \left(\omega_{j} t-\theta_{j}-\phi_{j 2}\right) \\\vdots \\r_{j n} \cos \left(\omega_{j} t-\theta_{j}-\phi_{j n}\right)\end{array}\right\}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\{s(t)\}_{1} \\&=C_{1} e^{-0.1792 t}\left\{\begin{array}{c}0.7389 \cos \left(1.0008 t-\theta_{1}-0.1288\right) \\-0.7242 \cos \left(1.0008 t-\theta_{1}-3.0108\right)\end{array}\right\}\end{aligned}\)

 

and

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\{s(t)\}_{2} \\&=C_{2} e^{-0.2708 t}\left\{\begin{array}{l}-0.7337 \cos \left(1.6819 t-\theta_{2}-2.9169\right) \\-0.7296 \cos \left(1.6819 t-\theta_{2}-0.2213\right)\end{array}\right\} .\end{aligned}\)

 

The general solution, given by Equation 6.117, is the superposition of these two real damped modes. As shown in Figure 6.42, there is a constant phase difference between
the two components in each mode, where \(\{s(t)\}_{1}\) and \(\{s(t)\}_{2}\) are matched with the specified initial conditions \(\{q(0)\}\) and \(\{\dot{q}(0)\}\). The constants of integration that match the initial conditions given in the problem statement are

 

\(\begin{aligned}\{q(t)\} &=\sum_{j=1}^{n}\left(a_{j}\{v\}_{j} e^{\lambda_{j} t}+a_{j}^{*}\{v\}_{j}^{*} e^{\lambda_{j}^{*} t}\right) \\&=\sum_{j=1}^{n}\{s(t)\}_{j}\end{aligned}\) (6.117)

 

\(C_{1}=-2.324, C_{2}=1.338, \theta_{1}=-9.389, \theta_{2}=4.152\)

 

If \(\{s(t)\}_{1}\) and \(\{s(t)\}_{2}\) are graphed simply with \(C_{1}\) =\(C_{2}\)= 1 and \(\theta_{1}\) = \(\theta_{2}\) = 0, the plots in Figure 6.43 are obtained.

Equation 6.120 provides the transformation from \(\{s\}_{j}\) to \(\{y\}_{j}\) to create phase synchronization, resulting in

 

\(\{y(t)\}_{j}=C_{j} e^{\alpha_{j} t} \cos \left(\omega_{j} t-\theta_{j}\right)\left\{\begin{array}{c}r_{j 1} e^{\alpha_{j} \phi_{j 1} / \omega_{j}} \\r_{j 2} e^{\alpha_{j} \phi_{j 2} / \omega_{j}} \\\vdots \\r_{j n} e^{\alpha_{j} \phi_{j n} / \omega_{j}}\end{array}\right\}\) (6.120)

 

\(\{y(t)\}_{1}=C_{1} e^{-0.1792 t} \cos \left(1.0008 t-\theta_{1}\right)\{z\}_{1}\)

 

and

 

\(\{y(t)\}_{2}=C_{2} e^{-0.2708 t} \cos \left(1.6819 t-\theta_{2}\right)\{z\}_{2}\)

 

where

 

\(\begin{aligned}[Z] &=\left[\begin{array}{ll}\{z\}_{1} & \{z\}_{2}\end{array}\right] \\&=\left[\begin{array}{cc}0.7221 & -0.4587 \\-0.4224 & -0.7040\end{array}\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}[D]_{1} &=-\operatorname{diag}\left[\lambda_{j}+\lambda_{j}^{*}\right] \\&=\left[\begin{array}{cc}0.3584 & 0 \\0 & 0.5416\end{array}\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

and

 

\(\begin{aligned}[\Omega]_{1} &=\operatorname{diag}\left[\lambda_{j} \lambda_{j}^{*}\right] \\&=\left[\begin{array}{cc}1.0337 & 0 \\0 & 2.9022\end{array}\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

Upon decoupling, the equation of motion becomes

 

\([I]\{\ddot{p}\}+[D]_{1}\{\dot{p}\}+[\Omega]_{1}\{p\}=\{0\}\)

 

which can be easily solved for \(\{p(t)\}\). The original response \(\{q(t)\}\) is then obtained using Equation 6.127. The decoupling transformation, as a linear time-shifting transformation, is given by

 

\(\{q(t)\}=\sum_{j=1}^{n}\left[\begin{array}{lll}p_{j}\left(t-\phi_{j 1} / \omega_{j}\right) & & \\& \ddots & \\& & p_{j}\left(t-\phi_{j n} / \omega_{j}\right)\end{array}\right]\)

 

\(\times\left\{\begin{array}{c}r_{j 1} e^{\alpha_{j} \phi_{j 1} / \omega_{j}} \\\vdots \\r_{j n} e^{\alpha_{j} \phi_{j n} / \omega_{j}}\end{array}\right\}\)(6.127)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\{q(t)\} &=\left[\begin{array}{cc}p_{1}\left(t-\phi_{11} / \omega_{1}\right) & 0 \\0 & p_{1}\left(t-\phi_{12} / \omega_{1}\right)\end{array}\right]\{z\}_{1} \\&+\left[\begin{array}{cc}p_{2}\left(t-\phi_{21} / \omega_{2}\right) & 0 \\0 & p_{2}\left(t-\phi_{22} / \omega_{2}\right)\end{array}\right]\{z\}_{2},\end{aligned}\)

 

which results in

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\left\{\begin{array}{c}q_{1}(t) \\q_{2}(t)\end{array}\right\} \\&=\left[\begin{array}{cc}p_{1}(t-0.1287) & 0 \\0 & p_{1}(t-3.0084)\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}0.7221 \\-0.4224\end{array}\right\} \\&+\left[\begin{array}{cc}p_{2}(t-1.7343) & 0 \\0 & p_{2}(t-0.1316)\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}-0.4587 \\-0.7040\end{array}\right\} \\&=\left[\begin{array}{c}0.7221 p_{1}(t-0.1287)-0.4587 p_{2}(t-1.7343) \\-0.4224 p_{1}(t-3.0084)-0.7040 p_{2}(t-0.1316)\end{array}\right] .\end{aligned}\)

 

It can be checked that \(\{q(t)\}\), whether generated by decoupling or by direct numerical solution of the original equation of motion, is the same.
