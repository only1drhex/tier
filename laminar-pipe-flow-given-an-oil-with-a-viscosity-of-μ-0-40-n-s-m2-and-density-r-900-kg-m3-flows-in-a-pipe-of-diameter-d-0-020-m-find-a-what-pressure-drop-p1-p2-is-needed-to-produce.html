Guest Question.

Laminar Pipe Flow

GIVEN An oil with a viscosity of \(\mu=0.40 N \cdot s / m ^{2}\) and density \(\rho=900 kg / m ^{3}\) flows in a pipe of diameter D = 0.020 m.

FIND (a) What pressure drop, \(p_{1}-p_{2}\), is needed to produce a flowrate of \(Q=2.0 \times 10^{-5} m ^{3} / s\) if the pipe is horizontal with \(x_{1}=0 \text { and } x_{2}=10 m ?\)

(b) How steep a hill, θ, must the pipe be on if the oil is to flow through the pipe at the same rate as in part 1a2, but with \(p_{1}-p_{2}\)?

(c) For the conditions of part (b), if \(p_{1}=200 kPa\), what is the pressure at section \(x_{3}=\) 5 m, where x is measured along the pipe?

Admin
Solution

(a) If the Reynolds number is less than 2100 the flow is laminar and the equations derived in this section are valid. Since the average velocity is \(V=Q / A=\left(2.0 \times 10^{-5} m ^{3} / s \right) /\) \(\left[\pi(0.020)^{2} m ^{2} / 4\right]=0.0637 m / s\), the Reynolds number is \(\operatorname{Re}=\) \(\rho V D / \mu=2.87<2100\). Hence, the flow is laminar and from Eq. 8.9 with \(h \ell=x_{2}-x_{1}=10 m\) the pressure drop is

 

\(Q=\frac{\pi D^{4} \Delta p}{128 \mu \ell}\) (8.9)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta p &=p_{1}-p_{2}=\frac{128 \mu \ell Q}{\pi D^{4}} \\&=\frac{128\left(0.40 N \cdot s / m ^{2}\right)(10.0 m )\left(2.0 \times 10^{-5} m ^{3} / s \right)}{\pi(0.020 m )^{4}}\end{aligned}\)

 

or

 

\(\Delta p=20,400 N / m ^{2}=20.4 kPa\)

 

(b) If the pipe is on a hill of angle θ such that \(\Delta p=p_{1}-p_{2}=0\), Eq. 8.12 gives

 

\(Q=\frac{\pi(\Delta p-\gamma \ell \sin \theta) D^{4}}{128 \mu \ell}\) (8.12)

 

\(\sin \theta=-\frac{128 \mu Q}{\pi \rho g D^{4}}\) (1)

 

or

 

\(\sin \theta=\frac{-128\left(0.40 N \cdot s / m ^{2}\right)\left(2.0 \times 10^{-5} m ^{3} / s \right)}{\pi\left(900 kg / m ^{3}\right)\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)(0.020 m )^{4}}\)

 

\(\text { Thus, } \theta=-13.34^{\circ} \text {. }\)

 

COMMENT This checks with the previous horizontal result as is seen from the fact that a change in elevation of \(\Delta z=\ell \sin \theta=(10 m ) \sin \left(-13.34^{\circ}\right)=-2.31\) m is equivalent to a pressure change of \(\Delta p=\rho g \Delta z=\left(900 kg / m ^{3}\right)\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)\) \((2.31 m )=20,400 N / m ^{2}\), which is equivalent to that needed for the horizontal pipe. For the horizontal pipe it is the work done by the pressure forces that overcomes the viscous dissipation. For the zero-pressure-drop pipe on the hill, it is the change in potential energy of the fluid “falling” down the hill that is converted to the energy lost by viscous dissipation. Note that if it is desired to increase the flowrate to \(Q=1.0 \times 10^{-4} m ^{3} / s \text { with } p_{1}=p_{2}\), the value of \(\theta\) given by Eq. 1 is \(\sin \theta=-1.15\). Since the sine of an angle cannot be greater than 1, this flow would not be possible. The weight of the fluid would not be large enough to offset the viscous force generated for the flowrate desired. A larger diameter pipe would be needed.

(c) With \(p_{1}=p_{2} \text { the length of the pipe, } \ell \text {, }\), does not appear in the flowrate equation (Eq. 1). This is a statement of the fact that for such cases the pressure is constant all along the pipe (provided the pipe lies on a hill of constant slope). This can be seen by substituting the values of Qand \(\theta\) from case (b) into Eq. 8.12 and noting that \(\Delta p=0\) for any \(\ell\). For example, \(\Delta p=p_{1}-p_{3}=0 \text { if } \ell=x_{3}-x_{1}=5 m\).

 

\(\text { Thus, } p_{1}=p_{2}=p_{3} \text { so that }\)

 

\(p_{3}=200 kPa\)

 

COMMENT Note that if the fluid were gasoline \((\mu=3.1 \times\) \(\left.10^{-4} N \cdot s / m ^{2} \text { and } \rho=680 kg / m ^{3}\right)\), the Reynolds number would be \(Re =2790\), the flow would probably not be laminar, and use of Eqs. 8.9 and 8.12 would give incorrect results. Also note from Eq. 1 that the kinematic viscosity, \(\nu=\mu / \rho\), is the important viscous parameter. This is a statement of the fact that with constant pressure along the pipe, it is the ratio of the viscous force \((\sim \mu) \text { to the weight force }(\sim \gamma=\rho g) \) that determines the value of θ.
