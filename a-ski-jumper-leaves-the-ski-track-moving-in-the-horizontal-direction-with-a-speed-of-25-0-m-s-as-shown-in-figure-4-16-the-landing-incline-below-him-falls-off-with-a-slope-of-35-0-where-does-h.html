Guest Question.

A ski-jumper leaves the ski track moving in the horizontal direction with a speed of 25.0 m/s, as shown in Figure 4.16. The landing incline below him falls off with a slope of 35.0°. Where does he land on the incline?

Admin
Solution

We can conceptualize this problem based on observations of winter Olympic ski competitions. We observe the skier to be airborne for perhaps 4 s and go a distance of about 100 m horizontally. We should expect the value of d, the distance traveled along the incline, to be of the same order of magnitude. We categorize the problem as that of a particle in projectile motion.
To analyze the problem, it is convenient to select the beginning of the jump as the origin. Because \( v_{x i}=25.0 m / s \) \( \text { and } v_{y i}=0 \) the x and y component forms of Equation 4.9a are

\( r _{f}= r _{i}+ v _{i} t+\frac{1}{2} a t^{2} \quad\left\{\begin{array}{l} x_{f}=x_{i}+v_{x i} t+\frac{1}{2} a_{x} t^{2} \\ y_{f}=y_{i}+v_{y i} t+\frac{1}{2} a_{y} t^{2} \end{array}\right. \) (4.9a).

\( \text { (1) } \quad x_{f}=v_{x i} t=(25.0 m / s ) t \).

\( \text { (2) } y_{f}=v_{y i} t+\frac{1}{2} a_{y} t^{2}=-\frac{1}{2}\left(9.80 m / s ^{2}\right) t^{2} \).

From the right triangle in Figure 4.16, we see that the jumper’s x and y coordinates at the landing point are \( x_{f}=d \cos 35.0^{\circ} \text { and } y_{f}=-d \sin 35.0^{\circ} \text {. Substituting these } \) relationships into (1) and (2), we obtain

\( \text { (3) } \quad d \cos 35.0^{\circ}=(25.0 m / s ) t \).

\( \text { (4) } \quad-d \sin 35.0^{\circ}=-\frac{1}{2}\left(9.80 m / s ^{2}\right) t^{2} \).

Solving (3) for t and substituting the result into (4), we find that d = 109 m. Hence, the x and y coordinates of the point at which the skier lands are

\( x_{f}=d \cos 35.0^{\circ}=(109 m ) \cos 35.0^{\circ}=89.3 m \).

\( y_{f}=-d \sin 35.0^{\circ}=-(109 m ) \sin 35.0^{\circ}=-62.5 m \).

To finalize the problem, let us compare these results to our expectations. We expected the horizontal distance to be on the order of 100 m, and our result of 89.3 m is indeed on this order of magnitude. It might be useful to calculate the time interval that the jumper is in the air and compare it to our estimate of about 4 s.
