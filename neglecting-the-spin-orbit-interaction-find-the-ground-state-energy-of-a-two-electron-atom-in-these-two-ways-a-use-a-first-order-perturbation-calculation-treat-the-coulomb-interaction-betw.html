Guest Question.

Neglecting the spin–orbit interaction, find the ground state energy of a two-electron atom in these two ways:

(a) Use a first-order perturbation calculation; treat the Coulomb interaction between the two electrons as a perturbation.

(b) Use the variational method.

Compare the results and discuss the merits of the two approximation methods.

Admin
Solution

Examples of such a system are the helium atom (Z = 2), the singly ionized \(Li ^{+}\) ion (Z = 3), the doubly ionized \(Be ^{2+}\) ion (Z = 4), and so on. Each electron of these systems feels the effects of two Coulomb fields: one from the Ze nucleus, \(V(r)=-Z e^{2} / r\), and the other from the other electron, \(V_{12}=e^{2} / r_{12}=e^{2} /\left|\vec{r}_{1}-\vec{r}_{2}\right|\); here we consider the nucleus to be located at the origin and the electrons at \(\vec{r}_{1}\) and \(\vec{r}_{2}\). Neglecting the spin–orbit interaction, we can write the Hamiltonian of the two-electron system as

\(\hat{H}=\hat{H}_{0}+\hat{V}_{12}=\hat{H}_{0}+\frac{e^{2}}{\left|\vec{r}_{1}-\vec{r}_{2}\right|}\), (9.462)

where

\(\hat{H}_{0}=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\mu}\left(\nabla_{1}^{2}+\nabla_{2}^{2}\right)-Z e^{2}\left(\frac{1}{r_{1}}+\frac{1}{r_{2}}\right)\) (9.463)

is the Hamiltonian of the atom when the interaction between the two electrons is neglected.
We have seen in Chapter 8 that, when the interaction between the two electrons is neglected, the ground state energy and wave function are given by

\(E_{0}=-2 \frac{Z^{2} e^{2}}{2 a}=-27.2 Z^{2} eV\), (9.464)

 

\(\Psi_{0}\left(\vec{r}_{1}, \vec{S}_{1} ; \vec{r}_{2}, \vec{S}_{2}\right)=\psi_{0}\left(\vec{r}_{1}, \vec{r}_{2}\right) \chi_{\text {singlet }}\left(\vec{S}_{1}, \vec{S}_{2}\right)\), (9.465)

where the spin part is antisymmetric,

\(\chi_{\text {singlet }}\left(\vec{S}_{1}, \vec{S}_{2}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\left|\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{1}\left|\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{2}-\left|\frac{1}{2},-\frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{1}\left|\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right\rangle_{2}\right)\), (9.466)

and the spatial part is symmetric, \(\psi_{0}\left(\vec{r}_{1}, \vec{r}_{2}\right)=\phi_{100}\left(\overrightarrow{r_{1}}\right) \phi_{100}\left(\overrightarrow{r_{2}}\right)\), with

\(\phi_{100}(\vec{r})=R_{10}(r) Y_{00}(\Omega)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}}\left(\frac{Z}{a}\right)^{3 / 2} e^{-Z r_{1} / a}\), (9.467)

that is,

\(\psi_{0}\left(\vec{r}_{1}, \vec{r}_{2}\right)=\frac{1}{\pi}\left(\frac{Z}{a}\right)^{3} e^{-Z\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right) / a}\). (9.468)

(a) To calculate the ground state energy using first-order perturbation theory, we have to treat \(\hat{V}_{12}\) as a perturbation. A first-order treatment yields

\(E=E_{0}+\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\hat{V}_{12}\right| \psi_{0}\right\rangle=-2 \frac{Z^{2} e^{2}}{2a}+\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\hat{V}_{12}\right|\psi_{0}\right\rangle\), (9.469)

where

\(\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\hat{V}_{12}\right| \psi_{0}\right\rangle=\int d^{3} r_{1} \int d^{3} r_{2} \psi_{0}^{*}\left(\vec{r}_{1}, \vec{r}_{2}\right) \hat{V}_{12} \psi_{0}\left(\vec{r}_{1}, \vec{r}_{2}\right)\)

 

\(=\int d^{3} r_{1} \int d^{3} r_{2}\left|\phi_{100}\left(\vec{r}_{1}\right)\right|^{2} \frac{e^{2}}{\left|\vec{r}_{1}-\vec{r}_{2}\right|}\left|\phi_{100}\left(\vec{r}_{2}\right)\right|^{2}\). (9.470)

The calculation of this integral is quite involved (I left it as an exercise); the result is

\(\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\hat{V}_{12}\right|\psi_{0}\right\rangle=\frac{5}{8} \frac{Z e^{2}}{a}\), (9.471)

which, when combined with (9.469), leads to

\(E=-\frac{Z e^{2}}{a}\left(Z-\frac{5}{8}\right)\). (9.472)

In the case of helium, Z = 2, we have

\(E=-108.8 eV +34 eV =-74.8 eV\); (9.473)

this result disagrees with the experimental value, \(E_{\exp }=-78.975 eV , \text { by } 4 eV\) or by a 5.3% relative error. Physically, this may be attributed to the fact that, in our calculation, we have not taken into account the “screening” effect: the presence of one electron tends to decrease the net charge “seen” by the other electron. Suppose electron 1 is “between” the nucleus and electron 2; then electron 2 will not “see” Z protons but (Z -1) protons (i.e., electron 2 feels an effective charge (Z – 1)e coming from the nucleus).

(b) By analogy with the exact form of the ground state function (9.468), we can choose a trial function that takes into account the screening effect. For this, we need simply to replace Z in (9.468) by a variational parameter α:

\(\psi_{0}\left(r_{1}, r_{2}\right)=A e^{-\alpha\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right) / a}\), (9.474)

where A is a normalization. Using the integral \(\int_{0}^{\infty} x^{n} e^{-b x} d x=n ! / b^{n+1}\), we can show that \(A=(\alpha / a)^{3} / \pi\); hence

\(\psi_{\alpha}\left(r_{1}, r_{2}\right)=\frac{1}{\pi}\left(\frac{\alpha}{a}\right)^{3} e^{-\alpha\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right) / a} .\). (9.475)

A combination of this relation with (9.471) leads to

\(E(\alpha)=\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\hat{H}_{0}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle+\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\hat{V}_{12}\right|\psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle=\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\hat{H}_{0}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle+\frac{5}{8} \frac{\alpha e^{2}}{a}\). (9.476)

The calculation of \(\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\hat{H}_{0}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle\) can be simplified by writing it as

\(\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\hat{H}_{0}\right|\psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 \mu}\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\nabla_{1}^{2}+\nabla_{2}^{2}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle-Z e^{2}\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\frac{1}{r_{1}}+\frac{1}{r_{2}}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle\)

 

\(=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\mu}\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\nabla_{1}^{2}+\nabla_{2}^{2}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle-\alpha e^{2}\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\frac{1}{r_{1}}+\frac{1}{r_{2}}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle\)

 

\(-(Z-\alpha) e^{2}\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\frac{1}{r_{1}}+\frac{1}{r_{2}}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle\). (9.477)

This form is quite suggestive; since \(-\hbar^{2}\left\langle\psi_{0}|\vec{\nabla}| \psi_{0}\right\rangle /(2 \mu)-Z e^{2}\left\langle\psi_{0}|1 / r| \psi_{0}\right\rangle=-Z^{2} e^{2} /(2 a)\) we can write

\(-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 \mu}\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\nabla_{1}^{2}+\nabla_{2}^{2}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle-\alpha e^{2}\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\frac{1}{r_{1}}+\frac{1}{r_{2}}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle=-2 \frac{\alpha^{2} e^{2}}{2 a} .\) (9.478)

Now since

\(\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\frac{1}{r_{1}}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle=\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\frac{1}{r_{2}}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle=4\left(\frac{\alpha}{a}\right)^{3} \int_{0}^{\infty} r e^{-2 \alpha r / a} d r=\frac{\alpha}{a},\) (9.479)

we can reduce (9.477) to

\(\left\langle\psi_{\alpha}\left|\hat{H}_{0}\right| \psi_{\alpha}\right\rangle=-2 \frac{\alpha^{2} e^{2}}{2 a}-2(Z-\alpha) e^{2} \frac{\alpha}{a}\), (9.480)

which, when combined with (9.476), leads to

\(E(\alpha)=-2 \frac{\alpha^{2} e^{2}}{2 a}-2(Z-\alpha) e^{2} \frac{\alpha}{a}+\frac{5}{8} \frac{\alpha e^{2}}{a}=\left[\alpha^{2}-2\left(Z-\frac{5}{16}\right) \alpha\right] \frac{e^{2}}{a}\). (9.481)

The minimization of \(E(\alpha), d E(\alpha) / d \alpha=0\), yields

\(\alpha_{0}=Z-\frac{5}{16}\); (9.482)

hence the ground state energy is

\(E\left(\alpha_{0}\right)=-\left[1-\frac{5}{8 Z}+\left(\frac{5}{16 Z}\right)^{2}\right] \frac{Z^{2} e^{2}}{a}\) (9.483)

and

\(\psi\left(r_{1}, r_{2}\right)=\frac{1}{\pi}\left(\frac{Z}{a}-\frac{5}{16 a}\right)^{3} \exp \left[-\left(\frac{Z}{a}-\frac{5}{16 a}\right)\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right)\right] .\) (9.484)

As a numerical illustration, the ground state energy of a helium atom is obtained by substituting Z = 2 into (9.483). This yields \(E_{0}=-77.456 eV\), in excellent agreement with the experimental value \(E_{\exp }=-78.975 eV\). The variational method, which overestimates the correct result by a mere 1.9%, is significantly more accurate than first-order perturbation theory. The reason is quite obvious; while the perturbation treatment does not account for the screening effect, the variational method includes it quite accurately. The wave function (9.484) shows that the second electron does not see a charge Ze, but a lower charge (Z – 5/16)e.
