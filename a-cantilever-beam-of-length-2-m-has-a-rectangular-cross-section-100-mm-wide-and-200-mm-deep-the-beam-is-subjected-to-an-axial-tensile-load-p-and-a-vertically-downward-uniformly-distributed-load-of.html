Guest Question.

A cantilever beam of length 2 m has a rectangular cross section 100 mm wide and 200 mm deep. The beam is subjected to an axial tensile load, P, and a vertically downward uniformly distributed load of intensity w. A rectangular strain gauge rosette attached to a vertical side of the beam at the built-in end and in the neutral plane of the beam recorded the following values of strain: \(\varepsilon_{ a }=1000 \times 10^{-6}, \varepsilon_{ b },=100 \times 10^{-6}, \varepsilon_{ c }=-300 \times 10^{-6}\). The arm ‘a’ of the rosette is aligned with the longitudinal axis of the beam while the arm ‘c’ is perpendicular to the longitudinal axis. Calculate the value of Poisson’s ratio, the principal strains at the point and hence the values of P and w. Young’s modulus, \(E=200000 N / mm ^{2}\).

Ans. \(v =0.3, \varepsilon_{ I }=1046.4 \times 10^{-6}, \varepsilon_{ II }=-346.4 \times 10^{-6}, w=255.3 kN / m\) .

Admin
Solution

Since the strain gauge is positioned on the neutral axis of the beam section it will record strains produced by the axial load and shear load but no strains due to bending.
Poisson’s ratio is given by (see Section 7.8)

\(v=\frac{\varepsilon_{ c }}{\varepsilon_{ a }}=\frac{300 \times 10^{-6}}{1000 \times 10^{-6}}=0.3\)

Substituting the given values of strain in Eqs (14.34) and (14.35)

\(\varepsilon_{ I }=\frac{1}{2}\left(\varepsilon_{ a }+\varepsilon_{ c }\right)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \sqrt{\left(\varepsilon_{ a }-\varepsilon_{ b }\right)^{2}+\left(\varepsilon_{ c }-\varepsilon_{ b }\right)^{2}}\) (14.34)

\(\varepsilon_{ II }=\frac{1}{2}\left(\varepsilon_{ a }+\varepsilon_{ c }\right)-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \sqrt{\left(\varepsilon_{ a }-\varepsilon_{ b }\right)^{2}+\left(\varepsilon_{ c }-\varepsilon_{ b }\right)^{2}}\) (14.35)

gives

\(\varepsilon_{ I }=1046.4 \times 10^{-6}, \quad \varepsilon_{ II }=-346.4 \times 10^{-6}\)

Then, substituting these values in Eqs (14.32) and (14.33)

\(\sigma_{ I }=\frac{E}{1-v^{2}}\left(\varepsilon_{ I }+v \varepsilon_{ II }\right)\) (14.32)

\(\sigma_{ II }=\frac{E}{1-v^{2}}\left(\varepsilon_{ II }+v \varepsilon_{ I }\right)\) (14.33)

and using the calculated value of Poisson’s ratio

\(\sigma_{ I }=207.1 N / mm ^{2}, \quad \sigma_{ II }=-7.1 N / mm ^{2}\)

Now adding Eqs (14.8) and (14.9)

\(\sigma_{ I }=\frac{\sigma_{x}+\sigma_{y}}{2}+\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\left(\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}\right)^{2}+4 \tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (14.8)

\(\sigma_{ II }=\frac{\sigma_{x}+\sigma_{y}}{2}-\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\left(\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}\right)^{2}+4 \tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (14.9)

and noting that \(\sigma_{y}=0\)

\(\sigma_{ I }+\sigma_{ II }=\sigma_{x}=200 N / mm ^{2}=\frac{P}{100 \times 200}\)

Note that \(\sigma_{x}\) could have been obtained directly from the strain gauge readings since

\(\sigma_{x}=\varepsilon_{ a } E=1000 \times 10^{-6} \times 200000=200 N / mm ^{2}\)

Then

\(P=4000 \times 10^{3} N =4000 kN\)

Again from Eqs (14.8) and (14.9)

\(\sigma_{ I }-\sigma_{ II }=\sqrt{\left(200.0^{2}+4 \tau_{x y}^{2}\right)}=214.2\)

from which

\(\tau_{x y}=38.3 N / mm ^{2}\)

The shear stress on the neutral axis of a rectangular section beam is \(3 S_{y} / 2 B D\) (see Fig. 10.5).
In this case \(S_{y}=2 \times 10^{3} w\), therefore

\(38.3=\frac{3 \times 2 \times 10^{3} w}{2 \times 100 \times 200}\)

from which

w = 255.3N/mm = 255.3kN/m .
