Guest Question.

Combinatorial chemistry is used in the pharmaceutical industry to produce large populations, or libraries, of compounds that are subsequently screened to identify candidates of therapeutic value. A microreactor chip subjects two reactants, A and B, to N unique time-temperature histories, resulting in N new compounds for testing. The 20 mm × 20 mm chip is fabricated by first coating a 1-mm-thick glass microscope slide with a photoresist material of thickness \(a=40 \mu m.\) Channels of width \(b=160 \mu m\) and length L = 20 mm are subsequently etched into the photoresist, and a second microscope slide is adhered to the top of the structure. The spacing between channels is \(s=40 \mu m,\) so that N=L/(b+s)=100 parallel channels are formed. Flow is induced through each channel by applying a pressure difference of \(\Delta p=500 k Pa\) from the entrance to the exit of the microreactor. The reactants enter each channel at \(T_{m,i}=5^{\circ}C,\) and the N products of reaction are cooled back to \(5^{\circ}C\) upon exiting the chip. The walls of each of the N channels are maintained at unique temperatures by holding the edges of the chip at temperatures \(T_{1}=125^{\circ}C\) and \(T_{2}=25^{\circ}C.\) For the warmest \((125^{\circ}C)\) and coolest \((25^{\circ}C)\) microchannels, estimate the time during which the liquid is within 1 degree Celsius of the wall temperature. Assume the liquid has thermophysical properties similar to ethylene glycol.

Admin
Solution

Known: Dimensions and operating conditions for reactants flowing in a microreactor.

Find: Time-at-temperature associated with the warmest and coolest channels.

Assumptions:
1. Linear temperature distribution across the width of the microreactor.
2. Steady-state conditions.
3. Incompressible liquid with constant properties.
4. Negligible viscous dissipation.
5. Uniform wall temperatures for each channel.

Properties: Table A.5, ethylene glycol \( \left (\bar{T}_{m}=288 K \right): \rho =1120.2 kg / m ^{3}, c_{p}=2359 J / kg \cdot K , \mu =2.82 \times 10^{-2} N \cdot s / m ^{2}, k=247 \times 10^{-3} W / m \cdot K , \operatorname {Pr} =269 .\left (\bar{T}_{m}=338 K \right): \rho = 1085 kg / m ^{3}, c_{p}=2583 J / kg \cdot K , \mu =0.427 \times 10^{-2} N \cdot s / m ^{2}, k=261 \times 10^{-3} W / m \cdot K , P r=45.2\)

Analysis: The hydraulic diameter of each microchannel is found from Equation 8.66 and is

 

\(D_{h} \equiv \frac{4 A_{c}}{P}\) (8.66)

 

\(D_{h}=\frac{4 A_{c}}{P}=\frac{4 a b}{(2 a+2 b)}=\frac{4 \times 40 \times 10^{-6} m \times 160 \times 10^{-6} m }{\left(80 \times 10^{-6} m \times 320 \times 10^{-6} m \right)}=64 \times 10^{-6} m\)

 

We begin by assuming laminar flow and a short entrance length, to be verified later, so the flow rate may be estimated by using the friction factor for fully developed conditions. From Table 8.1 for \( \text { b/a }=4, f=73 / \operatorname{Re}_{D_{h}} \text { . }\) Substituting this expression into Equation 8.22a, rearranging terms, and using properties at rearranging terms, and using properties at \(T_{m}=338 K\) for the \(125^{\circ} C\) microchannel (in this equation and those following) results in

 

\(\Delta p=-\int_{p_{1}}^{p_{2}} d p=f \frac{\rho u_{m}^{2}}{2 D} \int_{x_{1}}^{x_{2}} d x=f \frac{\rho u_{m}^{2}}{2 D}\left(x_{2}- x_{1}\right)\) (8.22a)

 

\(u_{m}=\frac{2}{73} \frac{D_{h}^{2} \Delta p}{\mu L}=\frac{2}{73} \times \frac{\left(64 \times 10^{-6} m \right)^{2} \times 500 \times 10^{3} N / m ^{2}}{0.427 \times 10^{-2} N \cdot s / m ^{2} \times 20 \times 10^{-3} m }=0.657 m / s\)

 

Hence, the Reynolds number is

 

\(R e_{D}=\frac{u_{m} D_{h} \rho}{\mu}=\frac{0.657 m / s \times 64 \times 10^{-6} m \times 1085 kg / m ^{3}}{0.427 \times 10^{-2} N \cdot s / m ^{2}}=10.7\)

 

and the flow is deep in the laminar regime. Equation 8.3 may be used to determine the hydrodynamic entrance length, which is

 

\(\left(\frac{x_{ fd , h}}{D}\right)_{ lam } \approx 0.05 R e_{D}\) (8.3)

 

\(x_{ fd , h} \approx 0.05 D_{h} R e_{D}=0.05 \times 64 \times 10^{-6} m \times 10.7=34.2 \times 10^{-6} m\)

 

and the thermal entrance length may be obtained from Equation 8.23, yielding

 

\(\left(\frac{x_{ fd , t}}{D}\right)_{ lam } \approx 0.05 R e_{D} \operatorname{Pr}\) (8.23)

 

\(x_{ fd , t} \approx x_{ fd , h} P r=34.2 \times 10^{-6} m \times 45.2=1.55 \times 10^{-3} m\)

 

Both entrance lengths occupy less than 10% of the total microchannel length, L = 20 mm. Therefore, use of fully developed values of f are justified, and the mass flow rate for the \(T=125^{\circ}C\) microchannel is

\(\begin{aligned} \dot{m} &=\rho A_{c} u_{m}=\rho a b u_{m}=1085 kg / m ^{3} \times 40 \times 10^{-6} m \times 160 \times 10^{-6} m \times 0.657 m / s \\ &=4.56 \times 10^{-6} kg / s \end{aligned}\)

 

Equation 8.42 may now be used to determine the distance from the entrance of the microchannel to the location, \(x_{c}\) , where \(T_{m,c}=124^{\circ}C\), that is, within \(1^{\circ}C\) of the surface temperature. The average heat transfer coefficient, \(\bar{h},\) is replaced by the fully developed value of the heat transfer coefficient, h, because of the relatively short entrance length. From Table 8.1, \(N u_{D} = h D_{h} / k=4.44 \text { for } b / a = 4 \) Therefore,

 

\(\frac{T_{s}-T_{m}(x)}{T_{s}-T_{m, i}}=\exp \left(-\frac{P x}{\dot{m} c_{p}} \bar{h}(x)\right) \quad T_{s}=\text { constant }\) (8.42)

 

\(\bar{h} \approx h=N u_{D} \frac{k}{D_{h}}=4.44 \times \frac{0.261 W / m \cdot K }{64 \times 10^{-6} m }=1.81 \times 10^{4} W / m ^{2} \cdot K\)

 

As expected, the convection coefficient is large. Rearranging Equation 8.42 yields

 

\(\begin{aligned} x_{c} &=\frac{\dot{m} c_{p}}{P h} \ln \left[\frac{T_{s}-T_{m, i}}{T_{s}-T_{m, c}}\right]=\frac{4.56 \times 10^{-6} kg / s \times 2583 J / kg \cdot K }{0.4 \times 10^{-3} m \times 1.81 \times 10^{4} W / m ^{2} \cdot K } \ln \left[\frac{(125-5){ }^{\circ} C }{(125-124)^{\circ} C }\right] \\ &=7.79 \times 10^{-3} m \end{aligned}\)

 

Therefore, the time-at-temperature is

 

\(t_{T}=\left(L-x_{c}\right) / u_{m}=\left(20 \times 10^{-3}-7.79 \times 10^{-3}\right) m / 0.657 m / s =0.019 s\)

 

Repeating the calculations for the microchannel associated with the smallest processing temperature of \(25^{\circ}C\) yields \( u_{m}=0.0995 m / s , \operatorname{Re}_{D}=0.253, x_{ fd , h}=8.09 \times 10^{-7} m , x_{ fd , t}=0.218 \times 10^{-3} m , h=1.71 \times 10^{4} W / m ^{2} \cdot K , x_{c}=0.73 \times 10^{-3} m , \text { and } t_{T}=0.19 s\)

1. The presence of such a small amount of fluid is expected to have a negligible effect on the linear temperature distribution that is established across the chip. The temperature difference across the bottom or top surface of each channel is approximately \( \Delta T=\left(T_{1}-T_{2}\right) b / L=(125-25)^{\circ} C \times\left(160 \times 10^{-6} m \right) / \left(20 \times 10^{-3} m \right)=0.8^{\circ} C \)

As discussed in Section 8.7, the non-uniform velocity and temperature distributions within a heated straight channel lead to shorter residence times and cooler temperatures for fluid parcels near the centerline of the channel, relative to residence times and temperatures associated with parcels near the channel walls. Achieving uniform time-at-temperature histories in fluids flowing in microreactors is made difficult by the laminar flow conditions. However, inducing swirl within the liquids by using curved microchannels, or by fabricating helical rib patterns onto the interior microchannel walls, can promote mixing and more uniform time- temperature histories for the flowing fluids [43].
