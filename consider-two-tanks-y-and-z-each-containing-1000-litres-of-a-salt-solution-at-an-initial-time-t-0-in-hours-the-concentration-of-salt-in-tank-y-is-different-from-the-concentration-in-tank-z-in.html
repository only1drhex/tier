Guest Question.

Consider two tanks, Y and Z, each containing 1000 litres of a salt solution. At an initial time, t = 0 (in hours), the concentration of salt in tank Y is different from the concentration in tank Z. In each tank the solution is well stirred, so that the concentration is constant throughout the tank. The two tanks are joined by pipes; through one pipe, solution is pumped from Y to Z at a rate of 20 L/h; through the other, solution is pumped from Z to Y at the same rate. Determine the amount of salt in each tank at time t.

Admin
Solution

Let y(t) be the amount of salt (in kilograms) in tank Y at time t, and let z(t) be the amount of salt (in kilograms) in the tank Z at time t. Then the concentration in Y at time t is (y(t)/1000) kg/L. Similarly, (z(t)/1000) kg/L is the concentration in Z. Then for tank Y, salt is flowing out through one pipe at a rate of (20)(y/1000) kg/h and in through the other pipe at a rate of (20)(z/1000) kg/h. Since the rate of change is measured by the derivative, we have \(\frac{dy}{dt} = −0.02y + 0.02z\). By consideration of Z, we get a second differential equation, so y and z are the solutions of the system of linear ordinary differential equations:

\(\frac{dy}{dt} = −0.02y + 0.02z\)

\(\frac{dz}{dt} = −0.02y – 0.02z\)

It is convenient to rewrite this system in the form \(\frac{d}{dt} \left [ \begin{matrix} y \\ z \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} -0.02 & 0.02 \\ 0.02 & -0.02 \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} y \\ z \end{matrix} \right ]\).

How can we solve this system? Well, it might be easier if we could change variables so that the 2 × 2 matrix is diagonalized. By standard methods, one eigenvalue of \(A = \left [ \begin{matrix} -0.02 & 0.02 \\ 0.02 & -0.02 \end{matrix} \right ]\) is \(\lambda _1 = 0\), with corresponding eigenvector \(\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ]\). The other eigenvalue is \(\lambda _2 = −0.04\), with corresponding eigenvector \(\left [ \begin{matrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ]\). Hence, A is diagonalized by \(P = \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ]\) to \(D = \left [ \begin{matrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -0.04 \end{matrix} \right ]\).

Introduce new coordinates \(\left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ]\) by the change of coordinates equation \(\left [ \begin{matrix} y \\ z \end{matrix} \right ] = P \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ]\).

See Section 4.4. Substitute this for \(\left [ \begin{matrix} y \\ z \end{matrix} \right ]\) on both sides of the system to obtain

\(\frac{d}{dt} P \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ] = AP \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Since the entries in P are constants, it is easy to check that

\(\frac{d}{dt} P \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ] = P \frac{d}{dt} \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Multiply both sides of the system of equations (on the left) by \(P^{−1}\). Since P diagonalizes A, we get

\(\frac{d}{dt} \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ] = P^{-1}AP \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -0.04 \end{matrix} \right ]\left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Now write the pair of equations:

\(\frac{dy^\ast}{dt} = 0\) and \(\frac{dz^\ast}{dt} = -0.04z^\ast\)

These equations are “decoupled,” and we can easily solve each of them by using simple one-variable calculus.

The only functions satisfying \(\frac{dy^\ast}{dt} = 0\) are constants: we write \(y^{\ast}(t) = a\). The only functions satisfying an equation of the form \(\frac{dx}{dt}=kx\) are exponentials of the form \(x(t) = ce^{kt}\) for a constant c. So, from \( \frac{dz^\ast}{dt} = -0.04z^\ast\), we obtain \(z^{\ast}(t) = be^{−0.04t}\), where b is a constant.

Now we need to express the solution in terms of the original variables y and z:

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} y \\ z \end{matrix} \right ] = P \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast \\ z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} y^\ast – z^\ast \\ y^\ast + z^\ast \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} a − be^{−0.04t}\\ a + be^{−0.04t}\end{matrix} \right ]\)

For later use, it is helpful to rewrite this as \(\left [ \begin{matrix} y \\ z \end{matrix} \right ] = a \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] + be^{−0.04t} \left [ \begin{matrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ]\). This is the general solution of the problem. To determine the constants a and b, we would need to know the amounts y(0) and z(0) at the initial time t = 0. Then we would know y and z for all t. Note that as \(t \rightarrow \infty\), y and z tend to a common value a, as we might expect.
