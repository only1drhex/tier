Guest Question.

Sketch the graph of the equation Sketch the graph of the equation \(3x^2_1 + 4x_{1}x_{2} = 16.\)

Admin
Solution

The quadratic form \(\mathit{Q}(\vec{{x}}) = 3x^2_1 + 4x_{1}x_{2}\) corresponds to the symmetric matrix \(A = \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 & 2 \\ 2 & 0 \end{matrix} \right ]\). Its characteristic polynomial is

\(C(\lambda) = \lambda^2 − 3\lambda − 4 = (\lambda − 4)(\lambda + 1)\)

Thus, the eigenvalues of A are \(\lambda_1 = 4\) and \(\lambda_2 = −1\). Thus, by an orthogonal change of coordinates, the equation can be brought into the diagonal form:

\(4y^2_1 − y^2_2 = 16\)

This is an equation of a hyperbola in the \(y_{1}y_{2}-\)plane. We observe that the \(y_{1}-\)intercepts are (2, 0) and (−2, 0), and there are no intercepts on the \(y_{2}-\)axis. The asymptotes of the hyperbola are determined by the equation \(4y^2_1 − y^2_2 = 0\). Solving for \(y_2\), we determine that the asymptotes are lines with equations \(y_2 = 2y_1\) and \(y_2 = −2y_1\). With this information, we obtain the graph in Figure 8.3.1.

Next, we need to find a basis for each eigenspace of A.

For \(\lambda_1 = 4\),

\(A − \lambda_{1} I = \left [ \begin{matrix} -1 & 2 \\ 2 & -4 \end{matrix} \right ] \sim \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Thus, a basis for the eigenspace is \(\left\{\vec{v_1}\right\}\), where \(\vec{v_1} = \left [ \begin{matrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ]\).

For \(\lambda_2 = −1\),

\(A − \lambda_{2}I = \left [ \begin{matrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ] \sim \left [ \begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Thus, a basis for the eigenspace is \(\left\{\vec{v_2}\right\}\), where \(\vec{v_2} = \left [ \begin{matrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ]\). Hence, A is orthogonally diagonalized by \(P = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5} } \left [ \begin{matrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{matrix} \right ]\).

Now we sketch the graph of \(3x^2_1 + 4x_{1}x_{2} = 16\). In the \(x_{1}x_{2}\)-plane, we draw the \(y_{1}\)-axis in the direction of \(\vec{v_1}\). (For clarity, in Figure 8.3.2 we have shown the vector \(\left [ \begin{matrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ]\) instead of \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} \left [ \begin{matrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ]\).) We also draw the \(y_{2}\)-axis in the direction of \(\vec{v_2}\). Then, relative to these new axes, we sketch the graph of the hyperbola \(4y^2_1 − y^2_2 = 16\). The graph in Figure 8.3.2 is also the graph of the original equation \(3x^2_1 + 4x_{1}x_{2} = 16\).

In order to include the asymptotes in the sketch, we solve the change of variables \(\vec{x} = P\vec{y}\) for \(\vec{y}\) to get

\(\vec{y} = P^{T} \vec{x}\)

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \end{matrix} \right ] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5} } \left [ \begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

This gives

\(y_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} (2x_1 + x_2)\)

\(y_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} (−x_1 + 2x_2)\)

Then one asymptote is

\(y_2 = 2y_1\)

\(\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} (−x_1 + 2x_2) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}} (2x_1 + x_2)\)

\(0 = x_1\)

The other asymptote is

\(y_2 = −2y_1\)

\(\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} (−x_1 + 2x_2) = – \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}} (2x_1 + x_2)\)

\(x_2 = – \frac{3}{4} x_1\)
