Guest Question.

A shaft of solid circular cross section has a diameter of 50 mm and is subjected to a torque, T, and axial load, P. A rectangular strain gauge rosette attached to the surface of the shaft recorded the following values of strain: \(\varepsilon_{ a }=1000 \times 10^{-6}, \varepsilon_{ b }=-200 \times 10^{-6}\) and \(\varepsilon_{ c }=-300 \times 10^{-6}\) where the gauges ‘a’ and ‘c’ are in line with and perpendicular to the axis of the shaft, respectively. If the material of the shaft has a Young’s modulus of \(70000 N / mm ^{2}\) and a Poisson’s ratio of 0.3, calculate the values of T and P.

Admin
Solution

Substituting the values of \(\varepsilon_{ a }\), \(\varepsilon_{ b }\) and \(\varepsilon_{ c }\) in Eq. (14.34)

\(\varepsilon_{ I }=\frac{1}{2}\left(\varepsilon_{ a }+\varepsilon_{ c }\right)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \sqrt{\left(\varepsilon_{ a }-\varepsilon_{ b }\right)^{2}+\left(\varepsilon_{ c }-\varepsilon_{ b }\right)^{2}}\) (14.34)

we have

\(\varepsilon_{ I }=\frac{10^{-6}}{2}(1000-300)+\frac{10^{-6}}{\sqrt{2}} \sqrt{(1000+200)^{2}+(-200+300)^{2}}\)

which gives

\(\varepsilon_{ I }=\frac{10^{-6}}{2}(700+1703)=1202 \times 10^{-6}\)

It follows from Eq. (14.35)

\(\varepsilon_{ II }=\frac{1}{2}\left(\varepsilon_{ a }+\varepsilon_{ c }\right)-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \sqrt{\left(\varepsilon_{ a }-\varepsilon_{ b }\right)^{2}+\left(\varepsilon_{ c }-\varepsilon_{ b }\right)^{2}}\) (14.35)

that

\(\varepsilon_{ II }=\frac{10^{-6}}{2}(700-1703)=-502 \times 10^{-6}\)

Substituting for \(\varepsilon_{ I }\) and \(\varepsilon_{ II }\) in Eq. (14.32)

\(\sigma_{ I }=\frac{E}{1-v^{2}}\left(\varepsilon_{ I }+v \varepsilon_{ II }\right)\) (14.32)

we have

\(\sigma_{ I }=\frac{70000 \times 10^{-6}}{1-(0.3)^{2}}(1202-0.3 \times 502)=80.9 N / mm ^{2}\)

Similarly from Eq. (14.33)

\(\sigma_{ II }=\frac{E}{1-v^{2}}\left(\varepsilon_{ II }+v \varepsilon_{ I }\right)\) (14.33)

 

\(\sigma_{ II }=\frac{70000 \times 10^{-6}}{1-(0.3)^{2}}(-502+0.3 \times 1202)=-10.9 N / mm ^{2}\)

Since \(\sigma_{y}=0\) (note that the axial load produces \(\sigma_{x}\) only), Eqs (14.8)

\(\sigma_{ I }=\frac{\sigma_{x}+\sigma_{y}}{2}+\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\left(\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}\right)^{2}+4 \tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (14.8)

and (14.9)

\(\sigma_{ II }=\frac{\sigma_{x}+\sigma_{y}}{2}-\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\left(\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}\right)^{2}+4 \tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (14.9)

reduce to

\(\sigma_{ I }=\frac{\sigma_{x}}{2}+\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\sigma_{x}^{2}+4 \tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (i)

and

\(\sigma_{ II }=\frac{\sigma_{x}}{2}-\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{\sigma_{x}^{2}+4 \tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (ii)

respectively. Adding Eqs (i) and (ii) we obtain

\(\sigma_{ I }+\sigma_{ II }=\sigma_{x}\)

Thus

\(\sigma_{x}=80.9-10.9=70 N / mm ^{2}\)

Substituting for \(\sigma_{x}\) in either of Eq. (i) or (ii) gives

\(\tau_{x y}=29.7 N / mm ^{2}\)

For an axial load P

\(\sigma_{x}=70 N / mm ^{2}=\frac{P}{A}=\frac{P}{(\pi / 4) \times 50^{2}}\) (Eq. (7.1))

\(\sigma=\frac{P}{A}\) (7.1)

so that

P = 137.4 kN

Also for the torque T and using Eq. (11.4)

\(\frac{T}{J}=\frac{\tau}{r}=G \frac{\theta}{L}\) (11.4)

we have

\(\tau_{x y}=29.7 N / mm ^{2}=\frac{T r}{J}=\frac{T \times 25}{(\pi / 32) \times 50^{4}}\)

which gives

T = 0.7 kNm

Note that P could have been found directly in this case from the axial strain \(\varepsilon_{ a }\). Thus from Eq. (7.8)

\(\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}=E\) (7.8)

 

\(\sigma_{x}=E \varepsilon_{ a }=70000 \times 1000 \times 10^{-6}=70 N / mm ^{2}\)

as before.
