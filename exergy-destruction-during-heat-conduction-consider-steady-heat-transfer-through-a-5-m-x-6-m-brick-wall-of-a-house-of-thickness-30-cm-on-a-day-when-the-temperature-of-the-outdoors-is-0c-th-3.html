Guest Question.

Exergy Destruction during Heat Conduction

Consider steady heat transfer through a 5-m × 6-m brick wall of a house of thickness 30 cm. On a day when the temperature of the outdoors is 0°C, the house is maintained at 27°C. The temperatures of the inner and outer surfaces of the brick wall are measured to be 20°C and 5°C, respectively, and the rate of heat transfer through the wall is 1035 W. Determine the rate of exergy destruction in the wall, and the rate of total exergy destruction associated with this heat transfer process.

Admin
Solution

Steady heat transfer through a wall is considered. For specified heat transfer rate, wall surface temperatures, and environment conditions, the rate of exergy destruction within the wall and the rate of total exergy destruction are to be determined.
Assumptions 1 The process is steady, and thus the rate of heat transfer through the wall is constant. 2 The exergy change of the wall is zero during this process since the state and thus the exergy of the wall do not change anywhere in the wall. 3 Heat transfer through the wall is one-dimensional.
Analysis We first take the wall as the system (Fig. 8–36). This is a closed system since no mass crosses the system boundary during the process. We note that heat and exergy are entering from one side of the wall and leaving from the other side.
Applying the rate form of the exergy balance to the wall gives

\( \underbrace{\dot{X}_{\text {in }}-\dot{X}_{\text {ouf }}}_{\begin{array}{c} \text { Rate of net exergy transfer } \\ \text { by heat, work, and mass } \end{array}}-\underbrace{\dot{X}_{\text {destroyed }}}_{\begin{array}{c} \text { Rate of exergy } \\ \text { destruction } \end{array}}=\underbrace{d X_{\text {system }} / dt^{\nearrow ^{0(steady)}}}_{\begin{array}{c} \text { Rate of change } \\ \text { in exergy } \end{array}}=0 \)

 

\( \begin{array}{r} \dot{Q}\left(1-\frac{T_{0}}{T}\right)_{\text {in }}-\dot{Q}\left(1-\frac{T_{0}}{T}\right)_{\text {out }}-\dot{X}_{\text {destroyed }}=0 \\[0.5cm] (1035 W )\left(1-\frac{273 K }{293 K }\right)-(1035 W )\left(1-\frac{273 K }{278 K }\right)-\dot{X}_{\text {destroyed }}=0 \end{array} \)

Solving, the rate of exergy destruction in the wall is determined to be

\( \dot{X}_{\text {destroyed }}=52.0 W \)

Note that exergy transfer with heat at any location is \( \left(1-T_{0} / T\right) Q \) at that location, and the direction of exergy transfer is the same as the direction of heat transfer.

To determine the rate of total exergy destruction during this heat transfer process, we extend the system to include the regions on both sides of the wall that experience a temperature change. Then one side of the system boundary becomes room temperature while the other side, the temperature of the outdoors. The exergy balance for this extended system (system + immediate surroundings) is the same as that given above, except the two boundary temperatures are 300 and 273 K instead of 293 and 278 K, respectively. Then the rate of total exergy destruction becomes

\( \dot{X}_{\text {destroyed, total }}=(1035 W )\left(1-\frac{273 K }{300 K }\right)-(1035 W )\left(1-\frac{273 K }{273 K }\right)= 9 3 . 2 W \)

The difference between the two exergy destructions is 41.2 W and represents the exergy destroyed in the air layers on both sides of the wall. The exergy destruction in this case is entirely due to irreversible heat transfer through a finite temperature difference.
Discussion This problem was solved in Chap. 7 for entropy generation. We could have determined the exergy destroyed by simply multiplying the entropy generations by the environment temperature of \( T_{0}=273 K \).
