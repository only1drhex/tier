Friction in a Pipe

Consider flow of an incompressible fluid of density ùúå and viscosity ùúá through a long, horizontal section of round pipe of diameter D. The velocity profile is sketched in Fig. 7‚Äì34; V is the average speed across the pipe cross section, which by conservation of mass remains constant down the pipe. For a very long pipe, the flow eventually becomes hydrodynamically fully developed, which means that the velocity profile also remains uniform down the pipe. Because of frictional forces between the fluid and the pipe wall, there exists a shear stress \(\tau_{w}\) on the inside pipe wall as sketched. The shear stress is also constant down the pipe in the fully developed region. We assume some constant average roughness height ùúÄ along the inside wall of the pipe. In fact, the only parameter that is not constant down the length of pipe is the pressure, which must decrease (linearly) down the pipe in order to ‚Äúpush‚Äù the fluid through the pipe to overcome friction. Develop a nondimensional relationship between shear stress \(\tau_{w}\) and the other parameters in the problem.

Solution

SOLUTION We are to generate a nondimensional relationship between shear stress and other parameters.

Assumptions 1 The flow is hydrodynamically fully developed. 2 The fluid is incompressible. 3 No other parameters are significant in the problem.

Analysis The step-by-step method of repeating variables is employed to obtain the nondimensional parameters.

Step 1 There are six variables and constants in this problem; n = 6. They are listed in functional form, with the dependent variable listed as a function of the independent variables and constants:

 

List of relevant parameters: \(\tau_{w}=f(V, \varepsilon, \rho, \mu, D) \quad n=6\)

 

Step 2 The primary dimensions of each parameter are listed. Note that shear stress is a force per unit area, and thus has the same dimensions as pressure.

 

\(\begin{array}{cccccc}\tau_{w} & V & \varepsilon & \rho & \mu & D \\\left\{ m ^{1} L ^{-1} t ^{-2}\right\} & \left\{ L ^{1} t ^{-1}\right\} & \left\{ L ^{1}\right\} & \left\{ m ^{1} L ^{-3}\right\} & \left\{ m ^{1} L ^{-1} t ^{-1}\right\} & \left\{ L ^{1}\right\}\end{array}\)

 

Step 3 As a first guess, j is set equal to 3, the number of primary dimensions represented in the problem (m, L, and t).

 

Reduction: j = 3

 

If this value of j is correct, the expected number of Œ†‚Äôs is k = n – j = 6 – 3 = 3.

 

Step 4 We choose three repeating parameters since j = 3. Following the guidelines of Table 7‚Äì3, we cannot pick the dependent variable \(\tau_{w}\). We cannot choose both ùúÄ and D since their dimensions are identical, and it would not be desirable to have ùúá or ùúÄ appear in all the Œ†‚Äôs. The best choice of repeating parameters is thus V, D, and ùúå .

Repeating parameters: V, D, and œÅ

\(\Pi_{1}=\tau_{w} V^{a_{1}} D^{b_{1}} \rho^{c_{1}} \quad \rightarrow \quad\left\{\Pi_{1}\right\}=\left\{\left( m ^{1} L ^{-1} t ^{-2}\right)\left( L ^{1} t ^{-1}\right)^{a_{1}}\left( L ^{1}\right)^{b_{1}}\left( m ^{1} L ^{-3}\right)^{c_{1}}\right\}\)

Discussion The result applies to both laminar and turbulent fully developed pipe flow; it turns out, however, that the second independent Œ† (roughness ratio ùúÄ /D) is not nearly as important in laminar pipe flow as in turbulent pipe flow. This problem presents an interesting connection between geometric similarity and dimensional analysis. Namely, it is necessary to match ùúÄ /D since it is an independent Œ† in the problem. From a different perspective, thinking of roughness as a geometric property, it is necessary to match ùúÄ /D to ensure geometric similarity between two pipes.
