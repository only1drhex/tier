Guest Question.

A hydrogen atom in its ground state is placed in an oscillating electric field \(\overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }(t)=\overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0} \sin (\omega t)\) of angular frequency ω with \(\hbar \omega>m_{e} e^{4} /\left(2 \hbar^{2}\right)\).

(a) Find the transition rate (probability per unit time) that the atom will be ionized.

(b) Use the expression derived in (a) to find the maximum transition rate.

Admin
Solution

After ionization we assume the electron to be in free motion: its energy is purely kinetic \(E_{k}=\hbar^{2} k^{2} / 2 m_{e}\) and its wave function is a plane wave \(\psi_{k}(\vec{r})=(2 \pi)^{-3 / 2} e^{i \vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}}\). Since the perturbation resulting fromthe interaction of the hydrogen’s electron with the external field \(\overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }(t)\) is harmonic,

\(\hat{V}(t)=-e \vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }(t)=-e \vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ E }_{0} \sin (\omega t)=\frac{e}{2 i} \vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0} e^{-i \omega t}-\frac{e}{2 i} \vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0} e^{i \omega t}\), (10.247)

we can infer, by analogy with the method that led to (10.54) from (10.50),

\(\Gamma_{i f}=\frac{2 \pi}{\hbar}\left|\left\langle\psi_{f}|\hat{v}| \psi_{i}\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}+\hbar \omega\right)+\frac{2 \pi}{\hbar}\left|\left\langle\psi_{f}\left|\hat{v}^{\dagger}\right| \psi_{i}\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}-\hbar \omega\right) .\) (10.54)

 

\(\hat{V}(t)=\hat{v} e^{i \omega t}+\hat{v}^{\dagger} e^{-i \omega t}\), (10.50)

the transition rate for the ionization of the hydrogen atom:

\(\Gamma_{0 k}=\frac{2 \pi}{\hbar}\left|\frac{e}{2 i}\left\langle\psi_{k}\left|\vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}\right| 100\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}+\hbar \omega\right)\)

 

\(+\frac{2 \pi}{\hbar}\left|-\frac{e}{2 i}\left\langle\psi_{k}\left|\vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}\right| 100\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}-\hbar \omega\right)\), (10.248)

where \(E_{0}=-m_{e} e^{4} / 2 \hbar^{2}=-13.6 eV\) is the ground state energy and \(E_{k}=\hbar^{2} k^{/ 2} m_{e}\) is the final energy of the electron. The first delta term, \(\delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}+\hbar \omega\right)\), in (10.248) does not contribute, since if \(\hbar \omega=E_{0}-E_{k}\) the ionization could not take place because the electric field would not be strong enough to ionize the atom. The transition rate (10.248) then becomes

\(\Gamma_{0 k}=\frac{\pi e^{2}}{2 \hbar}\left|\left\langle\psi_{k}\left|\vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}\right| 100\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}-\hbar \omega\right)\). (10.249)

To calculate \(\left\langle\psi_{k}\left|\vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}\right| 100\right\rangle\), let us take \(\vec{k}\) along the z-axis and hence \(\vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}=k r \cos \theta\) and \(\psi_{k}(\vec{r})=(2 \pi)^{-3 / 2} e^{i k r \cos \theta}\). Taking \((\theta, \phi)\) and (α, β) as the respective polar angles of \(\vec{r}\) and \(\overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}\), we have \(\vec{r}=r(\sin \theta \cos \phi \vec{i}+\sin \theta \sin \phi \vec{j}+\cos \theta \vec{k})\) and \(\overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}= \varepsilon _{0}(\sin \alpha \cos \beta \vec{i}+\sin \alpha \sin \beta \vec{j}+\cos \alpha \vec{k})\); hence

\(\vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}=r \varepsilon _{0}(\sin \theta \cos \phi \sin \alpha \cos \beta+\sin \theta \sin \phi \sin \alpha \sin \beta+\cos \theta \cos \alpha)\)

 

\(=r \varepsilon _{0}[\sin \theta \sin \alpha \cos (\phi-\beta)+\cos \theta \cos \alpha]\). (10.250)

Since \(\psi_{1 S }=\left(\pi a_{0}^{3}\right)^{-1 / 2} e^{-r / a_{0}}\) and \(d^{3} r=r^{2} d r \sin \theta \cos \phi d \theta d \phi\), we have

\(\left\langle\psi_{k}\left|\vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}\right| 100\right\rangle=\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{3 / 2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi a_{0}^{3}}} \int d^{3} r\left(\vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}\right) e^{-i k r \cos \theta-r / a_{0}}\)

 

\(=\frac{ \varepsilon _{0}}{\sqrt{8 \pi^{4} a_{0}^{3}}} \int_{0}^{\infty} r^{3} e^{-r / a_{0}} d r \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin \theta e^{-i k r \cos \theta} d \theta \int_{0}^{2 \pi}[\sin \theta \sin \alpha \cos (\phi-\beta)+\cos \theta \cos \alpha] d \phi\)

 

\(=\frac{2 \pi \varepsilon _{0} \cos \alpha}{\sqrt{8 \pi^{4} a_{0}^{3}}} \int_{0}^{\infty} r^{3} e^{-r / a_{0}} d r \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin \theta \cos \theta e^{-i k r \cos \theta} d \theta\), (10.251)

where we have used \(\int_{0}^{2 \pi} \cos (\phi-\beta) d \phi=0\), since \(\int_{0}^{2 \pi} \cos \phi d \phi=0\) and \(\int_{0}^{2 \pi} \sin \phi d \phi=0\). A change of variable \(x=\cos \theta\) and an integration by parts leads to

\(\int_{0}^{\pi} \sin \theta \cos \theta e^{-i k r \cos \theta} d \theta=\int_{-1}^{1} x e^{-i k r x} d x=\left.\frac{1}{-i k r} x e^{-i k r x}\right|_{-1} ^{1}-\left.\frac{1}{(-i k r)^{2}} e^{-i k r x}\right|_{-1} ^{1}\)

 

\(=\frac{i}{k r}\left[e^{-i k r}+e^{i k r}\right]+\frac{1}{k^{2} r^{2}}\left[e^{-i k r}-e^{i k r}\right]\). (10.252)

When we insert this integral into (10.251), we still need to calculate four radial integrals which can be carried out by parts:

\(\int_{0}^{\infty} r e^{\pm i k r-r / a_{0}} d r=\left.\frac{1}{\pm i k-1 / a_{0}} r e^{\pm i k r-r / a_{0}}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}-\left.\frac{1}{\left(\pm i k-1 / a_{0}\right)^{2}} e^{\pm i k r-r / a_{0}}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}=\frac{a_{0}^{2}}{\left(\pm i a_{0} k-1\right)^{2}}\), (10.253)

 

\(\int_{0}^{\infty} r^{2} e^{\pm i k r-r / a_{0}} d r=\left.\frac{1}{\pm i k-1 / a_{0}} r^{2} e^{\pm i k r-r / a_{0}}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}-\frac{2}{\pm i k-1 / a_{0}} \int_{0}^{\infty} r e^{\pm i k r-r / a_{0}} d r\)

 

\(=-\left.\frac{2}{\left(\pm i k-1 / a_{0}\right)^{2}} r e^{\pm i k r-r / a_{0}}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}+\left.\frac{2}{\left(\pm i k-1 / a_{0}\right)^{3}} e^{\pm i k r-r / a_{0}}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}\)

 

\(=-\frac{2 a_{0}^{3}}{\left(\pm i a_{0} k-1\right)^{3}}\). (10.254)

Inserting (10.252) to (10.254) into (10.251), we obtain

\(\left\langle\psi_{k}\left|\vec{r} \cdot \overrightarrow{ \varepsilon }_{0}\right| 100\right\rangle=\frac{2 \pi \varepsilon _{0} \cos \alpha}{\sqrt{8 \pi^{4} a_{0}^{3}}}\left[\frac{a_{0}^{2}}{k^{2}\left(-i a_{0} k-1\right)^{2}}-\frac{a_{0}^{2}}{k^{2}\left(i a_{0} k-1\right)^{2}}-\frac{2 i a_{0}^{3}}{k\left(i a_{0} k-1\right)^{3}}\right.\)

 

\(\left.-\frac{2 i a_{0}^{3}}{k\left(-i a_{0} k-1\right)^{3}}\right]\)

 

\(=-\frac{16 \varepsilon _{0} \cos \alpha}{\pi \sqrt{2 a_{0}^{5}}} \frac{i a_{0}^{6} k}{\left(a_{0}^{2} k^{2}+1\right)^{3}}\). (10.255)

A substitution of this expression into (10.249) leads to

\(\Gamma_{0 k}=\frac{\pi e^{2}}{2 \hbar} \frac{128 \varepsilon _{0}^{2} \cos ^{2} \alpha}{\pi^{2} a_{0}^{5}} \frac{k^{2} a_{0}^{12}}{\left(a_{0}^{2} k^{2}+1\right)^{6}} \delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}+\hbar \omega\right)\). (10.256)

This relation gives the transition rate for a single final state \(\psi_{k}\) corresponding to a given k. We need to sum over all final states of the electron. These represent a continuum; we must then integrate over all directions of emission and over all possible momenta:

\(\Gamma_{0}=\int \Gamma_{0 k} d^{3} k=\int k^{2} d k \int_{0}^{\pi} \Gamma_{0 k} \sin \alpha d \alpha \int_{0}^{2 \pi} d \beta\)

 

\(=2 \pi \frac{64 e^{2} \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{7}}{\pi \hbar} \int \frac{k^{4} \delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}-\hbar \omega\right)}{\left(a_{0}^{2} k^{2}+1\right)^{6}} d k \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin \alpha \cos ^{2} \alpha d \alpha\)

 

\(=\frac{256 e^{2} \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{7}}{3 \hbar} \int \frac{k^{4} \delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}-\hbar \omega\right)}{\left(a_{0}^{2} k^{2}+1\right)^{6}} d k\), (10.257)

where we have used \(\int_{0}^{\pi} \sin \alpha \cos ^{2} \alpha d \alpha=\int_{-1}^{1} x^{3} d x=\frac{2}{3}\). The integration over k can be converted into an integration over the final energy \(E_{k}\): since \(E_{k}=\hbar^{2} k^{2} /\left(2 m_{e}\right)\), a change of variable \(k=\sqrt{2 m_{e} E_{k} / \hbar^{2}}\), and hence \(k d k=\left(m_{e} / \hbar^{2}\right) d E_{k}\), reduces (10.257) to

\(\Gamma_{0}=\frac{256 e^{2} \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{7}}{3 \hbar} \int \frac{k^{3} \delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}-\hbar \omega\right)}{\left(a_{0}^{2} k^{2}+1\right)^{6}} k d k\)

 

\(=\frac{m_{e}}{\hbar^{2}} \frac{256 e^{2} \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{7}}{3 \hbar} \int \frac{\left(2 m_{e} E_{k} / \hbar^{2}\right)^{3 / 2} \delta\left(E_{k}-E_{0}-\hbar \omega\right)}{\left(2 m_{e} a_{0}^{2} E_{k} / \hbar^{2}+1\right)^{6}} d E_{k}\)

 

\(=\frac{256 e^{2} m_{e} \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{7}}{3 \hbar^{3}} \frac{\left(2 m_{e} / \hbar^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}\left(E_{0}+\hbar \omega\right)^{3 / 2}}{\left[2 m_{e} a_{0}^{2}\left(E_{0}+\hbar \omega\right) / \hbar^{2}+1\right]^{6}}\). (10.458)

This relation can be simplified if we use \(E_{0}=-m_{e} e^{4} /\left(2 \hbar^{2}\right)=-\hbar \omega_{0}\), which gives \(E_{0}+\hbar \omega=\hbar\left(\omega-\omega_{0}\right)=\hbar \omega_{0}\left(\omega / \omega_{0}-1\right)\). Since \(a_{0}=\hbar^{2} /\left(m_{e} e^{2}\right)\), we have \(\hbar \omega_{0} a_{0}^{2}=m_{e} e^{4} \hbar^{4} /\left(2 \hbar^{2} m_{e}^{2} e^{4}\right)=\hbar^{2} /\left(2 m_{e}\right)\) and hence \(2 m_{e} a_{0}^{2}\left(E_{0}+\hbar \omega\right) / \hbar^{2}=2 m_{e} \hbar \omega_{0} a_{0}^{2}\left(\omega / \omega_{0}-1\right) / \hbar^{2}=\omega / \omega_{0}-1\). Thus, inserting the expressions \(E_{0}+\hbar \omega=\hbar \omega_{0}\left(\omega / \omega_{0}-1\right)\) and \(2 m_{e} a_{0}^{2}\left(E_{0}+\hbar \omega\right) / \hbar^{2}+1=\omega / \omega_{0}\) into (10.258), we obtain

\(\Gamma_{0}=\frac{256 e^{2} m_{e} \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{7}}{3 \hbar^{3}} \frac{\left(2 m_{e} / \hbar^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}\left(\hbar \omega_{0}\right)^{3 / 2}\left(\omega / \omega_{0}-1\right)^{3 / 2}}{\left(\omega / \omega_{0}\right)^{6}}\). (10.259)

Finally, since \(\left(2 m_{e} / \hbar^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}\left(\hbar \omega_{0}\right)^{3 / 2}=\left(2 m_{e} / \hbar^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}\left(m_{e} e^{4} / 2 \hbar^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}=m_{e}^{3} e^{6} / \hbar^{6}\) and using \(a_{0}^{4}=\hbar^{8} /\left(m^{4} e^{8}\right)\), we can write (10.259) as

\(\Gamma_{0}=\frac{256 \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{3}}{3 \hbar}\left(\frac{\omega_{0}}{\omega}\right)^{6}\left(\frac{\omega}{\omega_{0}}-1\right)^{3 / 2}\). (10.260)

If the frequency of the oscillating electric field is smaller than or equal to \(\omega_{0}\), the atom will not be ionized; at \(\omega=\omega_{0}\) the probability of ionization will be zero.

(b) The maximum transition rate is obtained by taking the derivative of (10.260):

\(\frac{d \Gamma_{0}}{d \omega}=0 \Longrightarrow \frac{2}{\omega}\left(\frac{\omega}{\omega_{0}}-1\right)=\frac{1}{2 \omega_{0}} \Longrightarrow \omega=\frac{4}{3} \omega_{0} .\) (10.261)

Inserting \(\omega=\frac{4}{3} \omega_{0}\) into (10.260) we obtain the maximum transition rate

\(\Gamma_{0 \max }=\frac{256 \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{3}}{3 \hbar}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^{6}\left(\frac{4}{3}-1\right)^{3 / 2}=\frac{ \varepsilon _{0}^{2} a_{0}^{3}}{\hbar} \frac{3^{7 / 2}}{2^{4}}\). (10.262)
