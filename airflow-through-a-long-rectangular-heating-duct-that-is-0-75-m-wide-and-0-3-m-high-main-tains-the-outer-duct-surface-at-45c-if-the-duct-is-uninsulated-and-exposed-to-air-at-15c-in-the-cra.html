Guest Question.

Airflow through a long rectangular heating duct that is 0.75 m wide and 0.3 m high main-tains the outer duct surface at 45°C. If the duct is uninsulated and exposed to air at 15°C in the crawlspace beneath a home, what is the heat loss from the duct per meter of length?

Admin
Solution

Known: Surface temperature of a long rectangular duct.

Find: Heat loss from duct per meter of length.

Assumptions:
1. Ambient air is quiescent.
2. Surface radiation effects are negligible.
3. Ideal gas.
4. Constant properties.

Properties: , air \((T_{f} = 303 K): \nu = 16.2 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s, α = 22.9 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s,k = 0.0265 W/m.K, \beta = 0.0033 K^{-1}, Pr = 0.71\).

Analysis: Surface heat loss is by free convection from the vertical sides and the horizon-tal top and bottom. From Equation 9.25

\( Ra_{L} = \frac{g \beta (T_{s} – T_{∞})L^{3}}{ \nu α} =\frac{(9.8 m/s^{2})(0.0033 K^{-1})(30 K) L^{3} (m^{3})}{(16.2 × 10^{-6} m^{2} /s)(22.9 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s)} \)

 

\( Ra_{L} = 2.62 × 10^{9}L^{3}\)

For the two sides, L = H = 0.3 m. Hence \(Ra_{L} = 7.07 × 10^{7}\). The free convection boundary layer is therefore laminar, and from Equation 9.27

\(\overset{-}{Nu_{L}}= 0.68 + \frac{0.670 Ra_{L}^{1/4}}{[1 + (0.492/Pr)^{9/16}]^{4/9}} \)

The convection coefficient associated with the sides is then

\(\bar{h}_{s} = \frac{k}{H}\overset{-}{Nu_{L}} \)

 

\(\bar{h}_{s} = \frac{0.0265 W/m . K}{0.3 m} \left\{0.68 + \frac{0.670 (7.07 × 10^{7})^{1/4}}{[1 + (0.492//0.71)^{9/16}]^{4/9}}\right\} =4.23 W/m^{2} . K\)

For the top and bottom, \( L = (A_{s}/P)≈ (w/2) = 0.375 \ m. \) Hence \(Ra_{L} = 1.38 × 10^{8}\), and from Equations 9.31 and 9.32, respectively,

\( \overline{Nu}_L =0.15 \ Ra_L^{1/3} \quad \quad (10^7\lesssim Ra_L \lesssim 10^{11} , \text{ all } Pr) \) (9.31)

\( \overline{Nu}_L =0.52 \ Ra_L^{1/5} \quad \quad (10^4\lesssim Ra_L \lesssim 10^{9} , \ Pr \gtrsim 0.7) \) (9.32)

\(\bar{h}_{t} = [k/(w/2)] × 0.15 Ra^{1/3}_{L} = \frac{0.0265 W/m . K}{0.375 m} × 0.15 (1.38 × 10^{8})^{1/3} = 5.47 W/m^{2} . K\)

 

\(\bar{h}_{b} = [k/(w/2)] × 0.52 Ra^{1/5}_{L} = \frac{0.0265 W/m . K}{0.375 m} × 0.52 (1.38 × 10^{8})^{1/5} = 1.56 W/m^{2} . K\)

The rate of heat loss per unit length of duct is then

\(q^{\prime} = 2q^{\prime}_{s} + q^{\prime}_{t} + q^{\prime}_{b} =( 2\bar{h}_{s} .H +\bar{h}_{t} .w +\bar{h}_{b} .w ) (T_{s} – T_{∞})\)

 

\(q^{\prime} = (2 × 4.23 × 0.3 + 5.47 × 0.75 + 1.56 × 0.75)(45 – 15) W/m \)

 

\(q^{\prime} =234 W/m\)

Comments:
1. The heat loss may be reduced by insulating the duct. We consider this option for a 25- mm-thick layer of blanket insulation (k = 0.035 W/m.K) that is wrapped around the duct

The heat loss at each surface may be expressed as

\(q^{\prime} =\frac{T_{s,1} – T_{\infty }}{R^{\prime}_{cond}+R^{\prime}_{conv} }\)

where \(R^{\prime}_{conv}\) is associated with free convection from the outer surface and hence depends on the unknown temperature \(T_{s,2}\). This temperature may be determined by applying an energy balance to the outer surface, from which it follows that

\(q^{\prime \prime }_{cond} = q^{\prime \prime }_{conv}\)

or

\(\frac{T_{s,1} – T_{s,2 }}{(t/k) } = \frac{T_{s,2} – T_{\infty }}{(1/\bar{h} ) }\)

Since different convection coefficients are associated with the sides, top, and bottom \( (\bar{h}_{s} ,\bar{h}_{t}, and\bar{h}_{b})\), a separate solution to this equation must be obtained for each of the three surfaces. The solutions are iterative, since the properties of air and the convec-tion coefficients depend on \( T_{s}\). Performing the calculations, we obtain

Sides \(T_{s,2} = 24°C, \bar{h}_{s} = 3.18 W/m^{2} . K\)

Top \(T_{s,2} = 23°C,\bar{h}_{t} = 3.66 W/m^{2} . K\)

Bottom \(T_{s,2} = 30°C,\bar{h}_{b} = 1.36W/m^{2} . K\)

Neglecting heat loss through the corners of the insulation, the total heat rate per unit length of duct is then

\(q^{\prime} = 2q^{\prime}_{s} + q^{\prime}_{t} + q^{\prime}_{b}\)

 

\(q^{\prime} = \frac{2H(T_{s,1} – T_{\infty })}{(t/k) + (1/\bar{h}_{s})} + \frac{w (T_{s,1} – T_{\infty }) }{(t/k) + (1/\bar{h}_{t})} + \frac{w (T_{s,1} – T_{\infty }) }{(t/k) + (1/\bar{h}_{b})} \)

which yields

\(q^{\prime} = (17.5 + 22.8 + 15.5) W/m = 55.8 W/m\)

The insulation therefore provides a 76% reduction in heat loss to the ambient air by natural convection.
2. Although they have been neglected, radiation losses may still be significant. From Equation 1.7 \( q^{\prime \prime }_{rad} =\frac{q}{A} = \varepsilon E_{b} (T_{s})-\alpha G = \varepsilon \sigma (T^{4}_{s} – T^{4}_{sur}) \) with assumed to be unity and \(T_{sur} \)= 288 K,\(q^{\prime}_{rad}= 398 W/m \) for the uninsulated duct. Inclusion of radiation effects in the energy balance for the insulated duct would reduce the outer surface temperatures, thereby reducing the convection heat rates. With radiation, however, the total heat rate (\(q^{\prime}_{conv} + q^{\prime}_{rad})\) would increase.
