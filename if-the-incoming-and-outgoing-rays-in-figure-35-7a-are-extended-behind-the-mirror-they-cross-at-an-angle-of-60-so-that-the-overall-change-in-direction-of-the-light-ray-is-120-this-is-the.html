Guest Question.

If the incoming and outgoing rays in Figure 35.7a are extended behind the mirror, they cross at an angle of 60°, so that the overall change in direction of the light ray is 120°. This is the same as the angle between the mirrors. What if the angle between the mirrors is changed? Is the overall change in the direction of the light ray always equal to the angle between the mirrors?

Admin
Solution

Making a general statement based on one data point is always a dangerous practice! Let us investigate the change in direction for a general situation. Figure 35.7b shows the mirrors at an arbitrary angle θ and the incoming light ray striking the mirror at an arbitrary angle \( \phi \) with respect to the normal to the mirror surface. In accordance with the law of reflection and the sum of the interior angles of a triangle, the angle γ is 180° – (90° – φ) – θ = 90° + φ -θ. Considering the triangle highlighted in blue

in Figure 35.7b, we see that

\( \alpha+2 \gamma+2\left(90^{\circ}-\phi\right)=180^{\circ} \).

\( \alpha=2(\phi-\gamma) \).

The change in direction of the light ray is angle β, which is 180° – α:

\( \beta=180^{\circ}-\alpha=180^{\circ}-2(\phi-\gamma) \).

\( =180^{\circ}-2\left[\phi-\left(90^{\circ}+\phi-\theta\right)\right] \).

\( =360^{\circ}-2 \theta \).

\( \text { Notice that } \beta \text { is not equal to } \theta \text {. For } \theta=120^{\circ} \text {, we obtain } \beta= \) 120°, which happens to be the same as the mirror angle. But this is true only for this special angle between the mirrors. For example, if \( \theta=90^{\circ}, \text { we obtain } \beta=180^{\circ} \). In this case, the light is reflected straight back to its origin.
