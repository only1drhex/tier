Guest Question.

Oxygen gas \((O_{2})\) is dissolved in water. This circumstance is important for aquatic life. We want to know how much oxygen will be in the (surface) water of a stream, lake, or the ocean.

(a) Calculate the equilibrium concentration of \(O_{2}\) in pure water if the water is in contact with an atmosphere composed only of oxygen.

(b) What will the actual value for our atmosphere be?

Admin
Solution

Similar to the example of humidity in the air (), we consider the chemical equilibrium between \(O_{2}\) in the air and \(O_{2}\) dissolved in water:

\(\mu_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}\left(T_{0}, \bar{c}_{\mathrm{CO} 2(a q)}\right)=\mu_{\mathrm{O} 2(\mathrm{gas})}\left(T_{0}, P_{\mathrm{O} 2(\mathrm{gas})}\right)\)

 

The chemical potential of \(O_{2}\) in water is determined by its concentration. In air, it is determined by its (partial) pressure:

\(\mu_{\mathrm{O2}(a q)}\left(T_{0}, \bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}^{0}\right)+R T \ln \left(\frac{\bar{c}_{\mathrm{O2}(a q)}}{\bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}^{0}}\right)=\mu_{\mathrm{O2}(\text { gas })}\left(T_{0}, P_{0}\right)+R T \ln \left(\frac{P_{\mathrm{O} 2(\mathrm{gas})}}{P_{0}}\right)\)

 

(a) If the atmosphere is pure oxygen gas, the (partial) pressure of \(O_{2}\) is \(1 atm\) \((1.013 · 10^{5} Pa).\)

The previous equation can be transformed to yield the ration of concentration of \(O_{2}\) in water and the standard concentration \((1 mole/L = 1000 mole/m^{3})\):

\(\begin{aligned} \frac{\bar{c}_{\mathrm{O2}(a q)}}{\bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}^{0}} &=\exp \left[\frac{1}{R T}\left(-\mu_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}\left(T_{0}, \bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}^{0}\right)+\mu_{\mathrm{O} 2(\text { gas })}\left(T_{0}, P_{0}\right)+R T \ln (1)\right)\right] \\ &=\exp \left[\frac{1}{8.314 \cdot 298}(-16440-0)\right]=\exp (-6.63)=1.31 \cdot 10^{-3} \end{aligned}\)

 

(b) We need to know the partial pressure of \(O_{2}\) in the actual atmosphere. For ideal gases, the fraction of volume taken by a component is also the fraction of the total pressure. So, the partial pressure of \(O_{2}\) in the air is \(0.2 · 10^{5}\) \(Pa\). Therefore:

\(\frac{\bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}}{\bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}^{0}}=\exp \left[\frac{1}{R T}\left(-\mu_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}\left(T_{0}, \bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}^{0}\right)+\mu_{\mathrm{O} 2(\text { gas })}\left(T_{0}, P_{0}\right)+R T \ln (0.2)\right)\right]\)

 

or

\(\frac{\bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}}{\bar{c}_{\mathrm{O} 2(a q)}^{0}}=\exp \left[\frac{1}{8.314 \cdot 298}(-16440-0+8.314 \cdot 298 \cdot \ln (0.2))\right]=\exp (-8.25)=2.63 \cdot 10^{-4}\)

 

This corresponds to \(0.26 mole\) of oxygen gas per cubic meter of water. The result is approximately equal to \(20\)% of the concentration obtained for a pure oxygen atmosphere. Note that these results have been obtained for pure water. Ocean water, in particular, contains many other dissolved substances that substantially alter the chemistry of the fluid.
