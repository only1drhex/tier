Potential Flow—Half-body

GIVEN A 40 mi/hr wind blows toward a hill arising from a plain that can be approximated with the top section of a half-body as illustrated in Fig. E6.7a. The height of the hill approaches 200 ft as shown. Assume an air density of 0.00238 \(\text { slugs } / ft ^{3}\).

FIND (a) What is the magnitude of the air velocity at a point on the hill directly above the origin [point (2)]?

(b) What is the elevation of point (2) above the plain and what is the difference in pressure between point (1) on the plain far from the hill and point (2)?

Admin
Solution

(a) The velocity is given by Eq. 6.101 as

 

\(V^{2}=U^{2}\left(1+2 \frac{b}{r} \cos \theta+\frac{b^{2}}{r^{2}}\right)\) (6.101)

 

\(V^{2}=U^{2}\left(1+2 \frac{b}{r} \cos \theta+\frac{b^{2}}{r^{2}}\right)\)

 

At point (2), \(\theta=\pi / 2\), and since this point is on the surface (Eq. 6.100)

 

\(r=\frac{b(\pi-\theta)}{\sin \theta}\) (6.100)

 

\(r=\frac{b(\pi-\theta)}{\sin \theta}=\frac{\pi b}{2}\) (1)

 

Thus,

 

\(\begin{aligned}V_{2}^{2} &=U^{2}\left[1+\frac{b^{2}}{(\pi b / 2)^{2}}\right] \\&=U^{2}\left(1+\frac{4}{\pi^{2}}\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

and the magnitude of the velocity at (2) for a 40 mi/hr approaching wind is

 

\(V_{2}=\left(1+\frac{4}{\pi^{2}}\right)^{1 / 2}(40 mi / hr )=47.4 mi / hr\) (Ans)

 

(b) The elevation at (2) above the plain is given by Eq. 1 as

 

\(y_{2}=\frac{\pi b}{2}\)

 

Since the height of the hill approaches 200 ft and this height is equal to πb, it follows that

 

\(y_{2}=\frac{200 ft }{2}=100 ft\) (Ans)

 

From the Bernoulli equation 1with the y axis the vertical axis2

 

\(\frac{p_{1}}{\gamma}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 g}+y_{1}=\frac{p_{2}}{\gamma}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2 g}+y_{2}\)

 

so that

 

\(p_{1}-p_{2}=\frac{\rho}{2}\left(V_{2}^{2}-V_{1}^{2}\right)+\gamma\left(y_{2}-y_{1}\right)\)

 

and with

 

\(V_{1}=(40 mi / hr )\left(\frac{5280 ft / mi }{3600 s / hr }\right)=58.7 ft / s\)

 

and

 

\(V_{2}=(47.4 mi / hr )\left(\frac{5280 ft / mi }{3600 s / hr }\right)=69.5 ft / s\)

 

it follows that

 

\(\begin{aligned}p_{1}-p_{2}=& \frac{\left(0.00238 slugs / ft ^{3}\right)}{2}\left[(69.5 ft / s )^{2}-(58.7 ft / s )^{2}\right] \\&+\left(0.00238 slugs / ft ^{3}\right)\left(32.2 ft / s ^{2}\right)(100 ft -0 ft ) \\=& 9.31 lb / ft ^{2}=0.0647 psi\end{aligned}\) (Ans)

 

COMMENTS This result indicates that the pressure on the hill at point (2) is slightly lower than the pressure on the plain at some distance from the base of the hill with a 0.0533 psi difference due to the elevation increase and a 0.0114 psi difference due to the velocity increase.

By repeating the calculations for various values of the upstream wind speed, U, the results shown in Fig. E6.7b are obtained. Note that as the wind speed increases, the pressure difference increases from the calm conditions of \(p_{1}-p_{2}=\) 0.0533 psi.

The maximum velocity along the hill surface does not occur at point 122 but farther up the hill at \(\theta=63^{\circ}\). At this point \(V_{\text {sufface }}=1.26 U . \text { The minimum velocity }(V=0)\) and maximum pressure occur at point (3), the stagnation point.
