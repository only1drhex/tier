Guest Question.

Given:

An impervious (relative to the foundation) earth dam with the dimensions shown in Fig Ex. C.3. The dam is underlain by a sand and gravel foundation soil with a permeability of 25,000 ft/yr.

Required:

Compute the length of an upstream impervious blanket to reduce the amount of seepage below the dam by one-half.

Admin
Solution

First, change the k from ft/yr to m/sec

 

\(k=25,000 \frac{ ft }{ yr } \times(0.3048) \frac{ ft }{ m } \times 3.17098 \times 10^{-8} \frac{ yr }{ sec }=2.42 \times 10^{-4} \frac{ m }{ sec }\)

 

Consulting Table C.1, we find that the problem is a Type I fragment, with

 

TABLE C. 1 Summary of Fragment Types and Form Factors
Fragment Type Illustration Form Factor, \(\Phi\) (h is head loss through fragment)
I
\(\Phi=\frac{L}{a}\)
II
\(\begin{array}{l}\Phi=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{k h}{Q}\right) \\i_{E}=\frac{h_{m} \pi}{2 K T m}\\\text{See also Fig. C.1 and Table C.2}\end{array}\)
III
\(\begin{array}{l}\Phi=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{k h}{Q}\right) \\\text{See also Fig. C.1 and Table C.2}\end{array}\)
IV
\(\begin{array}{l}s \geq b: \\\Phi: \ln \left(1+\frac{b}{a}\right) \\b \geq s: \\\Phi=\ln \left(1+\frac{s}{a}\right)+\frac{b-s}{T}\end{array}\)
V
\(\begin{array}{l}L \leq 2 s: \\\Phi=2 \ln \left(1+\frac{L}{2 a}\right) \\L \geq 2 s: \\\Phi=2 \ln \left(1+\frac{s}{a}\right)+\left(\frac{L-2 s}{T}\right)\end{array}\)
VI
\(\begin{array}{l}L>s^{\prime}+s^{\prime \prime}: \\\quad \Phi=\ln \left[\left(1+\frac{s^{\prime}}{a^{\prime}}\right)\left(1+\frac{s^{\prime \prime}}{a^{\prime \prime}}\right)\right]+\frac{L-\left(s^{\prime}+s^{\prime \prime}\right)}{T} \\L=s^{\prime}+s^{\prime \prime}: \\\Phi=\ln \left[\left(1+\frac{s^{\prime}}{a^{\prime}}\right)\left(1+\frac{s^{\prime \prime}}{a^{\prime \prime}}\right)\right] \\L<s^{\prime}+s^{\prime \prime} \\\Phi=\ln \left[\left(1+\frac{b^{\prime}}{a^{\prime}}\right)\left(1+\frac{b^{\prime \prime}}{a^{\prime \prime}}\right)\right]\\\text{where}\\b^{\prime}=\frac{L+\left(s^{\prime}-s^{\prime \prime}\right)}{2} \\b^{\prime \prime}=\frac{L-\left(s^{\prime}-s^{\prime \prime}\right)}{2}\end{array}\)
VII
\(\begin{array}{l}\Phi=\frac{2 L}{h_{1}+h_{2}} \\Q=k \frac{h_{1}^{2}-h_{2}^{2}}{2 L}\end{array}\)
VIII
\(Q=k \frac{h_{1}-h}{\cot \alpha} \ln \frac{h_{d}}{h_{d}-h}\)
IX
\(Q=k \frac{a_{2}}{\cot \beta}\left(1+\ln \frac{a_{2}+h_{2}}{a_{2}}\right)\)
*After Harr (1962, 1977).

 

\(\Phi=\frac{L}{a}=\frac{75 m }{21 m }=3.57\)

 

From Eq. (C.4),

 

\(q=\frac{k h}{\sum_{m=1}^{n} \Phi}\) (C.4)

 

\(q=\frac{k h}{\Phi}=\frac{2.42 \times 10^{-4} \frac{ m }{ sec } \times(133.5 m -122.5 m )}{3.57}=7.46 \times 10^{-4} \frac{ m }{ sec }\)

 

To reduce the flow by one-half, \(\Phi\) would have to be doubled. Because the distance a (the thickness of the pervious layer) remains the same, the length L would have to double. Obviously we don’t want to make the dam larger, so the easiest way is to install an upstream impervious blanket with a length equal to 75 m, making the total length the water has to pass under the dam 150 m. Now, can you imagine how long it would have taken you to do this problem using flow nets?
