Guest Question.

In Fig. 9-11, when pump YA delivers 5.00 cfs, find the pressure heads at A and B. Draw the hydraulic grade lines.

Admin
Solution

Reduce section (loop) EC to an equivalent pipe, 16/r in diameter, C = 100. By so doing, a single-size pipe of the same relative roughness is readily handled for all conditions of flow. Assuming a drop in the grade line of 22 ft from B to C, the following values are obtained.

\(\begin{array}{ll}S_{10}=22 / 10=2.2 ft / 1000 ft , & Q_{10}=0.57 mgd \\S_{8}=22 / 11=2.0 ft / 1000 ft , & Q_{8}=0.34 mgd\end{array}\)

total Q =0.91 mgd.

\(\text { For } Q=0.91 mgd \text { and } D=16^{\prime \prime}(C=100), S_{16}=0.435 ft / 1000 ft =22.0 / L_{E} \text { and } L_{E}=50,600 ft \).

The flow from pump to reservoir is 5.00 cfs or 3.23 mgd. For (50,600 + 16,000) = 66,600 ft of equivalent 16″ pipe, the lost head from A to C will be

\(S_{16}=4.55 ft / 1000 ft , \quad \text { lost head }=4.55 \times 66.6=303 ft\).

Thus the elevation of the hydraulic grade line at A is (217 + 303) = 520′, as shown in Fig. 9-11. The drop from A to B = 4.55 Ã— 16 = 73 ft, and the elevation at B becomes (520 – 73) = 447′.
pressure head at A = 520 – 50 = 470 ft.
pressure head at B = 447 – 50 = 397 ft.
