Guest Question.

For the structural steel beam with an overhang as shown in Fig. 15.38(a), determine (a) the deflection equation, (b) the deflection at the free end A of the beam, (c) the maximum deflection at some point D between B and C, and (d) the minimum required moment of inertia I if the maximum allowable deflection is 1/2000 of the 9-ft span or 0.054 in. (Use a modulus of elasticity for steel E = 29 \(\times 10^3\) ksi.)

 

Admin
Solution

There are four sections of the beam, and four singularity functions are required from Table 15.2 to write a single bending-moment equation. From (b) of the table, the singularity function for a concentrated load or reaction \(M_x =\pm P_o 〈x-a〉^1\) For the 9-kip load, we have \(M_x=-9 〈x〉^1=-9 x\), for the 21.5-kip reaction \(M_x =21.5 〈x-3〉^1\). and for the 15-kip load \(M_x =-15 〈x-9〉^1\).From (c) of the table, the singularity function for the 1-kip/ft uniform load is \(M_x =-w_0 〈x-a〉^2/2=-1 〈x-3〉^2/2. \) Therefore, we have the following bending-moment equation in terms of singularity functions.

\(M_x=-9 x+21.5 〈x-3〉^1 – \frac{〈x-3〉^2 }{2}-15 〈x-9〉^1 \)

Substituting the moment equation into Eq. (15.12), we write

\(\frac{d^2\delta }{dx^2} =-9x + 21.5 〈x-3〉^1- \frac{〈x-3〉^2}{2} -15 〈x-9〉^1 \)

Multiplying by dx and integrating with respect to x, we have

\(\frac{d\delta }{dx} = -\frac{9x^2}{2}+\frac{21.5 〈x-3〉^2}{2} -\frac{〈x-3〉^3}{6} -\frac{15 〈x-9〉^2}{2} +C_1\) (a)

 

\( EI \delta= -\frac{9x^3}{6}+\frac{21.5 〈x-3〉^3}{6} -\frac{〈x-3〉^4}{24} -\frac{15 〈x-9〉^3}{6} +C_1x +C_2\) (b)

When x=3 ft, \(\delta\)=0; therefore, from Eq. (b),

\(0 = -9(3)^3/6 + 0 – 0 – 0 + 3C_1 + C_2\)

or \( 3C_1 + C_2 = 40.5 \) (c)

When x=12 ft, \(\delta\)=0; therefore, from Eq. (b),

\(0=-\frac{9(12)^3}{6} + \frac{21.5(12 – 3)^3}{6}-\frac{(12 – 3)^4}{24} -\frac{15(12 – 9)^3}{6} + 12C_1 + C_2 \)

or \( 12C_1 + C_2 = 320.625 \) (d)

Solving Eqs. (c) and (d) by elimination, we obtain

\(C_1 = +31.125 kip-ft^2; \) \(C_2 = -52.875 kip-ft^3 \)

At x=0 the deflection

\( EI \delta_A=-52.875\) or \( \delta_A=- \frac{52.9}{EI} kip-ft^3\)

To find the maximum deflection for the span from B to C, we first determine the location of a zero slope from the slope equation by trial and error. Setting the slope equation equal to R and simplifying the constants, we have

\(R = -4.2 x^2 + 10.75 〈x-3〉^2 – \frac{〈x-3〉^3}{6} 7.5 〈x-9〉^2 +31.125 \)

From an inspection of the moment diagram in Fig. 15.38(b) and the resulting deflection curve in Fig. 15.38(c), we let x= 8.3 ft in the equation for R as a first trial. We then proceed as shown in Table (A). The slope is negative for trial no. 1 and positive for trial no. 2. Therefore, the slope is zero between x=8.3 and 8.4 ft. We continue as in the table until we obtain the desired accuracy. Each new pair of trials, no. 3 and no. 4, etc., bracket the zero slope. Rounding off trial no. 8 to three decimal places, we have x = 8.368 ft.
From the deflection equation for x = 8.368 ft. we get

\( EI \delta_D=-\frac{9(8.368)^3}{6} +\frac{21.5(8.368 – 3)^3}{6} -\frac{(8.368 – 3)^4}{24} +31.125(8.368) – 52.875\)

 

\(\delta_D=-\frac{151.7}{EI} kip-ft^3\)

There is a second point of zero slope for a value of x less than x=3 ft Setting the slope equation equal to zero and simplifying the constants, we write

0 = -4.5 \(x^2\) + 31.125

\(x^2\) = 6.917 or x = 2.630 ft

Substituting x = 2.630 ft into the deflection equation, we have

\(EI \delta= -1.5(2.630)^3\) + 31.125(2.630) – 52.875

 

\(\delta=+\frac{1.697}{EI} kip-ft^3\)

The largest deflection occurs at D, and the maximum allowable deflection is 0.054 in.
Changing \(ft^3\) to \(in.^3\) by multiplying by \((12 in./ft)^3 = 1728 in.^3/ft^3\) and solving for the moment of inertia I
from the equation for \(\delta_D\) we get

\(I=\frac{151.7(1728) kip-in.^3}{E\delta_D} \)

The product \(E \delta_D = 29 \times 10^3 kip/in.^2(0.054 in.) = 29 \times 10^3(0.054) kip/in.; \) therefore,

\(I=\frac{151.7(1728) \cancel{kip}-in.^3}{29 \times 10^3(0.054) \cancel{kip} /in.} =167.4 in.^4 \)

From of the Appendix, we select a \(W14 \times 22 \) beam with a moment of inertia I = \(199 in.^4 \) and a section modulus S = 29.0 \(in.^3\) From Fig. 15.38(b), the maximum moment for the beam is 30 kip-ft at a distance of 3 ft from C. For the bending stress, we have

\(\sigma =\frac{M}{S}=\frac{30 kip- \cancel{ft}(12 in./\cancel{ft})}{29.0 in.^3}=12.41 ksi \lt 24 ksi \) OK

The allowable stress for A36 structural steel is 24 ksi; therefore, the \(W14 \times 22 \) beam is also safe in bending.
