Comparing Solutions to a Problem Assuming the Gas Is Ideal, Using a Thermodynamic Properties Chart, and Assuming That the Gas Can Be Described by the van der Waals Equation of State

Nitrogen gas is being withdrawn from a 0.15-m\(^3\) cylinder at the rate of 10 mol/min. The cylinder initially contains the gas at a pressure of 100 bar and 170 K. The cylinder is well insulated, and there is a negligible heat transfer between the cylinder walls and the gas. How many moles of gas will be in the cylinder at any time? What will the temperature and pressure of the gas in the cylinder be after 50 minutes?

a. Assume that nitrogen is an ideal gas.
b. Use the nitrogen properties chart of Fig. 3.3-3.
c. Assume that nitrogen is a van der Waals fluid.

Data: For parts (a) and (c) use

\(C_{\mathrm{P}}^{*}[\mathrm{~J} /(\mathrm{mol} \mathrm{K})]=27.2+4.2 \times 10^{-3} T(\mathrm{~K})\)

 

Admin
Solution

The mass and energy balances for the contents of the cylinder are

 

\(\frac{d N}{d t}=\dot{N} \quad \text { or } \quad N(t)=N(t=0)+\dot{N} t\) (a)

 

and

 

\(\frac{d(N \underline{U})}{d t}=\dot{N} \underline{H}\) (b)

 

where \(\dot{N}\) = -10 mol/min.

 

Following , the result of writing an entropy balance for that portion of the gas that always remains in the cylinder is

 

\(\frac{d S}{d t}=0 \quad \text { or } \quad \underline{S}(t=0)=\underline{S}(t)\) (c)

 

Also, from V = N V, we have

 

\(N(t=0)=\frac{V_{\mathrm{cyl}}}{\underline{V}(t=0)}=\frac{0.15 \mathrm{~m}^{3}}{\underline{V}(t=0)}\) (d)

 

Equations a, b, c, and d apply to both ideal and nonideal gases.

Computation of N(t)

 

a. Using the ideal gas equation of state

 

\(\begin{aligned}\underline{V}(t=0) &=\frac{R T(t=0)}{P(t=0)}=\frac{8.314 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{bar} \mathrm{} \mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{K} \times 170 \mathrm{~K}}{100 \mathrm{bar}} \\\\&=1.4134 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{mol}\end{aligned}\)

 

so that

 

\(N(t=0)=1061.3 \mathrm{~mol}\)

 

and

 

\(N(t)=1061.3-10 t \mathrm{~mol}\)

 

b. Using Figure 3.3-3

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\hat{V}(T=170 \mathrm{~K}, P=100 \text { bar })=\hat{V}(T=170 \mathrm{~K}, P=10 \mathrm{MPa}) \approx 0.0035 \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg} \\\\&\underline{V}(T=170 \mathrm{~K}, P=10 \mathrm{MPa})=28.014 \frac{\mathrm{g}}{\mathrm{mol}} \times 0.0035 \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{\mathrm{~kg}} \times \frac{1 \mathrm{~kg}}{1000 \mathrm{~g}} \\\\&=9.80 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{mol}\end{aligned}\)

 

and

 

\(N(t)=1529.8-10 t \mathrm{~mol}\)

 

c. Using the van der Waals equation of state The van der Waals equation is

 

\(P=\frac{R T}{\underline{V}-b}-\frac{a}{\underline{V}^{2}}\)

 

With the constants given in Table 6.4-1, we have

\(100 \mathrm{bar}=1 \times 10^{7} \mathrm{~Pa}=\frac{8.314 \times 170}{\underline{V}(t=0)-3.864 \times 10^{-5}}-\frac{0.1368}{[\underline{V}(t=0)]^{2}}\)

 

Solving this cubic equation, we obtain

 

\(\underline{V}(t=0)=9.435 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{mol}\)

 

so that

 

\(N(t=0)=1589.9 \mathrm{~mol}\)

 

and

 

\(N(t)=1589.9-10 t \mathrm{~mol}\)

 

Note also that since the volume of the cylinder is constant (0.15 m\(^{3}\)), and as N(t) is known, we can compute the molar volume of the gas at any later time from

 

\(\underline{V}(t)=\frac{0.15 \mathrm{~m}^{3}}{N(t)}\)

 

In particular, we can use this equation to compute V(t=50 min). These results, together with other information gathered so far, and some results from the following sections, are listed in Table 6.5-1.

Since we know the specific volume after 50 minutes, we need to determine only one further state property to have the final state of the system completely specified. In principle, either the energy or entropy balance could be used to find this property. The entropy balance is more convenient to use, especially for the nonideal gas calculations. Thus, all the calculations here are based on the fact (see Eq. c) that

 

\(\underline{S}(t=0)=\underline{S}(t=50 \mathrm{~min})\)

 

Computation of T(t=50 min) and P(t=50 min)

 

a. Using the ideal gas equation of state

From Eq. 4.4-1, (\(d \underline{S}=\frac{1}{T} d \underline{U}+\frac{\underline{P}}{T} d \underline{V}=\frac{C_{\mathrm{V}}^{*}}{T} d T+\frac{R}{\underline{V}} d \underline{V}\)

 

\(d \underline{S}=C_{\mathrm{V}} \frac{d T}{T}+R \frac{d \underline{V}}{\underline{V}}\)

 

 

Now for the ideal gas,

 

\(\begin{aligned}C_{\mathrm{V}} &=C_{\mathrm{V}}^{*}=C_{\mathrm{P}}^{*}-R=(27.2-8.314)+4.2 \times 10^{-3} T \\\\&=18.9+4.2 \times 10^{-3} T \mathrm{~J} /(\mathrm{mol} \mathrm{K})\end{aligned}\)

 

 

Also, since S = constant, or \(\Delta \underline{S}=0\), we have

 

\(\int_{T=170}^{T} \frac{\left(18.9+4.2 \times 10^{-3} T\right)}{T} d T+8.314 \int_{\underline{V}=1.413 \times 10^{-4}}^{\underline{V}=2.672 \times 10^{-4}} \frac{d \underline{V}}{\underline{V}}=0\)

 

or

 

\(18.9 \ln \frac{T}{170}+4.2 \times 10^{-3}(T-170)+8.314 \ln \frac{2.672 \times 10^{-4}}{1.413 \times 10^{-4}}=0\)

 

The solution to this equation is 130.3 K, so that

 

\(P(t=50 \mathrm{~min})=\frac{R T(t=50 \mathrm{~min})}{\underline{V}(t=50 \mathrm{~min})}=40.5 \mathrm{bar}\)

 

b. Using Fig. 3.3-3

\(\begin{aligned}\underline{V}(t=50 \mathrm{~min}) &=1.457 \times 10^{-4} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{\mathrm{~mol}} \times \frac{1 \mathrm{~mol}}{28.014 \mathrm{~g}} \times 1000 \frac{\mathrm{g}}{\mathrm{kg}} \\\\&=5.20 \times 10^{-3} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{\mathrm{~kg}}\end{aligned}\)

 

 

To find the final temperature and pressure using Fig. 3.3-3, we locate the init (T = 170 K, P = 10 MPa) and follow a line of constant entropy (dashed line) thrc point to the intersection with a line of constant specific volume \(\hat{V}=5.2 \times 10^{-3}m^3\) This intersection gives the pressure and temperature of the t = 50 min state. We find

 

\(\begin{aligned}&T(t=50 \mathrm{~min}) \approx 133 \mathrm{~K} \\\\&P(t=50 \mathrm{~min}) \approx 3.9 \mathrm{MPa}=39 \mathrm{bar}\end{aligned}\)

 

This construction is shown in the following diagram, which is a portion of Fig. 3.3-3.

 

 

c. Using the van der Waals equation of state
Here we start with Eq. 6.2-19, (\(d \underline{S}=\frac{C_{\mathrm{V}}}{T} d T+\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_{\underline{V}} d \underline{V}\))

 

\(d \underline{S}=\frac{C_{\mathrm{V}}}{T} d T+\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_{\underline{V}} d \underline{V}\)

 

and note that for the van der Waals gas

 

\(\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_{\underline{V}}=\frac{R}{\underline{V}-b}\)

 

The integration path to be followed is

i. \(T(t=0), \underline{V}(t=0) \rightarrow T(t=0), \underline{V}=\infty\)

ii. \(T(t=0), \underline{V}=\infty \rightarrow T(t=50 \mathrm{~min}), \underline{V}=\infty\left(C_{\mathrm{V}}=C_{\mathrm{V}}^{*}\right.\) for this step )

iii. \(T(t=50 \mathrm{~min}), \underline{V}=\infty \rightarrow T(t=50 \mathrm{~min}), \underline{V}(t=50 \mathrm{~min})\)

 

so that

 

\(\underline{S}(t=50 \min )-\underline{S}(t=0)=0=R \int_{\underline{\underline{V}}(t=0)}^{\underline{V}=\infty} \frac{d \underline{V}}{\underline{V}-b}+\int_{T=170 \mathrm{~K}}^{T(t=50)} \frac{C_{\mathrm{V}}^{*}}{T} d T+R \int_{\underline{V}=\infty}^{\underline{V}(t=50)} \frac{d \underline{V}}{\underline{V}-b}\)

 

or

 

\(0=R \int_{\underline{V}(t=0)}^{\underline{V}(t=50)} \frac{d \underline{V}}{\underline{V}-b}+\int_{T=170 \mathrm{~K}}^{T(t=50)} \frac{18.9+4.2 \times 10^{-3} T}{T} d T\)

 

Thus, using \(b=3.864 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{mol}\), we have

 

\(0=8.314 \ln \left[\frac{(13.77-3.864) \times 10^{-5}}{(9.437-3.864) \times 10^{-5}}\right]+18.9 \ln \frac{T}{170}+4.2 \times 10^{-3}(T-170)\)

 

The solution to the equation is

 

\(T(t=50 \mathrm{~min})=133.1 \mathrm{~K}\)

 

Now, using the van der Waals equation of state with T = 133.1 K and \(\underline{V}=13.76 \times 10^{-5}\mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{mol}\), we find

 

\(P(t=50 \mathrm{~min})=39.5 \mathrm{bar}\)

COMMENT

It is clear from the results of this problem that one cannot assume that a high-pressure gas is ideal and expect to make useful predictions. Of the techniques that have been considered for the thermodynamic calculations involving real fluids, the use of a previously prepared thermodynamic properties chart for the fluid is the most rapid way to proceed. The alternatives, the use of a simple volumetric equation of state for the fluid or of corresponding-states correlations (discussed in the following section), are always tedious, and their accuracy is hard to assess. However, if you do not have a thermodynamic properties chart available for the working fluid, you have no choice but to use the more tedious methods.
