Pressure Field in Solid Body Rotation

A fluid is rotating as a rigid body (solid body rotation) around the z-axis as illustrated in Fig. 10‚Äì20. The steady incompressible velocity field is given by \(u_{r}=0\), \(u_{\theta}=\omega r, \text { and } u_{z}=0\). The pressure at the origin is equal to \(P_{0^{.}}\) Calculate the pressure field everywhere in the flow, and determine the Bernoulli constant along each streamline.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION For a given velocity field, we are to calculate the pressure field and the Bernoulli constant along each streamline.

Assumptions 1 The flow is steady and incompressible. 2 Since there is no flow in the z– (vertical) direction, a hydrostatic pressure distribution exists in the vertical direction. 3 The entire flow field is approximated as an inviscid region of flow since viscous forces are zero. 4 There is no variation of any flow variable in the ùúÉ-direction.

Analysis Equation 10‚Äì18 can be applied directly because of assumption 3,

 

Steady incompressible Bernoulli equation in inviscid regions of flow:

 

\(\frac{P}{\rho}+\frac{V^{2}}{2}+g z=C=\text { constant along streamlines }\) (Eq. 10-18)

 

Bernoulli equation: \(P=\rho C-\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}-\rho g z\) (1)

 

where C is the Bernoulli constant that changes radially across streamlines as illustrated in Fig. 10‚Äì20. At any radial location \(r, V^{2}=\omega^{2} r^{2}\), and Eq. 1 becomes

 

\(P=\rho C-\rho \frac{\omega^{2} r^{2}}{2}-\rho g z\) (2)

 

At the origin (r = 0, z = 0), the pressure is equal to P0 (from the given boundary condition). Thus we calculate \(C=C_{0}\) at the origin (r = 0),

 

Boundary condition at the origin: \(P_{0}=\rho C_{0} \quad \rightarrow \quad C_{0}=\frac{P_{0}}{\rho}\)

 

But how can we find C at an arbitrary radial location r? Equation 2 alone is insufficient since both C and P are unknowns. The answer is that we must use the Euler equation. Since there is no free surface, we employ the modified pressure of Eq. 10‚Äì7. The r-component of the Euler equation in cylindrical coordinates (see Eq. 9‚Äì62b without the viscous terms) reduces to

 

Modified pressure: P π = P + œÅgz (Eq. 10‚Äì7)

 

r–component of the incompressible Navier‚ÄìStokes equation:

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\rho\left(\frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial t}+u_{r} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial r}+\frac{u_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta}-\frac{u_{\theta}^{2}}{r}+u_{z} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial z}\right) \\&\quad=-\frac{\partial P}{\partial r}+\rho g_{r}+\mu\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial r}\right)-\frac{u_{r}}{r^{2}}+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} u_{r}}{\partial \theta^{2}}-\frac{2}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial^{2} u_{r}}{\partial z^{2}}\right]\end{aligned}\) (Eq. 9-62b)

 

r-component of Euler equation: \(\frac{\partial P^{\prime}}{\partial r}=\rho \frac{u_{\theta}^{2}}{r}=\rho \omega^{2} r\) (3)

 

where we have substituted the given value of \(u_{\theta^{.}}\) Since hydrostatic pressure is already included in the modified pressure, P‚Ä≤ is not a function of z. By assumptions 1 and 4, respectively, P‚Ä≤ is also not a function of t or ùúÉ. Thus P‚Ä≤ is a function of r only, and we replace the partial derivative in Eq. 3 with a total derivative. Integration yields

 

Modified pressure field: \(P^{\prime}=\rho \frac{\omega^{2} r^{2}}{2}+B_{1}\) (4)

 

where \(B_{1}\) is a constant of integration. At the origin, modified pressure P‚Ä≤ is equal to actual pressure P, since z = 0 there. Thus, constant \(B_{1}\) is found by applying the known pressure boundary condition at the origin. It turns out therefore that \(B_{1}\) is equal to \(P_{0}\). We now convert Eq. 4 back to actual pressure using Eq. 10‚Äì7, P = P‚Ä≤ – ùúågz,

 

Actual pressure field: \(P=\rho \frac{\omega^{2} r^{2}}{2}+P_{0}-\rho g z\) (5)

 

At the reference datum plane (z = 0), we plot nondimensional pressure as a function of nondimensional radius, where some arbitrary radial location r = R is chosen as a characteristic length scale in the flow (Fig. 10‚Äì21). The pressure distribution is parabolic with respect to r .

Finally, we equate Eqs. 2 and 5 to solve for C,

 

Bernoulli constant as a function of r: \(C=\frac{P_{0}}{\rho}+\omega^{2} r^{2}\) (6)

 

At the origin, \(C=C_{0}=P_{0} / \rho\), which agrees with our previous calculation.

 

Discussion For a fluid in solid body rotation, the Bernoulli constant increases as \(r^{2}\). This is not surprising, since fluid particles move faster at larger values of r, and thus they possess more energy. In fact, Eq. 5 reveals that pressure itself increases as \(r^{2}\). Physically, the pressure gradient in the (inward) radial direction provides the centripetal force necessary to keep fluid particles revolving about the origin.
