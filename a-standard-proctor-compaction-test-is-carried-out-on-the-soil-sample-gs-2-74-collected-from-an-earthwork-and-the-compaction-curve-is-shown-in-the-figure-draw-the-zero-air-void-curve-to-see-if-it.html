Guest Question.

A standard Proctor compaction test is carried out on the soil sample (\(G_{s}\) = 2.74) collected from an earthwork and the compaction curve is shown in the figure. Draw the zero air void curve to see if it intersects the compaction curve.

The compaction specifications require that the earthwork be compacted to a relative compaction of at least 95% with respect to the standard Proctor compaction test, and that the water content be within \(\pm 1 \frac{1}{2} \%\) of the optimum water content. A field density test was later carried out to check the quality of compaction. A hole was dug in the compacted earthwork and 957 g soil was removed. The volume of the hole, as measured through a sand cone test, was 450 \(cm ^{3}\). A 26.3 g soil sample that was removed from the hole was then dried in the oven and had a mass of 22.8 g. Does the compaction meet the specifications?

Admin
Solution

Let’s use Equation 4.1 with \(G_{s}=2.74\) to locate a few points for the zero air void curve. This gives:

 

\(\rho_{d}=\frac{G_{s} \rho_{w}}{1+\frac{w G_{s}}{S}}\) (4.1)

 

\(\rho_{d}=2.74 /(1+2.74 w)\)

 

on the zero air void curve. Substituting w (%) = 17, 18, 19, and 20 in this equation gives \(\rho_{d}\left( t / m ^{3}\right)=1.87\), 1.84, 1.80, and 1.77. Plotting these four points on the above plot shows that the compaction curve fully lies to the left of the zero air void curve.

From the laboratory:

 

\(o w c_{ lab }=16.0 \% \text { and } \rho_{d, \max }=1.81 g / cm ^{3} \text { or } t / m ^{3} \text { (see figure) }\)

 

\(w_{\text {field }}=\frac{26.3-22.8}{22.8} \times 100=15.35 \%\)

 

and

 

\(\rho_{m, \text { field }}=\frac{957}{450}=2.13 g / cm ^{3}\)

 

\(\therefore \rho_{d, \text { field }}=\frac{2.13}{1+0.1535}=1.84 g / cm ^{3}\)

 

\(\therefore \text { Relative compaction }=\frac{1.84}{1.81} \times 100=102 \%\)

 

Specifications: \(\text { (a) } R \geq 95 \% \text { (standard Proctor) and (b) } 14.5 \% \leq w_{\text {field }} \leq 17.5 \%\)

The control test shows that the compaction meets the specifications with respect to both water content and relative compaction.
