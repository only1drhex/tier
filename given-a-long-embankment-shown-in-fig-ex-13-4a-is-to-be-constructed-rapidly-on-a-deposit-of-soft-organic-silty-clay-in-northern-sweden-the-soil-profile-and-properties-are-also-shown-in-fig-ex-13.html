Guest Question.

Given:

A long embankment shown in Fig. Ex. 13.4a is to be constructed rapidly on a deposit of soft organic silty clay in northern Sweden. The soil profile and properties are also shown in Fig. Ex. 13.4a. Assume \(K_{o}=0.6\). Also assume A before failure is about 0.35; at failure, \(A_{f}=0.5\) (after Holtz and Holm, 1979).

Required:

Determine the TSP, \(\left( T -u_{o}\right) SP\), and ESP for a typical element 5 m below the centerline of the embankment.

Admin
Solution

First, calculate the initial stress conditions for the element. Use Eqs. (6.13), (6.14), and (6.15).

 

\(\text { free swell }=\frac{(\text { final volume) }-\text { (initial volume) }}{\text { initial volume }} \times 100(\%)\) (6.13)

 

\(\sigma=\sigma^{\prime}+u\) (6.14)

 

\(\sigma_{v}=\int_{0}^{h} \rho g d z\) (6.15a)

 

\(\sigma_{v}=\rho g h\) (6.15b)

 

\(\sigma_{v}=\sum_{i=1}^{n} \rho_{i} g z_{i}\) (6.15c)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\sigma_{v o}=1.24(9.81)(1)+1.30(9.81)(4) &=63 kPa \\u_{o}=1.0(9.81)(4) &=39 kPa\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\begin{array}{l}\sigma_{v o}^{\prime}=\sigma_{v o}-u_{o}&=24kPa \\\sigma_{h o}^{\prime}=0.6 \sigma_{v o}^{\prime}(K=0.6)&=14 kPa \\\sigma_{h o}=\sigma_{h o}^{\prime}+u_{o}&=53 kPa\end{array}\)

 

Second, calculate the \(\Delta \sigma\) due to the embankment.

 

\(\Delta \sigma \text { at the surface }=2.1(9.81)(2.75)=57 kPa\)

 

\(\sigma_{z} \text { at }-5 m\); use Fig.10.6,

 

\(\begin{aligned}I &=0.45 \times 2=0.9 \\\sigma_{z} &=0.9 \times 57=51 kPa\end{aligned}\)

 

This is \(\Delta \sigma_{v}\) on the typical element.

To determine the increase in horizontal stress \(\Delta \sigma_{h}\), equations and some charts are available for a limited number of geometries (see, for example, Poulos and Davis, 1974). For this example, let’s assume the increase in horizontal stress is one-third of the increase in vertical stress.

 

\(\Delta \sigma_{h}=0.33(51)=17 kPa\)

 

Next, use Eqs. (13.1) and (13.2) to determine q, p, and \(p^{\prime}\) for both initial and final conditions. Don’t forget the conditions for the \(\left( T -u_{o}\right) SP\). To get the final effective stresses, we need to estimate the induced pore pressures. Use the pore pressure parameter information given. Assume initially that the soil is not stressed to failure, so A = 0.35 . B = 1 below the water table. Use Eq. (12.19).

 

\(\Delta u=B \Delta \sigma_{3}+\bar{A}\left(\Delta \sigma_{1}-\Delta \sigma_{3}\right)\) (12.19)

 

\(q=\frac{\sigma_{v}-\sigma_{h}}{2}\) (13.1)

 

\(p=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\sigma_{h}}{2}\) (13.2)

 

\(\Delta u=\Delta \sigma_{3}+A\left(\Delta \sigma_{1}-\Delta \sigma_{3}\right)=17+0.35(51-17)=29 kPa\)

 

If the embankment were overstressing the underlying soil, then the induced \(\Delta u\) would be 34 kPa (because \(A_{f}=0.5\)).

It is sometimes helpful when calculating stress paths to draw elements with the appropriate total, total –\(u_{o}\), pore pressure, and effective stresses indicated (similar to Fig. 12.27). This technique is shown in Fig. Ex. 13.4b both for initial conditions and after loading. Note that stresses on the elements for initial conditions are those we calculated at the beginning of this example. For final stresses, the vertical total stress increased by 51 kPa and the horizontal total stress increased by 17 kPa, as we determined previously from elastic theory. The induced pore pressures shown are those we found from the pore pressure equation. The calculations for p, \(p^{\prime}\), and q for both initial and final conditions are shown below the elements.

Finally, plot the stress paths on the p-q diagram, as shown in Fig. Ex. 13.4c. Sketch the ESP so it has a shape similar to those shown previously [for example, Figs. 13.13 and 13.14(a)] for normally consolidated clays.
