Centroid of a Volume Using Integration

Determine the position of the centroid for the solid hemisphere of radius r shown.

Admin
Solution

Road Map Because the object is symmetric about both the \(xy\) and \(xz\) planes, the centroid lies on the \(x\) axis and we can conclude that \(\overline{y} = \overline{z} = 0\). To determine the x position of the centroid, we will evaluate the expression for \(\overline{x}\) in Eq. (\(\overline{x} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dV} }{\int{ dV}} , \overline{y} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{y} dV} }{\int{ dV}} , \overline{z} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{z} dV} }{\int{ dV}}\)

where \(\overline{x}, \widetilde{y}\) and \(\widetilde{z}\) are the locations of the centroid of volume element dV measured from the origin of the coordinate system. ) on p. 399. Expressions for dV and \(\widetilde{x}\) are needed, and these can be developed using either a thin disk volume element or a thin shell volume element, and both of these approaches are shown.

Solution 1 â€“ thin disk volume element

Governing Equations & Computation Expressions for dV and \(\widetilde{x}\) can be written using the thin disk volume element shown in Fig. 2 as follows:

\(dV = \pi y^2 dx = \pi(r^2 – x^2) dx\), (1)

\(\widetilde{x} = x\). (2)

In Eq. (1), \(\pi y^2\) is the area of a disk where y is the radius and dx is the thickness. The entire disk element is at the same x position, so that \(\widetilde{x} = x\). Substituting Eqs. (1) and (2) into the first expression of Eq. (\(\overline{x} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dV} }{\int{ dV}} , \overline{y} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{y} dV} }{\int{ dV}} , \overline{z} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{z} dV} }{\int{ dV}}\)

where \(\overline{x}, \widetilde{y}\) and \(\widetilde{z}\) are the locations of the centroid of volume element dV measured from the origin of the coordinate system.) provides the x position of the centroid as

\(\overline{x} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dV} }{\int{ dV}} = \frac{\int\limits_{0}^{r}{x \pi(r^2 – x^2) dx} }{\int\limits_{0}^{r}{\pi(r^2 – x^2) dx}} = \frac{\pi (r^2 \frac{x^2}{2} – \frac{x^4}{4})|^r_0}{\pi (r^2 x – \frac{x^3}{3})|^r_0} = \frac{\frac{\pi r^4}{4}}{\frac{2\pi r^3}{3}} = \frac{3r}{8}\). (3)

Solution 2 â€“ thin shell volume element

Governing Equations & Computation Expressions for dV and \(\widetilde{x}\) can be written using the thin shell volume element shown in Fig. 3 as follows:

\(dV = 2\pi y x dy = 2\pi y dy \sqrt{r^2 – y^2}\) (4)

\(\widetilde{x} = \frac{x}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{r^2 – y^2}\) (5)

In Eq. (4), \(2\pi y\) is the circumference of the shell where y is the radius, x is the length of the shell, and dy is the thickness. In Eq. (5), the x position of the centroid of the shell element \(\widetilde{x}\), is given by x/2. Substituting Eqs. (4) and (5) into the first expression of Eq. (\(\overline{x} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dV} }{\int{ dV}} , \overline{y} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{y} dV} }{\int{ dV}} , \overline{z} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{z} dV} }{\int{ dV}}\)

where \(\overline{x}, \widetilde{y}\) and \(\widetilde{z}\) are the locations of the centroid of volume element dV measured from the origin of the coordinate system. ) provides the x position of the centroid as

\(\overline{x} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dV} }{\int{ dV}} = \frac{\int\limits_{0}^{r}{\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{r^2 – y^2}}2\pi y \sqrt{r^2 – y^2}dy }{\int\limits_{0}^{r}{2\pi y \sqrt{r^2 – y^2}dy }} = \frac{3r}{8}\). (6)

Discussion & Verification As expected, the solutions for \(\overline{x}\) obtained by using the two different volume elements agree. Also, the result for the denominator of Eq. (3), namely, \(2\pi r^3/3\), is the correct volume of a hemisphere (reported in the Table of Properties of Solids on the inside back cover).
