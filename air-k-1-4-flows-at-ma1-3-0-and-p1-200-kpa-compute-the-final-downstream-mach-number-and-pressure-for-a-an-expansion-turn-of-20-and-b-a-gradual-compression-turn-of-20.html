Guest Question.

Air (k = 1.4) flows at \(Ma_1\) = 3.0 and \(p_1\) = 200 kPa. Compute the final downstream Mach number and pressure for (a) an expansion turn of 20° and (b) a gradual compression turn of 20°.

Admin
Solution

Part (a)

The isentropic stagnation pressure is

\(p_0 = p_1 [1 + 0.2(3.0)^2]^{3.5} = 7347 kPa\)

and this will be the same at the downstream point. For \(Ma_1\) = 3.0 we find from Table B.5 or Eq. (9.99) that \(\omega_1\) = 49.757°. The flow expands to a new condition such that

\(\omega (Ma) = K^{1/2} \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{Ma^2 – 1}{K}\right)^{1/2} – \tan^{-1} (Ma^2 – 1)^{1/2}\) (9.99)

\(\omega_2 = \omega_1 + \Delta \omega = 49.757^{\circ} + 20^{\circ} = 69.757^{\circ}\)

EES

Inversion of Eq. (9.99), to find Ma when \(\omega\) is given, is impossible without iteration. Once again, our friend EES easily handles Eq. (9.99) with four statements (angles specified in degrees):

k = 1.4

C = ((k + 1)/(k – 1))^0.5

Omega = 69.757

Omega = C*ARCTAN((Ma^2 – 1)^0.5/C) – ARCTAN((Ma^2 – 1)^0.5)

Specify that Ma \(\gt\) 1, and EES readily reports an accurate result:\(^6\)

\(Ma_2\) = 4.32

\(^6\)The author saves these little programs for further use, giving them names such as Prandtl-Meyer.

The isentropic pressure at this new condition is

\(p_2 = \frac{p_0}{[1 + 0.2(4.32)^2]^{3.5}} = \frac{7347}{230.1} = 31.9 kPa\)

Part (b)

The flow compresses to a lower Prandtl-Meyer angle:

\(\omega_2 = 49.757^{\circ} – 20^{\circ} = 29.757^{\circ}\)

Again from Eq. (9.99), Table B.5, or EES we compute that

\(Ma_2\) = 2.125

\(p_2 = \frac{p_0}{[1 + 0.2(2.125)^2]^{3.5}} = \frac{7347}{9.51} = 773 kPa\)

Similarly, we compute density and temperature changes by noticing that \(T_0 and \rho_0\) are constant for isentropic flow.
