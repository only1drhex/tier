Guest Question.

Common Ion Effect in Chemical Reaction of Electrolytes

The solubility of silver chloride in water at 25ºC is 1.273 × \(10^{-5}\) kmol/\(m ^{3}\), and that of thallium chloride is 0.144 kmol/\(m ^{3}\). Estimate the simultaneous solubility of AgCl and TlCl in water.

Admin
Solution

The first step in the calculation is to compute the constants \(K_{ AgCl }^{\circ} \text { and } K_{ TlCl }^{\circ}\) using the solubility data for each of the pure salts. Recognizing that \(\nu_{ Ag }=\nu_{ Tl }=\nu_{ Cl }=1 \text { and that } z_{ Ag }=z_{ Tl }=+1\) and \(z_{ Cl }=-1\), we write for the pure salts

 

\(\ln K_{ AgCl }^{\circ}=\ln \left(C_{ Ag } C_{ Cl }\right)+2 \ln \gamma_{\pm}=2 \ln C_{ AgCl }+2 \ln \gamma_{\pm}\) (a)

 

and

 

\(\ln K_{ TlCl }^{\circ}=\ln \left(C_{ Tl } C_{ Cl }\right)+2 \ln \gamma_{\pm}=2 \ln C_{ TlCl }+2 \ln \gamma_{\pm}\) (b)

 

Also, for the pure salts, I(silver chloride) = 1.273 × \(10^{-5}\) kmol/\(m ^{3}\) and I(thallium chloride) = 0.144 kmol/\(m ^{3}\). Because of the high ionic strengths, Eq. 9.10-18 will be used to compute γ±; that is,

 

\(\ln \gamma_{\pm}=-\frac{\alpha\left|z_{+} z_{-}\right| \sqrt{I}}{1+\beta a \sqrt{I}}+\delta I\) (9.10-18)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\ln \gamma_{\pm} &=\frac{-1.178\left|z_{+} z_{-}\right| \sqrt{I}}{1+\sqrt{I}}+0.1\left|z_{+} z_{-}\right| I \\&=\frac{-1.178 \sqrt{I}}{1+\sqrt{I}}+0.1 I\end{aligned}\) (c)

 

Using Eq. c and the experimental solubility data in Eqs. a and b yields

 

\(K_{ AgCl }^{\circ}=1.607 \times 10^{-10}\left( kmol / m ^{3}\right)^{2}\)

 

and

 

\(K_{ TlCl }^{\circ}=1.116 \times 10^{-2}\left( kmol / m ^{3}\right)^{2}\)

 

To find the simultaneous solubility of silver chloride and thallium chloride, we must solve the equations

 

\(\ln \left(C_{ Ag } C_{ Cl }\right)=\ln K_{ AgCl }^{\circ}-2 \ln \gamma_{\pm}\)

 

and

 

\(\ln \left(C_{ Tl } C_{ Cl }\right)=\ln K_{ TlCl }^{\circ}-2 \ln \gamma_{\pm}\)

 

or, using Eq. c, the equations

 

\(\begin{array}{l}1.607 \times 10^{-10} \exp \left[\frac{2.356 \sqrt{I}}{1+\sqrt{I}}-0.2 I\right]=C_{ Ag } C_{ Cl }\\1.116 \times 10^{-2} \exp \left[\frac{2.356 \sqrt{I}}{1+\sqrt{I}}-0.2 I\right]=C_{ Tl } C_{ Cl }\end{array}\)

 

where

 

\(I=\frac{1}{2}\left(C_{ Ag }+C_{ Tl }+C_{ Cl }\right)=C_{ AgCl }+C_{ TlCl }\)

 

since \(C_{ Ag }+C_{ Tl }=C_{ Cl }\). These nonlinear equations can be solved by trial and error or using an equation-solving program.

Because of the low solubility of silver chloride, as a first guess, we will assume that the solubility of thallium chloride is unaffected by the presence of silver chloride, and that the ionization of silver chloride has little effect on either the ionic strength I or the total chloride ion concentration. In this case we have

 

\(C_{ Tl }=C_{ Cl }=0.144 kmol / m ^{3}\)

 

\(I=0.144 kmol / m ^{3} \quad \text { and } \quad \sqrt{I}=0.3975\left( kmol / m ^{3}\right)^{1 / 2}\)

 

The silver ion concentration (and the solubility of silver chloride) can then be computed from

 

\(1.607 \times 10^{-10} \exp \left[\frac{2.356 \times 0.3975}{1.3975}-0.2 \times 0.144\right]=C_{ Ag } \times 0.144\)

 

so that

 

\(C_{ Ag }=C_{ AgCl }=2.119 \times 10^{-9} kmol / m ^{3}\)

 

Therefore, as we assumed, AgCl is so slightly soluble in the aqueous TlCl solution that neither the ionic strength nor the solubility of thallium chloride is greatly affected by its presence. On the other hand, owing to the common ion effect, the solubility of silver chloride is reduced by almost four orders of magnitude from its value when only AgCl is present.

Comment

If, instead of thallium chloride, some other salt that does not have a common ion with AgCl (for example, NaNO3) had been introduced into the aqueous solution, the solubility of silver chloride would have increased because of the ionic-strength dependence of the ionic activity coefficient. Thus, if NaNO3 were present in the same concentration as, but instead of, thallium chloride, we would have

 

\(C_{ Ag } C_{ Cl }=1.607 \times 10^{-10} \exp \left\{\frac{2.356 \times 0.3975}{1.3975}-0.2 \times 0.144\right\}=C_{ Ag }^{2}=C_{ Cl }^{2}\)

 

or

 

\(C_{ Ag }=C_{ Cl }=C_{ AgCl }=1.75 \times 10^{-5} kmol / m ^{3}\)

 

which is an increase of 37 percent in the solubility of silver chloride over the value when only AgCl is present.
