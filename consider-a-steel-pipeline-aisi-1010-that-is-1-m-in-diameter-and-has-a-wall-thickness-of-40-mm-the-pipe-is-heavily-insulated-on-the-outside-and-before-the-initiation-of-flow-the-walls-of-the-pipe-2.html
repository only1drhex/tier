Guest Question.

Consider a steel pipeline (AISI 1010) that is 1 m in diameter and has a wall thickness of 40 mm. The pipe is heavily insulated on the outside, and, before the initiation of flow, the walls of the pipe are at a uniform temperature of -20 °C. With the initiation of flow,hot oil at 60 °C is pumped through the pipe, creating a convective condition corresponding to h = 500 W/m² . K at the inner surface of the pipe.
1. What are the appropriate Biot and Fourier numbers 8 min after the initiation of flow?
2. At t = 8 min, what is the temperature of the exterior pipe surface covered by the insulation?
3. What is the heat flux \(q^{\prime \prime }(W/m^{2} )\) to the pipe from the oil at t = 8 min?
4. How much energy per meter of pipe length has been transferred from the oil to the pipe at t = 8 min?

Admin
Solution

Known: Wall subjected to sudden change in convective surface condition.

Find:
1. Biot and Fourier numbers after 8 min.
2. Temperature of exterior pipe surface after 8 min.
3. Heat flux to the wall at 8 min.
4. Energy transferred to pipe per unit length after 8 min.

Assumptions:
1. Pipe wall can be approximated as plane wall, since thickness is much less than diameter.
2. Constant properties.
3. Outer surface of pipe is adiabatic.

Properties: , steel type AISI 1010 \( [T = (-20+ 60) °C/2 ≈ 300 K]: ρ = 7832 kg/m³ , c = 434 J/kg . K, k = 63.9 W/m. K, α = 18.8 ×10^{-6} m^{2}/s. \)

Analysis:
1. At t = 8 min, the Biot and Fourier numbers are computed from Equations 5.10 \( B i = \frac{h L_{c}}{k} \lt 0.1\) and 5.12 \(F o \equiv \frac{\alpha t}{L^{2}_{c}}\), respectively, with \(L_{c} = L\). Hence

\( B i = \frac{h L}{k} = \frac{500 W/m^{2} . K ×0.04 m}{63.9 W/m . K} = 0.313 \)

 

\( Fo =\frac{\alpha t}{L^{2}} = \frac{18.8 ×10^{-6} m^{2}/s ×8 min × 60 s/min}{(0.04 m)^{2}}= 5.64 \)

2. With Bi = 0.313, use of the lumped capacitance method is inappropriate. However, since Fo > 0.2 and transient conditions in the insulated pipe wall of thickness L correspond to those in a plane wall of thickness 2L experiencing the same surface condition, the desired results may be obtained from the one-term approximation for a plane wall. The midplane temperature can be determined from Equation 5.44

\( \theta ^{*}_{o} =\frac{T_{o} – T_{\infty }}{T_{i} – T_{\infty }} =C_{1} exp(-\zeta ^{2}_{1} Fo)\)

where, with Bi = 0.313, \(C_{1}\) = 1.047 and \(\zeta _{1}\)= 0.531 rad from Table 5.1. With Fo = 5.64,

\( \theta ^{*}_{o} =1.047 exp[-(0.531 rad )^{2} ×5.64] = 0.214\)

Hence after 8 min, the temperature of the exterior pipe surface, which corresponds to the midplane temperature of a plane wall, is

\(T(0, 8 min) = T_{∞} +\theta ^{*}_{o} (T_{i} – T_{∞} ) = 60 °C + 0.214(-20 – 60) °C = 42.9 °C \)

3. Heat transfer to the inner surface at x = L is by convection, and at any time t the heat flux may be obtained from Newton’s law of cooling. Hence at t = 480 s,

\(q^{\prime \prime }_{x}(L, 480 s) \equiv q^{\prime \prime }_{L} =h\left[T(L, 480 s) – T_{\infty } \right]\)

Using the one-term approximation for the surface temperature, Equation 5.43b \( \theta ^{*} =\theta ^{*}_{o} \cos (\zeta _{1} x^{*} ) \) with \(x^{*} \)= 1 has the form

\( \theta ^{*} =\theta ^{*}_{o} \cos (\zeta _{1} ) \)

 

\(T(L, t) = T_{\infty } + (T_{i} – T_{\infty })\theta ^{*}_{o} cos (\zeta _{1} )\)

 

\( T(L, 8 min) = 60 °C + (-20 – 60) °C × 0.214 × cos(0.531 rad) \)

 

T(L, 8 min) = 45.2 °C

The heat flux at t = 8 min is then

\( q^{\prime \prime }_{L} = 500 W/m^{2} . K (45.2 – 60) °C =-7400 W/m^{2} \)

4. The energy transfer to the pipe wall over the 8-min interval may be obtained from Equations 5.47 \( Q_{o}= \rho cV(T_{i} – T_{\infty })\) and 5.49 . With

\( \frac{Q}{Q_{o}} = 1- \frac{\sin (\zeta _{1})}{\zeta _{1}} \theta ^{*}_{o}\)

 

\( \frac{Q}{Q_{o}} = 1- \frac{\sin (0.531 rad)}{0.531 rad} × 0.214 = 0.80\)

it follows that

\( Q= 0.80 \rho cV(T_{i} – T_{\infty })\)

or with a volume per unit pipe length of \(V^{\prime}= πDL \),

\(Q^{\prime} = 0.80 \rho c πDL(T_{i} – T_{\infty })\)

 

\( Q^{\prime} = 0.80 ×7832 kg/m^{3}× 434 J/kg . K× π× 1 m ×0.04 m (-20 – 60) °C\)

 

\(Q^{\prime} = -2.73 × 10^{7} J/m \)

Comments:
1. The minus sign associated with \(q^{\prime \prime }\) and \( Q^{\prime}\) simply implies that the direction of heat transfer is from the oil to the pipe (into the pipe wall).
2. The solution for this example is provided as a ready-to-solve model in the Advanced section of IHT, which uses the Models, Transient Conduction, Plane Wall option. Since the IHT model uses a multiple-term approximation to the series solution, the results are more accurate than those obtained from the foregoing one-term approximation. IHT Models for Transient Conduction are also provided for the radial systems treated in Section 5.6.
