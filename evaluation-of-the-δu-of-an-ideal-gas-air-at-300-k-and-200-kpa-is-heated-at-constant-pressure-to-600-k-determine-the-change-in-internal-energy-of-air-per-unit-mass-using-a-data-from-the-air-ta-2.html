Guest Question.

Evaluation of the Δu of an Ideal Gas

Air at 300 K and 200 kPa is heated at constant pressure to 600 K. Determine the change in internal energy of air per unit mass, using (a) data from the air table (), (b) the functional form of the specific heat (), and (c) the average specific heat value ().

Admin
Solution

The internal energy change of air is to be determined in three different ways.

Assumptions At specified conditions, air can be considered to be an ideal gas since it is at a high temperature and low pressure relative to its critical point values.

Analysis The internal energy change Δu of ideal gases depends on the initial and final temperatures only, and not on the type of process. Thus, the following solution is valid for any kind of process.

(a) One way of determining the change in internal energy of air is to read the \(u\) values at \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) from and take the difference:

 

\(\begin{aligned}&u_{1}=u_{@ 300 \mathrm{~K}}=214.07 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \\&u_{2}=u_{@ 600 \mathrm{~K}}=434.78 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\end{aligned}\)

Thus,

\(\Delta u=u_{2}-u_{1}=(434.78-214.07) \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{kg}=220.71 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\)

(b) The \(\bar{c}_{p}(T)\) of air is given in in the form of a third-degree polynomial expressed as

\(\bar{c}_{p}(T)=a+b T+c T^{2}+d T^{3}\)

where \(a=28.11, b=0.1967 \times 10^{-2}, c=0.4802 \times 10^{-5}\), and \(d=-1.966 \times 10^{-9}\). From Eq. 4-30,

\(\bar{c}_{v}(T)=\bar{c}_{p}-R_{u}=\left(a-R_{u}\right)+b T+c T^{2}+d T^{3}\)

From Eq. 4-25,

\(\Delta \bar{u}=\int_{1}^{2} \bar{c}_{v}(T) d T=\int_{T_{1}}^{T_{2}}\left[\left(a-R_{u}\right)+b T+c T^{2}+d T^{3}\right] d T\)

Performing the integration and substituting the values, we obtain

\(\Delta \bar{u}=6447 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kmol}\)

The change in the internal energy on a unit-mass basis is determined by dividing this value by the molar mass of air ):

\(\Delta u=\frac{\Delta \bar{u}}{M}=\frac{6447 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kmol}}{28.97 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{kmol}}=222.5 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\)

which differs from the tabulated value by 0.8 percent.
(c) The average value of the constant-volume specific heat \(c_{v, \text { avg }}\) is determined from at the average temperature of \(\left(T_{1}+T_{2}\right) / 2=450 \mathrm{~K}\) to be

\(c_{v, \text { avg }}=c_{v @ 450 \mathrm{~K}}=0.733 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\)

Thus,

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta u &=c_{v, \text { avg }}\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)=(0.733 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K})[(600-300) \mathrm{K}] \\&=220 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\end{aligned}\)

 

Discussion This answer differs from the tabulated value \((220.71 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg})\) by only 0.4 percent. This close agreement is not surprising since the assumption that \(c_{v}\) varies linearly with temperature is a reasonable one at temperature intervals of only a few hundred degrees. If we had used the \(c_{v}\) value at \(T_{1}=300 \mathrm{~K}\) instead of at \(T_{\text {avg }}\), the result would be \(215.4 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\), which is in error by about 2 percent. Errors of this magnitude are acceptable for most engineering purposes.
