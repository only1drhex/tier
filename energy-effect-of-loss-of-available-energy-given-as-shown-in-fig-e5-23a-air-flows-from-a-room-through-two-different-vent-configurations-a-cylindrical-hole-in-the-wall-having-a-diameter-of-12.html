Guest Question.

Energy—Effect of Loss of Available Energy

GIVEN As shown in Fig. E5.23a, air flows from a room through two different vent configurations: a cylindrical hole in the wall having a diameter of 120 mm and the same diameter cylindrical hole in the wall but with a well-rounded entrance. The room pressure is held constant at 1.0 kPa above atmospheric pressure. Both vents exhaust into the atmosphere. As discussed in Section 8.4.2, the loss in available energy associated with flow through the cylindrical vent from the room to the vent exit is \(0.5 V_{2}^{2} / 2 \text { where } V_{2}\) is the uniformly distributed exit velocity of air. The loss in available energy associated with flow through the rounded entrance vent from the room to the vent exit is \(0.05 V_{2}^{2} / 2, \text { where } V_{2}\) is the uniformly distributed exit velocity of air.

FIND Compare the volume flowrates associated with the two different vent configurations.

Admin
Solution

We use the control volume for each vent sketched in Fig. E5.23a. What is sought is the flowrate, \(Q=A_{2} V_{2}, \text { where } A_{2}\) is the vent exit cross-sectional area, and \(V_{2}\) is the uniformly distributed exit velocity. For both vents, application of Eq. 5.79 leads to

 

\(\frac{p_{\text {out }}}{\rho}+\frac{V_{\text {out }}^{2}}{2}+g z_{\text {cut }}=\frac{p_{\text {in }}}{\rho}+\frac{V_{\text {in }}^{2}}{2}+g z_{\text {in }}-\text { loss }\) (5.79)

 

 

where \({ }_{1} \operatorname{loss}_{2}\) is the loss between sections (1) and (2). Solving Eq. 1 for \(V_{2}\) we get

 

\(V_{2}=\sqrt{2\left[\left(\frac{p_{1}-p_{2}}{\rho}\right)-{ }_{1} \operatorname{loss}_{2}\right]}\) (2)

 

Since

 

\({ }_{1} \operatorname{loss}_{2}=K_{L} \frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2}\) (3)

 

where \(K_{L} \text { is the loss coefficient }\left(K_{L}=0.5\right. \text { and }\) 0.05 for the two vent configurations involved), we can combine Eqs. 2 and 3 to get

 

\(V_{2}=\sqrt{2\left[\left(\frac{p_{1}-p_{2}}{\rho}\right)-K_{L} \frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2}\right]}\) (4)

 

Solving Eq. 4 for \(V_{2}\) we obtain

 

\(V_{2}=\sqrt{\frac{p_{1}-p_{2}}{\rho\left[\left(1+K_{L}\right) / 2\right]}}\) (5)

 

Therefore, for flowrate, Q, we obtain

 

\(Q=A_{2} V_{2}=\frac{\pi D_{2}^{2}}{4} \sqrt{\frac{p_{1}-p_{2}}{\rho\left[\left(1+K_{L}\right) / 2\right]}}\) (6)

 

For the rounded entrance cylindrical vent, Eq. 6 gives

 

\(\begin{aligned}Q &=\frac{\pi(120 mm )^{2}}{4(1000 mm / m )^{2}} \\& \times \sqrt{\frac{(1.0 kPa )(1000 Pa / kPa )\left[1\left( N / m ^{2}\right) /( Pa )\right]}{\left(1.23 kg / m ^{3}\right)[(1+0.05) / 2]\left[1\left( N \cdot s ^{2}\right) /( kg \cdot m )\right]}}\end{aligned}\)

 

or

 

\(Q=0.445 m ^{3} / s\) (Ans)

 

For the cylindrical vent, Eq. 6 gives us

 

\(\begin{aligned}Q &=\frac{\pi(120 mm )^{2}}{4(1000 mm / m )^{2}} \\& \times \sqrt{\frac{(1.0 kPa )(1000 Pa / kPa )\left[1\left( N / m ^{2}\right) /( Pa )\right]}{\left(1.23 kg / m ^{3}\right)[(1+0.5) / 2]\left[1\left( N \cdot s ^{2}\right) /( kg \cdot m )\right]}}\end{aligned}\)

 

or

 

\(Q=0.372 m ^{3} / s\) (Ans)

 

COMMENT By repeating the calculations for various values of the loss coefficient, \(K_{L}\), the results shown in Fig. E5.23b are obtained. Note that the rounded entrance vent allows the passage of more air than does the cylindrical vent because the loss associated with the rounded entrance vent is less than that for the cylindrical one. For this flow the pressure drop, \(p_{1}-p_{2}\), has two purposes: (1) overcome the loss associated with the flow, and (2) produce the kinetic energy at the exit. Even if there were no loss (i.e., \(K_{L}\) = 0), a pressure drop would be needed to accelerate the fluid through the vent.
