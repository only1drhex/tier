Guest Question.

Energy Required to Pressurize a Liquid
Compute the minimum work required and the heat that must be removed to isothermally pump liquid ethanol from 20째C and 1 bar to 1000 bar in a continuous process.

Admin
Solution

Consider the pump and its contents to be the system around which balance equations are to be written.

 

The steady-state mass balance (on a molar basis) is

 

\(\frac{d N}{d t}=0=\dot{N}_{1}+\dot{N}_{2} \quad \text { or } \quad \dot{N}_{2}=-\dot{N}_{1}=-\dot{N}\)

 

The steady-state energy balance is

 

\(\frac{d U}{d t}=0=\dot{N}_{1} \underline{H}_{1}+\dot{N}_{2} \underline{H}_{2}+\dot{Q}+\dot{W}\)

 

or

 

\(\frac{\dot{W}}{\dot{N}}=-\frac{\dot{Q}}{\dot{N}}+\left(\underline{H}_{2}-\underline{H}_{1}\right)\)

 

 

The steady-state entropy balance (with \(\dot{S}_{\text {gen }}=0\) for minimum work) is

 

 

\(\frac{d S}{d t}=0=\dot{N}_{1} \underline{S}_{1}+\dot{N}_{2} \underline{S}_{2}+\frac{\dot{Q}}{T}\)

 

or

 

\(\frac{\dot{Q}}{\dot{N}}=T\left(\underline{S}_{2}-\underline{S}_{1}\right)\)

 

From the previous illustrations, we have

 

\(\underline{S}_{2}-\underline{S}_{1}=\underline{S}\left(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 1000 \mathrm{bar}\right)-\underline{S}\left(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 1 \mathrm{bar}\right)=-6.534 \mathrm{~J} /(\mathrm{mol} \mathrm{K})\)

 

and

 

\(\underline{H}_{2}-\underline{H}_{1}=\underline{H}\left(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 1000 \mathrm{bar}\right)-\underline{H}\left(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 1 \mathrm{bar}\right)=3919 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol}\)

 

Therefore,

 

\(\frac{\dot{Q}}{\dot{N}}=293.15 \mathrm{~K} \times(-6.534) \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol} \mathrm{K}}=-1915 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol}}\)

 

and

 

\(\frac{\dot{W}}{\dot{N}}=1915+3919=5834 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol}}\)

 

COMMENT

If we neglected the effect of thermal expansion, that is, set 慣 = 0, then from Eq. 6.4-33,

 

\(\underline{H}(T, P+\Delta P)-\underline{H}(T, P)=\underline{V}[1-T \alpha] \Delta P=\underline{V} \Delta P\)

 

and from Eq. 6.4-36,

 

\(\underline{S}(T, P+\Delta P)-\underline{S}(T, P)=-\alpha \underline{V} \Delta P=0\)

 

Then, for the problem here,

 

\(\underline{H}\left(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 1000 \mathrm{bar}\right)-\underline{H}\left(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 1 \mathrm{bar}\right)=58.40 \frac{\mathrm{cc}}{\mathrm{mol}} \times 999 \mathrm{bar} \times \frac{1 \mathrm{~J}}{10 \mathrm{cc} \mathrm{bar}}=5834 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol}}\)

 

and

 

\(\underline{S}\left(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 1000 \mathrm{bar}\right)-\underline{S}\left(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, 1 \mathrm{bar}\right)=0\)

 

so that

 

\(\frac{\dot{Q}}{\dot{N}}=0 \quad \text { and } \quad \frac{\dot{W}}{\dot{N}}=5834 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol}}\)

 

Note that the total work is the same as before for this isothermal pressurization (why?), but in this case no heat would have to be removed to keep the liquid at constant temperature.

To pressurize liquid mercury, which is closer to being incompressible than ethanol, over the same conditions one obtains (without making the assumption that 慣 = 0 )

 

\(\frac{\dot{Q}}{\dot{N}}=293.15 \mathrm{~K} \times\left(-0.286 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol} \mathrm{K}}\right)=-78.6 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol}}\)

 

and

 

\(\frac{\dot{W}}{\dot{N}}=78.6+1401 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol}}=1478.6 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{mol}}\)

 

Much less work is needed for this less compressible fluid since very little work is being done to change the volume of the liquid; the work is largely going to change the fluid pressure.
