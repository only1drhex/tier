Guest Question.

Type II, Determine Flowrate

GIVEN The turbine shown in Fig. E8.11 extracts 50 hp from the water flowing through it. The 1-ft-diameter, 300-ft-long pipe is assumed to have a friction factor of 0.02. Minor losses are negligible.

FIND Determine the flowrate through the pipe and turbine.

Admin
Solution

The energy equation (Eq. 8.21) can be applied between the surface of the lake [point (1)] and the outlet of the pipe as

 

\(\frac{p_{1}}{\gamma}+\alpha_{1} \frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 g}+z_{1}=\frac{p_{2}}{\gamma}+\alpha_{2} \frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2 g}+z_{2}+h_{L}\) (8.21)

 

\(\frac{p_{1}}{\gamma}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 g}+z_{1}=\frac{p_{2}}{\gamma}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2 g}+z_{2}+h_{L}+h_{T}\) (1)

 

\(\text { where } h_{T} \text { is the turbine head, } p_{1}=V_{1}=p_{2}=z_{2}=0, z_{1}=90 ft \text {, }\) \(\text { and } V_{2}=V \text {, the fluid velocity in the pipe. The head loss is given by }\)

 

\(h_{L}=f \frac{\ell}{D} \frac{V^{2}}{2 g}=0.02 \frac{(300 ft )}{(1 ft )} \frac{V^{2}}{2\left(32.2 ft / s ^{2}\right)}=0.0932 V^{2} ft\)

 

where V is in ft/s. Also, the turbine head is

 

\(\begin{aligned}h_{T} &=\frac{ P a_{a}}{\gamma Q}=\frac{ P a_{a}}{\gamma(\pi / 4) D^{2} V} \\&=\frac{(50 hp )[(550 ft \cdot lb / s ) / hp ]}{\left(62.4 lb / ft ^{3}\right)\left[(\pi / 4)(1 ft )^{2} V\right]}=\frac{561}{V} ft\end{aligned}\)

 

Thus, Eq. 1 can be written as

 

\(90=\frac{V^{2}}{2(32.2)}+0.0932 V^{2}+\frac{561}{V}\)

 

or

 

\(0.109 V^{3}-90 V+561=0\) (2)

 

where V is in ft/s. The velocity of the water in the pipe is found as the solution of Eq. 2. Surprisingly, there are two real, positive roots: V = 6.58 ft/s or V = 24.9 ft/s. The third root is negative\((V=-31.4 ft / s )\) and has no physical meaning for this flow. Thus, the two acceptable flowrates are

 

\(Q=\frac{\pi}{4} D^{2} V=\frac{\pi}{4}(1 ft )^{2}(6.58 ft / s )=5.17 ft ^{3} / s\)

 

or

 

\(Q=\frac{\pi}{4}(1 ft )^{2}(24.9 ft / s )=19.6 ft ^{3} / s\)

 

COMMENTS Either of these two flowrates gives the same \(\text { power, } P _{a}=\gamma Q h_{T} \text {. }\) The reason for two possible solutions can be seen from the following. With the low flowrate \(\left(Q=5.17 ft ^{3} / s \right)\), we obtain the head loss and turbine head as \(h_{L}=4.04 ft\) and \(h_{T}=85.3 f\)t. Because of the relatively low velocity there is a relatively small head loss and, therefore, a large head available for the turbine. With the large flowrate \(\left(Q=19.6 ft ^{3} / s \right)\), we find \(h_{L}=57.8 ft \text { and } h_{T}=22.5 ft\). The high-speed flow in the pipe produces a relatively large loss due to friction, leaving a relatively small head for the turbine. However, in either case the product of the turbine head times the flowrate is the same. That is, the power extracted \(\left( P _{a}=\gamma Q h_{T}\right)\) is identical for each case. Although either flowrate will allow the extraction of 50 hp from the water, the details of the design of the turbine itself will depend strongly on which flowrate is to be used. Such information can be found in Chapter 12 and various references about turbomachines (Refs. 14, 19, 20).

If the friction factor were not given, the solution to the problem would be much more lengthy. A trial-and-error solution similar to that in would be required along with the solution of a cubic equation.
