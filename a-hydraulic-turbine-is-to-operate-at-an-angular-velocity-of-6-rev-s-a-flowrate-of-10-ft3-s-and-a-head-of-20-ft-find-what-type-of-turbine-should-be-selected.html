Guest Question.

A hydraulic turbine is to operate at an angular velocity of 6 rev/s, a flowrate of 10\({ft^{3}}/{s}\), and a head of 20 ft.

FIND What type of turbine should be selected?

Admin
Solution

The most efficient type of turbine to use can be obtained by calculating the specific speed, \(\grave{N}_{sd} \) and using the information of Fig. 11.24. To use the dimensional form of the specific speed indicated in Fig. 11.24 we must convert the given data into the appropriate units. For the rotor speed we get

\(\omega =6{ rev}/{s}\times 60{s}/{min}=360rpm\)

To estimate the shaft power, we assume all of the available head is converted into power (i.e., \(h_{T}\) = z = 20 ft) and multiply this amount by an assumed efficiency (94%).

\(\dot{W}_{shaft}=\gamma Qz\eta =(62.4{lb}/{ft^{3}})(10 {ft^{3}}/{s})[\frac{20ft(0.94)}{550{ft\cdot lb}/{s\cdot hp}} ]\)

\(\dot{W}_{shaft}=21.3hp\)

Thus, for this turbine,

\(\grave{N}_{sd} =\frac{\omega \sqrt{\dot{W}_{shaft}} }{(h_{T})^{{5}/{4}}} =\frac{(360rpm)\sqrt{21.3hp} }{(20ft)^{{5}/{4}}} =39.3\)

According to the information of Fig. 11.24,

A mixed-flow Francis turbine would probably give the highest efficiency and an assumed efficiency of 0.94 is appropriate.

COMMENT What would happen if we wished to use a Pelton wheel for this application? Note that with only a 20-ft head, the maximum jet velocity, \(V_{1}\), obtainable (neglecting viscous effects) would be

\(V_{1}=\sqrt{2gz}=\sqrt{2\times 32.2{ft}/{s^{2}}\times 20ft}=35.9{ft}/{s} \)

As shown by \(U_{ max power}=\frac{V_{1}}{2} \), for maximum efficiency of a Pelton wheel the jet velocity is ideally two times the blade velocity. Thus, \(V_{1}=2\omega R\), or the wheel diameter, D = 2R, is

\(D=\frac{V_{1}}{\omega }=\frac{35.9{ft}/{s}}{(6 {rev}/{s}\times 2\pi {rad}/{rev})}=0.952ft \)

To obtain a flowrate of \(Q=10{ft^{3}}/{s}\) at a velocity of \(V_{1}\) = 39.5 ft/s, the jet diameter, \(d_{1}\), must be given by

\(Q=\frac{\pi}{4} d_{1}^{2}V_{1}\)

or

\(d_{1}=[\frac{4Q}{\pi V_{1}} ]^{{1}/{2}}=[\frac{4(10{ft^{3}}/{s})}{\pi (35.9{ft}/{s})} ]^{{1}/{2}}=0.596ft\)

A Pelton wheel with a diameter of D = 0.952 ft supplied with water through a nozzle of diameter \(d_{1}\) = 0.596 ft is not a practical design. Typically \(d_{1}\ll D\) (see Fig. 11.17). By using multiple jets it would be possible to reduce the jet diameter. However, even with eight jets, the jet diameter would be 0.211 ft, which is still too large (relative to the wheel diameter) to be practical. Hence, the aforementioned calculations reinforce the results presented in Fig. 11.24â€”a Pelton wheel would not be practical for this application. If the flowrate were considerably smaller, the specific speed could be reduced to the range where a Pelton wheel would be the type to use (rather than a mixed-flow reaction turbine).
