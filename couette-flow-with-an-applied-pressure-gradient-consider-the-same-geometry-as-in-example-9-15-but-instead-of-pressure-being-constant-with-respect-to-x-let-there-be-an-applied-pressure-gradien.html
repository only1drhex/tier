Couette Flow with an Applied Pressure Gradient

Consider the same geometry as in , but instead of pressure being constant with respect to x, let there be an applied pressure gradient in the x-direction (Fig. 9‚Äì63). Specifically, let the pressure gradient in the x-direction, ‚àÇP/‚àÇx, be some constant value given by

 

Applied pressure gradient:

 

\(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}=\frac{P_{2}-P_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}}=\text { constant }\) (1)

 

where \(x_{1} \text { and } x_{2}\) are two arbitrary locations along the x-axis, and \(P_{1} \text { and } p_{2}\) are the pressures at those two locations. Everything else is the same as for . (a) Calculate the velocity and pressure field. (b) Plot a family of velocity profiles in dimensionless form.

Solution

SOLUTION We are to calculate the velocity and pressure field for the flow sketched in Fig. 9‚Äì63 and plot a family of velocity profiles in dimensionless form.

Assumptions The assumptions are identical to those of , except assumption 5 is replaced by the following: A constant pressure gradient is applied in the x-direction such that pressure changes linearly with respect to x according to Eq. 1.

Analysis (a) We follow the same procedure as in . Much of the algebra is identical, so to save space we discuss only the differences.

Step 1 See Fig. 9‚Äì63.

Step 2 Same as except for assumption 5.

Step 3 The continuity equation is simplified in the same way as in ,

Result of continuity: u = u(y) only (2)

The x-momentum equation is simplified in the same manner as in except that the pressure gradient term remains. The result is

 

Result of x–momentum: \(\frac{d^{2} u}{d y^{2}}=\frac{1}{\mu} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\) (3)

 

Likewise, the y-momentum and z-momentum equations simplify to

 

Result of y-momentum: \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}=0\) (4)

and

Result of z-momentum: \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}=-\rho g\) (5)

We cannot convert from a partial derivative to a total derivative in Eq. 5, because P is a function of both x and z in this problem, unlike in where P was a function of z only.

Step 4 We integrate Eq. 3 (x-momentum) twice, noting that ‚àÇP/‚àÇx is a constant,

Integration of x-momentum: \(u=\frac{1}{2 \mu} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} y^{2}+C_{1} y+C_{2}\) (6)

where \(C_{1} \text { and } C_{2}\) are constants of integration. Equation 5 (z-momentum) is integrated once, resulting in

Integration of z-momentum: \(P=-\rho g z+f(x)\) (7)

Note that since P is now a function of both x and z, we add a function of x instead of a constant of integration in Eq. 7. This is a partial integration with respect to z, and we must be careful when performing partial integrations (Fig. 9‚Äì64).

 

Step 5 From Eq. 7, we see that the pressure varies hydrostatically in the z-direction, and we have specified a linear change in pressure in the x-direction. Thus the function f(x) must equal a constant plus ‚àÇP/‚àÇx times x. If we set \(P=P_{0}\) along the line x = 0, z = 0 (the y-axis), Eq. 7 becomes

 

Final result for pressure field: \(P=P_{0}+\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} x-\rho g z\) (8)

We next apply the velocity boundary conditions (1) and (2) from step 2 of to obtain constants \(C_{1} \text { and } C_{2}\)

 

Boundary condition (1): \(u=\frac{1}{2 \mu} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} \times 0+C_{1} \times 0+C_{2}=0 \quad \rightarrow \quad C_{2}=0\)

 

and

 

Boundary condition (2): \(u=\frac{1}{2 \mu} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} h^{2}+C_{1} \times h+0=V \quad \rightarrow \quad C_{1}=\frac{V}{h}-\frac{1}{2 \mu} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} h\)

 

Finally, Eq. 6 becomes

 

\(u=\frac{V y}{h}+\frac{1}{2 \mu} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\left(y^{2}-h y\right)\) (9)

 

Equation 9 indicates that the velocity field consists of the superposition of two parts: a linear velocity profile from u = 0 at the bottom plate to u = V at the top plate, and a parabolic distribution that depends on the magnitude of the applied pressure gradient. If the pressure gradient is zero, the parabolic portion of Eq. 9 disappears and the profile is linear, just as in ; this is sketched as the dashed red line in Fig. 9‚Äì65. If the pressure gradient is negative (pressure decreasing in the x-direction, causing flow to be pushed from left to right), ‚àÇP/‚àÇx < 0 and the velocity profile looks like the one sketched in Fig. 9‚Äì65. A special case is when V = 0 (top plate stationary); the linear portion of Eq. 9 vanishes, and the velocity profile is parabolic and symmetric about the center of the channel (y = h/2); this is sketched as the dotted line in Fig. 9‚Äì65.

Step 6 You can use Eqs. 8 and 9 to verify that all the differential equations and boundary conditions are satisfied.

(b) We use dimensional analysis to generate the dimensionless groups (Œ† groups). We set up the problem in terms of velocity component u as a function of y, h, V, ùúá , and ‚àÇP/‚àÇx. There are six variables (including the dependent variable u), and since there are three primary dimensions represented in the problem (mass, length, and time), we expect 6 – 3 = 3 dimensionless groups. When we pick h, V, and ùúá as our repeating variables, we get the following result using the method of repeating variables (details are left for you to do on your own‚Äîthis is a good review of Chap. 7 material):

 

Result of dimensional analysis: \(\frac{u}{V}=f\left(\frac{y}{h}, \frac{h^{2}}{\mu V} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\right)\) (10)

 

Using these three dimensionless groups, we rewrite Eq. 9 as

 

Dimensionless form of velocity field: \(u^{*}=y^{*}+\frac{1}{2} P^{*} y^{*}\left(y^{*}-1\right)\) (11)

 

where the dimensionless parameters are

 

\(u^{*}=\frac{u}{V} \quad y^{*}=\frac{y}{h} \quad P^{*}=\frac{h^{2}}{\mu V} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\)

 

In Fig. 9‚Äì66, u* is plotted as a function of y* for several values of P* , using Eq. 11.

 

Discussion When the result is nondimensionalized, we see that Eq. 11 represents a family of velocity profiles. We also see that when the pressure gradient is positive (flow being pushed from right to left) and of sufficient magnitude, we can have reverse flow in the bottom portion of the channel. For all cases, the boundary conditions reduce to u* = 0 at y* = 0 and u* = 1 at y* = 1. If there is a pressure gradient but both walls are stationary, the flow is called two-dimensional channel flow, or planar Poiseuille flow (Fig. 9‚Äì67).
