Guest Question.

Given:

The e versus log σ data of Fig. Ex. 8.16. This consolidation data is from an undisturbed clay sample taken from the midpoint of a compressible layer 10 m thick. The OCR = 1.0.

Required:

a. Determine the slope of the field virgin cohipression curve using the Schmertmann procedure.

b. Compute the settlement of this clay layer if the stress increases from 275 to 800 kPa. Use both the laboratory and field virgin compression curves.

c. Comment on the difference, if any, in the calculated settlement.

Admin
Solution

a. First, establish the field virgin compression curve according to the Schmertmann procedure outlined above. Perform the Casagrande construction on the curve shown in Fig. Ex. 8.16 to obtain the preconsolidation pressure. to be about 275 kPa. Draw a horizontal line from \(e_{o}=0.91\) to the point where it intersects, the preconsolidation pressure to establish control point 1, shown by triangle 1. Extend the virgin compression curve to \(0.42 e_{o}(0.42 \times 0.91)\) or 0.38, to establish control point 2. Connecting the two control points t to 2 creates the field virgin compression curve.

You determine the value of \(C_{c}\) from the field virgin compression curve just as you did for the laboratory consolidation curve (see Examples , , and ). For the log cycle from 1000 to 10 000 kPa, \(e_{1000}=0.705 \text { and } e_{10,000}=0.329 ; \text { therefore } C_{c}=0.705-0.329=0.376\) The slope of the laboratory virgin compression curve is found in the same way and equals 0.31.
We’ll need this value later.

b. To compute the settlement, we may use either Eq. (8.4) or (8.11). Use Eq. (8.4) first:

 

\(s=\frac{\Delta e}{1+e_{o}} H_{o}=\varepsilon_{v} H_{o}\) (8.4)

 

\(s_{c}=C_{c} \frac{H_{o}}{1+e_{o}} \log \frac{\sigma_{v o}^{\prime}+\Delta \sigma_{v}}{\sigma_{v o}^{\prime}}=C_{c} \frac{H_{o}}{1 \div\left(e_{o}\right)}\left(\log \sigma_{v f}^{\prime}-\log \sigma_{v o}^{\prime}\right)\) (8.11)

 

\(s_{c}=\frac{\Delta e}{1+e_{o}} H_{o}\)

 

The change in void ratio, \(\Delta e\), is merely the difference in void ratio for \(\sigma=275\) kPa and \(\sigma=800\) kPa. These values are 0.912 at point a and 0.744 at point b in Fig. Ex. 8.16 on the field virgin compression curve. Therefore,

 

\(s_{c}=\frac{0.912-0.744}{1+0.912} 10 m =0.88 m\)

 

Using Eq. (8.11):

 

\(\begin{aligned}s_{c} &=\frac{C_{c}}{1+e_{o}} H_{o} \log \frac{\sigma_{v o}^{\prime}+\Delta \sigma_{v}}{\sigma_{v o}^{\prime}} \\&=\frac{0.376}{1+0.912}(10 m ) \log \frac{800}{275}=0.91 m\end{aligned}\)

 

The slight difference in. the calculated values of the consolidation settlement \(s_{c}\) is due to small errors in reading data points from Fig. Ex. 8.16.

If we calculate the consolidation settlement using the laboratory virgin compression curve to establish \(C_{c}\), we obtain [Eq. (8.11)]:

 

\(s_{c}=\frac{0.31}{1+0.912}(10 m ) \log \frac{800}{275}=0.75 m , \text { or } 16 \% \text { lower }\)

 

c. Comment on the difference. Sixteen percent could be significant in some cases, especially if the proposed structure is particularly sensitive to settlements. Ladd (1971a) has found the Schmertmann correction will increase compression indices about 15 % for fairly good samples of soft to medium clay. Since the procedure is simple, it would seem prudent to use it to make the best possible estimates of field compressibility. On the other hand, beware of too much precision in settlement calculations. When foundation engineers present their results in an engineering report, they commonly report the expected settlement as, for example, “approximately 0.9 m, ” because including more significant figures would imply more than the actual precision. It is often even better to give a range of settlements possible, along with your calculated “most probable” predicted valule.
