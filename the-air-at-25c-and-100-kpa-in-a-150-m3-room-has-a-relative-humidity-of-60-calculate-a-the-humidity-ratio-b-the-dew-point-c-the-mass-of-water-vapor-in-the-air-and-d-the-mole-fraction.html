\( \text { partial pressure of the air is then } P_{a}=P-P_{v}=100-1.9=98.1 kPa \text {, where we have used the total pressure of the air in } \) the room to be at 100 kPa. The humidity ratio is then

\( \omega=0.622 \frac{P_{v}}{P_{a}}=(0.622)\left(\frac{1.9}{98.1}\right)=0.01205 kg _{v} / kg _{a} \).

\( \text { where } kg _{\omega} \text { is the mass of water vapor and } kg _{a} \text { is the mass of dry air. } \)

\( \text { (b) The dew point is the temperature } T_{3} \text { of Fig. } 12.1 \text { associated with the partial pressure } P_{v} \text {. It is found by interpolation in } \) \( \text { Table C.l or Table C.2, whichever appears to be easier: } T_{\text {d.p. }}=16.6^{\circ} C \text {. } \)

(c) From the definition of the humidity ratio, the mass of water vapor is found to be

\( m_{v}=\omega m_{a}=\omega \frac{P_{a} V}{R_{a} T}=(0.01205)\left[\frac{(98.1)(150)}{(0.287)(298)}\right]=2.07 kg _{v} \).

(d) To find the mole fraction of the water vapor, we first find the total moles:

\( N_{v}=\frac{m_{v}}{M_{v}}=\frac{2.07}{18}=0.1152 mol \quad N_{a}=\frac{m_{a}}{M_{a}}=\frac{(98.1)(150) /(0.287)(298)}{28.97}=5.94 mol \).

The mole fraction of the water vapor is

\( y_{v}=\frac{0.1152}{5.94+0.1152}=0.0194 \).

This demonstrates that air with 60% humidity is about 2% water vapor by volume. We usually ignore this when analyzing air, as in , and consider air to be dry air. Ignoring the water vapor does not lead to significant error in most engineering applications. It must be included, however, when considering problems involving, for example, combustion and air-conditioning.
