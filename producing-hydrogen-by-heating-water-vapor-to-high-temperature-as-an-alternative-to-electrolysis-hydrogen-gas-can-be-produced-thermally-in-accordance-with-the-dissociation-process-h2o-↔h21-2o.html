Producing Hydrogen by Heating Water Vapor to High Temperature
As an alternative to electrolysis, hydrogen gas can be produced thermally in accordance with the dissociation process \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{2}+\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}\) by heating water vapor to very high temperatures (Fig. 16-8). Determine the percentage of water vapor that can be separated into hydrogen and oxygen when this reaction occurs at 4000 K and 10 kPa.

Admin
Solution

The reaction \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{2}+\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}\) is considered at a specified temperature and pressure. The percentage of water vapor that separates into hydrogen and oxygen is to be determined.

Assumptions 1 The equilibrium composition consists of \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}, \mathrm{H}_{2}\), and \(\mathrm{O}_{2}\) only, and dissociation into H, OH, and O is negligible. 2 The constituents of the mixture are ideal gases.

Analysis This is a dissociation process that is significant at very high temperatures only. For simplicity we consider 1 kmol of \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\). The stoichiometric and actual reactions in this case are as follows:

 

Stoichiometric: \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{2}+\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{O}_{2} \text { (thus } \nu_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}=1, \nu_{\mathrm{H}_{2}}=1, \text { and } \nu_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}=0.5 \text { ) }\)

 

Actual: \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \underbrace{x \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}_{ {\begin{array}{c}\text{reactants}\\\text{(leftover)}\end{array} }}+\underbrace{y \mathrm{H}_{2}+z \mathrm{O}_{2}}_{ (\text{products}) } \)

 

H balance: 2 = 2x + 2y → y = 1 — x

 

O balance: 1 = x + 2 z → z = (1 — x)/2

 

Total number of moles: \(N_\text{total}\) = x + y + z = 1.5 — 0.5x

 

Pressure (in atm): P = l0 kPa = 0.09869 atm (since 1 atm = 101.325 kPa)

 

 

The equilibrium constant for the reaction \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{2}+\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}\) at \(4000 \mathrm{~K}\) is given in to be In \(K_{p}=-0.542\) and thus \(K_{p}=0.5816\).

 

Assuming ideal gas behavior for all components in equilibrium composition, the equilibrium constant relation in terms of mole numbers can be expressed in this case as

 

\(K_{P}=\frac{N_{\mathrm{H}_{2}}^{\nu_{\mathrm{H}_2}} N_{\mathrm{O}_{2}}^{\nu_{O_2}}}{N_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}^{\nu_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}}}\left(\frac{P}{N_{\text {total }}}\right)^{\nu_{H_{2}}-\nu_{O_{2}}-\nu_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}}\)

 

 

Substituting,

 

\(0.5816=\frac{(1-x)[(1-x) / 2]^{1 / 2}}{x}\left(\frac{0.09869}{1.5-0.5 x}\right)^{1+0.5-1}\)

 

 

Using an equation solves such as EES or by trial and error, the unknown x is determined to be

x = 0.222

 

 

That is, for each mole of \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\) entering the reaction chamber there is only 0.222 mole of \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\) leaving. Therefore, the fraction of water vapor that dissociated into hydrogen and oxygen when heated to 4000 K is

 

Fraction of dissociation = 1 — x = 1 — 0.222 = 0.778 or 77.8 percent

 

Therefore, hydrogen can be produced at significant rates by heating water vapor to sufficiently high temperatures.

 

 

Discussion The dissociation of \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\) into atomic H, O, and the compound OH can be significant at high temperatures, and thus the first assumption is very simplistic. This problem can be solved more realistically by considering all possible reactions that are likely to occur simultaneously, as discussed later in this chapter.
