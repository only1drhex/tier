Matrix Condition Evaluation

The Hilbert matrix, which is notoriously ill-conditioned, can be represented generally as

\(\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 &1/2 &1/3 &. . . &1/n \\ 1/2 & 1/3 & 1/4 & . . . & 1/(n+1) \\ . & . & . & & . \\ . & . & . & & . \\ . & . &. & & . \\ 1/n & 1/(n+1) & 1/(n+2) & . . . & 1/(2n -1) \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Use the row-sum norm to estimate the matrix condition number for the 3 Ã— 3 Hilbert matrix,

\([A] = \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & 1/2 & 1/3 \\ 1/2 & 1/3 & 1/4 \\ 1/3 & 1/4 & 1/5 \end{matrix} \right ] \)
Admin
Solution

First, the matrix can be normalized so that the maximum element in each row is 1,

\([A] = \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & 1/2 & 1/3 \\ 1 & 2/3 & 1/2 \\ 1& 3/4 & 3/5 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Summing each of the rows gives 1.833, 2.1667, and 2.35. Thus, the third row has the largest sum and the row-sum norm is

\(\left\|A\right\|_\infty = 1 +\frac{3}{4}+\frac{3}{5}= 2.35\)

The inverse of the scaled matrix can be computed as

\([A]^{-1}= \left [ \begin{matrix} 9 & – 18 & 10 \\ – 36 & 96 & – 60 \\ 30 & – 90 & 60 \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Note that the elements of this matrix are larger than the original matrix. This is also refl ected in its row-sum norm, which is computed as

\(\left\|A^{-1}\right\|_\infty = |- 36| + |96| +|- 60|=192\)

Thus, the condition number can be calculated as

Cond [A] = 2.35(192) = 451.2

The fact that the condition number is considerably greater than unity suggests that the system is ill-conditioned. The extent of the ill-conditioning can be quantifi ed by calculating c = log 451.2 = 2.65. Computers using IEEE fl oating-point representation have approximately \( t = log 2^{-24} = 7.2 \) signifi cant base-10 digits (recall Sec. 3.4.1). Therefore, the solution could exhibit rounding errors of up to \(10^{(2.65 – 7.2)}= 3 \times 10^{-5}\) Note that such estimates almost always overpredict the actual error. However, they are useful in alerting you to the possibility that round-off errors may be signifi cant.
