Guest Question.

Given the following state equations, determine the transformation matrix P so that the new state equations are in the state canonical form:\(\left[\begin{matrix} \dot{x_1} \\ \dot{x_2} \end{matrix} \right]=\left[\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix} {x_1} \\ {x_2} \end{matrix} \right]+\left[\begin{matrix}0 \\2 \end{matrix} \right] \)

\(y=\left[\begin{matrix} 3 & 1 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix} {x_1} \\ {x_2} \end{matrix} \right] \)

 

\(\left[\begin{matrix} {x_1}(0) \\ {x_2}(0) \end{matrix} \right]=\left[\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right]\)
Admin
Solution

First find the eigenvalues of A:

\(\left|\lambda I -A\right|=0 \)

 

\(\left|\left[\begin{matrix} \lambda & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda \end{matrix} \right]-\left[\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3\end{matrix} \right] \right| =0 \)

 

\(\left|\begin{matrix} \lambda & -1 \\ 2 & \lambda+3 \end{matrix} \right|=0\)

or

\(\lambda ^2+3\lambda +2=0 \Rightarrow \lambda =-2 and \lambda =-1\)

The eigenvector for \(\lambda =-1\) is found using Equation (9.65):

\((\lambda _i-A)P_i=0 where i = 1,2,3 , . . . , n (9.65)\)

 

\(\left(\left[\begin{matrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{matrix} \right]-\left[\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3\end{matrix} \right]\right)\left[\begin{matrix} P_{11} \\ P_{21} \end{matrix} \right]=0 \)

 

\(-P_{11} – P_{21}=0\)

 

 

\(2P_{11} + 2 P_{21}=0\)

Both equations yield the same relationship between \(P_{11} and P_{21}\). We will arbitrarily select

\(P_{11}=1\)

then

\(P_{21}=-1\)

The eigenvector for \(\lambda =-1\) is

\(P_1 =\left[\begin{matrix}1\\ -1 \end{matrix} \right] \)

In a similar manner we can obtain the eigenvector for \(\lambda =-2\). Solving Equation (9.65) \((\lambda _i-A)P_i=0 where i = 1,2,3 , . . . , n (9.65)\)

yields the following equations:

\(\left(\left[\begin{matrix} -2 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{matrix} \right]-\left[\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3\end{matrix} \right]\right)\left[\begin{matrix} P_{12} \\ P_{22} \end{matrix} \right]=0 \)

or

\(-2P_{12} – P_{22}=0\)

 

\(2P_{12} + 1P_{22}=0\)

Again we will specify \(P_{12}=1\) and then solve for \(P_{22}\). The eigenvector \(P_2\) becomes

\(P_2 =\left[\begin{matrix}1\\ -2 \end{matrix} \right] \)

The transformation matrix P now can be constructed by stacking the eigenvectors as follows:

\(P=[P_1 P_2]\)

or

\(P=\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & -2 \end{matrix} \right] \)

To determine the new state equation we need the inverse of P:

\(P^{-1}=\left[\begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & -1 \end{matrix} \right]\)

The diagonal matrix A is defined in terms of P and A:

\(A=P^{-1} AP\)

 

\(A= \left[\begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & -1 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ -2 & -3\end{matrix} \right]\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & -2 \end{matrix} \right]\)

or
\(A= \left[\begin{matrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{matrix} \right]\)

where the eigenvalues are on the diagonal.

In a similar manner \(\bar{B} and \bar{C} \) can be computed

 

\(\bar{B}=P^{-1}B\)

 

\(\bar{B}=\left[\begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & -1 \end{matrix} \right]\left[\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right] \)

 

\(\bar{B}=\left[\begin{matrix} 2 \\ -2 \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

\(\bar{C}=CP\)

 

\(\bar{C}= \left[\begin{matrix} 3 & 1 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & -2 \end{matrix} \right] \)

 

\(\bar{C}= \left[\begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \end{matrix} \right]\)

The new state equations are

\(\left[\begin{matrix} \dot{z_1} \\ \dot{z_2} \end{matrix} \right]=\left[\begin{matrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{matrix} \right]\left[\begin{matrix} {z_1} \\ {z_2} \end{matrix} \right]+ \left[\begin{matrix} 2 \\ -2 \end{matrix} \right] [u]\)

 

\(y=\left[\begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \end{matrix} \right]\left[\begin{matrix} {z_1} \\ {z_2} \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

\(\left[\begin{matrix} {z_1}(0) \\ {z_2} (0) \end{matrix} \right]=P^{-1}x(0)\)

 

\(\left[\begin{matrix} {z_1}(0) \\ {z_2} (0) \end{matrix} \right] =\left[\begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right]\)

This example demonstrates an important property of eigenvalues; namely, that then eigenvalues and the corresponding characteristic equations are invariant under a nonsingular transformation. The eigenvalues of the A matrix and A are the same. In this example the transformed plant matrix is a purely diagonal matrix having the eigenvalues of the original A matrix along the diagonal. For this particular case, the state transition matrix can be shown to be the following:

\(\Phi (t)=e^{At}=\left[\begin{matrix} e^{\lambda _1t} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{\lambda _2t} \end{matrix} \right]\)

or

\(\Phi (t)=\left[\begin{matrix} e^{-t} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-2t} \end{matrix} \right]\)

The solution of the transformed state equations would be similar to Equation(9.42):

\(z(t)=\Phi (t)z(0)+\int_{0}^{t}{\Phi (t-\tau )\bar{B}\eta (\tau ) d\tau } \)

 

\(\left[\begin{matrix} {z_1}(t) \\ {z_2} (t) \end{matrix} \right]=\left[\begin{matrix} e^{-t} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-2t} \end{matrix} \right]\left[\begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right]+\left[\begin{matrix} \int_{0}^{t}{2e^{-(t-\tau )}d\tau } \\- \int_{0}^{t}{2e^{-2(t-\tau )}d\tau } \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

\(=\left[\begin{matrix} 2-e^{-t} \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right]\)

The output of the system is given by

\(y=\bar{C}z \)

 

ro \(y=\left[\begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix} 2-e^{-t} \\ -1 \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

\(y=3-2e^{-t}\)
