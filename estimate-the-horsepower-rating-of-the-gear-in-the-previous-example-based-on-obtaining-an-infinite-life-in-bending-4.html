Guest Question.

Estimate the horsepower rating of the gear in the previous example based on obtaining an infinite life in bending.

Admin
Solution

The rotating-beam endurance limit is estimated from Eq. (6–10)\(S^{′}_{e} = \begin{cases} 0.5S_{ut} &S_{ut} ≤ 200 kpsi (1400 MPa) \\ 100 kpsi & S_{ut} > 200 kpsi\\ 700 MPa&S_{ut} > 1400 MPa \end{cases} \) ,

\(S^{′}_{e} = 0.5S_{ut}\) = 0.5(55) = 27.5 kpsi

To obtain the surface finish Marin factor\( k_{a} \)we refer to Table 6–3 for machined surface, finding a = 2.00 and b = −0.217. Then Eq. (6–18) gives the surface finish Marin factor \(k_{a}\) as

\(k_{a} = aS^{b}_{ut}\) = 2.00(55)−0.217 = 0.838

The next step is to estimate the size factor \(k_{b}\). From Table 13–1, the sum of the addendum and dedendum is

\( l =\frac{ 1}{P}+\frac{1.25}{P} =\frac{ 1}{8} +\frac{1.25}{8} = 0.281 in \)

The tooth thickness t in Fig. 14–1b is given in Sec. 14–1 [Eq. (b)] as t = (4lx) \(^{1∕2}\) when x = 3Y∕(2P) from Eq. (14–3) \( Y = \frac{2xP}{3} \). Therefore, since from Y = 0.296 and P = 8,

\( x = \frac{3Y}{2P} =\frac{3(0.296)}{ 2(8)} = 0.0555 in \)

then

\(t = (4lx)^{ 1∕2} = [4(0.281)0.0555] ^{1∕2}\) = 0.250 in

We have recognized the tooth as a cantilever beam of rectangular cross section, so the equivalent rotating-beam diameter must be obtained from Eq. (6–24):

\(d_{e} = 0.808(hb)^{1∕2} = 0.808(Ft)^{1∕2} = 0.808[1.5(0.250)]^{ 1∕2} = 0.495 in\)

Then, Eq. (6–19) \( k_{b} =\begin{cases}0.91d^{−0.157} & 2 < d ≤ 10 in\\(d∕7.62)^{−0.107} = 1.24d^{-0.107} &7.62 ≤ d ≤ 51 mm \\1.51d^{−0.157} &51 < d ≤ 254 mm \end{cases}\) gives \(k_{b}\) as

\(k_{b} = (\frac{d_{e}}{0.30})^{−0.107}= (\frac{0.495}{0.30} )^{−0.107}= 0.948 \)

The load factor \(k_c\) from Eq. (6–25)\( k_{c}= \begin{cases} 1 &bending \\ 0.85 &axial\\ 0.59&torsion \end{cases} \)is unity. With no information given concerning temperature and reliability we will set\( k_{d} = k_{e}\) = 1.

In general, a gear tooth is subjected only to one-way bending. Exceptions include idler gears and gears used in reversing mechanisms. We will account for one-way bending by establishing a miscellaneous-effects Marin factor\( k_{f}.\)

For one-way bending the steady and alternating stress components are \(σ_{a} = σ_{m} \)= σ∕2 where σ is the larg-est repeatedly applied bending stress as given in Eq. (14–7) \( σ =\frac{ K_{v}W ^{t}P}{FY} \). If a material exhibited a Goodman failure locus,

\( \frac{σ_{a}}{S^{′}_{e}}+ \frac{σ_{m}}{S_{ut}}= 1 \)

Since \(σ_{a} \) and \(σ_{m} \)are equal for one-way bending, we substitute \( σ_{a} \)for \( σ_{m} \) and solve the preceding equation for \( σ_{a} \), giving

\( σ_{a} =\frac{ S^{′}_{e}S_{ut}}{S^{′}_{e} + S_{ut}} \)

Now replace \(σ_{a}\) with σ∕2, and in the denominator replace \(S^{′}_{ e} \)with 0.5\(S_{ut}\) to obtain

\( σ = \frac{2S^{′}_{e}S_{ut}}{0.5S_{ut} + S_{ut}}=\frac{ 2S^{′}_{e}}{0.5 + 1 }= 1.33S^{′}_{e} \)

Now defining a miscellaneous Marin factor \(k_{f} = σ∕S^{′}_{ e} = 1.33S^{′}_{ e}∕S^{′} _{e}\) = 1.33. Similarly, if we were to use a Gerber fatigue locus,

\( \frac{σ_{a}}{S^{′}_{e}}+ (\frac{σ_{m}}{S_{ut}})^{2}= 1 \)

Setting \(σ_{a} = σ_{m}\) and solving the quadratic in\( σ_{a}\) gives

\(σ_{a} = \frac{S^{2}_{ut}}{2S^{′}_{e}} (−1 + \sqrt{ 1 +\frac{4S^{′2}_{e}}{S^{2}_{ut}}} ) \)

Setting\( σ_{a} = σ∕2, S_{ut} = S^{′}_ {e}∕0.5\) gives

\( σ =\frac{ S^{′}_{e}}{0.5^{2}} [−1 + \sqrt{ 1 + 4(0.5)^{2}}] = 1.66S′e \)

and \(k_{f} = σ∕S′_{ e}\) = 1.66. Since a Gerber locus runs in and among fatigue data and Goodman does not, we will use\( k_{f} = \)1.66. The Marin equation for the fully corrected endurance strength is

\(S_{e} = k_{a} k_{b} k_{c} k_{d} k_{e} k_{f} S′_{ e}\)
= 0.838(0.948)(1)(1)(1)1.66(27.5) = 36.3 kpsi

For stress, we will first determine the fatigue stress-concentration factor\( K_{f}\). For a 20° full-depth tooth the radius of the root fillet is denoted \(r_{f}\), with a typically proportioned value of

\( r_{f} =\frac{ 0.300}{P} = \frac{0.300}{8} = 0.0375 in \)

From Fig. A–15–6

\( \frac{r}{d}=\frac{ r_{f}}{ t} =\frac{ 0.0375}{ 0.250} = 0.15 \)

Since D∕d = ∞, we approximate with D∕d = 3, giving\( K_{t}\) = 1.68. From Fig. 6–26, q = 0.62. From Eq. (6–32) \(K_{f} = 1 + q(K_{t} − 1) or K_{fs} = 1 + q_{s}(K_{ts} − 1) \),

\(K_{f}\) = 1 + (0.62)(1.68 − 1) = 1.42

For a design factor of\( n_{d}\) = 3, as used in , applied to the load or strength, the maximum bending stress is

\( σ_{max} = K_{f} σ_{all} = \frac{S_{e}} {n_{d}} \)

 

\( σ_{all} = \frac{S_{e}}{K_{f} n_{d}}= \frac{36.3}{1.42(3) }= 8.52 kpsi \)

The transmitted load \(W^{t}\) is

\( W^{t} =\frac{FYσ_{all}}{K_{v}P} =\frac{ 1.5(0.296)8520}{1.52(8)} = 311 lbf\)

and the power is, with V = 628 ft/min from ,

\( hp =\frac{ W ^{t}V}{33 000} =\frac{ 311(628)}{33 000} = 5.9 hp \)

Again, it should be emphasized that these results should be accepted only as preliminary estimates to alert you to the nature of bending in gear teeth.
