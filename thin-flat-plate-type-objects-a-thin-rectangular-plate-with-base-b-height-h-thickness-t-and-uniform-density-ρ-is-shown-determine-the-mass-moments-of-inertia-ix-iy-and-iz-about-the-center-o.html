Guest Question.

Thin, Flat Plate-type Objects

A thin rectangular plate with base b, height h, thickness t , and uniform density \(\rho\) is shown. Determine the mass moments of inertia \(I_x, I_y\), and \(I_z\) about the center of mass for the plate, expressing each in terms of the mass m of the plate.

Admin
Solution

Road Map We will use Eqs. (\(I_x = \int{\widetilde{r}^2_x dm}= \int{(\widetilde{y}^2 + \widetilde{z}^2) dm} , I_y = \int{\widetilde{r}^2_y dm}= \int{(\widetilde{x}^2 + \widetilde{z}^2) dm}\))–(\(I_z = \int{\widetilde{r}^2_z dm}= \int{(\widetilde{x}^2 + \widetilde{y}^2) dm}\)) on p. 553 to determine the three moments of inertia.

\(I_x\)

Governing Equations & Computation We begin with Eq. (\(I_x = \int{\widetilde{r}^2_x dm}= \int{(\widetilde{y}^2 + \widetilde{z}^2) dm}\) ) with \(dm = \rho dV\)

 

\(I_x = \int{(\widetilde{y}^2 + \widetilde{z}^2)\rho dV}\). (1)

If the plate is thin, then \(\widetilde{z}^2 \ll \widetilde{y}^2\) and hence \(\widetilde{z}^2\) can be neglected. Using a volume element parallel to the x axis as shown in Fig. 2, we obtain

\(dV = bt dy\) and \( \widetilde{y} = y\). (2)

Substituting Eq. (2) into Eq. (1), the moment of inertia about the x axis is

\(I_x = \int{\widetilde{y}^2 \rho dV} = \int_{-h/2}^{h/2}{y^2 \rho bt dy } = \rho t \frac{by^3}{3} |^{h/2}_{-h/2} = \rho t \frac{bh^3}{12}\). (3)

While the above result is satisfactory, it is customary to report mass moments of inertia in terms of the total mass of an object. The mass of the thin plate is \(m = \rho bht\). We express Eq. (3) in terms of mass m, using the following procedure:

\(I_x = \rho t \frac{by^3}{12}\underbrace{(\frac{m}{\rho bht})}_{=1} = \frac{mh^2}{12}\). (4)

\(I_y\)

Governing Equations & Computation We begin with Eq. (10.16)

\(I_y = \int{(\widetilde{x}^2 + \widetilde{z}^2)\rho dV}\). (5)

where \(\widetilde{z}^2\) can be neglected because \(\widetilde{z}^2 \ll \widetilde{x}^2\). Using a volume element parallel to the y axis as shown in Fig. 3, we obtain

\(dV = ht dx\) and \(\widetilde{x} = x\). (6)

Substituting Eq. (6) into Eq. (5), the moment of inertia about the y axis is

\(I_y = \int{\widetilde{x}^2 \rho dV} = \int_{-b/2}^{b/2}{x^2 \rho ht dx } = \rho t \frac{hx^3}{3} |^{b/2}_{-b/2} = \rho t \frac{hb^3}{12}\). (7)

Expressing Eq. (7) in terms of mass m provides

\(I_y =\rho t \frac{hb^3}{12}\frac{m}{\rho bht} = \frac{nb^2}{12}\). (8)

\(I_z\)

Governing Equations & Computation We begin with Eq. (\(I_z = \int{\widetilde{r}^2_z dm}= \int{(\widetilde{x}^2 + \widetilde{y}^2) dm}\))

\(I_z = \int{(\widetilde{x}^2 + \widetilde{y}^2)\rho dV}\). (9)

For the geometry of the plate in this problem, \(\widetilde{x}\) and \(\widetilde{y}\) are of comparable size, so neither can be neglected. This presents a difficulty if Eq. (9) is to be evaluated using a single integration. However, some ingenuity provides a straightforward solution, as follows. We break Eq. (9) into two separate integrals

\(I_z = \int{\widetilde{x}^2 \rho dV} + \int{\widetilde{y}^2 \rho dV}\). (10)

The first of the above integrals is identical to Eq. (7), and the second is identical to Eq. (3). Hence,

\(I_z = \frac{mb^2}{12} + \frac{mh^2}{12} = \frac{m}{12}(b^2 + h^2)\). (11)

In essence, the first and second integrals in Eq. (10) were evaluated using different volume elements.

Discussion & Verification

• A thin plate is a common object in structures and machines. Hence, the results obtained here are usually tabulated in handbooks and texts, such as the Table of Properties of Solids on the inside back cover of this book.

• By letting h or b in Fig. 1 become small, the plate considered here becomes a thin rod. For example, if \(h \longrightarrow 0\), then Eqs. (4), (8), and (11) become \(I_x = 0\) and \(I_y = I_z = mb^2/12\). You should verify that these mass moments of inertia agree with those reported for a thin rod in the Table of Properties of Solids on the inside back cover of this book (when making this comparison, you must note that some coordinate directions and/or dimensions may be defined differently).

A Closer Look While mass moments of inertia and area moments of inertia are generally quite different, they are related for thin flat plates, such as in this example. To see this relationship, rewrite Eq. (3) as

\(I_x = \int{\widetilde{y}^2 \rho dV} = \rho t \int{\widetilde{y}^2 dA}\). (12)

The second integral is obtained by using \(dV = t dA\) and factoring \(\rho t\) outside of the integral because the plate has uniform density and thickness. The second integral is seen to be the definition of the area moment of inertia about the x axis (i.e., from Eq. (\(I_x = \int{\widetilde{y}^2 dA}\)) on p. 534, \((I_x)_{area } = \int{\widetilde{y}^2 dA}\). Hence

\(I_x = \rho t(I_x)_{area } =\rho t \frac{bh^3}{12}\), (13)

where we have used the result from on p. 539 that \((I_x)_{area} = bh^3/12\) for a rectangular area.
