Pelton Wheel Turbine Characteristics

GIVEN Water to drive a Pelton wheel is supplied through a pipe from a lake as indicated in Fig. E12.6a. The head loss due to friction in the pipe is important, but minor losses can be neglected.

FIND (a) Determine the nozzle diameter, \(D_{1}\), that will give the maximum power output. (b) Determine the maximum power and the angular velocity of the rotor at the conditions found in part (a).

Admin
Solution

(a) As indicated by Eq. 12.51, the power output depends on the flowrate, \(Q=\dot{m} / \rho\), and the jet speed at the nozzle exit, \(V_{1}\), both of which depend on the diameter of the nozzle, \(D_{1}\), and the head loss associated with the supply pipe. That is

 

\(\dot{W}_{\text {shaft }}=T_{\text {shaft }} \omega=\dot{m} U\left(U-V_{1}\right)(1-\cos \beta)\) (12.51)

 

\(\dot{W}_{\text {shaft }}=\rho Q U\left(U-V_{1}\right)(1-\cos \beta)\) (1)

 

The nozzle exit speed, \(V_{1}\), can be obtained by applying the energy equation (Eq. 5.85) between a point on the lake surface
(where \(V_{0}=p_{0}=0\)) and the nozzle outlet (where \(z_{1}=p_{1}=0\)) to give

 

\(h_{s}=w_{\text {shaft net in }} / g=\frac{\dot{W}_{\text {shaft net in }}}{\dot{m} g}=\frac{\dot{W}_{\text {shaft net in }}}{\gamma Q}\) (5.85)

 

\(z_{0}=\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 g}+h_{L}\) (2)

 

where the head loss is given in terms of the friction factor, f, as (see Eq. 8.34)

 

\(h_{L \text { major }}=f \frac{\ell}{D} \frac{V^{2}}{2 g}\) (8.34)

 

\(h_{L}=f \frac{\ell}{D} \frac{V^{2}}{2 g}\)

 

The speed, V, of the fluid in the pipe of diameter D is obtained from the continuity equation

 

\(V=\frac{A_{1} V_{1}}{A}=\left(\frac{D_{1}}{D}\right)^{2} V_{1}\)

 

We have neglected minor losses associated with the pipe entrance and the nozzle. With the given data, Eq. 2 becomes

 

\(z_{0}=\left[1+f \frac{\ell}{D}\left(\frac{D_{1}}{D}\right)^{4}\right] \frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 g}\) (3)

 

or

 

\(\begin{aligned}V_{1} &=\left[\frac{2 g z_{0}}{1+f \frac{\ell}{D}\left(\frac{D_{1}}{D}\right)^{4}}\right]^{1 / 2} \\&=\left[\frac{2\left(32.2 ft / s ^{2}\right)(200 ft )}{1+0.02\left(\frac{1000 ft }{8 / 12 ft }\right)\left(\frac{D_{1}}{8 / 12}\right)^{4}}\right]^{1 / 2} \\&=\frac{113.5}{\sqrt{1+152 D_{1}^{4}}}\end{aligned}\) (4)

 

where \(D_{1}\) is in feet.

 

By combining Eqs. 1 and 4 and using \(Q=\pi D_{1}^{2} V_{1} / 4\) we obtain the power as a function of \(D_{1}\) and U as

 

\(\dot{W}_{\text {shaft }}=\frac{323 U D_{1}^{2}}{\sqrt{1+152 D_{1}^{4}}}\left[U-\frac{113.5}{\sqrt{1+152 D_{1}^{4}}}\right]\) (5)

 

where U is in feet per second and \(\dot{W}_{\text {shaft }} \text { is in } ft \cdot lb / s\). These results are plotted as a function of U for various values of \(D_{1}\) in Fig. 12.6b.

 

As shown by Eq. 12.52, the maximum power (in terms of its variation with U) occurs when \(U=V_{1} / 2\), which, when used with Eqs. 4 and 5, gives

 

\(\left.U\right|_{\max \text { power }}=\frac{V_{1}}{2}\) (12.52)

 

\(\dot{W}_{\text {shaft }}=-\frac{1.04 \times 10^{6} D_{1}^{2}}{\left(1+152 D_{1}^{4}\right)^{3 / 2}}\) (6)

 

The maximum power possible occurs when \(d \dot{W}_{\text {shaft }} / d D_{1}=0\), which according to Eq. 6 can be found as

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{d W_{\text {shaft }}}{d D_{1}}=&-1.04 \times 10^{6}\left[\frac{2 D_{1}}{\left(1+152 D_{1}^{4}\right)^{3 / 2}}\right.\\&\left.-\left(\frac{3}{2}\right) \frac{4(152) D_{1}^{5}}{\left(1+152 D_{1}^{4}\right)^{5 / 2}}\right]=0\end{aligned}\)

 

or

 

\(304 D_{1}^{4}=1\)

 

Thus, the nozzle diameter for maximum power output is

 

\(D_{1}=0.239 ft\)

 

(b) The corresponding maximum power can be determined from Eq. 6 as

 

\(\dot{W}_{\text {shatt }}=-\frac{1.04 \times 10^{6}(0.239)^{2}}{\left[1+152(0.239)^{4}\right]^{3 / 2}}=-3.25 \times 10^{4} ft \cdot lb / s\)

 

or

 

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{W}_{\text {shaft }} &=-3.25 \times 10^{4} ft \cdot lb / s \times \frac{1 hp }{550 ft \cdot lb / s } \\&=-59.0 hp\end{aligned}\)

 

The rotor speed at the maximum power condition can be obtained from

 

\(U=\omega R=\frac{V_{1}}{2}\)

 

where \(V_{1}\) is given by Eq. 4. Thus,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\omega &=\frac{V_{1}}{2 R}=\frac{\frac{113.5}{\sqrt{1+152(0.239)^{4}}} ft / s }{2\left(\frac{3}{2} ft \right)} \\&=30.9 rad / s \times 1 rev / 2 \pi rad \times 60 s / min \\&=295 rpm\end{aligned}\)

 

COMMENT The reason that an optimum diameter nozzle exists can be explained as follows. A larger diameter nozzle will allow a larger flowrate, but will produce a smaller jet velocity because of the head loss within the supply side. A smaller diameter nozzle will reduce the flowrate but will produce a larger jet velocity. Since the power depends on a product combination of flowrate and jet velocity (see Eq. 1), there is an optimum-diameter nozzle that gives the maximum power.

These results can be generalized (i.e., without regard to the specific parameter values of this problem) by considering Eqs. 1 and 3 and the condition that \(U=V_{1} / 2\) to obtain

 

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{W}_{\text {shaft } \mid U=V_{1} / 2} &=-\frac{\pi}{16} \rho(1-\cos \beta) \\& \times\left(2 g z_{0}\right)^{3 / 2} D_{1}^{2} /\left(1+f \frac{\ell}{D^{5}} D_{1}^{4}\right)^{3 / 2}\end{aligned}\)

 

By setting \(d \dot{W}_{\text {shaft }} / d D_{1}=0\), it can be shown (see Problem 12.67) that the maximum power occurs when

 

\(D_{1}=D /\left(2 f \frac{\ell}{D}\right)^{1 / 4}\)

 

which gives the same results obtained earlier for the specific parameters
of the example problem. Note that the optimum condition depends only on the friction factor and the length-to-diameter ratio of the supply pipe. What happens if the supply pipe is frictionless or of essentially zero length?
