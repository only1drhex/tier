Unforced Nonlinear Pendulum

The equation that approximately governs an undamped nonlinear pendulum, Equation 11.1,

 

\(\ddot{\theta}+\omega_{n}^{2}\left(\theta-\frac{\theta^{3}}{6}\right)=0\)

 

can be written in the more general form

 

\(\ddot{\theta}+\omega_{n}^{2} \theta+\varepsilon \theta^{3}=0\) (11.18)

 

where \(\omega_{n}^{2}=\sqrt{g / l}\) and \(\varepsilon=-\omega_{n}^{2} / 6\) Equation 11.18 is known as an unforced Duffing equation. Assume that the nonlinearity is weak, that is, ε is small compared to 1. Use a perturbation (series) solution for \(\theta(t)\) and solve to order one, that is, retain terms to the first power in ε.

Admin
Solution

The perturbation solution is given by the infinite series,

 

\(\theta(t, \varepsilon)=\theta_{0}(t)+\varepsilon \theta_{1}(t)+\varepsilon^{2} \theta_{2}(t)+\cdots\) (11.19)

 

Using a two-termapproximation for \(\theta(t)\) and substituting this into the equation of motion, we find

 

\(\left(\ddot{\theta}_{0}+\varepsilon \ddot{\theta}_{1}\right)+\omega_{n}^{2}\left(\theta_{0}+\varepsilon \theta_{1}\right)+\varepsilon\left(\theta_{0}+\varepsilon \theta_{1}\right)^{3}=0\)

 

Expanding and grouping terms according to the power, or order, of the perturbation parameter ε, we have

 

\(\begin{gathered}\left(\ddot{\theta}_{0}+\omega_{n}^{2} \theta_{0}\right)+\varepsilon\left(\ddot{\theta}_{1}+\omega_{n}^{2} \theta_{1}+\theta_{0}^{3}\right)+\varepsilon^{2}\left(3 \theta_{0}^{2} \theta_{1}\right) \\+\varepsilon^{3}\left(3 \theta_{0} \theta_{1}^{2}\right)+\varepsilon^{4} \theta_{1}^{3}=0 .\end{gathered}\)

 

Since ε is assumed to be small, terms of order \(\varepsilon^{2}\), \(\varepsilon^{3}\) and \(\varepsilon^{4}\) are of higher order than the order of the assumed solution and are therefore neglected. The solution obtained in this way is said to be correct to order ε. Each order of the solution is satisfied independently,\( ^{14 } \) as follows,

 

\(\varepsilon^{0}: \quad \ddot{\theta}_{0}+\omega_{n}^{2} \theta_{0}=0\) (11.20)

 

\(\varepsilon^{1}: \quad \ddot{\theta}_{1}+\omega_{n}^{2} \theta_{1}=-\theta_{0}^{3}\) (11.21)

 

\(\theta_{0}\) is known as the generating solution for the sequence of equations and is taken to satisfy the initial conditions. The Duffing equation requires two initial conditions. These can be specified in full generality, but we assume the values \(\theta(0)=C \text { and } \dot{\theta}(0)=0\) If the initial angular velocity is other than zero, only the phase is changed and not the character of the solution for the autonomous Equation 11.18. It is easiest to stipulate that the \(\varepsilon^{0}\) order equation satisfies the initial conditions. Then the remaining equations of higher order will satisfy zero initial conditions. The sum of solutions, as per Equation 11.19, will satisfy the initial conditions of the problem. The equations are solved in sequence. Equation 11.20 is solved for \(\theta_{0}\), which is then substituted as needed into the right-hand side of Equation 11.21 so that the solution \(\theta_{1}\) can be obtained. From Equation 11.20,

 

\(\theta_{0}(t)=A \sin \left(\omega_{n} t+\phi\right)\)

 

and satisfying the initial conditions results in \(A=C\) and Φ= π/2 rad. Equation 11.21 then becomes

 

\(\begin{aligned}\ddot{\theta}_{1}+\omega_{n}^{2} \theta_{1} &=-C^{3} \sin ^{3}\left(\omega_{n} t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \\&=-C^{3}\left[\frac{3}{4} \sin \left(\omega_{n} t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\right.\\&\left.-\frac{1}{4} \sin 3\left(\omega_{n} t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

where a trigonometric identity has been used to transform the cubic sine term. The solution of this equation is

 

\(\begin{aligned}\theta_{1}(t) &=\frac{3}{8 \omega_{n}} t C^{3} \cos \left(\omega_{n} t+\pi / 2\right) \\&-\frac{C^{3}}{32 \omega_{n}^{2}} \sin 3\left(\omega_{n} t+\pi / 2\right)\end{aligned}\) (11.22)

 

The two-term approximate solution is then \(\theta(t)\) = \(\theta_{0}(t)+\varepsilon \theta_{1}(t)\). We immediately see a problem with the first term on the right-hand side of Equation 11.22. It is called a secular term, that is, an expression that grows without bound due to the factor \(t\) multiplying the cosine. We know that the solution to Equation 11.18 should be periodic for small ε, and therefore this unbounded term should not be part of the solution.
The problem with this approach is that the truncation has removed terms that would balance the secular term and lead to a periodic solution. This effect is demonstrated by expanding the following sine function,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\sin \left(\omega_{n}+\varepsilon\right) t &=\sin \omega_{n} t \cos \varepsilon t+\cos \omega_{n} t \sin \varepsilon t \\&=\left(1-\frac{1}{2 !} \varepsilon^{2} t^{2}+\frac{1}{4 !} \varepsilon^{4} t^{4}-\cdots\right) \sin \omega_{n} t \\&+\left(\varepsilon t-\frac{1}{3 !} \varepsilon^{3} t^{3}+\frac{1}{5 !} \varepsilon^{5} t^{5}-\cdots\right) \cos \omega_{n} t\end{aligned}\)

 

Approximating the expression \(\sin \left(\omega_{n}+\varepsilon\right) t\) by two terms of the expansion on the right-hand side, we obtain

 

\(\begin{aligned}\sin \left(\omega_{n}+\varepsilon\right) t &=\left(1-\frac{1}{2 !} \varepsilon^{2} t^{2}\right) \sin \omega_{n} t \\&+\left(\varepsilon t-\frac{1}{3 !} \varepsilon^{3} t^{3}\right) \cos \omega_{n} t\end{aligned}\)

 

Since the sine function is harmonic, the secular terms should not be present and must be removed in a logical way. This defect in the direct method above is corrected with the use of the Lindstedt-Poincaré method, derived in the next section.

\( ^{14} \) When substituting the expansion into the differential equation, we obtain a power series in \( \varepsilon \) that must vanish identically in \( \varepsilon \) This is why each order in \( \varepsilon \) results in a differential equation that must be satisfied.
