Guest Question.

Find the particular solution of the ordinary differential equation,

 

\(y^{\prime \prime}(x)+3 y^{\prime}(x)+2 y=4 x^{2}\)

 

In this equation, the independent variable is \(x\), the dependent variable is \(y\), and the primes denote differentiation with respect to \(x\).

Admin
Solution

The particular solution is taken to be of the form \(y_{p}(x)=a x^{2}+b x+c\). To evaluate constants \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\), substitute \(y_{p}(x)\), \(y_{p}^{\prime}(x)\), and \(y_{p}^{\prime \prime}(x)\) into the governing equation,

 

\(\begin{aligned}2 a+3(2 a x+b)+2\left(a x^{2}+b x+c\right) &=4 x^{2} \\\text { or } \quad(2 a+2 c+3 b)+(6 a+2 b) x+(2 a) x^{2} &=4 x^{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

By equating the coefficients of the same powers of \(x\), we have the three equations that need to be solved for the three unknowns,

 

\(\begin{aligned}&x^{0}: 2 a+2 c+3 b=0 \\&x^{1}: 6 a+2 b=0 \\&x^{2}: 2 a=4 .\end{aligned}\)

 

Solving these equations yields \(a\) = 2, \(b\) = -6, and \(c\) = 7.

The particular solution is then \(y_{p}(x)=2 x^{2}-6 x+7\)
