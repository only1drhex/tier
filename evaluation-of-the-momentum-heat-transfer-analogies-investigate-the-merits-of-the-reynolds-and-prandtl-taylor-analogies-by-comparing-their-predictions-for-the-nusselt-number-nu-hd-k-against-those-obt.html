Guest Question.

Evaluation of the Momentum/Heat-Transfer Analogies

Investigate the merits of the Reynolds and Prandtl-Taylor analogies by comparing their predictions for the Nusselt number \(\mathrm{Nu}=h D / k\) against those obtained from the well-known Dittus-Boelter equation, a correlation that is based on experimentally determined heat-transfer coefficients in smooth pipe:

\(\mathrm{Nu}=0.023 \operatorname{Re}^{0.8} \operatorname{Pr}^{0.4}.\) (E9.6.1)

Admin
Solution
  1. Reynolds analogy. For simplicity, use the predictions of the Blasius equation for the friction factor, which holds over a fairly wide range of Reynolds numbers:

\(f_{\mathrm{F}}=0.0790 \mathrm{Re}^{-1 / 4}.\) (E9.6.2)

A combination of Eqns. (9.99) and (E9.6.2) gives:

\(\frac{1}{2} f_{\mathrm{F}}=\frac{h}{\rho \bar{v}_{z m} c_{p}}=\mathrm{St},\) (9.99)

\(\frac{h}{\rho \bar{v}_{z m} c_{p}}=\frac{1}{2} f_{\mathrm{F}}=0.0395\left(\frac{\rho \bar{v}_{z m} D}{\mu}\right)^{-1 / 4} .\) (E9.6.3)

Multiplication of both sides by \(\rho \bar{v}_{z m} c_{p} D / k\) and rearrangement leads to:

\(\frac{h D}{k}=0.0395\left(\frac{\rho \bar{v}_{z m} D}{\mu}\right)^{3 / 4} \frac{\mu c_{p}}{k},\) (E9.6.4)

or:

\(\mathrm{Nu}=0.0395 \mathrm{Re}^{3 / 4} \mathrm{Pr},\) (E9.6.5)

in which the definitions of the Nusselt and Prandtl numbers have been recognized. Thus, we now have a basis for predicting the heat-transfer coefficient \(h\) via the Nusselt number. For a representative Reynolds number of Re \(=50,000\), Table E9.6 gives in the column RA (Reynolds analogy) values of Nu from Eqn. (E9.6.5) for three different Prandtl numbers. Note also the column DB, which gives values for \(\mathrm{Nu}\) from the well-known Dittus-Boelter equation, (E9.6.1), representative of experimental values:

\(\mathrm{Nu}=0.023 \mathrm{Re}^{0.8} \mathrm{Pr}^{0.4}.\) (E9.6.6)

Table E9.6 Nusselt Numbers at Re = 50,000
Pr RA DB PTA
0.5 67 100 95
1.0 130 130 130
2.0 264 173 162

Observe the outstanding agreement at Pr \(=1\) between the prediction of the Reynolds analogy and the essentially experimental value given by the DittusBoelter equation. Note also the significant differences between the two for Pr \(=0.5\) and \(2 .\) The essence of this discrepancy may be found by reexpressing the Prandtl number as:

\(\operatorname{Pr}=\frac{\mu c_{p}}{k}=\frac{\frac{\mu}{\rho}}{\frac{k}{\rho c_{p}}}=\frac{\nu}{\alpha},\) (E9.6.7)

in which \(\nu\) and \(\alpha\) are the kinematic viscosity and thermal diffusivity, respectively. These last two represent the rates at which momentum and heat diffuse through a fluid by molecular action – factors that are completely ignored in the Reynolds analogy.

  1. Prandtl-Taylor analogy. By similar arguments, if the Blasius equation is again assumed for the friction factor, the Prandtl-Taylor analogy of Eqn. (9.102) yields:

\(\mathrm{St}=\frac{f_{\mathrm{F}} / 2}{1+\frac{\bar{v}_{z \delta}}{\bar{v}_{z m}}(\operatorname{Pr}-1)}.\) (9.102)

\(\mathrm{Nu}=\frac{0.0395 \mathrm{Re}^{3 / 4} \mathrm{Pr}}{1+\frac{\bar{v}_{z \delta}}{\bar{v}_{z m}}(\mathrm{Pr}-1)},\) (E9.6.8)

in which the velocity ratio is given in Eqn. (9.103).

\(\frac{\bar{v}_{z \delta}}{\bar{v}_{z m}}=\frac{2.44}{\operatorname{Re}^{1 / 8}}.\) (9.103)

The Nusselt numbers predicted from Eqn. (E9.6.8) can also be evaluated, three representative values being given in the last column of Table E9.4. Note that the agreement with the Dittus-Boelter values is now much improved for both \(\operatorname{Pr}=0.5\) and 2 , thus illustrating the success of the more realistic Prandtl-Taylor analogy.
