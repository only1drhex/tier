Calculating the Pressure Field in Cylindrical Coordinates

Consider the steady, two-dimensional, incompressible velocity field of with function f(ùúÉ, t) equal to 0. This represents a line vortex whose axis lies along the z-coordinate (Fig. 9‚Äì49). The velocity components are \(u_{r}=0 \text { and } u_{\theta}=K / r\), where K is a constant. Calculate the pressure as a function of r and ùúÉ.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION For a given velocity field, we are to calculate the pressure field.

Assumptions 1 The flow is steady. 2 The fluid is incompressible with constant properties. 3 The flow is two-dimensional in the rùúÉ-plane. 4 Gravity does not act in either the r– or the ùúÉ-direction.

Analysis The flow field must satisfy both the continuity and the momentum equations, Eqs. 9‚Äì62. For steady, two-dimensional, incompressible flow,

 

Incompressible continuity equation:

\(\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial\left(r u_{r}\right)}{\partial r}+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial\left(u_{\theta}\right)}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial\left(u_{z}\right)}{\partial z}=0\) (9-62a)

 

r-component of the incompressible Navier‚ÄìStokes equation:

\(\begin{aligned}&\rho\left(\frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial t}+u_{r} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial r}+\frac{u_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta}-\frac{u_{\theta}^{2}}{r}+u_{z} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial z}\right) \\&\quad=-\frac{\partial P}{\partial r}+\rho g_{r}+\mu\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial r}\right)-\frac{u_{r}}{r^{2}}+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} u_{r}}{\partial \theta^{2}}-\frac{2}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial^{2} u_{r}}{\partial z^{2}}\right]\end{aligned}\) (9-62b)

 

ùúÉ-component of the incompressible Navier‚ÄìStokes equation:

\(\begin{aligned}&\rho\left(\frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial t}+u_{r} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial r}+\frac{u_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{u_{r} u_{\theta}}{r}+u_{z} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial z}\right) \\&\quad=-\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial P}{\partial \theta}+\rho g_{\theta}+\mu\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial r}\right)-\frac{u_{\theta}}{r^{2}}+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta^{2}}+\frac{2}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial^{2} u_{\theta}}{\partial z^{2}}\right]\end{aligned}\) (9-62c)

 

z-component of the incompressible Navier‚ÄìStokes equation:

\(\begin{aligned}\rho\left(\frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial t}\right.&\left.+u_{r} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial r}+\frac{u_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial \theta}+u_{z} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial z}\right) \\&=-\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}+\rho g_{z}+\mu\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} u_{z}}{\partial \theta^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u_{z}}{\partial z^{2}}\right]\end{aligned}\) (9-62d)

 

\(\frac{1}{r} \underbrace{\frac{\partial(r u _r)}{\partial r}}_{0}+{\frac{1}{r}} \underbrace{\frac{\partial\left(u_{\theta}\right)}{\partial \theta}}_{0}+\underbrace{\frac{\partial\left(u_{z}\right)}{\partial z}}_{0}=0\)

 

Thus, the incompressible continuity equation is satisfied. Now we look at the ùúÉ component of the Navier‚ÄìStokes equation (Eq. 9‚Äì62c):

 

\(\rho(\underbrace{\frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial t}}_{0 \text { (steady) }}+\underbrace{u_{r} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial r}}_{(0)\left(-\frac{K}{r^{2}}\right)}+\underbrace{\frac{u_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta}}_{\left(\frac{K}{r^{2}}\right)(0)}+\underbrace{\frac{u_{r} u_{\theta}}{r}}_{0}+\underbrace{u_{z} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial z}}_{0(2-D)})\) \(=-\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial P}{\partial \theta}+\ \underbrace{\rho \text g_{\theta}}_{0}+\mu(\underbrace{\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial r}\right)}_{\frac{K}{r^{3}}}-\underbrace{\frac{u_{\theta}}{r^{2}}}_{\frac{K}{r^{3}}}+\underbrace{\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta^{2}}}_{0}+\underbrace{\frac{2}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta}}_{0}+\underbrace{\frac{\partial^{2} u_{\theta}}{\partial z^{2}}}_{0 \text { (2-D) }})\)

 

The ùúÉ-momentum equation therefore reduces to

 

ùúÉ-momentum: \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial \theta}=0\) (1)

 

Thus, the ùúÉ-momentum equation is satisfied if we can generate an appropriate pressure field that satisfies Eq. 1. In similar fashion, the r-momentum equation (Eq. 9‚Äì62b) reduces to

 

r–momentum: \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial r}=\rho \frac{K^{2}}{r^{3}}\) (2)

 

Thus, the r-momentum equation is satisfied if we can generate a pressure field that satisfies Eq. 2.

In order for a steady flow solution to exist, P cannot be a function of time. Furthermore, a physically realistic steady, incompressible flow field requires a pressure field P(r, ùúÉ) that is a smooth function of r and ùúÉ. Mathematically, this requires that the order of differentiation (r then ùúÉ versus ùúÉ then r ) should not matter (Fig. 9‚Äì50). We check whether this is so by cross-differentiating the pressure:

 

\(\frac{\partial^{2} P}{\partial r \partial \theta}=\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial \theta}\right)=0 \quad \text { and } \quad \frac{\partial^{2} P}{\partial \theta \partial r}=\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial r}\right)=0\) (3)

 

Equation 3 shows that P is indeed a smooth function of r and ùúÉ. Thus, the given velocity field satisfies the steady, two-dimensional, incompressible Navier‚ÄìStokes equation.

We integrate Eq. 1 with respect to ùúÉ to obtain an expression for P (r, ùúÉ),

 

Pressure field from ùúÉ-momentum: P(r, ùúÉ) = 0 + g(r) (4)

 

Note that we added an arbitrary function of the other variable r, rather than a constant of integration, since this is a partial integration. We take the partial derivative of Eq. 4 with respect to r to obtain

 

\(\frac{\partial P}{\partial r}=g^{\prime}(r)=\rho \frac{K^{2}}{r^{3}}\) (5)

 

where we have equated our result to Eq. 2 for consistency. We integrate Eq. 5 to obtain the function g(r):

 

\(g(r)=-\frac{1}{2} \rho \frac{K^{2}}{r^{2}}+C\) (6)

 

where C is an arbitrary constant of integration. Finally, we substitute Eq. 6 into Eq. 4 to obtain our final expression for P (r, ùúÉ). The result is

 

\(P(r, \theta)=-\frac{1}{2} \rho \frac{K^{2}}{r^{2}}+C\) (7)

 

Thus the pressure field for a line vortex decreases like 1/r¬≤ as we approach the origin. (The origin itself is a singular point.) This flow field is a simplistic model of a tornado or hurricane, and the low pressure at the center is the ‚Äúeye of the storm‚Äù (Fig. 9‚Äì51). We note that this flow field is irrotational, and thus Bernoulli‚Äôs equation can be used instead to calculate the pressure. If we call the pressure \(P_{\infty}\) far away from the origin (r ‚Üí ‚àû), where the local velocity approaches zero, Bernoulli‚Äôs equation shows that at any distance r from the origin,

 

Bernoulli equation: \(P+\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}=P_{\infty} \quad \rightarrow \quad P=P_{\infty}-\frac{1}{2} \rho \frac{K^{2}}{r^{2}}\) (8)

 

Equation 8 agrees with our solution (Eq. 7) from the Navier‚ÄìStokes equation if we set constant C equal to \(P_{\infty}\). A region of rotational flow near the origin would avoid the singularity there and would yield a more physically realistic model of a tornado.

Discussion For practice, try to obtain Eq. 7 by starting with Eq. 2 rather than Eq. 1; you should get the same answer.
