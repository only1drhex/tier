Guest Question.

Determine the strain energy in the cantilevered beam due to shear if the beam has a square cross section and is subjected to a uniform distributed load w, Fig. 14–14 a . EI and G are constant.

Admin
Solution

From the free-body diagram of an arbitrary section, Fig. 14–14 b , we have

\(+\uparrow \sum{F_y}=0; \quad\quad -V-wx=0 \quad\quad V=-wx \)

Since the cross section is square, the form factor \(f_s = \frac{6}{5}\) ( \(f_s =\frac{bh}{(\frac{1}{12}bh^3 )^2}\int_{-h/2}^{h/2}{\frac{b^2}{4b^2} \left(\frac{h^2}{4}-y^2 \right)^2 b dy }= \frac{6}{5}\)) and therefore \(U_i=\int_{0}^{L}{\frac{f_sV^2dx}{2GA} } \) becomes

\((U_i)_s=\int_{0}^{L}{\frac{\frac{6}{5}(-wx)^2dx}{2GA} } =\frac{3w^2}{5GA}\int_{0}^{L}{x^2dx} \)

or

\((U_i)_s=\frac{w^2L^3}{5GA}\)

NOTE: Using the results of , with \(A = a^2, I = \frac{1}{12}a^4\), the ratio of shear to bending strain energy is

\(\frac{(U_i)_s}{(U_i)_b} =\frac{w^2L^3/5Ga^2}{w^2L^5/40E(\frac{1}{12}a^4 )}=\frac{2}{3}\left(\frac{a}{L} \right)^2\frac{E}{G} \)

Since \(G = E/2(1 + v)\) and \(v ≤ \frac{1}{2}\) ( Sec. 10.6 ) , then as an upper bound, \(E = 3G\), so that

\(\frac{(U_i)_s}{(U_i)_b} =2\left(\frac{a}{L} \right)^2\)

It can be seen that this ratio will increase as L decreases. However, even for very short beams, where, say, \(L = 5a\), the contribution due to shear strain energy is only 8% of the bending strain energy. For this reason, the shear strain energy stored in beams is usually neglected in engineering analysis.
