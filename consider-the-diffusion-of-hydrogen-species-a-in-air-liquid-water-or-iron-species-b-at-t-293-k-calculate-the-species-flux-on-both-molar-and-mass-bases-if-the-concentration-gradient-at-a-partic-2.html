Guest Question.

Consider the diffusion of hydrogen (species A) in air, liquid water, or iron (species B) at T = 293 K.

Calculate the species flux on both molar and mass bases if the concentration gradient at a particular location is \( dC_{A}/dx = 1 kmol/m^{3} . m \). Compare the value of the mass diffusivity to the thermal diffusivity. The mole fraction of the hydrogen, \(x_{A}\), is much less than unity.

Admin
Solution

Known: Concentration gradient of hydrogen in air, liquid water, or iron at T = 293 K.

Find: Molar and mass fluxes of hydrogen and the relative values of the mass and thermal diffusivities for the three cases.

Assumptions: Steady-state conditions.

Properties: , hydrogen–air (298 K): \(D_{AB} = 0.41 × 10^{-4} m^{2}/s\), hydrogen– water (298 K): \(D_{AB} = 0.63 × 10^{-8} m^{2}/s\), hydrogen–iron (293 K): \(D_{AB} = 0.26 × 10^{-12} m^{2} /s.\) , air (293 K): \(α = 21.6 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s\); , water (293 K): \(k = 0.603 W/m.K, ρ = 998 kg/m^{3}, c_{p} = 4182 J/k \). K. , iron (300 K): \(α = 23.1 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s.\)

Analysis: Using Equation 14.14 \(D_{AB} \approx \frac{1}{3} \bar{c} \lambda _{mfp} \thicksim p^{-1} T^{3/2}\), we find that the mass diffusivity of hydrogen in air at T = 293 K is

\(D_{AB,T} = D_{AB,298 K}\times \left(\frac{T}{298 K} \right)^{3/2} = 0.41 \times 10^{-4} m^{2}/s \times \left(\frac{293 K}{298 K} \right)^{3/2} = 0.40 × 10^{-4} m^{2}/s\)

For the case where hydrogen is a dilute species, that is, \(x_{A}\) ≪ 1, the thermal properties of the medium can be taken to be those of the host medium, species B. The thermal diffusivity of water is

\(\alpha = \frac{k}{\rho c_{p}} = \frac{0.603 W/m . K}{998 kg/m^{3} . 4182 J/kg . K} =0.144 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s\)

The ratio of the thermal diffusivity to the mass diffusivity is the Lewis number, Le, defined in Equation 6.50 \(\overset{-}{Nu_{x}} = f(Re_{x} , Pr)\).

The molar flux of hydrogen is described by Fick’s law, Equation 14.13 \( J^{*}_{A} = -C D_{AB} \nabla x_{A} \)

\(J^{*}_{A} = -C D_{AB}\frac{dx_{A}}{dx}\)

The total molar concentration, C, is approximately constant since A is a dilute species; therefore

\(J^{*}_{A} = – D_{AB}\frac{dC_{A}}{dx}\)

Hence, for the hydrogen–air mixture,

\(J^{*}_{A} = -0.40 × 10^{-4} m^{2}/s × 1 \frac{ kmol}{m^{3} . m} =-4 × 10^{-5} \frac{ kmol}{s . m^{2}}\)

The mass flux of hydrogen in air is found from the expression

\( \dot{J}_{A} = M_{A} J^{*}_{A} = 2 \frac{kg}{ kmol} × \left(-4 × 10^{-5} \frac{ kmol}{s . m^{2}} \right)= -8 × 10^{-5} \frac{ kg}{s . m^{2}}\)

The results for the three different mixtures are summarized in the following table.

Species B \(α × 10^{6} (m^{2} /s)\) \(D_{AB} × 10^{6} (m^{2} /s)\) Le \(\dot{J}_{A} \times 10^{6} (kg/s . m^{2})\)
Air 21.6 40 0.54 80
Water 0.14 \(6.3 × 10^{-3}\) 23 \(13 × 10^{-3}\)
Iron 23.1 \(260 × 10^{-9}\) \(89× 10^{6}\) \(0.52 × 10^{-6}\)

Comments:
1. The thermal diffusivities of the three media vary by two orders of magnitude. We saw in Chapter 5 that this relatively broad range of thermal diffusivities is responsible for the different rates at which objects respond thermally during transient conduction processes. Mass diffusivities can vary by 8 or more orders of magnitude, with the highest diffusivities associated with diffusion in gases and the lowest diffusivities associated with diffusion in solids. Different materials respond to mass transfer at very different rates, depending on whether the host medium is a gas, liquid, or solid.
2. The ratio of the thermal diffusivity to the mass diffusivity, the Lewis number, is typi-cally of order unity for gases. This implies that changes in the thermal and species dis-tributions progress at similar rates in gases that undergo simultaneous heat and mass transfer by diffusion. In solids or liquids, thermal energy is conducted much more
readily than chemical species can be transferred by diffusion.
