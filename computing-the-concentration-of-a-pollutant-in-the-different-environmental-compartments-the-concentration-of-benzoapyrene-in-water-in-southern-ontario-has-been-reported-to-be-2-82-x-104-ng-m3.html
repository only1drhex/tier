Guest Question.

Computing the Concentration of a Pollutant in the Different Environmental Compartments

The concentration of benzo[a]pyrene in water in southern Ontario has been reported to be \(2.82 \times 10^{4} ng / m ^{3}\). Compute the likely concentration of this chemical in \(ng / m ^{3}\) in the air, soil, sediment, and fish.

Data:For benzo[a]pyrene \(\log _{10} K_{ OW , BP }=6.04\), and from we have \(K _{ AW , BP }=\) \(5.884 \times 10^{-5}\). Also, the density of soil is approximately 1500 \(kg / m ^{3}\) and that of sediment is about 1420 \(kg / m ^{3}\).

Admin
Solution

Starting from

 

\(C_{ BP }^{ A }=5.884 \times 10^{-5} C_{ BP }^{ W }=5.884 \times 10^{-5} \times 2.82 \times 10^{4}=1.66 \frac{ ng }{ m ^{3}}\)

 

and given that\(K_{ OW , BP }=10^{6.04}=1.096 \times 10^{6}, \text { we have }\)

 

\(K_{ SW , BP }=0.4 \times 0.02 \times 1.096 \times 10^{6}=8768 \frac{\frac{ g _{ BP }}{10^{6} g _{\text {soil }}}}{\frac{ g _{ BP }}{ m _{\text {water }}^{3}}}\)

 

so that

 

\(C_{ BP }^{ S }=8768 \times C_{ BP }^{ W }=8768 \frac{\frac{ g _{ BP }}{10^{6} g _{\text {soil }}}}{\frac{ g _{ BP }}{ m _{ water }^{3}}} \times 2.82 \times 10^{4} \frac{ ng _{ BP }}{ m _{\text {water }}^{3}}=2.47 \times 10^{8} \frac{ ng _{ BP }}{10^{6} g _{\text {soil }}}\)

 

\(\begin{array}{l}=2.47 \times 10^{8} \frac{ ng _{ BP }}{10^{6} g _{\text {soil }}} \times \frac{1 mg }{10^{6} ng } \times \frac{10^{3} g }{1 kg }=0.247 \frac{ mg _{ BP }}{ kg _{\text {soil }}} \\=0.247 ppm \text { (by weight) }\end{array}\)

 

and

 

\(C_{ BP }^{ S }=0.247 \frac{ mg _{ BP }}{ kg _{\text {soil }}} \times 1500 \frac{ kg _{\text {soil }}}{ m _{\text {soil }}^{3}} \times 10^{6} \frac{ ng }{ mg }=3.71 \times 10^{8} \frac{ ng _{ BP }}{ m _{\text {soil }}^{3}}\)

 

Also,

 

\(K_{ DW , BP }=0.4 \times 0.05 \times 1.096 \times 10^{6}=21920 \frac{\frac{ g _{ BP }}{10^{6} g _{\text {sediment }}}}{\frac{ g _{ BP }}{ m _{\text {water }}^{3}}}\)

 

and

 

\(C_{ BP }^{ D }=21920 \times C_{ BP }^{ W }=21920 \frac{\frac{ g _{ BP }}{10^{6} g _{\text {sediment }}}}{\frac{ g _{ BP }}{ m _{\text {water }}^{3}}} \times 2.82 \times 10^{4} \frac{ ng _{ BP }}{ m _{ water }^{3}}\)

 

\(\begin{array}{l}=6.18 \times 10^{8} \frac{ ng _{ BP }}{10^{6} g _{\text {sediment }}} \\=6.18 \times 10^{8} \frac{ ng _{ BP }}{10^{6} g _{\text {sediment }}} \times \frac{1 mg }{10^{6} ng } \times \frac{10^{3} g }{1 kg } \\=0.618 \frac{ mg _{ BP }}{ kg _{\text {sediment }}}=0.618 ppm \text { (by weight) }\end{array}\)

 

so that

 

\(C_{ BP }^{ D }=0.618 \frac{ mg _{ BP }}{ kg _{\text {sediment }}} \times 1420 \frac{ kg _{\text {sediment }}}{ m _{\text {sediment }}^{3}} \times 10^{6} \frac{ ng }{ mg }=8.78 \times 10^{8} \frac{ ng _{ BP }}{ m _{\text {sediment }}^{3}}\)

 

Finally, in the biota we have

 

\(\begin{aligned}C_{ BP }^{ B } &=0.05 \times K_{ OW , BP } \times C_{ BP }^{ W }=0.05 \times 1.096 \times 10^{6} \times 2.82 \times 10^{4}=1.55 \times 10^{9} \frac{ ng }{ m ^{3}} \\&=1.55 \frac{ mg _{ BP }}{ kg _{\text {biota }}}=1.55 ppm \text { (by weight) }\end{aligned}\)

 

Since the density of biota is approximately \(1 g / cc \text { or } 1000 kg / m ^{3}\) this concentration is also \(1.55 \times 10^{9} ng / m ^{3}\) (Note that in these calculations we have used the fact that 1 \(m^{3}\) of water is equal to \(10^{3}\) kg.)

The values computed above together with reported values of Mackay and Paterson\(^{13}\) are listed below.

 

Concentration \(\left(n g / m^{3}\right)\)
Environmental Compartment Calculated Reported
Water \(2.82 \times 10^{4}\)
Air 1.66 1.3 to 7.1
Soil \(3.71 \times 10^{8}\) \(1.1 \times 10^{8}\)
Sediment \(8.78 \times 10^{8}\) \(0.8 \times 10^{8} \text { to } 3 \times 10^{8}\)
Fish \(1.55 \times 10^{9}\) \(1.4 \times 10^{8}\)

 

Comment

The agreement between the results of the calculation here and reported data, while not perfect, is certainly reasonable given the simplicity of the thermodynamic model used and the complexity of environmental processes. In particular, the calculated results show that the large, orders-ofmagnitude difference in the concentration of a long-lived pollutant in the various environmental compartments can be explained using simple phase equilibrium concepts.

\(^{13}\)D. Mackay and S. Paterson, Environ. Sci. Technol., 25, 427–436 (1991).
