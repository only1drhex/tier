Guest Question.

Designing a warehouse layout with four docks

As an illustration of the solution procedure for designing a warehouse layout, consider the warehouse given in Figure \(10.29 a\). Storage bays are of size \(20^{\prime} \times 20^{\prime} .\) Docks \(P_{1}\) and \(P_{2}\) are for truck delivery; docks \(P_{3}\) and \(P_{4}\) are for rail delivery. Dedicated storage is used. Sixty percent of all item movement in and out of storage is from/to either \(P_{1}\) or \(P_{2}\), with each dock equally likely to be used. Forty percent of all item movement in and out of storage is equally divided between docks \(P_{3}\) and \(P_{4}\). Three products, \(\mathrm{A}, \mathrm{B}\), and \(\mathrm{C}\), are to be stored in the warehouse with only one type of product stored in a given storage bay.

Admin
Solution

Product A requires \(3,600 \mathrm{ft}^{2}\) of storage space and enters and leaves storage at a rate of 750 loads per month; product B requires \(6,400 \mathrm{ft}^{2}\) of storage space and enters and leaves storage at a rate of 900 loads per month; product \(\mathrm{C}\) requires \(4,000 \mathrm{ft}^{2}\) of storage space and enters and leaves storage at a rate of 800 loads per month. Rectilinear travel is used and is measured between the centroids of storage bays.

The \(f_{k}\) values are shown in each storage bay in Figure \(10.29 b\). As an illustration of the computation of an \(f_{k}\) value, suppose \(k=29\). Measuring the rectilinear distance from the centroid of storage bay 29 and each of the four docks gives \(d_{1,29}=120, d_{2,29}=100\), \(d_{3,29}=100\), and \(d_{4,29}=80 .\) Hence

\(\begin{aligned}f_{29} &=0.3(120)+0.3(100)+0.2(100)+0.2(80) \\&=102\end{aligned}\)

The number of storage bays required for each product equals \(S_{A}=3,600 / 400=9\), \(S_{B}=16\), and \(S_{C}=10 .\) The \(T_{j}\) values are \(T_{A}=750, T_{B}=900\), and \(T_{C}=800 .\) Therefore, the \(T_{j} / S_{j}\) values are \(T_{A} / S_{A}=83.33, T_{B} / S_{B}=56.25\), and \(T_{C} / S_{C}=80 .\) Hence the products will be numbered \(1(\mathrm{~A}), 2(\mathrm{C})\), and \(3(\mathrm{~B})\).

Product 1(A) requires nine storage bays; hence, the storage bays assigned to product A include \([17,18,19,9,25,20,10,26,21]\). Product \(2(\mathrm{C})\) requires 10 storage bays; hence, \([11,27,22,12,28,23,13,29,24,1]\) are assigned to product C. Product 3 (B) requires 16 storage bays; hence, product \(\mathrm{B}\) is assigned to storage locations \([14,30,33,2,15,31,34,3,\), \(16,32,35,4,36,5,37,6] .\) Storage bays \(7,8,38,39\), and 40 are available for equipment storage, restrooms, offices, and so on.

The layout design that minimizes expected distance traveled per unit time is given in Figure \(10.29 c\).

It is important to point out that the layout design obtained is not necessarily a final layout. Considerations other than expected distance traveled have not been incorporated; the use of the dedicated storage approach must be examined in a particular application. However, the layout does serve as a basis for evaluating other designs.
