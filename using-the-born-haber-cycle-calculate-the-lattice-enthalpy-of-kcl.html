Guest Question.

Using the Born–Haber cycle
Calculate the lattice enthalpy of KCl.

Admin
Solution

Collect your thoughts You need to construct a suitable Born–Haber cycle, such as the one shown in Fig. 15C.14. For the cycle to be useful, experimental values for the enthalpy changes for all the steps need to be available (for example, from tabulated data), apart, of course, from the step involving the formation of the lattice from the ions. For the cycle in Fig. 15C.14 the enthalpy changes are (for convenience, starting at the elements):

 

 

ΔH/(kJ \(mol^{−1}\))
[dissociation enthalpy of K(s)] +89 1. Sublimation of K(s)
[\(\frac{1}{2}\) × dissociation enthalpy of \(Cl_2\)(g)] +122 2. Dissociation of \(\frac{1}{2} Cl_2\)(g)
[ionization enthalpy of K(g)] +418 3. Ionization of K(g)
[electron-gain enthalpy of
Cl(g)]		 -349 4. Electron attachment to Cl(g)
[value to be determined] −Δ \(H_L\)/(kJ \(mol^{−1}\)) 5. Formation of solid from gaseous ions
[negative of enthalpy of
formation of KCl(s)]		 +437 6. Decomposition of compound to its elements in their reference states

Because this is a closed cycle, the sum of these enthalpy changes is equal to zero, and the lattice enthalpy can be inferred from the resulting equation.
The solution The sum of contributions around the cycle is
89 + 122 + 418 − 349 − Δ\(H_L\)/(kJ \(mol^{−1}\)) + 437 = 0
It follows that Δ\(H_L\) = +717 kJ \(mol^{−1}\).
