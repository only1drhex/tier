Guest Question.

A sonar transmitter operates at 2 impulses per second. If the device is held at the surface of fresh water at 40°F and the echo is received midway between impulses, how deep is the water? (The depth is known to be less than 2000 ft.)

Admin
Solution

The celerity of sound in water at 40°F is computed by using

\(c=\sqrt{\frac{\text { bulk modulus }}{\text { density of fluid }}}=\sqrt{\frac{296,000 \times 144}{1.94}}=4690 ft / sec\).

(a) The distance traveled by the sound wave (down and back again) in \(\frac{1}{2} \text { of } \frac{1}{2} sec , \text { or } \frac{1}{4}\) sec (one-half impulse time), is

2 × depth = velocity × time.

\(=4690 \times \frac{1}{4} \quad \text { and } \quad \text { depth }=586 ft \text { (least depth) }\).

(b) Had the depth exceeded 586 ft, for the echo to be heard midway between impulses the sound would have traveled for 3/2 of 1/2 sec, or 3/4 sec. Then

\(\text { depth }=\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)(4690) \times \frac{3}{4}=1760 ft\).

(c) For depths beyond the 2000-ft limit suggested, we obtain

\(\operatorname{depth}=\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)(4690) \times \frac{5}{4}=2930 ft\).

\(\text { depth }=\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)(4690) \times \frac{7}{4}=4100 ft \text {, and } so \text { on }\).
