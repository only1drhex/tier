Guest Question.

RADIOCARBON DATING

Before 1900 the activity per unit mass of atmospheric carbon due to the presence of \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\) averaged about 0.255 Bq per gram of carbon. (a) What fraction of carbon atoms were \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\)? (b) In analyzing an archaeological specimen containing 500 mg of carbon, you observe 174 decays in one hour. What is the age of the specimen, assuming that its activity per unit mass of carbon when it died was that average value of the air?

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

The key idea is that the present-day activity of a biological sample containing \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\) is related to both the elapsed time since it stopped taking in atmospheric carbon and its activity at that time. We use Eqs. (43.16) and (43.17) to solve for the age \(t\) of the specimen. In part (a) we determine the number of \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\) atoms \(N(t)\) from the activity \(-dN(t)/dt\) by using Eq. (43.16).

We find the total number of carbon atoms in 500 mg by using the molar mass of carbon (12.011 g/mol, given in Appendix D), and we use the result to calculate the fraction of carbon atoms that are \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\). The activity decays at the same rate as the number of \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\) nuclei; we use this and Eq. (43.17) to solve for the age \(t\) of the specimen.

\(-\frac{d N(t)}{d t}=\lambda N(t)\) (43.16)

\(N(t)=N_{0} e^{-\lambda t}\) (43.17)

EXECUTE:

(a) To use Eq. (43.16), we must first find the decay constant λ from Eq. (43.18) (\(T_{1 / 2}=\frac{\ln 2}{\lambda}=\frac{0.693}{\lambda}\)):

\(\begin{gathered}T_{1 / 2}=5730 \mathrm{y}=(5730 \mathrm{y})\left(3.156 \times 10^{7}\mathrm{~s} / \mathrm{y}\right)=1.808 \times 10^{11} \mathrm{~s} \\\lambda=\frac{\ln 2}{T_{1 / 2}}=\frac{0.693}{1.808 \times 10^{11} \mathrm{~s}}=3.83 \times 10^{-12} \mathrm{~s}^{-1}\end{gathered}\)

Then, from Eq. (43.16),

\(N(t)=\frac{-d N / d t}{\lambda}=\frac{0.255 \mathrm{~s}^{-1}}{3.83 \times 10^{-12} \mathrm{~s}^{-1}}=6.65 \times 10^{10} \text { atoms }\)

The total number of C atoms in 1 gram (1/12.011 mol) is (1/12.011)(6.022 × \(10^{23}\)) = 5.01 × \(10^{22}\). The ratio of \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\) atoms to all \(C\) atoms is

\(\frac{6.65 \times 10^{10}}{5.01 \times 10^{22}}=1.33 \times 10^{-12}\)

Only four carbon atoms in every 3 × \(10^{12}\) are \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\).

(b) Assuming that the activity per gram of carbon in the specimen when it died \((t=0) \text { was } 0.255 \mathrm{~Bq} / \mathrm{g}=\left(0.255 \mathrm{~s}^{-1} \cdot \mathrm{g}^{-1}\right) \times\) \((3600 \mathrm{~s} / \mathrm{h})=918 \mathrm{~h}^{-1} \cdot \mathrm{g}^{-1}\), the activity of 500 mg of carbon then was \((0.500 \mathrm{~g})\left(918 \mathrm{~h}^{-1} \cdot \mathrm{g}^{-1}\right)=459 \mathrm{~h}^{-1}\) The observed activity now, at time \(t\), is \(174 \mathrm{~h}^{-1}\). Since the activity is proportional to the number of radioactive nuclei, the activity ratio 174/459 = 0.379 equals the number ratio \(N(t) / N_{0}\).

Now we solve Eq. (43.17) for \(t\) and insert values for \(N(t) / N_{0}\) and λ:

\(t=\frac{\ln \left(N(t) / N_{0}\right)}{-\lambda}=\frac{\ln 0.379}{-3.83 \times 10^{-12} \mathrm{~s}^{-1}}=2.53 \times 10^{11} \mathrm{~s}=8020 \mathrm{y}\)

 

EVALUATE: After 8020 y the \({ }^{14} \mathrm{C}\) activity has decreased from 459 to 174 decays per hour. The specimen died and stopped taking \(CO_2\) out of the air about 8000 years ago.
