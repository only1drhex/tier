Guest Question.

Let us use Thevenin’s and Norton’s theorems to find \(V_o\) in the network in .

Admin
Solution

The circuit is redrawn in Fig. 5.8 a. To determine the Thévenin equivalent, we break the network at the 6-kΩ load as shown in Fig. 5.8 b. KVL indicates that the open-circuit voltage, \(V_{o c}\), is equal to 3 V plus the voltage \(V_{1}\), which is the voltage across the current source. The 2 mA from the current source flows through the two resistors (where else could it possibly go!) and, therefore, \(V_{1}=\left(2 \times 10^{-3}\right)(1 \mathrm{k}+2 \mathrm{k})=6 \mathrm{~V}\). Therefore, \(V_{\mathrm{oc}}=9 \mathrm{~V}\). By making both sources zero, we can find the Thévenin equivalent resistance, \(R_{\mathrm{Th}}\), using the circuit in Fig. 5.8 c. Obviously, \(R_{\mathrm{Th}}=3 \mathrm{k} \Omega\). Now our Thévenin equivalent circuit, consisting of \(V_{\mathrm{oc}}\) and \(R_{\mathrm{Th}}\), is connected back to the original terminals of the load, as shown in Fig. 5.8 d. Using a simple voltage divider, we find that \(V_{o}=6 \mathrm{~V}\).

 

To determine the Norton equivalent circuit at the terminals of the load, we must find the short-circuit current as shown in Fig. 5.8 e. Note that the short circuit causes the 3-V source to be directly across (i.e., in parallel with) the resistors and the current source. Therefore, \(I_{1}=3 /(1 \mathrm{k}+2 \mathrm{k})=1 \mathrm{~mA}\). Then, using KCL, \(I_{\mathrm{sc}}\)=3 mA. We have already determined \(R_{\mathrm{Th}}\), and, therefore, connecting the Norton equivalent to the load results in the circuit in Fig. 5.8 f. Hence, \(V_{o}\) is equal to the source current multiplied by the parallel resistor combination, which is 6 V.
