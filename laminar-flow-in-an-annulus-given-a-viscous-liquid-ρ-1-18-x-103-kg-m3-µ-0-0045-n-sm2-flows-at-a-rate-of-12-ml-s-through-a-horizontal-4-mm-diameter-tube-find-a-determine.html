Laminar Flow in an Annulus

GIVEN A viscous liquid \(\left(\rho=1.18 \times 10^{3} kg / m ^{3} ; \mu=\right.\) 0.0045 N . \(\left. s / m ^{2}\right)\)) flows at a rate of 12 ml/s through a horizontal, 4-mm-diameter tube.

FIND (a) Determine the pressure drop along a l-m length of the tube which is far from the tube entrance so that the only component of velocity is parallel to the tube axis. (b) If a 2-mm-diameter rod is placed in the 4-mm-diameter tube to form a symmetric annulus, what is the pressure drop along a l-m length if the flowrate remains the same as in part (a)?

Admin
Solution

a) We first calculate the Reynolds number, Re, to determine whether or not the flow is laminar. With the diameter D = 4 m, the mean velocity is

 

\(\begin{aligned}V &=\frac{Q}{(\pi / 4) D^{2}}=\frac{(12 ml / s )\left(10^{-6} m ^{3} / ml \right)}{(\pi / 4)(0.004 m )^{2}} \\&=0.955 m / s\end{aligned}\)

 

and, therefore,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\operatorname{Re} &=\frac{\rho V D}{\mu}=\frac{\left(1.18 \times 10^{3} kg / m ^{3}\right)(0.955 m / s )(0.004 m )}{0.0045 N \cdot s / m ^{2}} \\&=1000\end{aligned}\)

 

Since the Reynolds number is well below the critical value of 2100 we can safely assume that the flow is laminar. Thus, we can apply Eq. 6.151, which gives for the pressure drop

 

\(Q=\frac{\pi R^{4} \Delta p}{8 \mu \ell}\) (6.151)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta p &=\frac{8 \mu \ell Q}{\pi R^{4}} \\&=\frac{8\left(0.0045 N \cdot s / m ^{2}\right)(1 m )\left(12 \times 10^{-6} m ^{3} / s \right)}{\pi(0.002 m )^{4}} \\&=8.59 kPa\end{aligned}\)

 

(b) For flow in the annulus with an outer radius \(r_{o}=0.002 m\) and an inner radius \(5 r_{i}=0.001 m\), the mean velocity is

 

\(\begin{aligned}V &=\frac{Q}{\pi\left(r_{o}^{2}-r_{i}^{2}\right)}=\frac{12 \times 10^{-6} m ^{3} / s }{(\pi)\left[(0.002 m )^{2}-(0.001 m )^{2}\right]} \\&=1.27 m / s\end{aligned}\)

 

and the Reynolds number [based on the hydraulic diameter, \(\left.D_{h}=2\left(r_{o}-r_{i}\right)=2(0.002 m -0.001 m )=0.002 m \right] \text { is }\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\operatorname{Re} &=\frac{\rho D_{h} V}{\mu} \\&=\frac{\left(1.18 \times 10^{3} kg / m ^{3}\right)(0.002 m )(1.27 m / s )}{0.0045 N \cdot s / m ^{2}} \\&=666\end{aligned}\)

 

This value is also well below 2100 so the flow in the annulus should also be laminar. From Eq. 6.156,

 

\(Q=\frac{\pi \Delta p}{8 \mu \ell}\left[r_{o}^{4}-r_{i}^{4}-\frac{\left(r_{o}^{2}-r_{i}^{2}\right)^{2}}{\ln \left(r_{o} / r_{i}\right)}\right]\) (6.156)

 

\(\Delta p=\frac{8 \mu \ell Q}{\pi}\left[r_{o}^{4}-r_{i}^{4}-\frac{\left(r_{o}^{2}-r_{i}^{2}\right)^{2}}{\ln \left(r_{o} / r_{i}\right)}\right]^{-1}\)

 

so that

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta p=& \frac{8\left(0.0045 N \cdot s / m ^{2}\right)(1 m )\left(12 \times 10^{-6} m ^{3} / s \right)}{\pi} \\& \times\left\{(0.002 m )^{4}-(0.001 m )^{4}\right.\\&\left.-\frac{\left[(0.002 m )^{2}-(0.001 m )^{2}\right]^{2}}{\ln (0.002 m / 0.001 m )}\right\}^{-1} \\=& 68.2 kPa\end{aligned}\)

 

COMMENTS The pressure drop in the annulus is much larger than that of the tube. This is not a surprising result, since to maintain the same flow in the annulus as that in the open tube, the average velocity must be larger (the cross-sectional area is smaller) and the pressure difference along the annulus must overcome the shearing stresses that develop along both an inner and an outer wall.

By repeating the calculations for various radius ratios, \(r_{i} / r_{o}\), the results shown in Fig. E6.10 are obtained. It is seen that the pressure drop ratio, \(\Delta p_{\text {annulus }} / \Delta p_{\text {tube }}\) (i.e., the pressure drop in the annulus compared to that in a tube with a radius equal to the outer radius of the annulus, \(r_{0}\)), is a strong function of the radius ratio.

Even an annulus with a very small inner radius will have a pressure drop significantly larger than that of a tube. For example, if the inner radius is only 1/100 of the outer radius, \(\Delta p_{\text {annulus }} / \Delta p_{\text {tube }}=1.28\).

As shown in the figure, for larger inner radii, the pressure drop ratio is much larger [i.e., \(\Delta p_{\text {anmulus }} / \Delta p_{\text {tube }}=7.94 \text { for } r_{i} / r_{o}=0.50\) as in part (b) of this example].
