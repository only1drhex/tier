Guest Question.

Cooling of an Iron Block by Water

A 50-kg iron block at 80°C is dropped into an insulated tank that contains \(0.5 m^3\) of liquid water at 25°C. Determine the temperature when thermal equilibrium is reached.

Admin
Solution

An iron block is dropped into water in an insulated tank. The final temperature when thermal equilibrium is reached is to be determined.

Assumptions 1 Both water and the iron block are incompressible substances. 2 Constant specific heats at room temperature can be used for water and the iron. 3 The system is stationary, and thus the kinetic and potential energy changes are zero, ΔKE = ΔPE = 0 and ΔE = ΔU. 4 There are no electrical, shaft, or other forms of work involved. 5 The system is well insulated and thus there is no heat transfer.

Analysis We take the entire contents of the tank as the system (Fig. 4–35). This is a closed system since no mass crosses the system boundary during the process. We observe that the volume of a rigid tank is constant, and thus there is no boundary work. The energy balance on the system can be expressed as

\(\begin{aligned}\underbrace{E_{\mathrm{in}}-E_{\mathrm{out}}}_{\begin{array}{c}\text { Net energy transfer } \\\text { by heat, work, and mass }\end{array}} &=\underbrace{\Delta E_{\mathrm{system}}}_{\begin{array}{r}\text { Change in internal, kinetic, } \\\text { potential, etc., energies }\end{array}} \\0 &=\Delta U\end{aligned}\)

The total internal energy U is an extensive property, and therefore it can be expressed as the sum of the internal energies of the parts of the system. Then the total internal energy change of the system becomes

\(\begin{array}{r}\Delta U_{\text {sys }}=\Delta U_{\text {iron }}+\Delta U_{\text {water }}=0 \\\\{\left[m c\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)\right]_{\text {iron }}+\left[m c\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)\right]_{\text {water }}=0}\end{array}\)

The specific volume of liquid water at or about room temperature can be taken to be \(0.001 m^3/kg\). Then the mass of the water is

\(m_{\text {water }}=\frac{V}{v}=\frac{0.5 \mathrm{~m}^{3}}{0.001 \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg}}=500 \mathrm{~kg}\)

The specific heats of iron and liquid water are determined from to be \(c_{iron} = 0.45 kJ/kg·°C\) and \(c_{water} = 4.18 kJ/kg·°C\). Substituting these values into the energy equation, we obtain

\(\begin{array}{c}(50 \mathrm{~kg})\left(0.45 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)\left(T_{2}-80^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)+(500 \mathrm{~kg})\left(4.18 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)\left(T_{2}-25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)=0 \\T_{2}=25.6^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\end{array}\)

Therefore, when thermal equilibrium is established, both the water and iron will be at 25.6°C.

Discussion The small rise in water temperature is due to its large mass and large specific heat.
