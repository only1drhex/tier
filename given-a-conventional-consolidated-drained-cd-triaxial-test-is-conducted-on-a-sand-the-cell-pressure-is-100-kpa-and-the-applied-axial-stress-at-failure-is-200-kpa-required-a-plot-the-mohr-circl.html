Guest Question.

Given:

A conventional consolidated-drained (CD) triaxial test is conducted on a sand. The cell pressure is 100 kPa, and the applied axial stress at failure is 200 kPa.

Required:

a. Plot the Mohr circles for both the (1) initial and (2) failure stress conditions.

b. Determine \(\phi\) (assume c=0).

c. Determine (3) the shear stress on the failure plane at failure \(\tau_{f f}\), and find (4) the theoretical angle of the failure plane in the specimen. Also (5) determine the angle of maximum obliquity.

d. Determine (6) the maximum shear stress at failure \(\tau_{\max }\) and (7) the angle of the plane on which it acts; calculate (8) the available shear strength on this plane and (9) the factor of safety on this plane.

Admin
Solution

a. Refer to Fig. 11.14(b) and Fig. Ex. 11.8. (1) The initial conditions are shown at the top of Fig: Ex. 11.8 for the conventional triaxial test. The initial stress is equal to the cell pressure \(\sigma_{\text {cell }}\), and it is equal in all directions (hydrostatic). Therefore the Mohr circle for the initial stress conditions is a point at 100 kPa, as shown in the Mohr diagram of Fig. Ex. 11.8.

(2) At failure, the \(\sigma_{\text {axial }}=\left(\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}\right)_{f}=200\) kPa, and the cell pressure \(\sigma_{\text {cell }}=100\) kPa is held constant during the conventional test. So

 

\(\sigma_{1 f}=\left(\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}\right)_{f}+\sigma_{3 f}=200+100=300 kPa\)

 

Now we can plot the Mohr circle at failure; \(\sigma_{1 f}=300\) and \(\sigma_{3 f}=100\). The center is at \(\left(\sigma_{1}+\sigma_{3}\right) / 2=200\), and the radius is \(\left(\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}\right) / 2=100\). The circle at failure is shown in Fig. Ex. 11.8.

b. We find \(\phi\) graphically to be \(30^{\circ}\). We can also use Eq. (11.13) if we prefer an analytical solution. Thus

 

\(\sin \phi=\frac{\left(\sigma_{1 f}-\sigma_{3 f}\right)}{\left(\sigma_{1 f}+\sigma_{3 f}\right)}\) (11.13)

 

\(\phi=\arcsin \frac{\sigma_{1 f}-\sigma_{3 f}}{\sigma_{1 f}+\sigma_{3 f}}=\arcsin \frac{200}{400}=30^{\circ}\)

 

c. (3) From the Mohr failure hypothesis, the coordinates of the point of tangency of the Mohr failure envelope and the Mohr circle at failure are \(\left(\sigma_{f f}, \tau_{f f}\right)\). From Eq. (11.9), we know that \(\tau_{f f}=\sigma_{f f} \tan \phi\), but unlike the direct shear test we don’t know \(\sigma_{f f}\) in the triaxial test. Look carefully at Fig. 11.10. The small angle near the top of the Mohr circle is \(\phi\) (by a theorem from high school geometry). Therefore, since \(c=0\), and solving for \(\sigma_{f f}\), we obtain

 

\(\tau_{f f}=\sigma_{f f} \tan \phi+c\) (11.9)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\sigma_{f f} &=\frac{\sigma_{1 f}+\sigma_{3 f}}{2}-\frac{\sigma_{1 f}-\sigma_{3 f}}{2} \sin \phi \\&=200-100 \sin 30^{\circ}=150 kPa \\\tau_{f f} &=\sigma_{f f} \tan \phi=150 \tan 30^{\circ}=87 kPa\end{aligned}\)

 

(4) The theoretical angle of inclination of the failure plane can be found graphically by the pole method or analytically. From the stress conditions at failure shown in Fig. Ex. 11.8, the pole is at \((100,0)\), and \(\alpha_{f}\) can be measured to be \(60^{\circ}\).

For the analytical solution, use Eq. (11.10)

 

\(\alpha_{f}=45^{\circ}+\frac{\phi}{2}\) (11.10)

 

\(\alpha_{f}=45^{\circ}+\frac{\phi}{2}=60^{\circ}\)

 

(5) The inclination of the Mohr failure envelope is \(30^{\circ}\) and the point of tangency determines the condition of maximum obliquity. In other words, the ratio \(\tau_{f f} / \sigma_{f f}\) is a maximum at this point on the Mohr circle and thus the maximum obliquity angle \(\theta_{\max }=\phi\).

 

d. (6) \(\tau_{\max }=\frac{\sigma_{1 f}-\sigma_{3 f}}{2}=100\) kPa.

 

(7) From the pole, the plane of \(\tau_{\max }\) is inclined at \(45^{\circ}\) from the horizontal.

(8) The available \(\tau\) [see Fig. 11.9(b)] can be determined from

 

\(\begin{aligned}\tau_{\text {available }} &=\sigma_{n} \tan \phi=\frac{\sigma_{1 f}+\sigma_{3 f}}{2} \tan \phi \\&=200 \tan 30^{\circ}=115.5 kPa\end{aligned}\)

 

which is greater than \(\tau_{\max }=100 kPa\). Therefore the factor of safety on the \(45^{\circ}\) plane [Eq. (11.11)] is

 

\(\text { factor of safety (F.S.) }=\frac{\tau_{f f} \text { (available) }}{\tau_{f}(\text { applied })}\) (11.11)

 

\(F . S .=\frac{\tau_{\text {available }}}{\tau_{\max }}=\frac{115.5}{100}=1.16\)

 

(9) Note that the factor of safety on the \(\alpha_{f}=60^{\circ}\) plane is

 

\(F.S .=\frac{\tau_{\text {available }}}{\tau_{f f}}=\frac{86.6}{86.6}=1\)

 

A factor of safety of one makes sense in this latter case because the specimen is at failure.
