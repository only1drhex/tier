Guest Question.

The longitudinal equations for an airplane having poor handling qualities follow. Use state feedback to provide stability augmentation so that the augmented aircraft has the following short- and long-period (phugoid) characteristics:

\(\left[\begin{matrix} \Delta \dot{u } \\ \Delta \dot{w } \\ \Delta \dot{q } \\ \Delta \dot{\theta } \end{matrix} \right]=\left[\begin{matrix} -0.01& 0 .1 & 0 & - 32.2 \\ - 0.40 & - 0.8 & 180 & 0 \\ 0 & - 0.003 & - 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix} \Delta {u } \\ \Delta {w } \\ \Delta {q } \\ \Delta {\theta } \end{matrix} \right]+\left[\begin{matrix} 0 \\-10 \\ -2.8 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right]= [\Delta \delta _e]\)

 

 

\(\zeta _{sp}=0.6 \omega _{n_{sp}}=3.0 rad/s\)

 

\(\zeta _{p}=0.05 \omega _{n_{p}}=0.1 rad/s\)
Admin
Solution

As the order of the system increases beyond 3, simple hand calculations similar to become quite difficult. As stated earlier, software packages are available for solving state feedback design problems. Later in this chapter we use such programs to solve selected problems; however, for this example problem we use the Bass-Gura method, which lends itself to simple matrix manipulations. The state feedback gains can be estimated using the Bass-Gura technique described in Chapter 9. The feedback gains are found by solving the following equations:

\(k=[(VW)^T]^{-1}[\bar{a} -a]\)

where V is the controllability matrix, W is a transformation matrix, and \(\bar{a} and a \) are vectors made up of the coefficients of the characteristic equation of the augmented or closed-loop system \((A-Bk^T)\) and the characteristic equation of the open-loop plant matrix A.

The characteristic equation for the augmented or closed-loop system is determined by deciding on what closed-loop performance is desired. For this particular problem the desired eigenvalues are specified in terms of the short- and long-period damping ratio and undamped natural frequency. The desired characteristic equation can be written in terms of the damping and frequency as follows:

\((\lambda ^2+2\zeta _{sp}\omega _{n_{sp}}\lambda +\omega^2 _{n_{sp}})(\lambda ^2+2\zeta _{p}\omega _{n_{p}}\lambda +\omega^2 _{n_{p}})=0\)

Substituting the numerical values of \(\zeta _{sp}, \omega _{n_{sp}}, \zeta _{p} \text{ and } \omega _{n_{p}} \) into the preceding equation and expanding yields

\((\lambda ^2 + 3.6\lambda + 9)(\lambda ^2 + 0.01\lambda + 0.01) = 0\)

or
\(\lambda ^4+3.61\lambda ^3+9.05\lambda ^2+0.126\lambda +0.09=0\)

The vector \(\bar{a}\) is created from the coefficients of the desired characteristic equation:

\(\lambda ^4+\bar{a_1}\lambda ^3+\bar{a_2}\lambda ^2+\bar{a_3}\lambda +\bar{a_4}=0\)

or

\(\bar{a}= \left[\begin{matrix} \bar{a_1} \\\bar{a_2} \\ \bar{a_3} \\ \bar{a_4} \end{matrix} \right]= \left[\begin{matrix}3.61 \\9.05 \\ 0.126 \\ 0.09 \end{matrix} \right]\)

The characteristic equation of the open-loop system is obtained by solving the equation

\(\left|\lambda I-A\right| =0\)

which yields

\(\lambda ^4+1.31\lambda ^3+0.993\lambda ^2+0.0294\lambda +0.0386=0\)

The vector a is created from the coefficients of the open-loop characteristic equation:

\(\lambda ^4+a_1\lambda ^3+a_2\lambda ^2+a_3\lambda +a_4=0\)

or

\( a=\left[\begin{matrix}{a_1} \\{a_2} \\ {a_3} \\ {a_4} \end{matrix} \right]=\left[\begin{matrix} 1.31 \\0.993 \\ 0.0294 \\ 0.0386 \end{matrix} \right]\)

Continuing with the solution, we need to determine the controllability matrix, V. In Chapter 9 we showed that the controllability matrix is defined in terms of the plant and control matrices. For the fourth-order system under consideration here, the controllability matrix is

\(V= \left[\begin{matrix} B & AB & A^2B & A^3B\end{matrix} \right] \)

The elements of the V matrix can be readily calculated by performing the appropriate matrix multiplications:

\(AB= \left[\begin{matrix} -0.01& 0 .1 & 0 & – 32.2 \\ – 0.40 & – 0.8 & 180 & 0 \\ 0 & – 0.003 & – 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1.0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right]\left[\begin{matrix} 0 \\-10 \\ -2.8 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right]= \left[\begin{matrix}- 1.0 \\-496 \\ 1.43 \\- 2.8 \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

\(A^2B=A[AB]=\left[\begin{matrix} -0.01& 0 .1 & 0 & – 32.2 \\ – 0.40 & – 0.8 & 180 & 0 \\ 0 & – 0.003 & – 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1.0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix}- 1.0 \\-496 \\ 1.43 \\- 2.8 \end{matrix} \right] \)

 

\(A^2B= \left[\begin{matrix}40.57 \\654.6 \\0.773 \\1.43 \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

\(A^3B=A[A^2B]=\left[\begin{matrix} -0.01& 0 .1 & 0 & – 32.2 \\ – 0.40 & – 0.8 & 180 & 0 \\ 0 & – 0.003 & – 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1.0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix}40.57 \\654.6 \\0.773 \\1.43 \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

\(A^3B= \left[\begin{matrix}19.008 \\- 400.77 \\- 2.35 \\0.773 \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

Substituting the column. matrices into the definition of V yields

\(V=\left[\begin{matrix}0 & -1.0 & 40.57 & 1 9 .008 \\ – 10 & 4 9 6 & 65 4 .6 & -400.77 \\- 2.8 & 1.43 & 0 .773 & – 2.35 \\ 0 & – 2.8 & 1.43 & 0.7 7 3 \end{matrix} \right]\)

The transformation matrix W is required if the plant matrix A is not in the companion form. For this particular problem the A matrix is not in companion form; therefore, the transformation matrix must be developed. As was shown in Chapter 9 the transformation matrix is defined in terms of the coefficients of the characteristic equation of the plant matrix. For this particular example of a fourth-order system the W matrix is defined as

\(W=\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ 0 & 1 & a_1 & a_2 \\ 0 &0 & 1 & a_1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right]=\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 1.31 & 0.993 & 0.0294 \\ 0 & 1 & 1.31 &0.993 \\ 0 &0 & 1 & 1.31 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right]\)

Now we are in a position to calculate \([(VW)^T]^{-1}\). This will be accomplished in the following steps:

\(VW=\left[\begin{matrix}0 & -1 & 40.57 & 1 9 .008 \\ – 10 & 4 9 6 & 65 4 .6 & -400.781 \\- 2.8 & 1.43 & 0 .773 & – 2.35 \\ 0 & – 2.8 & 1.43 & 0.7 7 3 \end{matrix} \right] \left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 1.31 & 0.993 & 0.0294 \\ 0 & 1.0 & 1.31 &0.993 \\ 0 &0 & 1 .0& 1.31 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1.0 \end{matrix} \right] \)

 

\( VW=\left[\begin{matrix}0 & -1.0 & 39.23 & 71.2 \\ – 10.0 & – 509.1 & – 5.01 & – 36.1 \\- 2.8 & – 2.24 & – 0.134 & 0 \\ 0 & -2.8&-2.24&-0.13 \end{matrix} \right] \)

The transpose of the matrix VW is obtained by interchanging the rows and columns:

\( ( VW)^T=\left[\begin{matrix}0 & -10.0 & – 2.8 & 0 \\ – 1.0 & – 509 & -2.234 & – 2.8 \\39.3 & – 5. 1 & – 0.13 & – 2.24 \\ 71.2&-36.1 &0.0&-0.13 \end{matrix} \right] \)

The inverse of the matrix \([(VW)^T]^{-1}\) follows:

\([(VW)^T]^{-1}=\left[\begin{matrix} 0.0008 & -0.0010 & 0.0004 & 0.0 138\\ 0.0014 & – 0.0019 & 0.0025 & – 0.0014 \\ -0.3622 & 0.0068 & -0.0089 & 0 .0050 \\ 0.0321 & -0.0135 & -0.4446 & 0 .245 \end{matrix} \right] \)

The state feedback gains can now be calculated from the equation

\(k=[(VW)^T]^{-1}(\bar{a} -a)\)

 

\(=\left[\begin{matrix} 0.0008 & -0.0010 & 0.0004 & 0.0 138\\ 0.0014 & – 0.0019 & 0.0025 & – 0.0014 \\ -0.3622 & 0.0068 & -0.0089 & 0 .0050 \\ 0.0321 & -0.0135 & -0.4446 & 0 .245 \end{matrix} \right]\left( \left[\begin{matrix}3.61 \\9.05 \\ 0.126 \\ 0.09 \end{matrix} \right]- \left[\begin{matrix} 1.31 \\0.993 \\ 0.0294 \\ 0.0386 \end{matrix} \right]\right) \)

 

\(=\left[\begin{matrix} – 0.0055 \\- 0.0120 \\ – 0.7785 \\ – 0.0656 \end{matrix} \right]\)

Having determined the feedback gains we can now define the control law. The stability augmentation control law is

\(\Delta \delta _e=-k^Tx= -[ -0.0055 – 0.0120 -0.7785 -0.0656]\left[\begin{matrix} \Delta {u } \\ \Delta {w } \\ \Delta {q } \\ \Delta {\theta } \end{matrix} \right] \)

 

\(= 0.0055 \Delta {u } + 0.0120 \Delta {w } + 0.7785 \Delta {q } + 0.0656 \Delta {\theta }\)
