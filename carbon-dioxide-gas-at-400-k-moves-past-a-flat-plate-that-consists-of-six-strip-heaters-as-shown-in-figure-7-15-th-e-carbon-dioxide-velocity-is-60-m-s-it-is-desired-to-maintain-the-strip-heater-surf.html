Guest Question.

Carbon dioxide gas at 400 K moves past a flat plate that consists of six strip heaters, as shown in Figure 7.15. The carbon dioxide velocity is 60 m/s. It is desired to maintain the strip-heater-surface
temperature at 600 K. Determine the wattage requirement for each heater to do this. Each heater is
40 mm wide and has an effective heating length (into the page) of 160 mm.

Admin
Solution

Heat will be transferred from each heater to the carbon dioxide, and the carbon dioxide film temperature increases with increasing distance along the plate. Thus, we expect that the heater ratings will not all be equal.

Assumptions

1. Steady-state conditions exist.
2. Carbon dioxide properties are constant.
3. The bottom surface is insulated, and all heat is transferred via convection to the carbon
dioxide.

 

From Appendix Table D.2, for carbon dioxide at a film temperature of \(\left(400+600\right)/2=500K \) ,we read

 

\(\rho =1.0732 kg/m^{3}\)

 

\(c_{p} =1013 J/\left(kg.K\right)\)

 

\(\nu =21.67\times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s\)

 

\(k_{f} =0.03352W/\left(m.K\right)\)

 

\(\alpha =0.3084\times 10^{-4} m^{2}/s\)

 

Pr=0.702

 

To determine which correlation applies, first find where transition occurs. At transition

\(Re_{x_{cr} } =5\times 10^{5}=\frac{V_{\infty } x_{cr} }{\nu }\)

 

\(x_{cr} =\frac{5\times 10^{5}\left(21.67\times 10^{-6} \right) }{60}\)

 

\(x_{cr}=0.181m=18.1cm\)

 

Therefore, transition occurs on the fifth heater. Equation 7.20 applies to the plates over which laminar flow exists, and Equation 7.67 applies to the turbulent flows. For the first plate, we have

\(\overline{Nu} _{1} =0.664Re^{1/2}_{x} Pr^{1/3}\) (7.20)

or

\(\overline{h} _{1}=0.664\frac{k_{f} }{x}\left\lgroup\frac{V_{\infty }x_{1} }{\nu } \right\rgroup ^{1/2} Pr^{1/3}\)

 

Substituting and simplifying

 

 

\(\overline{h} _{1}=0.664\left(0.03352\right) \left\lgroup\frac{60}{21.67\times 10^{-6} } \right\rgroup ^{1/2} \left(0.702\right) ^{1/3}\frac{1}{x^{1/2}_{1} }\)

 

which becomes

\(\overline{h} _{1}=\frac{32.9}{x^{1/2}_{1} } \)

This equation applies over the entire first plate. With \(x_{1}\) = 0.04 m

\(\overline{h} _{1}=165W/\left(m^{2}.K \right) \)

 

The heat transferred by the first heater then is

 

\(q_{1} =\overline{h} _{1} \left(x_{1}b \right) \left(T_{w}-T_{\infty } \right) =165\left(0.04\right) \left(0.16\right)\left(600-400\right)\)

 

The first heater must supply

\(q_{1}=211W\)

We can now apply Equation i to determine how much heat the first two heaters together will supply. We can use Equation i because the physical properties are constant and the average convection coefficient varies only with length. Thus

\(\overline{h} _{2}=32.9/\left(0.08\right) ^{1/2} =116W/\left(m^{2}.K \right) \)

Therefore

\(q_{1}+q_{2} =116\left(0.08\right) \left(0.16\right) \left(200\right) =298W\)

 

So the second heater is to supply 298 âˆ’ 211, or

\(q_{2} =87W\)

 

Proceeding for the first three heaters

\(\overline{h} _{3} =\frac{32.9}{\left(0.12\right) ^{1/2} } =95W/\left(m^{2}.K \right) \)

 

\(q_{1}+q_{2}+q_{3} =95\left(0.12\right) \left(0.16\right) \left(200\right) =365\)

 

\(q_{3} =365-298\)

or

\(q_{3}=67W\)

For the frst four heaters

\(\overline{h} _{4}=\frac{32.9}{(0.16)^{1/2}}=82.3W/\left(m^{2}.K \right) ^{1/2} \)

 

\(q_{1}+q_{2} +q_{3} +q_{4} =82.3\left(0.16\right) \left(0.16\right) \left(200\right) =421\)

or

\(q_{4} =56W\)

At the end of the fifth heater, the Reynolds number is

 

\(Re_{5}=\frac{V_{\infty }x_{5} }{\nu } =\frac{60\left[5\left(0.04\right) \right] }{21.67\times 10^{-6} }=5.54\times 10^{5} \)

 

which is turbulent. Equation 7.39 applies

\(\overline{Nu_{L} } =\frac{\overline{h_{L} } x}{k_{f} } =\left[0.0539\left(Re_{L} \right) ^{4/5}-830 \right] Pr^{1/3} \)

 

The average convection coefficient for the first five heaters then is

\(\overline{h} _{5}=\frac{0.03352}{5\left(0.04\right) } \left[0.0359\left(5.54\times 10^{5} \right) ^{4/5}-830 \right] \left(0.702\right) ^{1/3} \)

 

 

\(\overline{h} _{5}=86.7W/\left(m^{2}.K \right) \)

 

The heat transferred from the first five heaters then is

\(q_{1}+q_{2}+q_{3}+q_{4} +q_{5} =86.7\left(0.2\right) \left(0.16\right)\left(200\right) =555\)

which gives

\(q_{5}=134W\)

 

At the end of the sixth heater

 

\(Re_{L}=\frac{60\left[6\left(0.04\right) \right] }{21.67\times 10^{-6} } =6.65\times 10^{5} \)

The convection coefficient becomes

\(\overline{h} _{6} =\frac{0.03352}{6\left(0.04\right) } \left[0.0359\left(6.65\times 10^{5} \right) ^{4/5}-830 \right] \left(0.702\right) ^{1/3}\)

 

\(\overline{h} _{6}=99.7W/\left(m^{2}.K \right)\)

 

The total heat to be supplied by all six heaters is

 

\(q_{1}+q_{2}+q_{3}+q_{4} +q_{5}+q_{6} =99.7\left[6\left(0.04\right) \right] \left(0.16\right)\left(200\right) =766\)

 

We now obtain

\(q_{6}=211W\)
