Guest Question.

The equations of motion for a station-keeping satellite (such as a weather satellite) are

\(\ddot{x}-2 \omega \dot{y}-3 \omega^{2} x=0, \quad \ddot{y}+2 \omega \dot{x}=u,\),

where,

x = radial perturbation,

y = longitudinal position perturbation,

u = engine thrust in the y – direction,

as depicted in Fig. 7.94. If the orbit is synchronous with the earth’s rotation, then \(\omega = 2 \pi /(3600 \times 24) rad / sec\).

a) Is the state \(\left[\begin{array}{llll}x & \dot{x} & y & \dot{y}\end{array}\right]^{T}\) observable?

b) Choose \(x =\left[\begin{array}{llll} x & \dot{x} & y & \dot{y}\end{array}\right]^{T}\) as the state vector and y as the measurement, and design a full-order observer with poles placed at \( s=-2 \omega,-3 \omega\) , and \(-3 \omega \pm 3 \omega j\).

Admin
Solution

(a) There is not enough information to answer this question. Recall, as mentioned in the chapter, that both observability and controllability are properties of realizations. Thus if you are only given differential equations or transfer functions you will not be able to conclude anything about the observability or controllability of the system. This problem was designed to heighten the readers awareness of this issue.

(b) Choosing \(x_{1}=x, x_{2}=\dot{x}, x_{3}=y, x_{4}=\dot{y}\), and z as the output of the system (so that it doesn’t conflict with the variable y, we have the following in state space equations.

\(\left[\begin{array}{c}\dot{x}_{1} \\ \dot{x}_{2} \\ \dot{x}_{3} \\ \dot{x}_{4}\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cccc}0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 3 \omega^{2} & 0 & 0 & 2 \omega \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\0 & -2 \omega & 0 & 0\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l}x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ x_{3} \\ x_{4}\end{array}\right]+\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 1\end{array}\right] u\),

\(z=\left[\begin{array}{llll}0 & 0 & 1 & 0\end{array}\right] x\).

Now that we have a realization for the system, we can check the observability to verify that we can arbitrarily place the estimator poles. The observability matrix is,

\(\mathcal O =\left[\begin{array}{cccc}0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -2 \omega & 0 & 0 \\- 6 \omega^{3} & 0 & 0 & -4 \omega^{2}\end{array}\right]\).

Since \(\mathcal O\) is full rank, the realization can now be declared observable. The desired and actual estimator characteristic equations are,

\(\begin{aligned}\alpha_{e, \text { desired }}(s) &=(s+2 \omega)(s+3 \omega)(s+3 \omega-j 3 \omega)(s+3 \omega+j 3 \omega) \\ &=s^{4}+11 \omega s^{3}+54 \omega^{2} s^{2}+126 \omega^{3} s+108 \omega^{4}\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\alpha_{e}(s)=\operatorname{det}(s I – F + L H )=s^{4}+l_{3} s^{3}+\left(l_{4}+\omega^{2}\right) s^{2}+\left(-2 \omega l_{2}+\omega^{2} l_{3}\right) s+\left(-3 \omega^{2} l_{4}-6 \omega^{3} l_{1}\right)\).

Equating coefficients gives,

\(l_{1}=-44.5 \omega, l_{2}=-57.5 \omega^{2}, l_{3}=11\omega, l_{4}=53 \omega^{2}\).
