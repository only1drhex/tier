Fully Developed Flow in a Round Pipe‚ÄîPoiseuille Flow

Consider steady, incompressible, laminar flow of a Newtonian fluid in an infinitely long round pipe of diameter D or radius R = D/2 (Fig. 9‚Äì71). We ignore the effects of gravity. A constant pressure gradient ‚àÇP/‚àÇx is applied in the x-direction,

 

Applied pressure gradient: \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}=\frac{P_{2}-P_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}}= constant\) (1)

 

where \(x _{1}\) and \(x _{2}\) are two arbitrary locations along the x-axis, and \(P _{1}\) and \(P _{2}\) are the pressures at those two locations. Note that we adopt a modified cylindrical coordinate system here with x instead of z for the axial component, namely, (r, ùúÉ, x) and \(\left(u_{r}, u_{\theta}, u\right)\). Derive an expression for the velocity field inside the pipe and estimate the viscous shear force per unit surface area acting on the pipe wall.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION For flow inside a round pipe we are to calculate the velocity field, and then estimate the viscous shear stress acting on the pipe wall.

Assumptions 1 The pipe is infinitely long in the x-direction. 2 The flow is steady (all partial time derivatives are zero). 3 This is a parallel flow (the r-component of velocity, \(u _{r}\), is zero). 4 The fluid is incompressible and Newtonian with constant properties, and the flow is laminar (Fig. 9‚Äì72). 5 A constant pressure gradient is applied in the x-direction such that pressure changes linearly with respect to x according to Eq. 1. 6 The velocity field is axisymmetric with no swirl, implying that \(u_{\theta}=0\) and all partial derivatives with respect to ùúÉ are zero. 7 We ignore the effects of gravity.

Analysis To obtain the velocity field, we follow the step-by-step procedure outlined in Fig. 9‚Äì52.

Step 1 Lay out the problem and the geometry. See Fig. 9‚Äì71.

Step 2 List assumptions and boundary conditions. We have listed seven assumptions. The first boundary condition comes from imposing the no-slip condition at the pipe wall: (1) at \(r=R, \vec{V}=0\). The second boundary condition comes from the fact that the centerline of the pipe is an axis of symmetry: (2) at r = 0, ‚àÇu/‚àÇr = 0.

Step 3 Write out and simplify the differential equations. We start with the incompressible continuity equation in cylindrical coordinates, a modified version of Eq. 9‚Äì62a,

 

Incompressible continuity equation: \(\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial\left(r u_{r}\right)}{\partial r}+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial\left(u_{\theta}\right)}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial\left(u_{z}\right)}{\partial z}=0\) (Eq. 9-62a)

 

\(\underbrace{\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial\left(r u_{r}\right)}{\partial r}}_{\text {assumption } 3}+ \underbrace{\frac{1}{r} \frac{ \partial\left(u_{\theta}\right)}{\partial \theta}}_{\text {assumption } 6}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=0 \quad \rightarrow \quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=0\) (2)

 

Equation 2 tells us that u is not a function of x. In other words, it doesn‚Äôt matter where we place our origin‚Äîthe flow is the same at any x-location. This can also be inferred directly from assumption 1, which tells us that there is nothing special about any x-location since the pipe is infinite in length‚Äîthe flow is fully developed. Furthermore, since u is not a function of time (assumption 2) or ùúÉ (assumption 6), we conclude that u is at most a function of r,

 

Result of continuity: u = u(r) only (3)

 

We now simplify the axial momentum equation (a modified version of Eq. 9‚Äì62d) as far as possible:

 

z–component of the incompressible Navier‚ÄìStokes equation:

 

\(\rho\left(\frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial t}+u_{r} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial r}+\frac{u_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial \theta}+u_{z} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial z}\right)\)

 

\(=-\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}+\rho g_{z}+\mu\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} u_{z}}{\partial \theta^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u_{z}}{\partial z^{2}}\right]\) (Eq. 9-62 d)

 

\(\rho(\underbrace{\frac{\partial \mu}{\partial t}}_{\text {assumption } 2}+\underbrace{u_{r} \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial r}}_{\text {assumption } 3}+ \underbrace{\frac{u_{\theta}}r {\frac{ \partial \ u}{\partial \theta}}}_{\text {assumption } 6}+\underbrace{u \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial x}}_{\text {continuity }})\)

 

\(=-\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\underbrace{\rho \text g_{x}}_{\text {assumption 7 }}+\mu(\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u}{\partial r}\right)+\underbrace{\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} {u}}{\partial \theta^{2}}}_{\text {assumption 6 }}+\underbrace{\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}}_{\text {continuity }})\)

 

or

 

\(\frac{1}{r} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r \frac{d u}{d r}\right)=\frac{1}{\mu} \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\) (4)

 

As in through , the material acceleration (entire left side of the x-momentum equation) is zero, implying that fluid particles are not accelerating at all in this flow field, and linearizing the Navier‚ÄìStokes equation (Fig. 9‚Äì73). We have replaced the partial derivative operators for the u-derivatives with total derivative operators because of Eq. 3.

In similar fashion, every term in the r-momentum equation (Eq. 9‚Äì62b) except the pressure gradient term is zero, forcing that lone term to also be zero,

 

z-component of the incompressible Navier‚ÄìStokes equation:

\(\rho\left(\frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial t}+u_{r} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial r}+\frac{u_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial \theta}+u_{z} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial z}\right)\)

 

 

\(=-\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}+\rho g_{z}+\mu\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} u_{z}}{\partial \theta^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} u_{z}}{\partial z^{2}}\right]\) (Eq. 9-62b)

 

r-momentum: \(\frac{\partial P}{\partial r}=0\) (5)

 

In other words, P is not a function of r. Since P is also not a function of time (assumption 2) or ùúÉ (assumption 6), P can be at most a function of x,

 

Result of r-momentum: P = P(x) only (6)

 

Therefore, we replace the partial derivative operator for the pressure gradient in Eq. 4 by the total derivative operator since P varies only with x. Finally, all terms of the ùúÉ-component of the Navier‚ÄìStokes equation (Eq. 9‚Äì62c) go to zero.

 

ùúÉ-component of the incompressible Navier‚ÄìStokes equation:

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\rho\left(\frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial t}+u_{r} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial r}+\frac{u_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{u_{r} u_{\theta}}{r}+u_{z} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial z}\right) \\&\quad=-\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial P}{\partial \theta}+\rho g_{\theta}+\mu\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial r}\right)-\frac{u_{\theta}}{r^{2}}+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta^{2}}+\frac{2}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial^{2} u_{\theta}}{\partial z^{2}}\right]\end{aligned}\) (Eq. 9-62 c)

 

Step 4 Solve the differential equations. Continuity and r-momentum have already been ‚Äúsolved,‚Äù resulting in Eqs. 3 and 6, respectively. The ùúÉ-momentum equation has vanished, and thus we are left with Eq. 4 (x-momentum). After multiplying both sides by r, we integrate once to obtain

 

\(r \frac{d u}{d r}=\frac{r^{2}}{2 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x}+C_{1}\) (7)

 

where \(C _{1}\) is a constant of integration. Note that the pressure gradient dP/dx is a constant here. Dividing both sides of Eq. 7 by r, we integrate a second time to get

 

\(u=\frac{r^{2}}{4 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x}+C_{1} \ln r+C_{2}\) (8)

 

where \(C _{2}\) is a second constant of integration.

 

Step 5 Apply boundary conditions. First, we apply boundary condition (2) to Eq. 7,

 

Boundary condition (2): \(0=0+C_{1} \quad \rightarrow \quad C_{1}=0\)

 

An alternative way to interpret this boundary condition is that u must remain finite at the centerline of the pipe. This is possible only if constant \(C _{1}\) is equal to 0, since ln(0) is undefined in Eq. 8. Now we apply boundary condition (1),

 

Boundary condition (1): \(u=\frac{R^{2}}{4 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x}+0+C_{2}=0 \rightarrow C_{2}=-\frac{R^{2}}{4 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x}\)

 

Finally, Eq. 8 becomes

 

Axial velocity: \(u=\frac{1}{4 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x}\left(r^{2}-R^{2}\right)\) (9)

 

The axial velocity profile is thus in the shape of a paraboloid, as sketched in Fig. 9‚Äì74.

 

Step 6 Verify the results. You can verify that all the differential equations and boundary conditions are satisfied.

 

We calculate some other properties of fully developed laminar pipe flow as well. For example, the maximum axial velocity obviously occurs at the centerline of the pipe (Fig. 9‚Äì74). Setting r = 0 in Eq. 9 yields

 

Maximum axial velocity: \(u_{\max }=-\frac{R^{2}}{4 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x}\) (10)

 

The volume flow rate through the pipe is found by integrating Eq. 9 through a cross section of the pipe,

 

\(\dot{V}=\int_{\theta=0}^{2 \pi} \int_{r=0}^{R} u r d r d \theta=\frac{2 \pi}{4 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x} \int_{r=0}^{R}\left(r^{2}-R^{2}\right) r d r=-\frac{\pi R^{4}}{8 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x}\) (11)

 

Since volume flow rate is also equal to the average axial velocity times cross-sectional area, we easily determine the average axial velocity V:

 

Average axial velocity: \(V=\frac{\dot{V}}{A}=\frac{\left(-\pi R^{4} / 8 \mu\right)(d P / d x)}{\pi R^{2}}=-\frac{R^{2}}{8 \mu} \frac{d P}{d x}\) (12)

 

Comparing Eqs. 10 and 12 we see that for fully developed laminar pipe flow, the average axial velocity is equal to exactly half of the maximum axial velocity.

To calculate the viscous shear force per unit surface area acting on the pipe wall, we consider a differential fluid element adjacent to the bottom portion of the pipe wall (Fig. 9‚Äì75). Pressure stresses and mathematically positive viscous stresses are shown. From Eq. 9‚Äì63 (modified for our coordinate system), we write the viscous stress tensor as

 

\(\tau_{i j}=\left(\begin{array}{lll}\tau_{r r} & \tau_{r \theta} & \tau_{r z} \\\tau_{\theta r} & \tau_{\theta \theta} & \tau_{\theta z} \\\tau_{z r} & \tau_{z \theta} & \tau_{z z}\end{array}\right)\)
\(=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}2 \mu \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial r} & \mu\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(\frac{u_{\theta}}{r}\right)+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta}\right] & \mu\left(\frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial r}\right) \\\mu\left[r \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(\frac{u_{\theta}}{r}\right)+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial \theta}\right] & 2 \mu\left(\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{u_{r}}{r}\right) & \mu\left(\frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial z}+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial \theta}\right) \\\mu\left(\frac{\partial u_{r}}{\partial z}+\frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial r}\right) & \mu\left(\frac{\partial u_{\theta}}{\partial z}+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial \theta}\right) & 2 \mu \frac{\partial u_{z}}{\partial z}\end{array}\right)\) (Eq. 9-63)

 

\(\tau_{i j}=\left(\begin{array}{lll}\tau_{r r} & \tau_{r \theta} & \tau_{r x} \\\tau_{\theta r} & \tau_{\theta \theta} & \tau_{\theta x} \\\tau_{x r} & \tau_{x \theta} & \tau_{x x}\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 0 & \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial r} \\0 & 0 & 0 \\\mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial r} & 0 & 0\end{array}\right)\) (13)

 

We use Eq. 9 for u, and set r = R at the pipe wall; component \(\tau_{r x}\) of Eq. 13 reduces to

 

Viscous shear stress at the pipe wall: \(\tau_{r x}=\mu \frac{d u}{d r}=\frac{R}{2} \frac{d P}{d x}\) (14)

 

For flow from left to right, dP/dx is negative, so the viscous shear stress on the bottom of the fluid element at the wall is in the direction opposite to that indicated in Fig. 9‚Äì75. (This agrees with our intuition since the pipe wall exerts a retarding force on the fluid.) The shear force per unit area on the wall is equal and opposite to this; hence,

 

Viscous shear force per unit area acting on the wall: \(\frac{\vec{F}}{A}=-\frac{R}{2} \frac{d P}{d x} \vec{i}\) (15)

 

The direction of this force again agrees with our intuition; namely, the fluid tries to pull the bottom wall to the right, due to friction, when dP/dx is negative.

Discussion Since du/dr = 0 at the centerline of the pipe, \(\tau_{r x}\) = 0 there. You are encouraged to try to obtain Eq. 15 by using a control volume approach instead, taking your control volume as the fluid in the pipe between any two x-locations, \(x_{1} \text { and } x_{2}\) (Fig. 9‚Äì76). You should get the same answer. (Hint: Since the flow is fully developed, the axial velocity profile at location 1 is identical to that at location 2.) Note that when the volume flow rate through the pipe exceeds a critical value, instabilities in the flow occur, and the solution presented here is no longer valid. Specifically, flow in the pipe becomes turbulent rather than laminar; turbulent pipe flow is discussed in more detail in Chap. 8. This problem is also solved in Chap. 8 using an alternative approach.
