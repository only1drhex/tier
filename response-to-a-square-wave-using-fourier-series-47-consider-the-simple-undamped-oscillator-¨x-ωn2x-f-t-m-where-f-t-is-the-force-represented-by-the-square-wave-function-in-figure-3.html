Response to a Square Wave Using Fourier Series \( ^{47} \)

Consider the simple undamped oscillator \(\ddot{x}+\omega_{n}^{2} x=\) \(F(t) / m\), where \(F(t)\) is the force represented by the square wave function in Figure 3.49. The square wave is the simplest of all non-harmonic, periodic functions since the amplitude over each half-period is constant. Solve for the response using the Fourier series representation for \(F(t)\), assuming zero initial conditions.

\( ^{47} \) This undamped problem is included in this chapter to demonstrate the approach and character of the response. With damping the analysis is more complicated.

Admin
Solution

The square wave is defined by

\(F(t)=\left\{\begin{array}{cc}-F_{0} & \text { if }-T / 2<t<0 \\F_{0} & \text { if } 0<t<T / 2\end{array}\right.\)

for one period of the function. Since \(F(t)\) is an odd function, the \(a_{P}\) coefficients equal zero. The \(b_{P}\) coefficients are evaluated as follows,

\(\begin{aligned}b_{p} &=\frac{2}{T}\left[\int_{-T / 2}^{0} F(t) \sin p \omega_{T} t d t+\int_{0}^{T / 2} F(t) \sin p \omega_{T} t d t\right] \\&=\frac{4}{T} \int_{0}^{T / 2} F_{0} \sin p \omega_{T} t d t \\&=\frac{4 F_{0}}{T p \omega_{T}}\left[1-\cos \left(p \omega_{T} T / 2\right)\right]\end{aligned}\)

where \(\omega_{T}=2 \pi / T\) and the cosine term can be simplified as \(\cos \left(p \omega_{T} T / 2\right)=\cos p \pi=(-1)^{p}\). Therefore,

\(b_{p}=\frac{2 F_{0}}{p \pi}\left[1-(-1)^{p}\right]=\frac{4 F_{0}}{p \pi}, \quad p \text { odd }\),

and zero otherwise. The square wave loading is given by the series

\(F(t)=\frac{4 F_{0}}{\pi} \sum_{p=1,3 \ldots . .}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p} \sin p \omega_{T} t\).

The equation of motion is then

\(\ddot{x}+\omega_{n}^{2} x=\frac{4 F_{0}}{m \pi} \sum_{p=1,3}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p} \sin p \omega_{T} t\).

The complete response to this infinite series of harmonic loads is

\(\begin{aligned}x(t) &=c_{1} \sin \omega_{n} t+c_{2} \cos \omega_{n} t \\&+\frac{4 F_{0}}{\pi m \omega_{n}^{2}} \sum_{p \text { odd }} \frac{\sin p \omega_{x} t}{p\left[1-\left(p \omega_{r} / \omega_{n}\right)^{2}\right]}\end{aligned}\).

Applying the initial conditions, \(x(0)=0\) and \(\dot{x}(0)=0\), we find that both constants of integration are equal to zero. This is not always true. Let us see how this comes about,

\(\begin{aligned}&x(0)=c_{2}=0 \\&\dot{x}(0)=\omega_{n} c_{1}+\frac{4 F_{0}}{m \pi} \sum_{p=1,3, . .} \frac{p \omega_{T}}{p\left(\omega_{n}^{2}-p^{2} \omega_{T}^{2}\right)} \\&c_{1}=-\frac{4 F_{0}}{m \pi} \sum_{p=1,3, . .} \frac{\omega_{r} / \omega_{n}}{\left(\omega_{n}^{2}-p^{2} \omega_{T}^{2}\right)} .\end{aligned}\)

In this example, for \(\omega_{n}=2 \omega_{T}\),

\(c_{1}=-\frac{4 F_{0}}{\pi m \omega_{n}^{2}} \sum_{p=1,3, \ldots} \frac{1}{2} \frac{1}{\left(1-p^{2} / 4\right)}\)

which converges to zero (shown below). Then, with \(m \omega_{n}^{2}=k\),

\(\begin{aligned}c_{1} &=-\frac{4 F_{0}}{\pi k} \sum_{p=1,3, \ldots} \frac{4}{2} \frac{1}{\left(4-p^{2}\right)} \\&=-\frac{8 F_{0}}{\pi k} \sum_{p=1,3, . .} \frac{1}{\left(4-p^{2}\right)}\end{aligned}\)

The following calculation shows that \(\sum_{p=1,3, \ldots} 1 /\left(4-p^{2}\right)=0\), so that \(c_{1}=0\),

\(\sum_{p=1,3, . .} \frac{1}{\left(4-p^{2}\right)}=\frac{1}{4} \sum_{p=1,3, . .} \frac{1}{(2-p)}+\frac{1}{(2+p)}\)

where

\(\begin{aligned}\sum_{p=1,3, . .} \frac{1}{(2-p)} &=-\sum_{p=1,3, \ldots} \frac{1}{(p-2)} \\&=-\left(\frac{1}{-1}+\frac{1}{1}+\sum_{p=5,7, . .} \frac{1}{(p-2)}\right) \\&=-\sum_{p=5,7, \ldots} \frac{1}{(p-2)}=-\sum_{p=1,3, \ldots} \frac{1}{(p+2)} .\end{aligned}\).

Then,

\(\begin{aligned}&\sum_{p=1,3, . .} \frac{1}{(2-p)}+\frac{1}{(2+p)} \\&=-\sum_{p=1,3, . .}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(p+2)}+\sum_{p=1,3, . .}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(p+2)}=0\end{aligned}\)

Therefore, \(c_{1}=0\).

Equation 3.61 shows that resonances occur for \(p \omega_{T}=\omega_{n}\). Figure 3.50 shows the time histories of the loading and the response using four terms in the respective Fourier series representations. The response was obtained for the case \(\omega_{n}=2 \omega_{r}, m=1 / \pi kg\), and \(k=4 N / m\). Although only four terms were used in the series for the force, the response turns out to be very close to exact. Had there been a response component at 2\(w_{T}\) , this undamped system would have infinite amplification. (This would not occur with damping.) If \(\omega_{n}=6 \omega_{T}\), there would be a magnification about that frequency. However, since there is relatively little energy driving the system at the higher frequencies, amplification may not be a concern.

\( \begin{aligned} x(t) &=c_{1} \sin \omega_{n} t+c_{2} \cos \omega_{n} t \\ &+\frac{4 F_{0}}{\pi m \omega_{n}^{2}} \sum_{p \text { odd }} \frac{\sin p \omega_{T} t}{p\left[1-\left(p \omega_{T} / \omega_{n}\right)^{2}\right]} . \end{aligned} \) (3.61)
