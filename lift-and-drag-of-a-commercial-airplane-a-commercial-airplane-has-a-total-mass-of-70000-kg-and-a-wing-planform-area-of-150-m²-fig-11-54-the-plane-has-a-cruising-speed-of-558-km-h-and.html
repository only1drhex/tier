Lift and Drag of a Commercial Airplane

A commercial airplane has a total mass of 70,000 kg and a wing planform area of 150 m¬≤ (Fig. 11‚Äì54). The plane has a cruising speed of 558 km/h and a cruising altitude of 12,000 m, where the air density is 0.312 kg/m¬≥. The plane has double-slotted flaps for use during takeoff and landing, but it cruises with all flaps retracted. Assuming the lift and the drag characteristics of the wings can be approximated by NACA 23012 (Fig. 11‚Äì45), determine (a) the minimum safe speed for takeoff and landing with and without extending the flaps, (b) the angle of attack to cruise steadily at the cruising altitude, and (c) the power that needs to be supplied to provide enough thrust to overcome wing drag.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION The cruising conditions of a passenger plane and its wing characteristics are given. The minimum safe landing and takeoff speeds, the angle of attack during cruising, and the power required are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 The drag and lift produced by parts of the plane other than the wings, such as the fuselage are not considered. 2 The wings are assumed to be two-dimensional airfoil sections, and the tip effects of the wings are not considered. 3 The lift and the drag characteristics of the wings are approximated by NACA 23012 so that Fig. 11‚Äì45 is applicable. 4 The average density of air on the ground is 1.20 kg/m¬≥.

Properties The density of air is 1.20 kg/m¬≥ on the ground and 0.312 kg/m¬≥ at cruising altitude. The maximum lift coefficient \(C_{L, \max }\) of the wing is 3.48 and 1.52 with and without flaps, respectively (Fig. 11‚Äì45).

Analysis (a) The weight and cruising speed of the airplane are

 

\(\begin{aligned}&W=m g=(70,000 kg )\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)\left(\frac{1 N }{1 kg \cdot m / s ^{2}}\right)=686,700 N \\&V=(558 km / h )\left(\frac{1 m / s }{3.6 km / h }\right)=155 m / s\end{aligned}\)

 

The minimum velocities corresponding to the stall conditions without and with flaps, respectively, are obtained from Eq. 11‚Äì24,

 

\(W=F_{L}=\frac{1}{2} C_{L, \max } \rho V_{\min }^{2} A \quad \rightarrow \quad V_{\min }=\sqrt{\frac{2 W}{\rho C_{L, \max } A}}\) (Eq. 11-24)

 

\(V_{\min 1}=\sqrt{\frac{2 W}{\rho C_{L, \max 1} A}}=\sqrt{\frac{2(686,700 N )}{\left(1.2 kg / m ^{3}\right)(1.52)\left(150 m ^{2}\right)}\left(\frac{1 kg \cdot m / s ^{2}}{1 N }\right)}=70.9 m / s\)

 

\(V_{\min 2}=\sqrt{\frac{2 W}{\rho C_{L, \max 2} A}}=\sqrt{\frac{2(686,700 N )}{\left(1.2 kg / m ^{3}\right)(3.48)\left(150 m ^{2}\right)}\left(\frac{1 kg \cdot m / s ^{2}}{1 N }\right)}=46.8 m / s\)

 

Then the ‚Äúsafe‚Äù minimum velocities to avoid the stall region are obtained by multiplying the values above by 1.2:

 

\(\text { Without flaps: } \quad V_{\min 1, safe }=1.2 V_{\min 1}=1.2(70.9 m / s )=85.1 m / s = 3 0 6 km / h\)

 

\(\text { With flaps: } \quad V_{\min 2, \text { safe }}=1.2 V_{\min 2}=1.2(46.8 m / s )=56.2 m / s =202 km / h\)

 

since 1 m/s = 3.6 km/h. Note that the use of flaps allows the plane to take off and land at considerably lower velocities, and thus on a shorter runway.

(b) When an aircraft is cruising steadily at a constant altitude, the lift must be equal to the weight of the aircraft, \(F_{L}=W\). Then the lift coefficient is

 

\(C_{L}=\frac{F_{L}}{\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2} A}=\frac{686,700 N }{\frac{1}{2}\left(0.312 kg / m ^{3}\right)(155 m / s )^{2}\left(150 m ^{2}\right)}\left(\frac{1 kg \cdot m / s ^{2}}{1 N }\right)=1.22\)

 

For the case with no flaps, the angle of attack corresponding to this value of \(C_{L}\) is determined from Fig. 11‚Äì45 to be ùõº ‚âÖ 10¬∞.

(c) When the aircraft is cruising steadily at a constant altitude, the net force acting on the aircraft is zero, and thus thrust provided by the engines must be equal to the drag force. The drag coefficient corresponding to the cruising lift coefficient of 1.22 is determined from Fig. 11‚Äì45 to be \(C_{D} \cong 0.03\) for the case with no flaps. Then the drag force acting on the wings becomes

 

\(\begin{aligned}F_{D} &=C_{D} A \frac{\rho V^{2}}{2}=(0.03)\left(150 m ^{2}\right) \frac{\left(0.312 kg / m ^{3}\right)(155 m / s )^{2}}{2}\left(\frac{1 kN }{1000 kg \cdot m / s ^{2}}\right) \\&=16.9 kN\end{aligned}\)

 

Noting that power is force times velocity (distance per unit time), the power required to overcome this drag is equal to the thrust times the cruising velocity:

 

\(\begin{aligned}\text { Power } &=\text { Thrust } \times \text { Velocity }=F_{D} V=(16.9 kN )(155 m / s )\left(\frac{1 kW }{1 kN \cdot m / s }\right) \\&=2620 kW\end{aligned}\)

 

Therefore, the engines must supply 2620 kW of power to overcome the drag on the wings during cruising. For a propulsion efficiency of 30 percent (i.e., 30 percent of the energy of the fuel is utilized to propel the aircraft), the plane requires energy input at a rate of 8730 kJ/s.

Discussion The power determined is the power to overcome the drag that acts on the wings only and does not include the drag that acts on the remaining parts of the aircraft (the fuselage, the tail, etc.). Therefore, the total power required during cruising will be much greater. Also, it does not consider induced drag, which can be dominant during takeoff when the angle of attack is high (Fig. 11‚Äì45 is for a 2-D airfoil, and does not include 3-D effects).
