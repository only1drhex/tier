Specific Energy Diagram—Quantitative

GIVEN Water flows up a 0.5-ft-tall ramp in a constant width rectangular channel at a rate \(q=5.75 ft ^{2} / s\) as is shown in Fig. E10.2a. (For now disregard the “bump.”) The upstream depth is 2.3 ft and viscous effects are negligible.

FIND Determine the elevation of the water surface downstream of the ramp, \(y_{2}+z_{2}\).

Admin
Solution

With \(S_{0} \ell=z_{1}-z_{2} \text { and } h_{L}=0\), conservation of energy (Eq. 10.6 which, under these conditions, is actually the Bernoulli equation) requires that

 

\(y_{1}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 g}+S_{0} \ell=y_{2}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2 g}+h_{L}\) (10.6)

 

\(y_{1}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 g}+z_{1}=y_{2}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2 g}+z_{2}\)

 

For the conditions given \(\left(z_{1}=0, z_{2}=0.5 ft , y_{1}=2.3 ft \right.\), and \(\left.V_{1}=q / y_{1}=2.5 ft / s \right)\), this becomes

 

\(1.90=y_{2}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{64.4}\)

 

where \(V_{2} \text { and } y_{2} \text { are in } ft / s\) and feet, respectively. The continuity equation provides the second equation

 

\(y_{2} V_{2}=y_{1} V_{1}\)

 

or

 

\(y_{2} V_{2}=5.75 ft ^{2} / s\) (2)

 

Equations 1 and 2 can be combined to give

 

\(y_{2}^{3}-1.90 y_{2}^{2}+0.513=0\)

 

which has solutions

 

\(y_{2}=1.72 ft , \quad y_{2}=0.638 ft , \quad \text { or } \quad y_{2}=-0.466 ft\)

 

Note that two of these solutions are physically realistic, but the negative solution is meaningless. This is consistent with the previous discussions concerning the specific energy (recall the three roots indicated in Fig. 10.7). The corresponding elevations of the free surface are either

 

\(y_{2}+z_{2}=1.72 ft +0.50 ft =2.22 ft\)

 

or

 

\(y_{2}+z_{2}=0.638 ft +0.50 ft =1.14 ft\)

 

The question is which of these two flows is to be expected? This can be answered by use of the specific energy diagram obtained from Eq. 10.10, which for this problem is

 

\(E=y+\frac{q^{2}}{2 g y^{2}}\) (10.10)

 

\(E=y+\frac{0.513}{y^{2}}\)

 

where E and y are in feet. The diagram is shown in Fig. E10.2b. The upstream condition corresponds to subcritical flow; the downstream condition is either subcritical or supercritical, corresponding to points 2 or \(2^{\prime} \text {. Note that since } E_{1}=E_{2}+\) \(\left(z_{2}-z_{1}\right)=E_{2}+0.5 ft\), it follows that the downstream conditions are located 0.5 ft to the left of the upstream conditions on the diagram.

With a constant width channel, the value of q remains the same for any location along the channel. That is, all points for the flow from (1) to (2) or \(\left(2^{\prime}\right) \text { must lie along the } q=5.75 ft ^{2} / s\) curve shown. Any deviation from this curve would imply either a change in q or a relaxation of the one-dimensional flow assumption. To stay on the curve and go from (1) around the critical point (point c) to point \(\left(2^{\prime}\right)\) would require a reduction in specific energy to \(E_{\min }\) As is seen from Fig. E10.2a, this would require a specified elevation (bump) in the channel bottom so that critical conditions would occur above this bump. The height of this bump can be obtained from the energy equation (Eq. 10.9) written between points (1) and (c) with \(S_{f}=0(\text { no viscous }\) \(\text { effects) and } S_{0} \ell=z_{1}-z_{c} \text {. That is, } E_{1}=E_{\min }-z_{1}+z_{c} \text {. In par- }\) \(\text { ticular, since } E_{1}=y_{1}+0.513 / y_{1}^{2}=2.40 ft \text { and } E_{\min }=3 y_{c} / 2=\) \(3\left(q^{2} / g\right)^{1 / 3} / 2=1.51 ft\), the top of this bump would need to be \(z_{c}-z_{1}=E_{1}-E_{\min }=2.40 ft -1.51 ft =0.89\) ft above the channel bottom at section (1). The flow could then accelerate to supercritical conditions \(\left( Fr _{2^{\prime}}>1\right)\) as is shown by the free surface represented by the dashed line in Fig. E10.2a.

 

\(E_{1}=E_{2}+\left(S_{f}-S_{0}\right) \ell\) (10.9)

 

Since the actual elevation change 1a ramp2 shown in Fig. E10.2a does not contain a bump, the downstream conditions will correspond to the subcritical flow denoted by (2), not the supercritical condition \(\left(2^{\prime}\right)\). Without a bump on the channel bottom, the state \(\left(2^{\prime}\right)\) is inaccessible from the upstream condition state (1). Such considerations are often termed the accessibility of flow regimes. Thus, the surface elevation is

 

\(y_{2}+z_{2}=2.22 ft\)

 

Note that since \(y_{1}+z_{1}=2.30 ft \text { and } y_{2}+z_{2}=2.22 ft \text {, the }\) elevation of the free surface decreases as it goes across the ramp.

COMMENT If the flow conditions upstream of the ramp were supercritical, the free-surface elevation and fluid depth would increase as the fluid flows up the ramp. This is indicated in Fig. E10.2c along with the corresponding specific energy diagram, as is shown in Fig. E10.2d. For this case the flow starts at (1) on the lower 1supercritical2 branch of the specific energy curve and ends at (2) on the same branch with \(y_{2}>y_{1} .\) Since both y and z increase from (1) to (2), the surface elevation, \(y+z\), also increases. Thus, flow up a ramp is different for subcritical than it is for supercritical conditions.
