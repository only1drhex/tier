Guest Question.

The measured resistance in fresh water of an 8-ft ship model moving at 6.50 ft/sec was 9.60 lb. (a) What would be the velocity of the 128-ft prototype? (b) What force would be required to drive the prototype at this speed in salt water?

Admin
Solution

(a) Since gravity forces predominate, we obtain

\(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}}=\sqrt{L_{r}}=\sqrt{8/128} \) and \(V_{p} =\frac{6.50}{(1/16)^{1/2}}=26.0 \) ft/sec

(b) \(\frac{F_{m}}{F_{p}}=\gamma _{r}L^3_{r} \) and \(F_{p}=\frac{9.60}{(62.4/64.0)(1/16)^3}=40,330 \) lb

This latter value can be obtained by using the drag formula: Drag force = \(C_{f}\rho \frac{A}{2}V^2 \)

For model, \(9.60=C_{f}\left(\frac{62.4}{2g} \right)\left(\frac{A}{(16)^2} \right)(6.50)^2 \) and \( \frac{C_{f}A}{2g}=\frac{(9.60)(16)^2}{(62.4)(6.50)^2} \) (1)

For prototype, force \(= C_{f}\frac{64.0}{2g}A(26.0)^2 \) and \( \frac{C_{f}A}{2g}=\frac{force}{(64.0)(26.0)^2} \) (2)

Equating (1) and (2) since the value of \(C_{f} \) is the same for model and prototype, we obtain

\(\frac{(9.60)(16)^2}{(62.4)(6.50)^2} =\frac{force}{(64.0)(26.0)^2} \) , from which force \(=40,330 \) lb, as before
