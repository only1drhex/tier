Guest Question.

Air flows from a reservoir where p = 300 kPa and T = 500 K through a throat to section 1 in Fig. E9.6, where there is a normal shock wave. Compute (a) \(p_1, (b) p_2, (c) p_{02}, (d) A_2^* (e) p_{03}, (f) A_3^* (g) p_3, and (h) T_{03}\).

Admin
Solution

• System sketch: This is shown in Fig. E9.6. Between sections 1 and 2 is a normal shock.

• Assumptions: Isentropic flow before and after the shock. Lower \(p_0 and \rho_0\) after the shock.

• Approach: After first noting that the throat is sonic, work your way from 1 to 2 to 3.

• Property values: For air, \(R = 287 m^2/(s^2 \cdot K)\), k = 1.40, and \(c_p = 1005 m^2/(s^2 \cdot K)\). The inlet stagnation pressure of 300 kPa is constant up to point 1.

• Solution step (a): A shock wave cannot exist unless \(Ma_1\) is supersonic. Therefore the throat is sonic and choked: \(A_{throat} = A_1^* = 1 m^2\). The area ratio gives \(Ma_1\) from Eq. (9.45) for k = 1.4:

\(\frac{A}{A^*} = \frac{1}{Ma} \frac{(1 + 0.2 Ma^2)^3}{1.728}\) (9.45)

\(\frac{A_1}{A^*_1} = \frac{2 m^2}{1 m^2} = 2.0 = \frac{1}{Ma_1} \frac{(1 + 0.2 Ma_1^2)^3}{1.728} solve for Ma_1 = 2.1972\)

Such four-decimal-place accuracy might require iteration or the use of EES. Linear interpolation in Table B.1 would give \(Ma_1\) ≈ 2.194, quite good also. The pressure at section 1 then follows from the isentropic relation, Eq. (9.28):

\(\frac{p_0}{p} = \left(\frac{T_0}{T}\right)^{k/(k-1)} = \left[1 + \frac{1}{2} (k – 1) Ma^2\right]^{k/(k-1)}\) (9.28a)

\(\frac{\rho_0}{\rho} = \left(\frac{T_0}{T}\right)^{1/(k-1)} = \left[1 + \frac{1}{2} (k – 1) Ma^2\right]^{1/(k-1)}\) (9.28b)

\(p_1 = \frac{p_{01}}{(1 + 0.2Ma_1^2)^{3.5}} = \frac{300 kPa}{[1 + 0.2(2.194)^2]^{3.5}} = 28.2 kPa\)

• Steps (b, c, d): The pressure \(p_2\) is found from the normal shock Eq. (9.55) or Table B.2:

\(\frac{p_2}{p_1} = \frac{1}{k + 1} [2k Ma^2_1 – (k – 1)]\) (9.55)

\(p_2 = \frac{p_1}{k + 1} [2k Ma_1^2 – (k-1)] = \frac{28.2 kPa}{(1.4 + 1)} [2(1.4)(2.194)^2 – (1.4 – 1)] = 154 kPa\)

Similarly, for \(Ma_1 \approx 2.20\), Table B.2 gives \(p_{02}/p_{01} \approx 0.628\) (EES gives 0.6294) and \(A_2^*/A_1^* \approx 1.592\) (EES gives 1.5888). Thus, to good accuracy,

\(p_{02} \approx 0.628p_{01} =\) 0.628(300 kPa) ≈ 188 kPa

\(A^*_2 = 1.59 A^*_1 = 1.59(1.0 m^2) \approx 1.59 m^2\)

• Comment: To calculate \(A^*_2\) directly, without Table B.2, you would need to pause and calculate \(Ma_2\) ≈ 0.547 from Eq. (9.57), since Eq. (9.59) involves both \(Ma_1\) and \(Ma_2\).

\(Ma^2_2 = \frac{(k – 1) Ma_1^2 + 2}{2k Ma_1^2 – (k – 1)}\) (9.57)

\(\frac{A^*_2}{A^*_1} = \frac{Ma_2}{Ma_1} \left[\frac{2 + (k – 1) Ma^2_1}{2 + (k – 1) Ma^2_2}\right]^{(1/2)(k+1)/(k-1)}\) (9.59)

• Step (e, f): The flow from 2 to 3 is isentropic (but at higher entropy than upstream of the shock); therefore

\(p_{03} = p_{02}\) ≈ 188 kPa

\(A^*_3 = A^*_2 \approx 1.59 m^2\)

• Steps (g, h): The flow is adiabatic throughout, so the stagnation temperature is constant:

\(T_{03} = T_{02} = T_{01} = 500 K\)

Next, the area ratio, using the new sonic area, gives the Mach number at section 3:

\(\frac{A_3}{A^*_3} = \frac{3 m^2}{1.59 m^2} = 1.89 = \frac{1}{Ma_3} \frac{(1 + 0.2 Ma^2_3)^3}{1.728} solve for Ma_3 \approx 0.33\)

EES would yield \(Ma_3 = 0.327\). Finally, with \(p_{02}\) known, Eq. (9.28) yields \(p_3\):

\(p_3 = \frac{p_{02}}{(1 + 0.2 Ma_3^2)^{3.5}} \approx \frac{188 kPa}{[1 + 0.2(0.33)^2]^{3.5}} \approx 174 kPa\)

• Comments: EES would give \(p_3\) = 175 kPa, so we see that Table B.2 is satisfactory for this type of problem. A duct flow with a normal shock wave requires straightforward application of algebraic perfect-gas relations, coupled with a little thought as to which formula is appropriate for the given property.
