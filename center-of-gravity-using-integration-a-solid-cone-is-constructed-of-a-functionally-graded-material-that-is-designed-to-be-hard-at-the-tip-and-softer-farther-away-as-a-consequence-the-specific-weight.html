Center of Gravity Using Integration

A solid cone is constructed of a functionally graded material that is designed to be hard at the tip and softer farther away. As a consequence, the specific weight of the material varies linearly from \(\gamma_0\) at the tip to \(\gamma_0/2\) at x = L. Determine the location of the center of gravity and the weight of the cone.

Admin
Solution

Road Map Because the cone has specific weight that changes with position, the center of gravity will most likely not coincide with the centroid. To determine the center of gravity, we have little choice but to use integration, with the integral expressions in Eq. (\(\overline{x} = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}{\widetilde{x}_i w_i } }{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} {w_i }} =\frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dw } }{\int{dw} } , \overline{y} = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}{\widetilde{y}_i w_i } }{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} {w_i }} =\frac{\int{\widetilde{y} dw } }{\int{dw} }, \overline{z} = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}{\widetilde{z}_i w_i } }{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} {w_i }} =\frac{\int{\widetilde{z} dw } }{\int{dw} }\) ) on p. 415, or equivalently the volume integral expressions in Eq. (\(\overline{x} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dw}}{\int{dw}} = \frac{\int{\widetilde{x} \gamma dV }+\int{\widetilde{x} \gamma_A dA }+\int{\widetilde{x} \gamma_L dL }}{\int{\gamma dV} +\int{ \gamma_A dA }+\int{\gamma_L dL }}\)). Noting that the specific weight changes with x coordinate only, the cone is symmetric about the x axis, and thus its center of gravity must lie on the x axis, and \(\overline{y} =\overline{z} = 0\).

Governing Equations & Computation Using the expression \(\overline{x} = \int{\widetilde{x} dw}\) in Eq. (\(\overline{x} = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}{\widetilde{x}_i w_i } }{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} {w_i }} =\frac{\int{\widetilde{x} dw } }{\int{dw} } , \overline{y} = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}{\widetilde{y}_i w_i } }{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} {w_i }} =\frac{\int{\widetilde{y} dw } }{\int{dw} }, \overline{z} = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}{\widetilde{z}_i w_i } }{\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} {w_i }} =\frac{\int{\widetilde{z} dw } }{\int{dw} }\) ) with \(dw = \gamma dV\) gives

\(\overline{x} =\frac{\int{\widetilde{x} \gamma dV}}{\int{\gamma dV}}\) (1)

In Eq. (1), \(\widetilde{x}\) is the location of the center of gravity for the weight element \(\gamma dV\). Because of the variable specific weight, a thin disk weight (volume) element, as shown in Fig. 2, will be more convenient than a thin shell weight (volume) element (see Prob. 7.37 for further discussion). Expressions for dV and \(\widetilde{x}\) are

\(dV = \pi r^2 dx\) and \(\widetilde{x} = x\). (2)

In Eq. (2), \(\pi r^2\) is the area of a disk where, as shown in Fig. 2, r is the radius and dx is the thickness. The center of gravity of the disk element is located at \(\widetilde{x} = x\). Before we evaluate Eq. (1), it is necessary to express both the radius r and specific weight \(\gamma\) of the cone as functions of x position. These expressions are

\(r = R\frac{x}{L}\) and \(\gamma = \gamma_0 (1 – \frac{x}{2L})\). (3)

The validity of Eqs. (3) is easily verified by evaluating each expression at x = 0 and x = L to see that they produce the proper results. The margin note on this page provides some tips for developing expressions such as these.

Substituting Eqs. (2) and (3) into Eq. (1) provides the center of gravity

\(\overline{x} =\frac{\int{\widetilde{x} \gamma dV}}{\int{\gamma dV}} = \frac{\int\limits_{0}^{L}{x \gamma_0 } (1 – \frac{x}{2L})\pi (R\frac{x}{L})^2 dx}{\int\limits_{0}^{L}{ \gamma_0 } (1 – \frac{x}{2L})\pi (R\frac{x}{L})^2 dx} = \frac{\frac{3\pi L^2 R^2}{20}\gamma_0 }{\frac{5\pi L R^2}{24}\gamma_0 } = \frac{18L}{25}\). (8)

The denominator in Eq. (8) is the total weight of the cone, hence

\(w = \frac{5\pi L R^2}{24}\gamma_0\). (9)

Discussion & Verification The centroid of a solid cone, as given in the Table of Properties of Solids on the inside back cover of this book, is \(3L/4\) from the tip, while the center of gravity in this example, from Eq. (8), is \(\overline{x} = 18 L/25 = (0.72)L\). As expected, because of the specific weight distribution in this problem, we see that the center of gravity is closer to the tip than the centroid. Of course, if the specific weight were uniform, the center of mass and the centroid would have the same location.
