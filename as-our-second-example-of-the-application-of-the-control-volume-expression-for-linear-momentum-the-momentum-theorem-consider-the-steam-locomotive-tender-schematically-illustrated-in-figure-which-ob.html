Guest Question.

As our second example of the application of the control-volume expression for linear momentum (the momentum theorem), consider the steam locomotive tender schematically illustrated in Figure, which obtains water from a trough by means of a scoop. The force on the train due to the water is to be obtained.

Admin
Solution

The logical choice for a control volume in this case is the water-tank/scoop combination. Our control-volume boundary will be selected as the interior of the tank and scoop. As the train is moving with a uniform velocity, there are two possible choices of coordinate systems. We may select a coordinate system either fixed in space or \( moving^{1} \) with the velocity of the train, \( \upsilon_{0} \). Let us first analyze the system by using a moving coordinate system.
The moving control volume is shown in Figure with the xy coordinate system moving at velocity \( \upsilon_{0} \). All velocities are determined with respect to the x and y axes.

The applicable expression is equation :

\( \sum F_{x} = \iint_{c.s.}^{} \upsilon_{x} \rho \left ( v \cdot n \right ) dA + \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \int \int \int_{c.v.}^{} \upsilon_{x} \rho dV\)

 

In Figure, \( \sum F_{x} \) is represented as \( F_{x} \) and is shown in the positive sense. As the forces due to pressure and shear are to be neglected, \( F_{x} \) is the total force exerted on the fluid by the train and scoop. The momentum flux term is

\( \iint_{c.s.}^{} \upsilon_{x} \rho \left ( v \cdot n \right ) dA = \rho \left ( – \upsilon_{0} \right )\left ( – 1 \right ) \left ( \upsilon_{0} \right )\left ( h \right ) \left ( per unit length \right ) \)

 

and the rate of change of momentum within the control volume is zero, as the fluid in the control volume has zero velocity in the x direction.
Thus,

\( F_{x} = \rho \upsilon_{0}^{2} h \)

 

This is the force exerted by the train on the fluid. The force exerted by the fluid on the train is the opposite of this, or \( – \rho \upsilon_{0}^{2} h \).
Now let us consider the same problem with a stationary coordinate system (see Figure ). Employing once again the control-volume relation for linear momentum

 

\( \sum F_{x} = \iint_{c.s.}^{} \upsilon_{x} \rho \left ( v \cdot n \right ) d A + \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \int \int \int_{c.v.}^{} \upsilon_{x} \rho d V \)

we obtain

\( F_{x} = 0 + \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \int \int \int_{c.v.}^{} \upsilon_{x} \rho d V \)

 

where the momentum flux is zero, as the entering fluid has zero velocity. There is, of course, no fluid leaving the control volume. The terms \( \partial / \partial t \int \int \int_{c.v.}^{} \upsilon_{x} \rho dV \), as the velocity, \( \upsilon_{x} = \upsilon_{0} = \) constant, may be written as \( \upsilon_{0} \partial / \partial t \int \int \oint c.v. \rho dV \) or \( \upsilon_{0} \left ( \partial m / \partial t \right ) \), where mÌ‡ is the mass of fluid entering the control volume at the rate \( \partial m / \partial t = \rho \upsilon_{0}h \) so that \( F_{x} = \rho \upsilon_{0}^{2} h \) as before.

The student should note that in the case of a stationary coordinate system and a moving control volume, care must be exercised in the interpretation of the momentum flux

\( \iint_{c.s.}^{} v\rho \left ( v \cdot n \right ) dA \)

 

Regrouping the terms, we obtain

\( \iint_{c.s.}^{} v\rho \left ( v \cdot n \right ) dA = \iint_{c.s.}^{} v d \dot{m} \)

 

Thus, it is obvious that while v is the velocity relative to fixed coordinates, \( v \cdot n \) is the velocity relative to the control-volume boundary.
