Guest Question.

Pipe Flow of a Bingham Plastic

Investigate the nature of the shear stress and velocity profile for flow of a Bingham plastic, with yield stress \(\tau_{0}\) and effective viscosity \(\eta\), in a cylindrical pipe of radius \(a\) and length \(L\). The inlet and outlet pressures are \(p_{1}\) and \(p_{2}\), respectively.

Admin
Solution

The shear-stress distribution is independent of the nature of the fluid, and a steady-state momentum balance in the \(z\) direction on a cylinder of fluid of radius \(r\) and length \(L\) gives:

\(\underbrace{2 \pi r L \tau_{r z}}_{\text {Shear }}+\underbrace{\left(p_{1}-p_{2}\right) \pi r^{2}}_{\text {Pressure }}=0,\) (E11.2.1)

or:

\(\tau_{r z}=-\frac{r}{2}\left(\frac{p_{1}-p_{2}}{L}\right)=\underbrace{-\frac{r}{2} \underbrace{\left(-\frac{d p}{d z}\right)}_{\text {Positive }}}_{\text {Negative }},\) (E11.2.2)

which can also be derived by integration of the last of Eqn. (5.74). Fig. E11.2 shows the resulting linear variation of the shear stress, and the arrows pointing to the left reflect that \(\tau_{r z}\) (by convention, the stress exerted by the region of greater \(r\) on the region of lesser \(r\) ) is physically in the negative- \(z\) direction. Let \(\tau_{w}\) denote the value of \(\tau_{r z}\) at the wall.

\(\begin{aligned} \rho\left(\frac{\partial v_{z}}{\partial t}+v_{r} \frac{\partial v_{z}}{\partial r}+\frac{v_{\theta}}{r} \frac{\partial v_{z}}{\partial \theta}\right.&\left.+v_{z} \frac{\partial v_{z}}{\partial z}\right) \\&=-\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial\left(r \tau_{r z}\right)}{\partial r}+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial \tau_{\theta z}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial \tau_{z z}}{\partial z}+\rho g_{z} . (5.74)\end{aligned}\)

Next consider the stress and strain-rate tensors for the case of \(v_{r}=v_{\theta}=0\) :

\(\tau=\left(\begin{array}{lll}\tau_{r r} & \tau_{r z} & \tau_{r \theta} \\ \tau_{z r} & \tau_{z z} & \tau_{z \theta} \\ \tau_{\theta r} & \tau_{\theta z} & \tau_{\theta \theta} \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}0 & \tau_{r z} & 0 \\ \tau_{z r} & 0 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 0\end{array}\right), \quad \dot{\gamma}=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}0 & \frac{d v_{z}}{d r} & 0 \\ \frac{d v_{z}}{d r} & 0 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 0\end{array}\right), \quad(E 11.2.3)\)

leading to:

\(\tau: \tau=2 \tau_{r z}^{2}, \quad \dot{\gamma}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{2} I_{2}}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{2} \dot{\gamma}: \dot{\gamma}}=\left|\frac{d v_{z}}{d r}\right|.\) (E11.2.4)

The Bingham model of Eqn. (11.21) then reduces to:

Low shears: \(\quad \frac{1}{2}(\tau: \tau) \leq \tau_{0}^{2}, \quad \dot{\gamma}=0\),

High shears: \(\quad \frac{1}{2}(\tau: \tau)>\tau_{0}^{2}, \quad \tau=\left(\eta+\frac{\tau_{0}}{\dot{\gamma}}\right) \dot{\gamma}\), (11.21)

Low shears: \(\quad\left|\tau_{r z}\right| \leq \tau_{0}, \quad \frac{d v_{z}}{d r}=0,\)

High shears: \(\quad\left|\tau_{r z}\right|>\tau_{0}, \quad \tau_{r z}=\left(\eta+\frac{\tau_{0}}{\dot{\gamma}}\right) \frac{d v_{z}}{d r}=-\tau_{0}+\eta \frac{d v_{z}}{d r}.\) (E11.2.5)

There are two cases to be considered, depending on the relative values of \(\left|\tau_{w}\right|\) and \(\tau_{0}\) :

  1. If the maximum shear stress never exceeds the threshold value, \(\left|\tau_{w}\right| \leq \tau_{0}\), Eqn. (11.2.5) indicates a zero velocity gradient everywhere. Since the velocity is zero at the wall, the velocity is also zero at all radial locations. That is, the applied pressure gradient is insufficient to move the fluid, which effectively remains as a rigid body.
  2. If the maximum shear stress exceeds the threshold value, \(\left|\tau_{w}\right|>\tau_{0}\) (the case shown in Fig. E11.2), let \(\left|\tau_{r z}\right|=\tau_{0}\) at the radial location \(r_{0}\). There are two regions for consideration:

(a) A central zone, \(r \leq r_{0}\), in which \(\left|\tau_{r z}\right| \leq \tau_{0}\), so that from Eqn. (E11.2.5) there is zero velocity gradient and the velocity profile is flat.

(b) An outer zone stretching to the wall, \(a \geq r>r_{0}\), in which \(\left|\tau_{r z}\right|>\tau_{0}\). The resulting velocity profile can be obtained by integration of the high-shear part of Eqn. (E11.2.5) and will be curved as shown in Fig. E11.2.

Derivation of the expressions for the two parts of the velocity profile for the second of these cases is left as an exercise see Problem 11.1.
