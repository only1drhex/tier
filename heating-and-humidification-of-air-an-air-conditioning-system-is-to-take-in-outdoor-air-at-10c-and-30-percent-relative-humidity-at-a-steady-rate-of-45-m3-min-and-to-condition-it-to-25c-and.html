Guest Question.

Heating and Humidification of Air

An air-conditioning system is to take in outdoor air at 10°C and 30 percent relative humidity at a steady rate of \(45 m^3/min\) and to condition it to 25°C and 60 percent relative humidity. The outdoor air is first heated to 22°C in the heating section and then humidified by the injection of hot steam in the humidifying section. Assuming the entire process takes place at a pressure of 100 kPa, determine (a) the rate of heat supply in the heating section and (b) the mass flow rate of the steam required in the humidifying section.

Admin
Solution

Outdoor air is first heated and then humidified by steam injection. The rate of heat transfer and the mass flow rate of steam are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 This is a steady-flow process and thus the mass flow rate of dry air remains constant during the entire process. 2 Dry air and water vapor are ideal gases. 3 The kinetic and potential energy changes are negligible.

Properties The constant-pressure specific heat of air at room temperature is \(c_{p}=1.005 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\), and its gas constant is \(R_{a}=0.287 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\) (. The saturation pressure of water is \(1.2281 \mathrm{kPa}\) at \(10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\), and \(3.1698 \mathrm{kPa}\) at \(25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\). The enthalpy of saturated water vapor is \(2519.2 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\) at \(10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\), and \(2541.0 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\) at \(22^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\) .

Analysis We take the system to be the heating or the humidifying section, as appropriate. The schematic of the system and the psychrometric chart of the process are shown in Fig. \(14-24\). We note that the amount of water vapor in the air remains constant in the heating section \(\left(\omega_{1}=\omega_{2}\right)\) but increases in the humidifying section \(\left(\omega_{3}>\omega_{2}\right)\).

(a) Applying the mass and energy balances on the heating section gives

\(\text{Dry air mass balance:}\quad\quad\dot{m}_{a_{1}}=\dot{m}_{a_{2}}=\dot{m}_{a}\)

 

\(\text{Water mass balance:}\quad\quad \dot{m}_{a_{1}} \omega_{1}=\dot{m}_{a_{2}} \omega_{2} \rightarrow \omega_{1}=\omega_{2}\)

 

\(\text{Energy balance:} \quad\quad \dot{Q}_{\text {in }}+\dot{m}_{a} h_{1}=\dot{m}_{a} h_{2} \rightarrow \dot{Q}_{\text {in }}=\dot{m}_{a}\left(h_{2}-h_{1}\right)\)

 

The psychrometric chart offers great convenience in determining the properties of moist air. However, its use is limited to a specified pressure only, which is 1 atm (101.325 kPa) for the one given in the appendix. At pressures other than \(1 \mathrm{~atm}\), either other charts for that pressure or the relations developed earlier should be used. In our case, the choice is clear:

\(\begin{array}{c}P_{v_{1}}=\phi_{1} P_{g_{1}}=\phi P_{\text {sat } @ 10^{\circ} C}=(0.3)(1.2281 \mathrm{kPa})=0.368 \mathrm{kPa} \\\\P_{a_{1}}=P_{1}-P_{v_{1}}=(100-0.368) \mathrm{kPa}=99.632 \mathrm{kPa} \\\\v_{1}=\frac{R_{a} T_{1}}{P_{a}}=\frac{\left(0.287 \mathrm{kPa} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\right)(283 \mathrm{~K})}{99.632 \mathrm{kPa}}=0.815 \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg} \text { dry air } \\\\\dot{m}_{a}=\frac{\dot{V}_{1}}{v_{1}}=\frac{45 \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{min}}{0.815 \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg}}=55.2 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{min} \\\\\omega_{1}=\frac{0.622 P_{v_{1}}}{P_{1}-P_{v_{1}}}=\frac{0.622(0.368 \mathrm{kPa})}{(100-0.368) \mathrm{kPa}}=0.0023 \mathrm{~kg} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} / \mathrm{kg} \text { dry air } \\\\h_{1} =c_{p} T_{1}+\omega_{1} h_{g_{1}}=\left(1.005 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)\left(10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)+(0.0023)(2519.2 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}) \\\\ =15.8 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \text { dry } \text { air } \\\\h_{2} =c_{p} T_{2}+\omega_{2} h_{g_{2}}=\left(1.005 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)\left(22^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)+(0.0023)(2541.0 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}) \\\\ =28.0 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \text { dry air }\end{array}\)

since \(\omega_{2}=\omega_{1}\). Then, the rate of heat transfer to air in the heating section becomes

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{Q}_{\text {in }} &=\dot{m}_{a}\left(h_{2}-h_{1}\right)=(55.2 \mathrm{~kg} / \min )[(28.0-15.8) \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{kg}] \\&=\mathbf{6 7 3} \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{min}\end{aligned}\)

(b) The mass balance for water in the humidifying section can be expressed as

\(\dot{m}_{a_{2}} \omega_{2}+\dot{m}_{w}=\dot{m}_{a_{3}} \omega_{3}\)

or

\(\dot{m}_{w}=\dot{m}_{a}\left(\omega_{3}-\omega_{2}\right)\)

where

\(\begin{aligned}\omega_{3} &=\frac{0.622 \phi_{3} P_{g_{3}}}{P_{3}-\phi_{3} P_{g_{3}}}=\frac{0.622(0.60)(3.1698 \mathrm{kPa})}{[100-(0.60)(3.1698)] \mathrm{kPa}} \\&=0.01206 \mathrm{~kg} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} / \mathrm{kg} \text { dry air }\end{aligned}\)

Thus,

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{m}_{w} &=(55.2 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{min})(0.01206-0.0023) \\&=0.539 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{min}\end{aligned}\)

Discussion The result \(0.539 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{min}\) corresponds to a water requirement of close to one ton a day, which is significant.
