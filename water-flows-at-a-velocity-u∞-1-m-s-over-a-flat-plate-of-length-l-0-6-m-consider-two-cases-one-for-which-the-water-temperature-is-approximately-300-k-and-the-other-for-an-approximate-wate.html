Guest Question.

Water flows at a velocity \(u_{∞} = 1 m/s\) over a flat plate of length L = 0.6 m. Consider two cases, one for which the water temperature is approximately 300 K and the other for an approximate water temperature of 350 K. In the laminar and turbulent regions, experimental measurements show that the local convection coefficients are well described by

 

\(\begin{matrix} h_{1am}(x)=C_{1am}x^{-0.5} & h_{turb}(x)=C_{turb}x^{-0.2} \end{matrix}\)

 

where x has units of m. At 300 K,

 

\(\begin{matrix} C_{1am,300}=395W/m^{1.5}\cdot K & C_{turb,300}=2330W/m^{1.8}\cdot K \end{matrix}\)
Admin
Solution

Known: Water flow over a flat plate, expressions for the dependence of the local convection coefficient with distance from the plate’s leading edge x, and approximate temperature of the water.

Find: Average convection coefficient, \(\bar{h}.\)

Assumptions:
1. Steady-state conditions.
2. Transition occurs at a critical Reynolds number of \(Re_{x,c}=5\times 10^{5}.\)

Properties: Table A.6, water \((\bar{T}\approx 300K):\rho =v_{f}^{-1}=997kg/m^{3},\mu =855\times 10^{-6}N\cdot s/m^{2}.\) Table A.6 \((\bar{T}\approx 350K):\rho =v_{f}^{-1}=974kg/m^{3},\mu =365\times 10^{-6}N\cdot s/m^{2}.\)

Analysis: The local convection coefficient is highly dependent on whether laminar or turbulent conditions exist. Therefore, we first determine the extent to which these conditions exist by finding the location where transition occurs, \(x_{c}\). From Equation 6.24, we know that at 300 K,

 

\(Re_{x,c}\equiv \frac{\rho u_{\infty }x_{c}}{\mu }=5\times 10^{5}\) (6.24)

 

\(x_{c}=\frac{Re_{x,c}\mu }{\rho u_{\infty }}=\frac{5\times 10^{5}\times 855\times 10^{-6}N\cdot s/m^{2}}{997kg/m^{3}\times 1m/s}=0.43m\)

 

while at 350 K,

 

\(x_{c}=\frac{Re_{x,c}\mu }{\rho u_{\infty }}=\frac{5\times 10^{5}\times 365\times 10^{-6}N\cdot s/m^{2}}{974kg/m^{3}\times 1m/s}=0.19m\)

 

From Equation 6.14

 

\(\bar{h}=\frac{1}{L}\int_{0}^{L}hdx\) (6.14)

we know that :

\(\bar{h}=\frac{1}{L}\int_{0}^{L}hdx=\frac{1}{L}\left [ \int_{0}^{x_{c}} h_{1am} dx +\int_{x_{c}}^{L} h_{turb} dx\right ]\)

 

or

 

\(\bar{h}=\frac{1}{L}\left [ \frac{C_{1am}}{0.5}x^{0.5}\left|_{0}^{x_{c}}+\frac{C_{turb}}{0.8}x^{0.8} \right |_{x_{c}}^{L} \right]\)

 

At 300 K,

 

\(\bar{h}=\frac{1}{0.6m}\left [ \frac{395W/m^{1.5}\cdot K}{0.5}\times (0.43^{0.5})m^{0.5}+\frac{2330W/m^{1.8}\cdot K}{0.8} \times (0.6^{0.8}-0.43^{0.8})m^{0.8} \right ]=1620W/m^{2}\cdot K\)

 

while at 350 K,

 

\(\bar{h}=\frac{1}{0.6m}\left [ \frac{477W/m^{1.5}\cdot K}{0.5}\times (0.19^{0.5})m^{0.5}+\frac{3600W/m^{1.8}\cdot K}{0.8} \times (0.6^{0.8}-0.19^{0.8})m^{0.8} \right ]=3710W/m^{2}\cdot K\)

Comments:
1. The average convection coefficient at \(T \approx 350 K\) is over twice as large as the value at \(T \approx 300 K.\) This strong temperature dependence is due primarily to the shift of \(x_{c}\) that is associated with the smaller viscosity of the water at the higher temperature. Careful consideration of the temperature dependence of fluid properties is crucial when performing
a convection heat transfer analysis.

2. Spatial variations in the local convection coefficient are significant. The largest local convection coefficients occur at the leading edge of the flat plate, where the laminar thermal boundary layer is extremely thin, and just downstream of \(x_{c},\) where the turbulent boundary layer is thinnest.
