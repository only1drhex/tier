Guest Question.

A metal-clad heating element of 6-mm diameter and emissivity ε = 1 is horizontally immersed in a water bath. The surface temperature of the metal is 255°C under steady-state boiling conditions. Estimate the power dissipation per unit length of the heater.

Admin
Solution

Known: Boiling from outer surface of horizontal cylinder in water.

Find: Power dissipation per unit length for the cylinder, \(q^{\prime }_{s}\).

Assumptions:
1. Steady-state conditions.
2. Water exposed to standard atmospheric pressure and at uniform temperature \(T_{sat}\).

Properties: , saturated water, liquid (100°C)\(: ρ_{l} = 1/v_{f}= 957.9 kg/m^{3},h_{fg} = 2257 kJ/kg.\) , water vapor at atmospheric pressure \((T_{f} ≈ 450 K): ρ_{v} = 0.4902 kg/m^{3} , c_{p,v} = 1.980 kJ/kg . K, k_{v} = 0.0299 W/m. K, μ_{v} = 15.25 × 10^{-6} N .s/m^{2}.\)

Analysis: The excess temperature is

\(ΔT_{e} = T_{s} – T_{sat} = 255°C – 100°C = 155°C\)

According to the boiling curve of Figure 10.4, film pool boiling conditions are achieved, in which case heat transfer is due to both convection and radiation. The heat transfer rate fol-lows from Equation 10.3, written on a per unit length basis for a cylindrical surface of diameter D:

\( q^{\prime \prime}_{s} = h (T_s – T_{sat}) = h ΔT_e \) (10.3)

\(q^{\prime }_{s} = q^{\prime \prime }_{s}π D = \bar{h} πD ΔT_{e}\)

The heat transfer coefficient \( \bar{h}\) is calculated from Equation 10.9,

\(\bar{h}^{4/3} = \bar{h}^{4/3}_{conv} + \bar{h}_{rad}\bar{h}^{1/3}\)

where the convection and radiation heat transfer coefficients follow from Equations 10.8 and 10.11, respectively. For the convection coefficient:

\( \overline{Nu}_D = \frac{\overline{h}_{conv}D }{k_v} = C \left[ \frac{g(\rho _l – \rho _v)h_{fg}^{\prime}D^3}{\nu _v k_v (T_s -T_{sat})} \right] ^{1/4} \) (10.8)

\( \bar{h}_{conv} = 0.62 \left[\frac{k_{v}^{3} \rho_{v}(\rho_{l} – \rho_{v})g(h_{fg} + 0.8c_{p,v} \Delta T_{e})}{\mu _{v} D \Delta T_{e}} \right]^{1/4}\)

 

\(\bar{h}_{conv} = 0.62 × \left[\frac{(0.0299 W/m . K)^{3} × 0.4902 kg/m^{3} (957.9 – 0.4902) kg/m^{3}× 9.8 m/s^{2} }{1}× \frac{(2257 × 10^{3} J/kg+ 0.8 × 1.98 × 10^{3} J/kg . K × 155°C)}{15.25 × 10^{-6} N . s/m^{2} × 6 × 10^{-3} m × 155°C} \right]^{1/4}\)

 

\( \bar{h}_{conv} = 238 W/m^{2} . K\)

For the radiation heat transfer coefficient:

\(\bar{h}_{rad} = \frac{\varepsilon \sigma (T^{4}_{s} -T^{4}_{sat} )}{T_{s} -T_{sat}} \)

 

\(\bar{h}_{rad} = \frac{ 5.67 × 10^{-8} W/m^{2} . K^{4} (528^{4} – 373^{4} )K^{4}}{(528 – 373) K} = 21.3 W/m^{2} . K \)

Solving Equation 10.9 by trial and error,

\(\bar{h}^{4/3} = 238^{4/3} + 21.3 \bar{h}^{1/3}\)

it follows that

\(\bar{h} = 254.1 W/m^{2} . K \)

Hence the heat transfer rate per unit length of heater element is

\(q^{\prime }_{s} = 254.1 W/m^{2} . K × π × 6 × 10^{-3} m × 155°C = 742 W/m\)

Comments: Equation 10.10 is appropriate for estimating \(\bar{h} \); it provides a value of \(254.0 W/m^{2} . K.\)

\( \overline{h} = \overline{h}_{conv} + \frac{3}{4}\overline{h}_{rad} \) (10.10)
