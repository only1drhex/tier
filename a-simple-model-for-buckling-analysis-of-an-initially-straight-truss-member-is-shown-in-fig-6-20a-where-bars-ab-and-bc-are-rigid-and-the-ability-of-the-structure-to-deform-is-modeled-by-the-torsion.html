Guest Question.

A simple model for buckling analysis of an initially straight truss member is shown in Fig. 6.20(a) where bars AB and BC are rigid, and the ability of the structure to deform is modeled by the torsional spring. Determine the value of P for which the structure buckles.

Admin
Solution

Although this is a crude model for a straight truss member, it is easy to analyze and its results are revealing. The FBD for the buckled structure is shown in Fig. 6.20(b). Note that because of the torsional spring, members AB and BC are not two-force members, although the structure ABC as a whole is. For this reason (or by writing \(\sum{M_A}=0\) and \(\sum{M_C}=0\)), we determine \(A_x = C_x = 0\), and \(\sum{F_y}=0\) gives \(C_y = P\). Next, we remove* member AB from the structure, giving the FBD shown in Fig. 6.20(c). Then

\(\sum{M_B}=0\): \(-M_t + P \frac{L}{2}\sin \theta = 0\). (6.23)

Noting that the torsional spring is twisted by \(2\theta\), the spring law, Eq. (\(M_t = k_t \theta\) where

\(M_t\) is the moment produced by the spring;

\(\theta\) is the rotation of the spring measured in radians from the unloaded geometry;

\(k_t\) is the spring stiffness (units: moment/radian).) on p. 272, gives \(M_t = k(2\theta)\). Assuming small angles with \(\theta\) measured in radians, \(\sin \theta \approx \theta\), and Eq. (6.23) becomes

\((2k – P\frac{L}{2})\theta = 0\). (6.24)

Equation (6.24) has two solutions:

Solution 1: \(\theta = 0\) trivial solution, (6.25)

Solution 2: \(2k – P\frac{L}{2} = 0\) buckling solution. (6.26)

Solution 1 is called the trivial solution because it says that structure ABC remains straight. Solution 2 applies when

\(P_{critical} = 4k/L\), (6.27)

where the subscript critical is added to emphasize that this value of P causes the structure to buckle. When \(P = P_{critical}\), according to Eq. (6.24), \(\theta\) can have arbitrary value. Observe that if the spring becomes stiffer, \(P_{critical}\) increases whereas if the length of the structure increases, \(P_{critical}\) decreases.

This solution and its ramifications are summarized in Fig. 6.21. Let force P slowly increase from zero value. As long as \(P \lt P_{critical} = 4k/L\), equilibrium [Eq. (6.24)] requires \(\theta = 0\). When \(P = P_{critical} = 4k/L\), equilibrium is satisfied regardless of the value of \(\theta\), which can have arbitrary positive or negative values. Thus, as P reaches \(P_{critical}\), the structure undergoes an abrupt change from \(\theta = 0\) to \(\theta \neq 0\). Further, there is no particular preference for \(\theta\) to have a positive or negative value, and this branching of the response is often called a bifurcation. Any attempt to apply a force greater than \(P_{critical}\) will cause a dynamic event in which the member rapidly collapses.

* Section 6.4 discusses procedures for taking a structure such as this apart for purposes of determining the forces supported by multiforce members.
