Airflow through a C onverging-D iverging Nozzle

Air enters a converging-diverging nozzle, shown in Fig. 17-28, at 1.0 MPa and 800 K with a negligible velocity. The flow is steady, one-dimensional, and isentropic with k = 1.4. For an exit Mach number of Ma = 2 and a throat area of 20 cm\(^2\), determine (a) the throat conditions, (b) the exit plane conditions, including the exit area, and (c) the mass flow rate through the nozzle.

Admin
Solution

Air flows through a converging-diverging nozzle. The throat and the exit conditions and the mass flow rate are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 Air is an ideal gas with constant specific heats at room temperature. 2 Flow through the nozzle is steady, one-dimensional, and isentropic.

Properties The specific heat ratio of air is given to be k = 1.4. The gas constant of air is 0.287 kJ/kg.K.

 

Analysis The exit Mach number is given to be 2. Therefore, the flow must be sonic at the throat and supersonic in the diverging section of the nozzle. Since the inlet velocity is negligible, the stagnation pressure and stagnation temperature are the same as the inlet temperature and pressure, \(P_0\) = 1.0 MPa and \(T_0\) = 800 K. The stagnation density is

 

\(\rho_{0}=\frac{P_{0}}{R T_{0}}=\frac{1000 \mathrm{kPa}}{\left(0.287 \mathrm{kPa} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\right)(800 \mathrm{~K})}=4.355 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\)

 

(a) At the throat of the nozzle M a=1, and from we read

\(\frac{P^{*}}{P_{0}}=0.5283 \quad \frac{T^{*}}{T_{0}}=0.8333 \quad \frac{\rho^{*}}{\rho_{0}}=0.6339\)

 

Thus,

\(P^{*}=0.5283 P_{0}=(0.5283)(1.0 \mathrm{MPa})=0.5283 \mathrm{MPa} \)

 

\(T^{*}=0.8333 T_{0}=(0.8333)(800 \mathrm{~K})=666.6 \mathrm{~K} \)

 

\(\rho^{*}=0.6339 \rho_{0}=(0.6339)\left(4.355 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)=2.761 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\)

 

Also,

 

\(V^{*} =c^{*}=\sqrt{k R T^{*}}:=\sqrt{(1.4)(0.287 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K})(666.6 \mathrm{~K})\left(\frac{1000 \mathrm{~m}^{2} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}{1 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}}\right)} \)

 

\(=517.5 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

(b) Since the flow is isentropic, the properties at the exit plane can also be calculated by using data from . For Ma =2 we read

\(\frac{P_{e}}{P_{0}}=0.1278 \frac{T_{e}}{T_{0}}=0.5556 \frac{\rho_{e}}{\rho_{0}}=0.2300 \mathrm{Ma}_e^{*}=1.6330 \frac{A_{e}}{A^{*}}=1.6875\)

 

Thus,

 

\(P_{e}=0.1278 P_{0}=(0.1278)(10 \mathrm{MPa})=0.1278 \mathrm{MPa} \)

 

\(T_{e}=0.5556 T_{0}=(0.5556)(800 \mathrm{~K})=444.5 \mathrm{~K} \)

 

\(\rho_{e}=0.2300 \rho_{0}=(0.2300)\left(4.355 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)=1.002 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3} \)

 

\(A_{e}=1.6875 A^{*}=(1.6875)\left(20 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}\right)=33.75 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}\)

 

and

\(V_{e}=\mathrm{Ma}_{e}{ }^{*} c^{*}=(1.6330)(517.5 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})=845.1 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

The nozzle exit velocity could also be determined from \(V_{e}=\mathrm{Ma}_{e} c_{e}\), where \(c_{e}\) is the speed of sound at the exit conditions:

 

\(V_{e} =\mathrm{Ma}_{e} c_{e}=\mathrm{Ma}_{e} \sqrt{k R T_{e}}=2 \sqrt{(1.4)(0.287 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K})(444.5 \mathrm{~K})\left(\frac{1000 \mathrm{~m}^{2} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}{1 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}}\right)} \)

 

\(=845.2 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

(c) Since the flow is steady, the mass flow rate of the fluid is the same at all sections of the nozzle. Thus it may be calculated by using properties at any cross section of the nozzle. Using the properties at the throat, we find that the mass flow rate is

\(\dot{m}=\rho ^* A^{*} V^{*}=\left(2.761 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\left(20 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{2}\right)(517.5 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})=2.86 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

 

Discussion Note that this is the highest possible mass flow rate that can flow through this nozzle for the specified inlet conditions.
