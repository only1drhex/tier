Guest Question.

Given:

Figures 13.3 and 13.4.

Required:

Verify that stress paths A, B, and C of Fig. 13.3 and A and D of Fig. 13.4 are correct as shown.

Admin
Solution

The initial conditions for all stress paths in Fig. 13.3 are \(p_{o}=\left(\sigma_{v}+\sigma_{h}\right) / 2=\sigma_{v}=\sigma_{h}\) and \(q_{o}=0\). Final conditions are [Eqs. (13.1) and (13.2)].

 

\(q=\frac{\sigma_{v}-\sigma_{h}}{2}\) (13.1)

 

\(p=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\sigma_{h}}{2}\) (13.2)

 

\(\begin{aligned}&q_{f}=\frac{\left(\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}\right)-\left(\sigma_{h}+\Delta \sigma_{h}\right)}{2} \\&p_{f}=\frac{\left(\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}\right)+\left(\sigma_{h}+\Delta \sigma_{h}\right)}{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

For stress path A, For stress path A, \(\Delta \sigma_{v}=\Delta \sigma_{v}\); so

 

\(\begin{aligned}&q_{f}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}-\sigma_{v}-\Delta \sigma_{v}}{2}=0 \\&p_{f}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}+\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}}{2}=\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}\end{aligned}\)

 

Thus the stress path A moves out on the p-axis by an amount \(\Delta \sigma_{v}=\Delta \sigma_{h}\).

For stress path B, For stress path B, \(\Delta \sigma_{h}=1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}\); so

 

\(\begin{gathered}q_{f}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}-\sigma_{v}-1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}}{2}=\frac{1}{4} \Delta \sigma_{v} \\P_{f}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}+\sigma_{v}+1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}}{2}=\sigma_{v}+\frac{3}{4} \Delta \sigma_{v}\end{gathered}\)

 

These values are the (p, q) coordinates of the end of stress path B. Thus the q and p both increase by an amount \(\Delta q=3 / 4 \Delta \sigma_{v}\) and \(\Delta p=3 / 4 \Delta \sigma_{v}\), which means that the stress path has a slope of 1/3 or is inclined at \(18.4^{\circ}\), as shown in Fig. 13.3.

For stress path C, \(\Delta \sigma_{h}=0\) and \(\Delta \sigma_{v}\) increases by some amount.

 

\(\begin{aligned}&q_{f}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}-\sigma_{v}}{2}=\frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_{v} \\&p_{f}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}+\sigma_{v}}{2}=\sigma_{v}+\frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_{v}\end{aligned}\)

 

So \(\Delta q=1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}\) and \(\Delta p=1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}\): Therefore the slope of the stress path must be 1 or inclined at \(45^{\circ}\). This solution holds also for stress path A in Fig. 13.4. Here initial conditions are nonhydrostatic, so

 

\(\begin{aligned}&q_{o}=\frac{\sigma_{v}-\sigma_{h}}{2} \\&p_{o}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\sigma_{h}}{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

The final coordinates for path A are

 

\(\begin{aligned}&q_{f}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}-\sigma_{h}}{2} \\&p_{f}=\frac{\sigma_{v}+\Delta \sigma_{v}+\sigma_{h}}{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

So \(\Delta q=1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}\) and \(\Delta p=1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}\), which is the same as for stress path C in Fig. 13.3.

For stress path D in Fig. 13.4, \(\Delta \sigma_{v}\) decreases while \(\Delta \sigma_{h}\) increases. Initial \(\left(p_{o}, q_{o}\right)\) are the same as path A in this figure, while the final values of \(\left(p_{f}, q_{f}\right)\) are

 

\(\begin{aligned}&q_{f}=\frac{\left(\sigma_{v}-\Delta \sigma_{v}\right)-\left(\sigma_{h}+\Delta \sigma_{h}\right)}{2} \\&p_{f}=\frac{\left(\sigma_{v}-\Delta \sigma_{v}\right)+\left(\sigma_{h}+\Delta \sigma_{h}\right)}{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

So

 

\(\Delta q=-1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}-1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{h} \quad \text { and } \quad \Delta p=-1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{v}+1 / 2 \Delta \sigma_{h}\)

 

The actual slope of the stress path depends on the relative magnitudes of \(\Delta \sigma_{v}\) and \(\Delta \sigma_{h}\), but in general it trends down and out, as shown in Fig. 13.4.
