Guest Question.

The circuit in Fig. 14.39 is called a Wein bridge oscillator. Its output voltage is a sine wave whose frequency can be tuned. Let us design this circuit for an oscillation frequency of 10 kHz.

Admin
Solution

This network looks odd for two reasons. First, there is no input signal! Second, we have not seen an op-amp circuit in which the output is connected back to the noninverting input terminal. However, we do know that if the op-amp is working properly, its input currents are zero and the difference in voltage between the two input terminals is zero. We will employ these constraints to write two transfer functions from the op-amp output back to each of the op-amp inputs. The first is defined as

 

\(\mathbf{H}_{\text {neg }}(s)=\frac{\mathbf{V}_{\text {neg }}}{\mathbf{V}_{O}}=\frac{R_{1}}{R_{1}+R_{2}}\) 14.34

 

and the second is

 

\(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{pos}}(s)=\frac{\mathbf{V}_{\text {pos }}}{\mathbf{V}_{0}}=\frac{\mathbf{Z}_{1}}{\mathbf{Z}_{1}+\mathbf{Z}_{2}}\) 14.35

 

where \(\mathbf{Z}_{1}\) is the parallel R-C network and \(\mathbf{Z}_{2}\) is the series R-C network. Thus,

 

 

\(\mathbf{Z}_{1}=\frac{R / s C}{(1 / s C)+R}=\frac{R}{1+s R C} \qquad\) and \(\qquad \mathbf{Z}_{2}=R+\frac{1}{s C}=\frac{1+s R C}{s C}\) 14.36

 

Substituting Eq. (14.36) into (14.35) yields

 

\(\mathbf{H}_{\text {pos }}(s)=\frac{\mathbf{V}_{\text {pos }}}{\mathbf{V}_{o}}=\frac{\frac{R}{1+s R C}}{\frac{R}{1+s R C}+\frac{1+s R C}{s C}}=\frac{s R C}{s^{2}(R C)^{2}+3 s R C+1}\) 14.37

 

Since the voltage across the op-amp inputs is zero, \(\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{neg}}=\mathbf{V}_{\text {pos }}\) and, thus, \(\mathbf{H}_{\text {pos }}(s)=\mathbf{H}_{\text {neg }}(s)\) ! Note that \(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{neg}}(s)\) in Eq. (14.34) is just a resistor ratio and is therefore real. The op-amp forces the same to be true for \(\mathbf{H}_{\text {pos }}(s)\) at the frequency of oscillation! Now look at Eq. (14.37). Its numerator is purely imaginary. If \(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{pos}}(s)\) is to be real, then its denominator must also be purely imaginary. The result is

 

\((j \omega)^{2}(R C)^{2}+1=0 \Rightarrow \omega=\frac{1}{R C} \Rightarrow f=\frac{1}{2 \pi R C}\)

 

We arbitrarily select C = 10 nF and find

 

\(R=\frac{1}{2 \pi C f}=\frac{1}{2 \pi\left(10^{-8}\right)\left(10^{4}\right)}=1.59 \mathrm{k} \Omega\)

 

We still must determine values for \(R_{1}\) and \(R_{2}\). Examine once again the fact that \(\mathbf{H}_{\text {pos }}(s)=\mathbf{H}_{\text {neg }}(s)\). At 10 kHz, \(\mathbf{H}_{\text {pos }}(s)\) becomes

 

\(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{pos}}(s)=\frac{s R C}{s^{2}(R C)^{2}+3 s R C+1}=\frac{s R C}{3 s R C}=\frac{1}{3}\)

 

 

The same must be true for \(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{neg}}(s)\).

 

\(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{neg}}(s)=\frac{R_{1}}{R_{1}+R_{2}}=\frac{1}{3}\)

 

The only possible solution is \(R_{2}=2 R_{1}\). Arbitrarily selecting \(R_{1}=R=1.59 \mathrm{k} \Omega\), we find \(R_{2}=3.18 \mathrm{k} \Omega\).

 

 

What happens if \(\mathbf{H}_{\text {pos }}(s)\) does not equal \(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{neg}}(s)\) in the constructed circuit? If \(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{neg}}(s)\) is larger, the oscillations will die out. But if \(\mathbf{H}_{\text {pos }}(s)\) is larger, the oscillations grow until the op-amp output reaches the power supply limits. At that point, the output is more of a square wave than a sinusoid. Since it is physically impossible to ensure that \(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{pos}}(s)\) and \(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{neg}}(s)\) are exactly the same at 10 kHz, engineers usually replace \(R_{2}\) with a nonlinear resistor whose resistance decreases with increasing temperature. In this way, if the output oscillations begin to grow, more power is dissipated in the nonlinear resistance, decreasing its value. This decrease in resistance will increase \(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{neg}}(s)\) and bring the oscillator back to a balanced operating point.
