Guest Question.

Quick proof of Cramer’s rule. The determinant is a linear function of column 1. It is zero if two columns are equal. When \(b = Ax = {x}_{1}{a}_{1} + {x}_{2}{a}_{2} + {x}_{3}{a}_{3}\) goes into the first column of A, the determinant of this matrix \({B}_{1}\) is

\(\left| \begin{matrix} b & {a}_{2} & {a}_{3}\end{matrix} \right| =\left| \begin{matrix} {x}_{1}{a}_{1} + {x}_{2}{a}_{2} + {x}_{3}{a}_{3} & {a}_{2} & {a}_{3} \end{matrix} \right| ={ x }_{ 1 }\left| \begin{matrix} {a}_{1} & {a}_{2} & {a}_{3}\end{matrix} \right|={ x }_{ 1 }\) detA.

(a) What formula for \({ x }_{ 1 }\) comes from left side = right side?

(b) What steps lead to the middle equation?

Admin
Solution

(a) \({x}_{1} \)= det \(( \left[ \begin{matrix} b & {a}_{2} & {a}_{3}\end{matrix} \right])\)/ det A, if det \(A \neq 0\)

(b) The determinant is linear in its first column so

\({x}_{1} \left| \begin{matrix} {a}_{1} & {a}_{2} & {a}_{3}\end{matrix} \right|+ {x}_{2} \left| \begin{matrix} {a}_{2} & {a}_{2} & {a}_{3}\end{matrix} \right|+{x}_{3} \left| \begin{matrix} {a}_{3} & {a}_{2} & {a}_{3}\end{matrix} \right|\).

The last two determinants are zero because of repeated columns, leaving

\({x}_{1} \left| \begin{matrix} {a}_{1} & {a}_{2} & {a}_{3}\end{matrix} \right| \)which is \({x}_{1}\) det A.
