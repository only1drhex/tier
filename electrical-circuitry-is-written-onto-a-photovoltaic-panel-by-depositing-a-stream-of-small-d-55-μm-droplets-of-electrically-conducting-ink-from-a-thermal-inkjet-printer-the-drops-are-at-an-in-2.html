Guest Question.

Electrical circuitry is written onto a photovoltaic panel by depositing a stream of small (D = 55 μm) droplets of electrically conducting ink from a thermal inkjet printer.

The drops are at an initial temperature of \( T_{i} \) = 200°C , and it is desirable for them to strike the panel at a temperature of \(T_{final}\) = 50°C. The quiescent air and surroundings are at \( T_{∞} = T_{sur} \) = 25°C , and the drops are ejected from the print head at their terminal velocity. Determine the required standoff distance L between the printer and the photovoltaic panel. The properties of the electrically conducting ink drop are \( ρ_{d} = 2400 kg/m^{3}, c_{d} = 800 J/kg . K\), and \( k_{d} = 5.0 W/m. K.\)

Admin
Solution

Known: Droplet size and properties, initial and desired final droplet temperature. Droplet injected at its terminal velocity.

Find: Required standoff distance between the printer and the photovoltaic panel.

Assumptions:
1. The Nusselt number is approximated by the Ranz and Marshall correlation for a falling droplet, Equation 7.57 \(\overset{-}{Nu}_{D} = 2 + 0.6 Re^{1/2}_{D} Pr^{1/3}\).
2. Constant air properties evaluated at 25°C.
3. Negligible radiation effects.
4. Negligible temperature variation within the droplets (lumped capacitance approximation).
5. Drag coefficient is determined by Stokes’ law.

Properties: , air \((T_{f} = 75°C): ρ = 1.002 kg/m^{3}, \nu = 20.72 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s.\) , air \((T_{∞} = 25°C): \nu = 15.71 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s, k = 0.0261 W/m. K, Pr = 0.708.\)

Analysis: Since the droplets travel at their terminal velocities, the net force on each drop must be zero. Hence the weight of the drop is offset by the buoyancy force associated with the displaced air and the drag force:

\(\rho _{d}g \left(\pi \frac{D^{3}}{6} \right) =\rho g \left(\pi \frac{D^{3}}{6} \right) + C_{D}\left( \frac{\pi D^{2}}{4} \right) \left(\rho \frac{V^{2}}{2} \right) \) (1)

where Equation 7.50 \(C_{D} \equiv \frac{F_{D}}{A_{f} (\rho V^{2}/2)} \)has been used to express the drag force \(F_{D}\). Since the droplets are small, we anticipate that the Reynolds number will also be small. If this is the case, Stokes’ law, Equation 7.55 \(C_{D} = \frac{24}{Re_{D}}\) \(Re_{D} \leq 0.5\) may be used to express the drag coefficient as

\(C_{D} = \frac{24}{Re_{D}} = \frac{24\nu }{ V D}\) (2)

Substituting Equation (2) into Equation (1) and solving for the velocity,

\( V = \frac{g D^{2} }{ 18 \nu \rho }(\rho_{d} – \rho) = \frac{9.81 m/s^{2} ×(55 × 10^{-6} m)^{2} }{ 18 × 20.72 × 10^{-6} m^{2}/s × 1.002 kg/m } × (2400 – 1.002)kg/m^{3} = 0.190 m/s = 190 mm/s\)

Therefore, the Reynolds number is \(Re_{D} = VD/ \nu = 0.190 m/s × 55 × 10^{-6} m/20.72 × 10^{-6} m^{2} /s = 0.506\), and use of Stokes’ law is appropriate. The Nusselt number and convection coefficient can be calculated from the Ranz and Marshall correlation, Equation 7.57 \(\overset{-}{Nu}_{D} = 2 + 0.6 Re^{1/2}_{D} Pr^{1/3}\) , using properties evaluated at the free stream temperature (see Table 7.7):

\(\overset{-}{Nu}_{D} = 2 + 0.6 Re^{1/2}_{D} Pr^{1/3} = 2 + 0.6 \times \left(\frac{0.190 m/s \times 55 \times 10^{-6} m}{15.71\times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s} \right)^{1/2} \times 0.708^{1/3} =2.44\)

 

\( \bar{h} = \frac{\overset{-}{Nu}_{D} k }{D} = \frac{2.44 × 0.0261 W/m . K}{55 × 10^{-6}m} = 1160 W/m^{2} . K\)

Applying the lumped capacitance method, Equation 5.5, the required time-of-flight is then

\(t = \frac{\rho _{d}V c_{d}}{\bar{h} A_{s}} \ln \left(\frac{\theta _{i}}{\theta _{final}} \right) = \frac{\rho _{d} c_{d} D}{6 \bar{h} }\ln \left(\frac{T_{i} – T_{\infty }}{T_{final} – T_{\infty }} \right) = \frac{2400 kg/m^{3} \times 800 J/kg . K \times 55 \times 10^{-6} m}{6 \times 1160 W/m^{2} . K} \ln \left(\frac{(200 – 25)°C}{(50 – 25)°C} \right) =0.030 s\)

and the standoff distance is

L = V t = 0.190 m/s × 0.030 s = 0.0056 m = 5.6 mm

Comments:
1. The validity of the lumped capacitance method may be determined by calculating the Biot number. Applying Equation 5.10 in the conservative fashion with \(L_{c} = D/2,\)

\(Bi = \frac{\bar{h} (D/2) }{k_{p}} = \left(\frac{1160 W/m^{2} . K × 55 × 10^{-6} m}{2} \right) | 5.0 W/m . K = 0.006 < 0.1\)

and the criterion is satisfied.

2. Use of Equation 7.55, Stokes’ law, to describe the Reynolds number dependence of the drag coefficient is valid since \(Re_{D}≤ 0.5\). For larger particles, Figure 7.9 would need to be consulted to determine the relationship between \(C_{D}\) and \(Re_{D}\).
3. If the particles were not injected at their terminal velocity, they would either accelerate or decelerate during flight, complicating the analysis.

4. Assuming blackbody behavior and using the maximum (initial) temperature of the particle, \(T_{s} = 473 K\), the maximum radiation heat transfer coefficient is \(h_{r} = σ (T_{s} + T_{sur})(T_{s}^{2} + T^{2}_{sur}) = 5.67 × 10^{-8} W/m^{2} . K^{4} × (473 K + 298 K) × [(473 K)^{2} + (298 K)^{2}] =13.7 W/m^{2}. K.\) Since \(h_{r} ≪ \bar{h} \) , radiation heat transfer is negligible.
