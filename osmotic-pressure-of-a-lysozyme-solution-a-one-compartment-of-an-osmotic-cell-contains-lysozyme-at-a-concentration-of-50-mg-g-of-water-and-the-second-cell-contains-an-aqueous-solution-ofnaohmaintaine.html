Osmotic Pressure of a Lysozyme Solution

a. One compartment of an osmotic cell contains lysozyme at a concentration of 50 mg/g of water, and the second cell contains an aqueous solution ofNaOHmaintained at a concentration of \(1.585 \times 10^{-3}\) M, which has a pH of 11.2. Compute the osmotic pressure difference between the two cells.

b. One compartment of an osmotic cell contains lysozyme at a concentration of 50 mg/g of water, and the second cell contains an aqueous solution of \(NaOH\) maintained at a concentration of 0.1 M, which has a pH of 13. Compute the osmotic pressure difference between the two cells.

Admin
Solution

a. Since at pH = 11.2, lysozyme is at its isoelectric point and therefore uncharged. Consequently, the concentration of sodium ions and hydroxyl ions in each phase is the same, 1.585 × \(10^{-3}\) moles per kg of water. Also, from the previous illustration, the lysozyme concentration is 3.517 × \(10^{-3}\) moles per kg of water, so that the water mole fractions in each cell are

 

\(x_{\text {water }}^{ I }=\frac{55.51}{2 \times 1.585 \times 10^{-3}+55.51}=0.999943\)

 

and

 

\(x_{\text {water }}^{ II }=\frac{55.51}{2 \times 1.585 \times 10^{-3}+55.51+3.571 \times 10^{-3}}=0.999879\)

 

Therefore, the osmotic pressure is

 

\(\Pi=\frac{R T}{\underline{V}_{\text {solvent }}} \ln \left(\frac{x_{\text {solvent }}^{ I }}{x_{\text {solvent }}^{ II }}\right)=\frac{8.314 \times 10^{-5} \frac{ bar m ^{3}}{ mol K } \times 298.15 K }{18 \times 10^{-6} \frac{ m ^{3}}{ mol }} \ln \left(\frac{0.999943}{0.999879}\right)=0.881\) bar

b. Using the results from , the water mole fraction in compartment I is

 

\(x_{\text {water }}^{ I }=\frac{55.51}{2 \times 0.1+55.51}=0.996410\)

 

the water mole fraction in compartment II is

 

\(x_{\text {water }}^{ II }=\frac{55.51}{0.085213+0.117352+55.51+3.571 \times 10^{-3}}=0.996300\)

 

and the osmotic pressure difference is

 

\(\Pi=\frac{R T}{\underline{V_{\text {solvent }}}} \ln \left(\frac{x_{\text {solvent }}^{ I }}{x_{\text {solvent }}^{ II }}\right)=\frac{8.314 \times 10^{-5} \frac{ bar m ^{3}}{ mol K } \times 298.15 K }{18 \times 10^{-6} \frac{ m ^{3}}{ mol }} \ln \left(\frac{0.996410}{0.996300}\right)=1.520\) bar

 

Comment

If the sodium ions partitioned equally in this last case, the concentrations in compartment II would be

 

\(M_{\text {lys }}^{ II }=3.571 \times 10^{-3} M \quad M_{ Na }^{ II }=0.1\) M

 

and

 

\(M_{ OH }^{ II }=0.1-9 \times 3.571 \times 10^{-3}=0.067861\) M

 

\(x_{\text {water }}^{ II }=\frac{55.51}{0.1+0.067861+55.51+3.571 \times 10^{-3}}=0.996921\)

 

and the osmotic pressure difference would be

 

\(\Pi=\frac{8.314 \times 10^{-5} \frac{ bar m ^{3}}{ mol K } \times 298.15 K }{18 \times 10^{-6} \frac{ m ^{3}}{ mol }} \ln \left(\frac{0.996410}{0.996921}\right)=-7.060\) bar

 

We see from this the importance of including the Gibbs-Donnan equilibrium effect when determining the partitioning of ions across a membrane when there is an ionic species (here the protein) that cannot pass through the membrane. In this case we see that by neglecting this effect, not only is the magnitude of the osmotic pressure changed, but so is its sign.
