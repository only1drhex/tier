Guest Question.

Given the state equation \(\dot{x}(t)=Ax(t)+B\eta(t) \) where \(A=\left[\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 &0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ -5 & -7 & -3 \end{matrix} \right] \)

\(B=\left[\begin{matrix}0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right] \)

find the transformation x = Pz that transforms A into a canonical form.

Admin
Solution

The eigenvalues of A are complex:

\(\left|\lambda I -A\right|=0 \)

 

\(\left|\begin{matrix} \lambda & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda & -1 \\ 5 & 7 & \lambda +3 \end{matrix} \right|=0 \)

 

\(\lambda +3\lambda ^2 +7\lambda +5\)

The roots of the characteristic equation are:

\(\lambda =-1 , \lambda =-1\pm 2i\)

The eigenvectors can be found by solving the equation:

\((\lambda _i-A)P_i=0\)

For \(\lambda =-1\)

\(-P_{11}- P_{21}=0\)

 

\(-P_{21}- P_{31}=0\)

 

\(5P_{11}+7P_{21}+2P_{31}=0\)

If in the first equation we let \(P_{11}=1\) we find that \(P_{21}=-1\). From the second equation we can determine \(P_{31}=-P_{21}=1\). Therefore the first eigenvector is:

\(P_1 = \left[\begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right] \)

The third equation can be used to check if an error has been made.

The eigenvector for the complex root \(\lambda = – 1 + 2i\) can be found in a similar manner. The equations are

\(( – 1 + 2i)P_{12} – P_{22} = 0\)

 

\(( – 1 + 2i)P_{22} – P_{32} = 0\)

 

\(5P_{12}+7P_{22}+ (2 + 2i) P_{32}=0\)

Again we let the first element of the eigenvector be 1, \(P_{12}=1\) then \(P_{22} = -1 + 2i.\). From the second equation

\(P_{32}=( – 1 + 2i)P_{22}\)

 

\(P_{32}=-3 -4i\)

Therefore, the eigenvector for \(\lambda = – 1 + 2i\) is

\(P_2=\left[\begin{matrix} 1 \\ – 1+2i \\ – 3 – 4i \end{matrix} \right] \)

The eigenvector for the complex conjugate eigenvalue \(\lambda = – 1 – 2i\) will be the complex conjugate of \(P_2\).

\(P_3=\left[\begin{matrix} 1 \\ – 1-2i \\ – 3 + 4i \end{matrix} \right] \)

Now the new plant matrix can be determined

\(A=P^{-1}AP\)

 

\(A=\left[\begin{matrix} -1. & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 +2i & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 -2i \end{matrix} \right] \)

When the eigenvalues are complex an alternate way to express the A matrix is as follows:

\(A=\left[\begin{matrix} \lambda _1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma _1 & \omega _1 \\ 0 & -\omega _1 & \sigma _1 \end{matrix} \right] \)

where \(\sigma _1\) is the real part and \(\omega _1\), is the imaginary part of the complex root. The elements of transformation matrix P for the complex eigenvalues can be expressed as

\(P_1 = \alpha + i\beta (9.72)\) \(P_2 = \alpha – i\beta \)

The complex eigenvector can be expressed in terms of the real and imaginary parts as
follows:

\(P= [\begin{matrix} \alpha & \beta \end{matrix} ] (9.73) \)

For this example we have one real eigenvector and one complex eigenvector and its conjugate. The transformation matrix can be expressed for this example as

\(P= [\begin{matrix} P_1 & \alpha & \beta \end{matrix} ] (9.74) \)

where \(P_1\) is the eigenvector for the real root and \(\alpha and \beta \) are determined from the complex eigenvector as follows:

\(P_2 =\left[\begin{matrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ -3 \end{matrix} \right]+i\left[\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ -4 \end{matrix} \right] \)

therefore \(P=\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & -1 & 2 \\ 1 & -3 & -4 \end{matrix}\right] \)

The diagonalized matrix A is

\(A=P^{-1}AP= \left[\begin{matrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \\ 0 & -2 & -1 \end{matrix}\right] \)
