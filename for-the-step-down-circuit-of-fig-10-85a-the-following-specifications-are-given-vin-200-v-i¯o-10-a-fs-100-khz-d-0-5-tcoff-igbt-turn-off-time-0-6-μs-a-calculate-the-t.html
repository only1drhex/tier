Guest Question.

For the step-down circuit of Fig. 10.85(a), the following specifications are given:

\(V_{in} = 200 V, \overline{I}_o = 10 A , f_s = 100 kHz , D = 0.5, t_{c(off)}\) = IGBT turn-off time = \(0.6 \mu s\).

a) Calculate the turn-off losses of the IGBT without a snubber and using the criterion that the IGBT voltage reaches its final value \(V_{in}\) in \(0.1 \mu s\).

b) Design a turn-off snubber using the criterion that the IGBT voltage reaches its final value at the same time that the IGBT current reaches zero.

c) When the snubber is applied to IGBT calculate the IGBT turn-off losses and the power dissipated in the snubber resistor.

Admin
Solution

a) According to the above specifications the IGBT switching characteristics and power losses before the snubber is added are shown in Fig. 10.91.

As can be seen from Fig. 10.91, during turn-off transition the IGBT reaches 200 V while the current remains to have a value of 10 A which results to peak instantaneous power of (200)(10) = 2 kW. According to Fig. 10.91 the energy losses of the IGBT during turn-off transition, are given by:

\(W_{Q(off)}\) = IGBT turn-off transition energy losess =\(\int_{0}^{t_{c(off)}}{v_Q(t) i_Q(t) dt = \frac{1}{2}V_{in}\overline{I}_o t_{c(off)}}\)

 

\(= \frac{1}{2}(200)(10)(0.6 \times 10^{-6}) = 600 \mu J\) or \(\mu W s\)

Therefore, the turn-off transition power losses consumed on the IGBT are:

\(P_{Q(off)(without snubber)} =\frac{W_{Q(off)(without snubber)}}{T_s} = W_{Q(off)(without snubber)} \cdot f_s\)

 

\(=(600 \times 10^{-6})(100000) = 60 W\)

b) Using Eq. (\(C_s = \frac{\overline{I}_o t_{fi}}{2V_f}\)

where \(V_f\) = desired capacitor (and IGBT) voltage when IGBT current reaches zero.) the snubber capacitance is:

\(C_s = \frac{\overline{I}_o t_{fi}}{2V_f} = \frac{(10)(0.5 \times 10^{-6})}{(2)(200)} = 0.0125 \mu F\)

Since the duty cycle D = 0.5, then the time interval of the conduction time of the \(IGBT t_{on}\) is 50% of the switching period and is given by:

\(T_s\) = switching period =\(\frac{1}{f_s} = \frac{1}{100 \times 10^3}= 10 \mu s\)

 

\(t_{on} = DT_s = (0.5)(10 \mu s) = 5 \mu s\)

Using Eq. (\(R_s \leq \frac{t_{on}}{5C_s}\)) the snubber resistor value is:

\(R_s \leq \frac{t_{on}}{5C_s} = \frac{5 \times 10^{-6}}{5(0.0125 \times 10^{-6})} = 80 \Omega\)

Therefore: \(\tau = R_sC_s = (80)(0.0125 \mu F) = 1 \mu s\) (capacitor discharging in \(5\tau = 5 \mu s\)).

c) Using Eq. (\(R_s \leq \frac{t_{on}}{5C_s}\)) the power dissipated by the snubber resistor during turn-off transition is given by:

\(P_{Rs} = \frac{1}{2}C_sV^2_{in}f_s =\frac{1}{2}(0.0125 \times 10^{-6})(200)^2(100 \times 10^3) = 25 W\)

Using Eq. (\(P_{Q(off)(with nubber)} = \frac{1}{T_s}\int_{0}^{t_{fi}}{v_{Cs}i_Q dt}=\frac{1}{T_s}\int_{0}^{t_{fi}}{(\frac{\overline{I}_o t^2}{2C_s t_{fi}})\overline{I}_o(1-\frac{1}{t_f})dt}\)

 

\(= – \frac{\overline{I}^2_o t^2_{fi} f_s}{24C_s}\) for \(t_{fi} \leq t_{charge}\)) the turn-off power losses of the IGBT with a snubber are given by:

\(P_{Q(off)(with snubber)} =\frac{\overline{I}^2_o t^2_{fi} f_s }{24C_s} =\frac{10^2 (0.5 \times 10^{-6})^2(100 \times 10^3)}{24(0.0125 \times 10^{-6})} = 8.3 W\)

Therefore:

Total power losses at turn-off =\(P_{Q(off)(with snubber)} + P_{Rs} = 8.3 + 25 = 33.3 W\)

which has been reduced from 60 W (i.e., almost by half) when snubber circuit was not used.

Fig. 10.92 shows the simulation results of Example 10.10. As can be seen from Fig. 10.92(e) and (f), when the snubber is used the \(dv_Q/dt\) is increased and \(di_Q/dt\) is decreased resulting to lower IGBT turn-off power losses.
