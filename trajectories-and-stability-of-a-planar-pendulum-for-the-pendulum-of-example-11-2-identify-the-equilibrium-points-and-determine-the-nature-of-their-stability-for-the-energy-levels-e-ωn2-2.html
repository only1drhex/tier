Trajectories and Stability of a Planar Pendulum

For the pendulum of identify the equilibrium points and determine the nature of their stability for the energy levels \(E=\omega_{n}^{2}, 2 \omega_{n}^{2}, 3 \omega_{n}^{2}\)

Admin
Solution

From the equation of motion is

 

\(\ddot{\theta}+\omega_{n}^{2} \sin \theta=0\)

 

and the equations of the trajectories, with \(\theta=x\), are

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{x}=y \\&\dot{y}=-\omega_{n}^{2} \sin x .\end{aligned}\)

 

The equilibrium equations are obtained for zero velocity\(\dot{x}=0\) and acceleration \(\dot{y}=0\), and thus,

 

\(y=0, \quad \sin x=0\)

 

The equilibrium points are then given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}&y=0 \\&x=\pm j \pi, \quad j=0,1,2, \cdots\end{aligned}\)

 

Physically, there are only two equilibrium points since the pendulum can only trace a circle,

 

\(y=0 \text { with } x=0\) (11.78)

 

\(y=0 \text { with } x=\pi rad \text {. }\) (11.79)

 

The first equilibrium point, Equation 11.78, is the one usually considered in which the pendulum hangs at rest. The other equilibrium point, Equation 11.79, is where the pendulum is at rest in the upright position. If the pendulum is composed of a rigid massless rod with a point mass at one end, then it might be possible, if perfectly balanced, that the upright position can be a rest equilibrium point. However, it is unstable under the slightest disturbance.
Using the method of Section 11.3.1, the coefficient matrix in Equation 11.14 for the linearized state equations for the first equilibrium point \(x=0, \text { with } \sin x \simeq x\) becomes

\( \begin{Bmatrix} \dot{x}\\ \dot{y} \end{Bmatrix} \simeq \left[\begin{array}{ll}d_{11} & d_{12} \\d_{21} & d_{22}\end{array}\right] \begin{Bmatrix} x \\ y \end{Bmatrix}, \) (11.14)

 

\(\left[\begin{array}{ll}d_{11} & d_{12} \\d_{21} & d_{22}\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cc}0 & 1 \\-\omega_{n}^{2} & 0\end{array}\right]\)

 

The eigenvalues of the coefficient matrix are found by setting the following determinant to zero,

 

\(\left|\begin{array}{cc}-\lambda & 1 \\-\omega_{n}^{2} & -\lambda\end{array}\right|=0\)

 

which yields the eigenvalues \(\lambda_{1,2}=\pm i \omega_{n}\) Both eigenvalues are purely imaginary and the equilibrium point is a center and stable.
In the neighborhood of the second equilibrium point, \(x=\pi\), we expand \(\sin (x+\pi)\) for small values of \(x\) to find the approximation \(\sin (x+\pi) \simeq-x\), with the coefficient matrix

 

\(\left[\begin{array}{cc}0 & 1 \\\omega_{n}^{2} & 0\end{array}\right]\)

 

from which the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_{1,2}=\pm \omega_{n}\). Both eigenvalues are real, with one positive and the other negative, implying that the point is a saddle and unstable.
The trajectories are shown in Figure 11.21 for three energy levels. For \(E=\omega_{n}^{2}\) the trajectory is closed, indicating periodic motion. For small values of \(E\) where \(E<\omega_{n}^{2}\) the motion is approximately harmonic. As \(E\) increases such that \(\omega_{n}^{2}<E<2 \omega_{n}^{2}\), the motion ceases to be approximately harmonic but remains periodic. For \(E \geq 2 \omega_{n}^{2}\), the trajectory is open and the motion is rotary, like a propeller, as the mass rotates about the contact point. \(E=2 \omega_{n}^{2}\) is a separatrix, signifying the boundary between two types of motion. The separatrix also describes rotary motion.
