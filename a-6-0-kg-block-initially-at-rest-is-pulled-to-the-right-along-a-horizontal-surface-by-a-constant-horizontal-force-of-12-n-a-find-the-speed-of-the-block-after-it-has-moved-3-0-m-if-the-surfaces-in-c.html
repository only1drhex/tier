Guest Question.

A 6.0-kg block initially at rest is pulled to the right along a horizontal surface by a constant horizontal force of 12 N.

(A) Find the speed of the block after it has moved 3.0 m if the surfaces in contact have a coefficient of kinetic friction of 0.15. (This is , modified so that the surface is no longer frictionless.)

(B) Suppose the force F is applied at an angle Î¸ as shown in Figure 7.18b. At what angle should the force be applied to achieve the largest possible speed after the block has moved 3.0 m to the right?

Solution

Conceptualize this problem by realizing that the rough surface is going to apply a friction force opposite to the applied force. As a result, we expect the speed to be lower than that found in . The surface is rough and we are given forces and a distance, so we categorize this as a situation involving kinetic friction that must be handled by means of Equation 7.21. To analyze the problem, we have made a drawing of this situation in Figure 7.18a. We identify the block as the system, and there are four external forces interacting with the system.

\( \Delta K=-f_{k} d+\sum W_{\text {other forces }} \). (7.21a)

\( K_{f}=K_{i}-f_{k} d+\sum W_{\text {other forces }} \). (7.21b).
The normal force balances the gravitational force on the block, and neither of these vertically acting forces does work on the block because their points of application are displaced horizontally. The applied force does work just as in :

\( W=F \Delta x=(12 N )(3.0 m )=36 J \).

In this case we must use Equation 7.21a to calculate the kinetic energy change due to friction, \( \Delta K_{\text {friction }} \). Because the block is in equilibrium in the vertical direction, the normal force n counterbalances the gravitational force mg, so we have \( n=m g \). Hence, the magnitude of the friction force is

\( \Delta K=-f_{k} d+\sum W_{\text {other forces }} \). (7.21a)

\( f_{k}=\mu_{k} n=\mu_{k} m g=(0.15)(6.0 kg )\left(9.80 m / s ^{2}\right)=8.82 N \).

The change in kinetic energy of the block due to friction is

\( \Delta K_{\text {friction }}=-f_{k} d=-(8.82 N )(3.0 m )=-26.5 J \).

The final speed of the block follows from Equation 7.21b:

\( K_{f}=K_{i}-f_{k} d+\sum W_{\text {other forces }} \). (7.21b).

\( \frac{1}{2} m v_{f}^{2}=\frac{1}{2} m v_{i}{ }^{2}-f_{k} d+\sum W_{\text {other forces }} \).

\( v_{f}=\sqrt{v_{i}{ }^{2}+\frac{2}{m}\left(-f_{k} d+\sum W_{\text {other forces }}\right)} \).

\( =\sqrt{0+\frac{2}{6.0 kg }(-26.5 J +36 J )} \).

= 1.8 m/s.

To finalize this problem note that, after covering the same distance on a frictionless surface (see ), the speed of the block was 3.5 m/s.
