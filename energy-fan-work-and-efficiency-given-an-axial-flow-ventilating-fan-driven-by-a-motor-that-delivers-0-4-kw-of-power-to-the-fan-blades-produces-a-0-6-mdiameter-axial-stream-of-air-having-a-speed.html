Energy—Fan Work and Efficiency

GIVEN An axial-flow ventilating fan driven by a motor that delivers 0.4 kW of power to the fan blades produces a 0.6-mdiameter axial stream of air having a speed of 12 m/s. The flow upstream of the fan involves negligible speed.

FIND Determine how much of the work to the air actually produces useful effects, that is, fluid motion and a rise in available energy. Estimate the fluid mechanical efficiency of this fan.

Admin
Solution

We select a fixed and nondeforming control volume as is illustrated in Fig. E5.24. The application of Eq. 5.82 to the contents of this control volume leads to

 

\(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int_{ cv }\left( W + V _{ cv }\right) \rho d V+\int_{ cs }\left( W + V _{ cv }\right) \rho W \cdot \hat{ n } d A=\sum F _{\substack{\text { contents of the } \\ \text { control volume }}}\) (5.24)

 

\(\frac{p_{\text {out }}}{\rho}+\frac{V_{\text {out }}^{2}}{2}+g z_{\text {out }}=\frac{p_{\text {in }}}{\rho}+\frac{V_{\text {in }}^{2}}{2}+g z_{\text {in }}+w_{\substack{\text { shaft } \\ \text { net in }}}-\text { loss }\) (5.82)

 

 

where \(w_{\text {shaft net in }}-\text { loss }\) is the amount of work added to the air that produces a useful effect. Equation 1 leads to

 

\(\begin{array}{l}w_{\substack{\text { shaft } \\ \text { net in }}}-\operatorname{loss}=\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2}=\frac{(12 m / s )^{2}}{2\left[1( kg \cdot m ) /\left( N \cdot s ^{2}\right)\right]}\\=72.0 N \cdot m / kg\end{array}\) (2) (Ans)

 

A reasonable estimate of efficiency, \(\eta\), would be the ratio of amount of work that produces a useful effect, Eq. 2, to the amount of work delivered to the fan blades. That is

 

To calculate the efficiency, we need a value of \(W_{\text {shaft net in }}\), which is related to the power delivered to the blades, \(W_{\text {shaft net in }}\). We note that

 

\(w_{\substack{\text { shaft } \\ \text { net in }}}=\frac{W_{\substack{\text { shaft } \\ \text { net in }}}}{\dot{m}}\) (4)

 

where the mass flowrate, \(\dot{m}\), is (from Eq. 5.6)

 

\(\dot{m}=\rho A V=\rho \frac{\pi D_{2}^{2}}{4} V_{2}\) (5)

 

For fluid density, ρ, we use 1.23 \(kg / m ^{3}\) (standard air) and, thus, from Eqs. 4 and 5 we obtain

 

\(\begin{array}{l}w_{\text {shaft }}=\frac{W_{\substack{\text { shaft } \\ \text { net in }}}}{\left(\rho \pi D_{2}^{2} / 4\right) V_{2}}\\=\frac{(0.4 kW )[1000( Nm ) /( skW )]}{\left(1.23 kg / m ^{3}\right)\left[(\pi)(0.6 m )^{2} / 4\right](12 m / s )}\end{array}\)

 

or

 

\(\underset{\substack{\text { shaft in } \\ \text { net }}}{w_{\text {in }}}=95.8 N \cdot m / kg\) (6)

 

From Eqs. 2, 3, and 6 we obtain

 

\(\eta=\frac{72.0 N \cdot m / kg }{95.8 N \cdot m / kg }=0.752\) (Ans)

 

COMMENT Note that only 75% of the power that was delivered to the air resulted in useful effects, and, thus, 25% of the shaft power is lost to air friction.
