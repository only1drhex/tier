(A) Under steady-state conditions, find the unknown currents \( I_{1}, I_{2}, \text { and } I_{3} \) in the multiloop circuit shown in Figure 28.18.

(B) What is the charge on the capacitor?

Admin
Solution

First note that because the capacitor represents an open circuit, there is no current between g and b along path ghab under steady-state conditions. Therefore, when the charges associated with \( I_{1} \) reach point g, they all go toward point b through the 8.00-V battery; hence, \( I_{g b}=I_{1} \).
Labeling the currents as shown in Figure 28.18 and applying Equation 28.9 to junction c, we obtain

\( \sum I_{\text {in }}=\sum I_{\text {out }} \) (28.9).

(1) \( I_{1}+I_{2}=I_{3} \).

Equation 28.10 applied to loops defcd and cfgbc, traversed clockwise, gives

\( \sum_{\begin{array}{c} \text { closed } \\ \text { loop } \end{array}} \Delta V=0 \) (28.10).

(2) \( \text { defcd } 4.00 V -(3.00 \Omega) I_{2}-(5.00 \Omega) I_{3}=0 \).

(3) \( c f g b c(3.00 \Omega) I_{2}-(5.00 \Omega) I_{1}+8.00 V =0 \).

From Equation (1) we see that \( I_{1}=I_{3}-I_{2} \) which, when
substituted into Equation (3), gives

(4) \( (8.00 \Omega) I_{2}-(5.00 \Omega) I_{3}+8.00 V =0 \).

Subtracting Equation (4) from Equation (2), we eliminate \( I_{3} \) and find that

\( I_{2}=-\frac{4.00 V }{11.0 \Omega}=-0.364 A \).

Because our value for \( I_{2} \) is negative, we conclude that the direction of \( I_{2} \) is from c to f in the 3.00-Î© resistor. Despite this interpretation of the direction, however, we must continue to use this negative value for \( I_{2} \) in subsequent calculations because our equations were established with our original choice of direction.

\( \text { Using } I_{2}=-0.364 A \text { in Equations (3) and (1) gives } \)

\( I_{1}=1.38 A \quad I_{3}=1.02 A \).

(B) What is the charge on the capacitor?

We can apply Kirchhoffâs loop rule to loop bghab (or any other loop that contains the capacitor) to find the potential difference \( \Delta V_{\text {cap }} \) across the capacitor. We use this potential difference in the loop equation without reference to a sign convention because the charge on the capacitor depends only on the magnitude of the potential difference.
Moving clockwise around this loop, we obtain

\( -8.00 V +\Delta V_{\text {cap }}-3.00 V =0 \).

\( \Delta V_{\text {cap }}=11.0 V \).

\(\text { Because } Q=C \Delta V_{\text {cap }} \text { (see Eq. 26.1), the charge on the } \) capacitor is

\( C \equiv \frac{Q}{\Delta V} \) (26.1)

\( Q=(6.00 \mu F )(11.0 V )=66.0 \mu C \).
