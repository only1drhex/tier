Oil Film Flowing Down a Vertical Wall by Gravity

Consider steady, incompressible, parallel, laminar flow of a film of oil falling slowly down an infinite vertical wall (Fig. 9‚Äì68). The oil film thickness is h, and gravity acts in the negative z-direction (downward in Fig. 9‚Äì68). There is no applied (forced) pressure driving the flow‚Äîthe oil falls by gravity alone. Calculate the velocity and pressure fields in the oil film and sketch the normalized velocity profile. You may neglect changes in the hydrostatic pressure of the surrounding air.

Solution

SOLUTION For a given geometry and set of boundary conditions, we are to calculate the velocity and pressure fields and plot the velocity profile.

Assumptions 1 The wall is infinite in the yz-plane (y is into the page for a righthanded coordinate system). 2 The flow is steady (all partial derivatives with respect to time are zero). 3 The flow is parallel (the x-component of velocity, u, is zero everywhere). 4 The fluid is incompressible and Newtonian with constant properties, and the flow is laminar. 5 Pressure \(P=P_{ atm }=\) constant at the free surface. In other words, there is no applied pressure gradient pushing the flow; the flow establishes itself due to a balance between gravitational forces and viscous forces. In addition, since there is no gravity force in the horizontal direction, \(P=P_{ atm }=\) everywhere. 6 The velocity field is purely two-dimensional, which implies that velocity component ùúà = 0 and all partial derivatives with respect to y are zero. 7 Gravity acts in the negative z-direction. We express this mathematically as \(\vec{g}=-g \vec{k}, \text { or } g_{x}=g_{y}\) \(=0 \text { and } g_{z}=-g\).

Analysis We obtain the velocity and pressure fields by following the step-by-step procedure for differential fluid flow solutions (Fig. 9‚Äì52).

Step 1 Set up the problem and the geometry. See Fig. 9‚Äì68.

Step 2 List assumptions and boundary conditions. We have listed seven assumptions. The boundary conditions are: (1) There is no slip at the wall; at x = 0, u = ùúê = w = 0. (2) At the free surface (x = h), there is negligible shear (Eq. 9‚Äì68), which for a vertical free surface in this coordinate system means ‚àÇw/‚àÇx = 0 at x = h.

Step 3 Write out and simplify the differential equations. We start with the incompressible continuity equation in Cartesian coordinates,

 

\(\underbrace{\frac{\partial \mu}{\partial x}}_{\text {assumption } 3}+\underbrace{\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}}_{\text {assumption } 6}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial z}=0 \quad \rightarrow \quad \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}=0\) (1)

 

Equation 1 tells us that w is not a function of z; i.e., it doesn‚Äôt matter where we place our origin‚Äîthe flow is the same at any z-location. In other words, the flow is fully developed. Since w is not a function of time (assumption 2), z (Eq. 1), or y (assumption 6), we conclude that w is at most a function of x,

 

Result of continuity: w = w(x) only (2)

 

We now simplify each component of the Navier‚ÄìStokes equation as far as possible. Since u = ùúê = 0 everywhere, and gravity does not act in the x- or y-directions, the x- and y-momentum equations are satisfied exactly (in fact all terms are zero in both equations). The z-momentum equation reduces to

 

\(\rho(\underbrace{\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}}_{\text {assumption 2 }}+\underbrace{u \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}}_{\text {assumption 3 }}+\underbrace{v \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}}_{\text {assumption 6 }}+\underbrace{w \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}}_{\text {continuity }})\)

\(=\underbrace{-\underbrace{\partial p}_{\partial z}}_{\text {assumption 5 }}+\underbrace{\rho g_{z}}_{-\rho g}\) \(+\mu(\frac{\partial^{2} w}{\partial x^{2}}+\underbrace{\frac{\partial^{2} w}{\partial y^{2}}}_{\text {assumption } 6}+\underbrace{\frac{\partial^{2} w}{\partial z^{2}}}_{\text {continuity }}) \rightarrow \frac{d^{2} w}{d x^{2}}=\frac{\rho g}{\mu}\) (3)

 

The material acceleration (left side of Eq. 3) is zero, implying that fluid particles are not accelerating in this flow field, neither by local nor advective acceleration. Since the advective acceleration terms make the Navier‚ÄìStokes equation nonlinear, this greatly simplifies the problem. We have changed from a partial derivative (‚àÇ/‚àÇx) to a total derivative (d/dx) in Eq. 3 as a direct result of Eq. 2, reducing the partial differential equation (PDE) to an ordinary differential equation (ODE). ODEs are of course much easier than PDEs to solve (Fig. 9‚Äì69).

 

Step 4 Solve the differential equations. The continuity and x- and y-momentum equations have already been ‚Äúsolved.‚Äù Equation 3 (z-momentum) is integrated twice to get

 

\(w=\frac{\rho g}{2 \mu} x^{2}+C_{1} x+C_{2}\) (4)

 

Step 5 Apply boundary conditions. We apply boundary conditions (1) and (2)
from step 2 to obtain constants \(C_{1} \text { and } C_{2}\),

 

Boundary condition (1): \(w=0+0+C_{2}=0 \quad C_{2}=0\)

 

and

 

Boundary condition (2): \(\left.\frac{d w}{d x}\right)_{x=h}=\frac{\rho g}{\mu} h+C_{1}=0 \rightarrow C_{1}=-\frac{\rho g h}{\mu}\)

 

Finally, Eq. 4 becomes

 

Velocity field: \(w=\frac{\rho g}{2 \mu} x^{2}-\frac{\rho g}{\mu} h x=\frac{\rho g x}{2 \mu}(x-2 h)\) (5)

 

Since x < h in the film, w is negative everywhere, as expected (flow is downward). The pressure field is trivial; namely, \(P=P_{ atm } \text { everywhere. }\)

 

Step 6 Verify the results. You can verify that all the differential equations and boundary conditions are satisfied.

 

We normalize Eq. 5 by inspection: we let \(x^{*}=x / h \text { and } w^{*}=w \mu /\left(\rho g h^{2}\right)\). Equation 5 becomes

 

Normalized velocity profile: \(w^{*}=\frac{x^{*}}{2}\left(x^{*}-2\right)\) (6)

 

We plot the normalized velocity field in Fig. 9‚Äì70.

 

Discussion The velocity profile has a large slope near the wall due to the no-slip condition there (w = 0 at x = 0), but zero slope at the free surface, where the boundary condition is zero shear stress (‚àÇw/‚àÇx = 0 at x = h). We could have introduced a factor of -2 in the definition of w* so that w* would equal 1 instead of –¬Ω at the free surface.
