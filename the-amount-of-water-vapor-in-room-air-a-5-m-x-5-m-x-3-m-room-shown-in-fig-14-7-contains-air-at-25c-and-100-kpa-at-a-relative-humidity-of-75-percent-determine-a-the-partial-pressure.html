Guest Question.

The Amount of Water Vapor in Room Air
A 5-m × 5-m × 3-m room shown in Fig. 14-7 contains air at 25°C and 100 kPa at a relative humidity of 75 percent. Determine (a) the partial pressure of dry air, (b) the specific humidity, (c) the enthalpy per unit mass of the dry air, and (d) the masses of the dry air and water vapor in the room.

Admin
Solution

The relative humidity of air in a room is given. The dry air pressure, specific humidity, enthalpy, and the masses of dry air and water vapor in the room are to be determined.

Assumptions The dry air and the water vapor in the room are ideal gases.

Properties The constant-pressure specific heat of air at room temperature is \(c_p\)= 1.005 kJ/kg-K (). For water at 25°C, we have \(T_{sat}\) = 3.1698 kPa and \(h_g\) = 2546.5 kJ/kg ().

Analysis (a) The partial pressure of dry air can be determined from Eq. 14-2:

 

\(P_{a}=P-P_{v}\)

 

 

where

\(P_{v}=\phi P_{g}=\phi P_{\mathrm{sat} @{25^{\circ}} \mathrm{C}}=(0.75)(3.1698 \mathrm{kPa})=2.38 \mathrm{kPa}\)

 

Thus,

\(P_{a}=(100-2.38) \mathrm{kPa}=97.62 \mathrm{kPa}\)

 

 

(b) The specific humidity of air is determined from Eq. 14-8:

\(\omega=\frac{0.622 P_{v}}{P-P_{v}}=\frac{(0.622)(2.38 \mathrm{kPa})}{(100-2.38) \mathrm{kPa}}=0.0152 \mathrm{~kg} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} / \mathrm{kg} \text { dry air }\)

 

(c) The enthalpy of air per unit mass of dry air is determined from Eq. 14-12:

 

\(h =h_{a}+\omega h_{v} \cong c_{p} T+\omega h_{g} \)

 

\(=\left(1.005 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)\left(25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)+(0.0152)(2546.5 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}) \)

 

\(=63.8 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \text { dry air }\)

 

The enthalpy of water vapor (2546.5 kJ/kg) could also be determined from the approximation given by Eq. 14-4:

 

\(h_{g @ 25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}} \cong 2500.9+1.82(25)=2546.4 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\)

 

which is almost identical to the value obtained from .

 

(d) Both the dry air and the water vapor fill the entire room completely. Therefore, the volume of each gas is equal to the volume of the room:

\(V_{a}=V_{v}=V_{\text {room }}=(5 \mathrm{~m})(5 \mathrm{~m})(3 \mathrm{~m})=75 \mathrm{~m}^{3}\)

 

The masses of the dry air and the water vapor are determined from the ideal-gas relation applied to each gas separately:

\(m_{a}=\frac{P_{a} V_{a}}{R_{a} T}=\frac{(97.62 \mathrm{kPa})\left(75 \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)}{\left(0.287 \mathrm{kPa} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\right)(298 \mathrm{~K})}=85.61 \mathrm{~kg} \)

 

\(m_{v}=\frac{P_{v} V_{v}}{R_{v} T}=\frac{(2.38 \mathrm{kPa})\left(75 \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)}{\left(0.4615 \mathrm{kPa} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\right)(298 \mathrm{~K})}=1.30 \mathrm{~kg}\)

 

The mass of the water vapor in the air could also be determined from Eq. 14-6:

\(m_{v}=\omega m_{a}=(0.0152)(85.61 \mathrm{~kg})=1.30 \mathrm{~kg}\)
