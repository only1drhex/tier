AERODYNAMIC DRAG AND MOMENT ON A CHIMNEY

A cylindrical chimney 1 m in diameter and 25 m tall is exposed to a uniform 50 km/hr wind at standard atmospheric conditions. End effects and gusts may be neglected. Estimate the bending moment at the base of the chimney due to wind forces.

Admin
Solution

Given: Cylindrical chimney, \(D=1 \mathrm{~m}, L=25 \mathrm{~m}\), in uniform flow with

\(V=50 \mathrm{~km} / \mathrm{hr} \quad p=101 \mathrm{kPa}(\mathrm{abs}) \quad T=15^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\)

Neglect end effects.
Find: Bending moment at bottom of chimney.
Solution:
The drag coefficient is given by \(C_{D}=F_{D} / \frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2} A\), and thus \(F_{D}=C_{D} A \frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}\). Since the force per unit length is uniform over the entire length, the resultant force, \(F_{D}\), will act at the midpoint of the chimney. Hence the moment about the chimney base is

\(\begin{aligned}M_{0} &=F_{D} \frac{L}{2}=C_{D} A \frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2} \frac{L}{2}=C_{D} A \frac{L}{4} \rho V^{2} \\V &=50 \frac{\mathrm{km}}{\mathrm{hr}} \times 10^{3} \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{km}} \times \frac{\mathrm{hr}}{3600 \mathrm{~s}}=13.9 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\end{aligned}\)

For air at standard conditions, \(\rho=1.23 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\), and \(\mu=1.79 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{~kg} /(\mathrm{m} \cdot \mathrm{s})\). Thus

\(R e=\frac{\rho V D}{\mu}=1.23 \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^{3}} \times 13.9 \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}} \times 1 \mathrm{~m} \times \frac{\mathrm{m} \cdot \mathrm{s}}{1.79 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{~kg}}=9.55 \times 10^{5}\)

 

From Fig. 9.13, \(C_{D} \approx 0.35 .\) For a cylinder, \(A=D L\), so

\(\begin{aligned}M_{0} &=C_{D} A \frac{L}{4} \rho V^{2}=C_{D} D L \frac{L}{4} \rho V^{2}=C_{D} D \frac{L^{2}}{4} \rho V^{2} \\&=\frac{1}{4} \times 0.35 \times 1 \mathrm{~m} \times(25)^{2} \mathrm{~m}^{2} \times 1.23 \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^{3}} \times(13.9)^{2} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{2}}{\mathrm{~s}^{2}} \times \frac{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{2}}{\mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}} \\M_{0} &=13.0 \mathrm{kN} \cdot \mathrm{m}\quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad M_{0}}\end{aligned}\)

 

This problem illustrates application of drag-coefficient data to calculate the force and moment on a structure. We modeled the wind as a uniform flow; more realistically, the lower atmosphere is often modeled as a huge turbulent boundary layer, with a power-law velocity profile, \(u âˆ¼ y^{1/n}\) (y is the elevation).
