Guest Question.

Show that free electrons can neither emit nor absorb photons.

Admin
Solution

If the electron is free both before and after it interacts with the photon, its initial and final wave functions are given by plane waves: \(\psi_{i}(\vec{r})=(2 \pi)^{-3 / 2} e^{i \vec{k}_{i} \cdot \vec{r}}\) and \(\psi_{f}(\vec{r})=(2 \pi)^{-3 / 2} e^{i \vec{k}_{f} \cdot \vec{r}}\). Let us assume, for argument sake, that a free electron can absorb and emit a photon; the corresponding absorption and emission transition rates would be given as follows (see (10.95)

\(\Gamma_{i \rightarrow f}^{e m i}=\frac{4 \pi^{2} e^{2}}{m_{e}^{2} \omega V}\left|\left\langle\psi_{f}\left|e^{-i \vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}} \vec{\varepsilon} \cdot \vec{P}\right| \psi_{i}\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}+\hbar \omega\right),\) (10.95)

and (10.96)):

\(\Gamma_{i \rightarrow f}^{a b s}=\frac{4 \pi^{2} e^{2}}{m_{e}^{2} \omega V}\left|\left\langle\psi_{f}\left|e^{i \vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}} \vec{\varepsilon} \cdot \vec{P}\right| \psi_{i}\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}-\hbar \omega\right)\). (10.96)

 

\(\Gamma_{i \rightarrow f}^{a b s}=\frac{4 \pi^{2} e^{2}}{m_{e}^{2} \omega V}\left|\left(\vec{\varepsilon} \cdot \vec{k}_{i}\right)\left\langle\psi_{f}\left|e^{i \vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}}\right| \psi_{i}\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}-\hbar \omega\right),\) (10.242)

 

\(\Gamma_{i \rightarrow f}^{e m i}=\frac{4 \pi^{2} e^{2}}{m_{e}^{2} \omega V}\left|\left(\vec{\varepsilon} \cdot \vec{k}_{i}\right)\left\langle\psi_{f}\left|e^{-i \vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}}\right| \psi_{i}\right\rangle\right|^{2} \delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}+\hbar \omega\right),\) (10.243)

where we have used \(\vec{P} \psi_{i}(\vec{r})=\vec{k}_{i} \psi_{i}(\vec{r})\). Since

\(\left\langle\psi_{f}\left|e^{\pm i \vec{k} \cdot \vec{r}}\right| \psi_{i}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{2}} \int d^{3} r e^{i\left(\vec{k}_{i}-\vec{k}_{f} \pm \vec{k}\right) \cdot \vec{r}}=\delta\left(\vec{k}_{i}-\vec{k}_{f} \pm \vec{k}\right)\), (10.244)

the delta functions \(\delta\left(\vec{k}_{i}-\vec{k}_{f} \pm \vec{k}\right)\) give the conservation laws of the linear momentum for both the absorption and emission processes.

Let us show first that a free electron cannot absorb a photon. For this, we are going to show that the momentum conservation condition \(\delta\left(\vec{k}_{i}-\vec{k}_{f}+\vec{k}\right)\) is incompatible with the energy conservation condition \(\delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}-\hbar \omega\right)\). Combining equations (10.242) and (10.244), we see that the absorption rate is proportional to the product of two delta functions: \(\Gamma_{i \rightarrow f}^{a b s} \sim\delta\left(\vec{k}_{i}-\vec{k}_{f}+\vec{k}\right) \delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}-\hbar \omega\right)\), one pertaining to the conservation of momentum

\(\delta\left(\vec{k}_{i}-\vec{k}_{f}+\vec{k}\right) \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \vec{p}_{i}-\vec{p}_{f}+\vec{p}_{\text {photon }}=0\), (10.245)

the other dealing with the conservation of energy

\(\delta\left(E_{f}-E_{i}-\hbar \omega\right) \quad \Longrightarrow \quad E_{f}-E_{i}-c p_{\text {photon }}=0\), (10.246)

where \(\vec{p}_{i}=\hbar \vec{k}_{i}\) and \(E_{i}\) are the initial momentum and energy of the electron, \(\vec{p}_{f}=\hbar \vec{k}_{f}\) and \(E_{f}\) are its final momentum and energy, and \(\vec{p}_{\text {photon }}=\hbar \vec{k}\) and \(c p_{\text {photon }}\) are the linear momentum and energy of the absorbed photon. We are now ready to show that the condition (10.245) is incompatible with (10.246). If we work within the rest frame of the initial electron, we have \(\vec{p}_{i}=0\). Thus, on the one hand, (10.245) leads to \(\vec{p}_{\text {photon }}=\vec{p}_{f}\) and, on the other hand, (10.246) leads to \(E_{f}=c p_{p h o t o n}\) or \(p_{f}^{2} / 2 m_{e}=c p_{\text {photon }}\). Indeed, conditions (10.245) and (10.246) are contradictory since, inserting \(\vec{p}_{i}=0\) and \(\vec{p}_{\text {photon }}=\vec{p}_{f}\) into (10.246), we end up with \(p_{f}^{2} / 2 m_{e}=c p_{f}\) or \(p_{f}=2 m_{e} c\). This suggests either that \(v_{f}=0\) and this is meaningless since, as \(\vec{p}_{\text {photon }}=\vec{p} f\), the speed of the photon would also be zero; or that \(v_{f}=2 c\) and this is impossible. So both results are impossible. In summary, having started with the assumption that a free electron can absorb a photon (10.242), we have ended up with a momentum conservation law and an energy conservation law that are contradictory. Thus, a free electron cannot absorb a photon.

Following the same procedure, we can also show that the assumption of a free electron emitting a photon leads to a momentum conservation law and an energy conservation law that are incompatible; thus, a free electron cannot emit a photon.
