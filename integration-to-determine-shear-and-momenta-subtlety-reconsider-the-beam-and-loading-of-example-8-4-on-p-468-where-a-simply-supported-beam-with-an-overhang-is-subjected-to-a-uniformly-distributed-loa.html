Guest Question.

Integration to Determine Shear and Momenta Subtlety

Reconsider the beam and loading of on p. 468, where a simply supported beam with an overhang is subjected to a uniformly distributed load. Use integration to determine the shear and moment as functions of position.

Admin
Solution

Road Map This problem was previously solved using the equilibrium approach in on p. 468. Here we develop the solution using integration, and we point out a subtlety that arises when a support is between the ends of the beam.

In Fig.1 the distributed loading is uniform throughout the beam with value \(w = 100 lb/ft\). We will determine the shear V and moment M, using Eqs. (\(V =V_P – \int\limits_{x_P}^{x}{w dx}\)) and (\(M = M_P + \int\limits_{x_P}^{x}{V dx}\)) with point P taken to be at end A of the beam. The results for V and M will be valid only for \(0 \leq x \leq 4 ft\), as we know that the shear undergoes a discontinuity (i.e., jump in value) at the roller at B, and clearly this discontinuity is not contained in the results from Eq. (\(V =V_P – \int\limits_{x_P}^{x}{w dx}\) ). To determine V and M in the remainder of the beam, we will reapply Eqs. (\(V =V_P – \int\limits_{x_P}^{x}{w dx}\)) and (\(M = M_P + \int\limits_{x_P}^{x}{V dx}\)) with point P taken to be at end C of the beam, and the results will be valid for \(4 ft \leq x \leq 6 ft\).

Modeling The FBD for the entire beam was shown in Fig. 2 of on p. 468, and the support reactions were determined to be \(A_y = 150 lb\) and \(B_y = 450 lb\). In this solution, we will need the shear and moment at points A and C, and to this end we draw the FBDs shown in Fig. 2.

Governing Equations & Computation Using the FBD of end A of the beam shown in Fig. 2(a), we write the equilibrium equations.

\(\sum{F_y}=0\): \(150 lb – V_A = 0 \Rightarrow V_A = 150 lb\), (1)

\(\sum{M_A}=0\): \(M_A = 0 \Rightarrow M_A = 0\). (2)

Similarly, using the FBD of end C of the beam shown in Fig. 2(b), we write the equilibrium equations.

\(\sum{F_y}=0\): \(V_C = 0 \Rightarrow V_C = 0\), (3)

\(\sum{M_C}=0\): \(-M_C = 0 \Rightarrow M_C = 0\). (4)

To obtain the shear and moment for the left-hand portion of the beam, we take point P to be at end A. Noting that \(V_A = 150 lb\) and \(w = 100 lb/ft\), Eq. (\(V =V_P – \int\limits_{x_P}^{x}{w dx}\)) provides

\(V = V_A – \int\limits_{x_A=0}^{x}{(100 \frac{lb}{ft})}dx \Rightarrow V = 150 lb -(100 \frac{lb}{ft})x\). (5)

Noting that \(M_A = 0\) and V is given in Eq. (5), Eq. (\(M = M_P + \int\limits_{x_P}^{x}{V dx}\)) provides

\(M = M_A + \int\limits_{x_A=0}^{x}{[150 lb -(100 \frac{lb}{ft})x]}dx \Rightarrow M = (150 lb)x – (50 \frac{lb}{ft})x^2\). (6)

The shear and moment determined in Eqs. (5) and (6) are valid for \(0 \leq x \leq 4 ft\).

To obtain the shear and moment for the right-hand portion of the beam, we take point P to be at end C. Noting that \(V_C = 0\) and \(w = 100 lb/ft\), Eq. (\(V =V_P – \int\limits_{x_P}^{x}{w dx}\)) provides*

\(V = V_C – \int\limits_{x_C = 6 ft}^{x}{(100 \frac{lb}{ft}) dx} \Rightarrow V = 600 lb – (100 \frac{lb}{ft})x\). (7)

Noting that \(M_C = 0\) and V is given in Eq. (7), Eq. (8.11) provides

\(M = M_C + \int\limits_{x_C = 6 ft}^{x}{[600 lb – (100 \frac{lb}{ft})x] dx} \) (8)

\( \Rightarrow M = -1800 ft\cdot lb + (600 lb)x – (50 \frac{lb}{ft}) x^2\). (9)

The shear and moment determined in Eqs. (7) and (9) are valid for \(4 ft \leq x \leq 6 ft\).

Discussion & Verification

• The results for shear and moment agree with those obtained in . Of course, the shear and moment diagrams will also be the same, and these are shown again in Fig. 3 for purposes of the following discussion.

• Because the distributed force is uniform over the full length of the beam, the shear is linear, as expected. However, Eqs. (5) and (7) are different linear functions, although they share the same slope. Because \(dV/dx = -w\) (i.e., the slope of the shear is equal to the negative of the distributed load), the slopes of Eqs. (5) and (7) are both -100 lb/ft.

• Since the shear is linear, the moment is quadratic. However, Eqs. (6) and (9) are different quadratic functions.

• Observe in the moment diagram in Fig. 3 that the moment displays a local maximum at a position between 1 and 2 ft. To determine exactly where this occurs, and the value of the moment at that location, we use the expression dM/dx = V as follows. At this local maximum, dM/dx = 0, which means that V = 0 at this location. Thus, we set Eq. (5) equal to zero and solve for x to obtain 1.5 ft. We then substitute x = 1.5 ft into Eq. (6) to obtain the moment 112.5 ft\(\cdot\)lb.

* You should carry out the integrations in Eqs. (7) and (8) and carefully evaluate the limits of integration, to verify the results that are reported. Incorrect evaluation of such limits of integration is a common source of error.
