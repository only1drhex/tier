Guest Question.

The fluid of is characterized by a thermal conductivity, density, specific heat, and dynamic viscosity of 0.705 W/m. K, 1146 kg/m³ , 3587 J/kg . K, and \(962 × 10^{-6}N .s/m^{2}\) , respectively. An experiment is conducted in which a long aluminum rod of diameter D = 20 mm and initial temperature \(T_{i} \)= 32°C is suddenly immersed horizon-tally into a large bath of the fluid at a temperature of \(T_{∞} \) = 22°C. At t = 65 s, the mea-sured temperature of the rod is\(T_{f}\) = 23°C. Determine the thermal expansion coefficient of the fluid β.

Admin
Solution

Known: Initial and final temperatures of aluminum rod of known diameter. Temperature and properties of the fluid.

Find: Thermal expansion coefficient of the fluid.

Assumptions:
1. Constant properties.
2. Spatially uniform rod temperature (applicability of the lumped capacitance approxi-mation).

Properties: , aluminum \( (\overset{-}{T} =300 K): ρ_{s} = 2702 kg/m^{3}, c_{p,s} = 903 J/kg . K,k_{s} = 237 W/m . K\)

Analysis: Because the temperature difference between the rod and the fluid decreases with time, we expect the convection heat transfer coefficient to decrease as cooling pro-ceeds. Since \(\bar{h} \) depends on buoyancy forces established by temperature differences, the analysis of Section 5.3.3 may be applied. From Equations 5.28 and 5.26

\( h= C(T-T_\infty )^n \) (5.26)

\( \frac{\theta }{\theta _{i}} = \left[ \frac{n C A_{s,c}\theta ^{n}_{i}}{\rho Vc}t +1 \right]^{-1/n} \) (5.28)

\(\frac{\theta }{\theta _{i}} = \left[ \frac{n C_{1} A_{s,c}\theta ^{n}_{i}}{\rho _{s}Vc_{p,s}}t +1 \right]^{-1/n}\) (1)

where \(\bar{h} = C_{1}(T_{s} – T_{∞})^{n} \) and \(θ = T_{s} – T_{∞}\). From Equation 9.33, \( \overset{-}{Nu_{D}} = C Ra^{n}_{D}\) . Substituting definitions of the Nusselt and Rayleigh numbers into Equation 9.33 yields

\(\bar{h} = C \frac{k_{l}}{D} \left[\frac{g\beta D^{3}}{\nu _{l} \alpha _{l}} \right]^{n}(T_{s} – T_{\infty })^{n}\) (2)

From a comparison of Equation 2 with the expression \(\bar{h} = C_{1}(T_{s} – T_{∞})^{n} \) , it is evident that

\(C_{1}= C \frac{k_{l}}{D} \left[\frac{g\beta D^{3}}{\nu _{l} \alpha _{l}} \right]^{n}\) (3)

Defining a final excess temperature as \(θ_{f} = T_{s,f} – T_{∞}\) at t = \(t_{f}\) and noting that \( \nu _{l} =\mu _{l} /ρ_{l},\alpha _{l} = k_{l} /ρ_{l} c_{p,l}\), and \(A_{s,c} /V = 4/D\), Equation 3 may be substituted into Equation 1, yielding

\(\beta = \frac{k_{l} \mu _{l}}{c_{p,l} \rho^{2} _{l} g D^{3}} \left\{\frac{\rho _{s} c_{p,s} D^{2}}{4k_{l} Cnt_{f} \theta ^{n}_{i}} \left[\left(\frac{\theta _{f}}{\theta _{i}} \right)^{-n}-1 \right] \right\} ^{1/n} \) (4)

For now we will assume the Rayleigh number falls in the range \(10^{4}≤ Ra_{D} ≤ 10^{7}\), for which C = 0.480 and n = 0.250 from Table 9.1. Hence the thermal expansion coefficient is

\(10^{-2}–10^{2}\) 1.02 0.148

\( \beta = \frac{0.705 W/m . K × 962 × 10^{-6} N .s/m^{2}}{3587 J/kg. K × (1146 kg/m^{3})^{2} × 9.81 m/s^{2} × (20 × 10^{-3} m)^{3}} \times \left\{\frac{2702 kg/m^{3} × 903 J/kg . K × (20 × 10^{-3} m)^{2}}{4 × 0.705 W/m . K × 0.480 × 0.25 × 65 s × (10 K)^{0.25}} \left[\left(\frac{1.0}{10} \right)^{-0.25}-1 \right] \right\} ^{1/0.25} = 261 × 10^{-6}K^{-1} \)

Using this value of the thermal expansion coefficient, the Rayleigh number based on the initial temperature difference is

\( Ra_{D,max} = \frac{g\beta \theta _{i}D^{3}}{\nu _{l} \alpha _{l}} = \frac{g \beta \rho ^{2} c_{p,l}\theta _{i}D^{3} }{\mu _{l} k_{l}} \)

 

\( = \frac{9.81 m/s^{2} × 261× 10^{-6} K^{-1} × (1145 kg/m^{3})^{2} × 3587 J/kg . K × 10 K × (20 × 10^{-3}m)^{3}}{962 × 10^{-6} N . s/m^{2} × 0.705 W/m . K} = 1.42 × 10^{6}\)

Since \(θ_{f} = θ_{i}/10\), the minimum value of the Rayleigh number during the cooling process is \(Ra_{D,min} = Ra_{D,max}/10 = 1.42 × 10^{5}\) . Therefore, \(10^{4} <Ra_{D,min} < Ra_{D,max} < 10^{7}\), and the values of C and n selected from Table 9.1 are appropriate. Hence the foregoing value of the thermal expansion coefficient is correct

Comments:
1. The manner in which the rod temperature decreases during the cooling process may be determined from Equation 1 with \(C_{1}\) obtained from Equation 3. The rod temperature history is shown in the figure.

2. Since the temperature difference between the rod and the fluid decreases with time, the Rayleigh number also decreases as cooling proceeds. This leads to a gradual reduction in the convection heat transfer coefficient during cooling, as can be determined by solving Equation 2 once θ(t) is known.

3. The maximum value of the convection heat transfer coefficient is \(\bar{h}_{max} = 584 W/m^{2}. K.\) This corresponds to a maximum Biot number of \(Bi_{max} = \bar{h}_{max} (D/2)/k_{s} = 584 W/m^{2}. K(20 × 10^{-3} m/2)/237 W/m.K = 0.025\) when the criterion of Equation 5.10\(Bi =\frac{h L_{c}}{k} \lt 0.1\) is applied in a conservative fashion. Since \(Bi_{max} < 0.1\), we conclude that the lumped capacitance approximation is valid.
4. Because the rod temperature continually decreases, the buoyancy forces within the fluid decrease with time. Hence fluid velocities continually evolve as the temperature difference between the rod and the fluid slowly decays. Equation 9.33\(\overset{-}{Nu}_{D}= \frac{\bar{h} D }{k} = C Ra^{n}_{D}\) is strictly applicable only for steady-state conditions. In applying the correlation here, we have implicitly assumed that the instantaneous heat transfer rate from the rod is the same as the steady-state heat transfer rate if the same temperature difference exists between the rod and the fluid. This assumption often yields predictions of acceptable accuracy and is referred to as the quasi-steady approximation.
