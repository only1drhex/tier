HEIGHT OF A BASEBALL FROM ENERGY CONSERVATION

You throw a 0.145-kg baseball straight up, giving it an initial velocity of magnitude 20.0 m/s. Find how high it goes, ignoring air resistance.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

After the ball leaves your hand, only gravity does work on it. Hence mechanical energy is conserved, and we can use Eq. (7.4). We take point 1 to be where the ball leaves your hand and point 2 to be where it reaches its maximum height.
As in Fig. 7.2, we take the positive y-direction to be upward. The ball’s speed at point 1 is \(v_1\) = 20.0 m/s; at its maximum height it is instantaneously at rest, so \(v_2\) = 0. We take the origin at point 1, so \(y_1\) = 0 (Fig. 7.4). Our target variable, the distance the ball moves vertically between the two points, is the displacement \(y_{2}-y_{1}=y_{2}-0=y_{2}\).

EXECUTE:

We have \(y_{1}=0, \quad U_{\text {grav }, 1}=m g y_{1}=0, \text { and } K_{2}=\) \(\frac{1}{2} m v_{2}^{2}=0\). Then Eq. (7.4), \(K_{1}+U_{\text {grav, } 1}=K_{2}+U_{\text {grav, } 2}\), becomes

\(K_{1}=U_{\text {grav }, 2}\)

As the energy bar graphs in Fig. 7.4 show, this equation says that the kinetic energy of the ball at point 1 is completely converted to gravitational potential energy at point 2. We substitute \(K_{1}=\frac{1}{2} m v_{1}^{2}\) and \(U_{\text {grav }, 2}=m g y_{2}\) and solve for \(y_2\):

\(\frac{1}{2} m v_{1}^{2}=m g y_{2}\)

 

\(y_{2}=\frac{v_{1}^{2}}{2 g}=\frac{(20.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})^{2}}{2\left(9.80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)}=20.4 \mathrm{~m}\)

 

EVALUATE: As a check, use the given value of \(v_1\) and our result for \(y_2\) to calculate the kinetic energy at point 1 and the gravitational potential energy at point 2. You should find that these are equal:

\(K_{1}=\frac{1}{2} m v_{1}^{2}=29.0 \mathrm{~J} \text { and } U_{\mathrm{grav}, 2}=m g y_{2}=29.0 \mathrm{~J}\). . Note that we could have found the result \(y_{2}=v_{1}^{2} / 2 g\) by using Eq. (2.13) (\(v_{x}^{2}=v_{0 x}^{2}+2 a_{x}\left(x-x_{0}\right)\)) in the form \(v_{2 y}^{2}=v_{1 y}^{2}-2 g\left(y_{2}-y_{1}\right)\).

What if we put the origin somewhere else—for example, 5.0 m below point 1, so that \(y_1\) = 5.0 m? Then the total mechanical energy at point 1 is part kinetic and part potential; at point 2 it’s still purely potential because \(v_2\)= 0. You’ll find that this choice of origin yields \(y_2\)= 25.4 m, but again \(y_2\)– \(y_1\)= 20.4 m. In problems like this, you are free to choose the height at which \(U_{grav}\) = 0. The physics doesn’t depend on your choice.
