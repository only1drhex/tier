Internal Energy, Enthalpy, and Density for an Ideal Gas

GIVEN Air flows steadily between two sections in a long straight portion of 4-in.-diameter pipe as is indicated in Fig. E11.1. The uniformly distributed temperature and pressure at each section are \(T_{1}=540^{\circ} R , p_{1}=100 \text { psia, and } T_{2}=453{ }^{\circ} R , p_{2}=18.4 \text { psia }\).

FIND Calculate the (a) change in internal energy between sections (1) and (2), (b) change in enthalpy between sections (1) and (2), and (c) change in density between sections (1) and (2).

Admin
Solution

(a) Assuming air behaves as an ideal gas, we can use Eq. 11.5 to evaluate the change in internal energy between sections (1) and (2). Thus

 

\(\check{u}_{2}-\check{u}_{1}=c_{v}\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)\) (11.5)

 

\(\check{u}_{2}-\check{u}_{1}=c_{v}\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)\) (1)

 

From Eq. 11.15 we have

 

\(c_{v}=\frac{R}{k-1}\) (11.15)

 

\(c_{v}=\frac{R}{k-1}\) (2)

 

and from Table 1.7, \(R=1716( ft \cdot lb ) /\left(\text { slug } \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right) \text { and } k=1.4\). Throughout this book, we use the nominal values of k for common gases listed in Tables 1.7 and 1.8 and consider these values as being representative. Since English Engineering System units are used more often than British Gravitational System units in compressible flow discussions, we use

1 slug = 32 .174 lbm

to get

 

\(R=\frac{1716( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( slug \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right)}{32.174( lbm / slug )}=53.3( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right)\)

 

From Eq. 2 we obtain

 

\(\begin{aligned}c_{v} &=\frac{53.3}{(1.4-1)}( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right) \\&=133( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right)\end{aligned}\) (3)

 

Combining Eqs. 1 and 3 yields

 

\(\begin{aligned}\check{u}_{2}-\check{u}_{1}=& c_{v}\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)=133( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right) \\& \times\left(453^{\circ} R -540^{\circ} R \right) \\=&-11,600 ft \cdot lb / lbm\end{aligned}\)

 

Or, if Btu are wanted as energy units, we note that

778 ft · lb = 1 Btu

and so

 

\(\check{u}_{2}-\check{u}_{1}=-\frac{11,600( ft \cdot lb ) / lbm }{778( ft \cdot lb ) / Btu }=-14.9 Btu / lbm\)

 

(b) For enthalpy change we use Eq. 11.9. Thus

 

\(\check{h}_{2}-\check{h}_{1}=c_{p}\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)\) (11.9)

 

\(\check{h}_{2}-\check{h}_{1}=c_{p}\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)\) (4)

 

where since \(k=c_{p} / c_{v}\) we obtain

 

\(\begin{aligned}c_{p} &=k c_{v}=(1.4)\left[133( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right)\right] \\&=186( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right)\end{aligned}\) (5)

 

From Eqs. 4 and 5 we obtain

 

\(\begin{aligned}\breve{h}_{2}-\breve{h}_{1}=& c_{p}\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)=186( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right) \\& \times\left(453{ }^{\circ} R -540^{\circ} R \right) \\=&-16,200 ft \cdot lb / lbm\end{aligned}\)

 

(c) For density change we use the ideal gas equation of state (Eq. 11.1) to get

 

\(\rho=\frac{p}{R T}\) (11.1)

 

\(\rho_{2}-\rho_{1}=\frac{p_{2}}{R T_{2}}-\frac{p_{1}}{R T_{1}}=\frac{1}{R}\left(\frac{p_{2}}{T_{2}}-\frac{p_{1}}{T_{1}}\right)\) (6)

 

Using the pressures and temperatures given in the problem statement we calculate from Eq. 6

 

\(\begin{aligned}\rho_{2}-\rho_{1}=& \frac{1}{53.3( ft \cdot lb ) /\left( lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right)} \\& \times\left[\frac{(18.4 psia )\left(144 in. ^{2} / ft ^{2}\right)}{453{ }^{\circ} R }\right.\\&\left.-\frac{(100 psia )\left(144 in. ^{2} / ft ^{2}\right)}{540{ }^{\circ} R }\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

or

 

\(\rho_{2}-\rho_{1}=-0.389 lbm / ft ^{3}\)

 

COMMENT This is a significant change in density when compared with the upstream density

 

\(\begin{aligned}\rho_{1} &=\frac{p_{1}}{R T_{1}}=\frac{(100 psia )\left(144 in .^{2} / ft ^{2}\right)}{\left[53.3( ft \cdot lb ) /\left(1 lbm \cdot{ }^{\circ} R \right)\right]\left(540^{\circ} R \right)} \\&=0.499 lbm / ft ^{3}\end{aligned}\)

 

Compressibility effects are important for this flow.
