Guest Question.

Consider the system,

\(\dot{ x }=\left[\begin{array}{rrrr}0 & 4 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & -4 & 0 & 0 \\ 5 & 7 & 1 & 15 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 & -3\end{array}\right] x +\left[\begin{array}{l}0 \\0 \\1 \\ 0\end{array}\right] u\).

a) Find the eigenvalues of this system. (Hint: Note the block-triangular structure.)

b) Find the controllable and uncontrollable modes of this system.

c) For each of the uncontrollable modes, find a vector v such that,
\(v ^{T} G =0, \quad v ^{T} F =\lambda v ^{T}\).

d) Show that there are an infinite number of feedback gains K that will relocate the modes of the system to – 5, -3, -2, and -2.

e) Find the unique matrix K that achieves these pole locations and prevents initial conditions on the uncontrollable part of the system from ever affecting the controllable part.

Admin
Solution

(a) Because the system is block lower triangular, we can determine the eigenvalues of the system by taking the union of the eigenvalues of each of the blocks along the main (block)diagonal.

\(s^{2}+4 s+4=0 \Longrightarrow-2,-2\).

\(s^{2}+2 s-48=0 \Longrightarrow 6,-8\)

Thus, the eigenvalues of the system are – 2, – 2 , 6, and – 8. (Easily checked with MATLABâ€™s eig command).

(b) To find the controllable or uncontrollable modes of the system, we follow method learned in . Specifically, we find an orthogonal similarity transformation which transforms (F, G, H) to \((\overline{ F }, \overline{ G }, \overline{ H })\) where \(\overline{ F }\) is an upper-Hessenberg matrix. (See for details). Observe that this system is almost in the desired form already! Simply by interchanging the state variables \(x_{3} and x_{4}\), we can transform the system into the proper form.

\(\left[\begin{array}{l} \dot{x}_{1} \\ \dot{x}_{2} \\ \dot{x}_{4} \\ \dot{x}_{3}\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cccc} 0 & 4 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & -4 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -3 & 3 \\ 5 & 7 & 15 & 1\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ x_{4} \\ x_{3}\end{array}\right]+\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 1\end{array}\right] u\).

Now the controllable and uncontrollable modes can be determined by inspection. The uncontrollable modes correspond to the eigenvalues of the \(F_{11}\) block, so – 2 and – 2 are both uncontrollable modes. Similarly, the controllable modes from the \(F_{22}\) block are – 8 and 6.

MATLAB function ctrbf will give a similar result, although the order of the state variables may be switched.

(c) Notice that we need the left eigenvectors of F that is orthogonal to G. The only left eigenvector of F that is orthogonal to G is \(\left[\begin{array}{llll}1 & 2 & 0 & 0\end{array}\right]^{T}\).

(d) Because the modes at – 2 and – 2 are uncontrollable, we expect that state feedback will not have any affect on these modes. Writing an expression for the feedback we have,

\(\left[\begin{array}{l} \dot{x}_{1} \\ \dot{x}_{2} \\ \dot{x}_{3} \\ \dot{x}_{4}\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cccc} 0 & 4 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & -4 & 0 & 0 \\ 5-k_{1} & 7-k_{2} & 1-k_{3} & 15-k_{4} \\ 0 & 0 & 3 & -3\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ x_{3} \\ x_{4}\end{array}\right]\).

Notice that the system matrix is still block diagonal. The characteristic equation of the \(F_{22}\) block gives,

\( \operatorname{det}\left(s I – F_{22}\right)=s^{2}+\left(2+k_{3}\right) s+\left(3 k_{3}+3 k_{4}-48\right)=0\).

Picking \(k_{3} = 6 and k_{4} = 15\) will place the controllable roots at – 3 and – 5. Since \(k_{1} and k_{2}\) are arbitrary, there are an infinite number of feedback gains that will relocate the modes of the system to the desired locations.

(e) To completely decouple the controllable and uncontrollable portions of the system, we make the \(F_{21}\) block identically zero by setting \(k_{1} = 5 and k_{2} = 7\).
