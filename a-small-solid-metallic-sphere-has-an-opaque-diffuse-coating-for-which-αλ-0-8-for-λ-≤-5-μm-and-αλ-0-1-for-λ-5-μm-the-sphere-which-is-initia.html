Guest Question.

A small, solid metallic sphere has an opaque, diffuse coating for which \(\alpha _{\lambda } \)= 0.8 for \(\lambda \) ≤ 5 μm and \(\alpha _{\lambda } \) = 0.1 for \(\lambda \) >5 μm. The sphere, which is initially at a uniform tempera-ture of 300 K, is inserted into a large furnace whose walls are at 1200 K. Determine the total, hemispherical absorptivity and emissivity of the coating for the initial condition and for the final, steady-state condition.

Admin
Solution

Known: Small metallic sphere with spectrally selective absorptivity, initially at \(T_{s}\) = 300 K, is inserted into a large furnace at \(T_{f}\) = 1200 K.

Find:
1. Total, hemispherical absorptivity and emissivity of sphere coating for the initial condition.
2. Values of α and ε after sphere has been in furnace a long time.

Assumptions:
1. Coating is opaque and diffuse.
2. Since furnace surface is much larger than that of sphere, irradiation approximates emission from a blackbody at \(T_{f}\).

Analysis:
1. From Equation 12.52 the total, hemispherical absorptivity is

\( \alpha = \frac{\int_{0}^{\infty }{\alpha _{\lambda }(\lambda ) G_{\lambda } (\lambda ) d\lambda } }{\int_{0}^{\infty }{ G_{\lambda } (\lambda ) d\lambda } } \)

or, with \(G_{\lambda } = E_{\lambda ,b} (T_{f}) = E_{\lambda ,b}(\lambda , 1200 K),\)

\( \alpha = \frac{\int_{0}^{\infty }{\alpha _{\lambda }(\lambda ) E_{\lambda ,b}(\lambda , 1200 K) d\lambda } }{ E_{b}( 1200 K) }\)

Hence

\( \alpha = \alpha _{\lambda , 1}\frac{\int_{0}^{\lambda_{1}}{ E_{\lambda ,b}(\lambda , 1200 K) d\lambda } }{ E_{b}( 1200 K) } + \alpha _{\lambda , 2}\frac{\int_{\lambda_{1}}^{\infty }{ E_{\lambda ,b}(\lambda , 1200 K) d\lambda } }{ E_{b}( 1200 K) }\)

or

\( \alpha = \alpha _{\lambda , 1} F_{(0→\lambda_{1 })} + \alpha _{\lambda , 2}[1 – F_{(0→\lambda_{1 })}]\)

\( \lambda_{1 } T_{f} = 5 μm × 1200 K = 6000 μm . K:\) \( F_{(0→ \lambda_{1 })} = 0.738\)

Hence

\( \alpha = 0.8 × 0.738 + 0.1 (1 – 0.738) = 0.62 \)

The total, hemispherical emissivity follows from Equation 12.43 \( \varepsilon (T) = \frac{ \int_{0}^{\infty }{\varepsilon _{\lambda } (\lambda ,T) E_{\lambda , b}(\lambda ,T) d\lambda } }{E_{b}(T)}\).

\( \varepsilon (T_{s}) = \frac{ \int_{0}^{\infty }{\varepsilon _{\lambda } E_{\lambda , b}(\lambda ,T_{s}) d\lambda } }{E_{b}(T_{s})} \)

Since the surface is diffuse, \(\varepsilon _{\lambda } =\alpha_{\lambda }\) and it follows that

\( \varepsilon = \alpha_{\lambda , 1} \frac{ \int_{0}^{\lambda_{1} }{ E_{\lambda , b}(\lambda ,300 K) d\lambda } }{E_{b}(300 K)} + \alpha_{\lambda , 2} \frac{ \int_{\lambda_{1} }^{\infty }{ E_{\lambda , b}(\lambda ,300 K) d\lambda } }{E_{b}(300 K)} \)

or

\( \varepsilon = \alpha_{\lambda , 1} F_{(0→\lambda_{1})}+ \alpha_{\lambda , 2} [1 -F_{(0→\lambda_{1})} ]\)

From Table 12.2,

\(\lambda_{1} T_{s} = 5 μm × 300 K = 1500 μm . K:\) \(F_{(0→\lambda_{1})} = 0.014 \)

Hence

\( \varepsilon = 0.8 × 0.014 + 0.1(1 – 0.014) = 0.11 \)

2. Because the spectral characteristics of the coating and the furnace temperature remain fixed, there is no change in the value of α with increasing time. However, as \(T_{s}\) increases with time, the value of \( \varepsilon \) will change. After a sufficiently long time, \(T_{s} = T_{f}\) , and \( \varepsilon = \alpha (\varepsilon = 0.62).\)

Comments:
1. The equilibrium condition that eventually exists (\(T_{s} = T_{f}\) ) corresponds precisely to the condition for which Kirchhoff’s law was derived. Hence α must equal \( \varepsilon \).
2. Approximating the sphere as a lumped capacitance and neglecting convection, an energy balance for a control volume about the sphere yields

\(\dot{E}_{in} – \dot{E}_{out} = \dot{E}_{st} \)

 

\((\alpha G) A_{s} – ( \varepsilon \sigma T^{4}_{s}) A_{s} = M c_{p} \frac{d T_{s}}{dt} \)

The differential equation could be solved to determine T(t) for t > 0, and the variation in \( \varepsilon \) that occurs with increasing time would have to be included in the solution.
