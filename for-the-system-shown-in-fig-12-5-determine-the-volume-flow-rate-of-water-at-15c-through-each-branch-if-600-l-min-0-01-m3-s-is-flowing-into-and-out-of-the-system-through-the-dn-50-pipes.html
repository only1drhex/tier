Guest Question.

For the system shown in Fig. 12.5, determine the volume flow rate of water at 15°C through each branch if 600 L/min (\( 0.01 m^3/s\)) is flowing into and out of the system through the DN 50 pipes.

Admin
Solution

The head loss in each pipe should now be expressed in the form of \( h = kQ^2\) as Step 1 of the procedure. Consider branch a first and write an expression for the head loss \( h_a\).

The total head loss for the branch is due to the two elbows (each with \( L_e/D = 30\)), the restriction (with K = 4.0 based on the velocity head in the pipe), and friction in the pipe. Then,

\( h_a = 2\left(f_{aT}\right) \left(30\right) \left(v_a^2/2g\right) +4.0\left(v_a^2/2g\right)+f_a\left(L_a/D_a\right) \left(v_a^2/2g\right)\)

The friction factor \( f_a\) for flow in the pipe is dependent on the Reynolds number and, therefore, on the volume flow rate. Because that is the objective of the network analysis, we cannot determine that value explicitly at this time. Furthermore, the flow rate will, in general, be different in each segment of the flow system, resulting in different values for the friction factor. We will take that into account in the present analysis by computing the value of the friction factor after assuming the magnitude of the volume flow rate in each pipe, a step that is inherent in the Cross technique. We will use the Swamee–Jain method to compute the friction factor from Eq. (8–7). Then, we will recompute the values of the friction factors for each trial as the value of the volume flow rate is refined.

First, let’s simplify the equation for \( h_a\) by completing as many calculations as we can. What values can be determined?

The total length of pipe in branch a is 12 m, and for the DN 25 Schedule 40 pipe D = 0.0266 m and \( A = 5.574 \times 10^{-4} m^2\) . From Table 10.5 we can find that the value of \( f_{aT}\) = 0.022 for a DN 25 Schedule 40 steel pipe with flow in the completely turbulent zone. The water at 15°C has a kinematic viscosity \( \nu = 1.15 \times 10^{-6} m^2/s\). We can introduce the volume flow rate Q into the equation by noting that, as shown before,

\( v_a^2 = Q_a^2/A_a^2\)

Now substitute these values into the equation for \( h_a\) and simplify it as much as possible.

You should have something like this:

\( h_a = \left[60\left(f_{aT}\right)+4.0+\left(f_a\right) \left(12/0.0266\right) \right] \left(v_a^2/2g\right) \)

\( h_a = \left[60\left(f_{aT}\right)+4.0+451\left(f_a\right) \right] \left(Q_a^2/2gA^2\right) \)

\( h_a = \left[60\left(0.0022\right)+4.0+451\left(f_a\right) \right]\left[\frac{Q_a^2}{2\left(9.81\right)\left(5.574 \times 10^{-4}\right)^2 } \right] \)

\( h_a = \left[5.32+451 \left(f_a\right) \right] \left(1.64 \times 10^5\right) Q_a^2\)

It is also convenient to express the Reynolds number in terms of the volume flow rate Q and to compute the value for the relative roughness \( D/\epsilon\). Do that now.

Because all three branches have the same size and type of pipe, these calculations apply to each branch. If different pipes are used throughout the network, these calculations must be redone for each pipe. For the DN 25 steel pipe,

\( D/\epsilon = \left(0.0266m\right) /\left(4.6 \times 10^{-5}m\right) =578\)

We should modify the Reynolds number formula as

\( N_{Ra}=\frac{v_aD_a}{\nu} =\frac{Q_aD_a}{A_a \nu} =\frac{Q_a\left(0.0266\right) }{\left(5.574 \times 10^{-4}\right)\left(1.15 \times 10^{-6}\right) } \)

\( N_{Ra}=\left(4.15 \times 10^7\right) Q_a\)

Now create expressions for the head losses in the other two pipes, \( h_b\) and \( h_c\), using similar procedures.

Compare your results with these. Note that the pipe size in branches b and c is the same as that in branch a. For branch b:

\( h_b = 8.0\left(v_b^2/2g\right) +f_b\left(L_b/D_b\right) \left(v_b^2/2g\right)\)

\( h_b = \left[8.0 + f_b \left(6/0.0266\right) \right] \left(v_b^2/2gA^2\right)\)

\( h_b = \left[8.0 +225.6 \left(f_b\right) \right]\left(1.64 \times 10^5\right) Q_b^2\)

For branch c:

\( h_c = 2 \left(f_{cT}\right) \left(30\right) \left(v_c^2/2g\right) +12.0\left(v_c^2/2g\right) +f_c\left(L_c/D_c\right) \left(v_c^2/2g\right) \)

\( h_c = \left[60\left(f_cT\right)+12.0+f_c\left(12/0.0266\right) \left(v_c^2/2g\right) \right] \)

\( h_c =\left[ 60\left(0.022\right)+12.0+451f_c \right] \left(Q_c^2/2gA^2\right) \)

\( h_c = \left[13.32 + 451 \left(f_c\right) \right] \left(1.64 \times 10^5\right) Q_c^2\)

Equations (12–18) to (12–21) will be used in the calculations of head losses as the Cross iteration process continues. When the values for the friction factors are known or assumed, the head loss equations can be reduced to the form of Eq. (12–16). Often it is satisfactory to assume reasonable values for the various friction factors because minor changes have little effect on the flow distribution and the total head loss. However, we will demonstrate the more complete solution procedure in which new friction factors are calculated for each pipe for each trial.

Step 2 of the procedure calls for estimating the volume flow rate in each branch. Which pipe should have the greatest flow rate and which should have the least?

Although the final values for the friction factors could affect the magnitudes of the resistances, it appears that pipe b has the least resistance and, therefore, it should carry the greatest flow. Pipe c has the most resistance and it should carry the least flow. Many different first estimates are possible for the flow rates, but we know that

\( Q_a + Q_b + Q_c = Q1 = 0.01 m^3/s\)

Let’s use the initial assumptions

\( Q_a = 0.0033 m^3/s Q_b = 0.0036 m^3/s Q_c = 0.0031 m^3/s\)

Step 3 of the procedure is already shown in Fig. 12.6. To complete Step 4 we need values for the friction factor in each pipe. With the assumed values for the volume flow rates we can compute the Reynolds numbers and then the friction factors. Do that now.

You should have, using Eq. (12–21) and \( D/\epsilon\) = 578,

\( N_{Ra} = \left(4.15 \times 10^7\right) Q_a = \left(4.15\times 10^7\right) \left(0.0033m^3/s\right) =1.37\times 10^5\)

\( N_{Rb} = \left(4.15 \times 10^7\right) Q_b = \left(4.15\times 10^7\right) \left(0.0036m^3/s\right) =1.49\times 10^5\)

\( N_{Rc} = \left(4.15 \times 10^7\right) Q_c = \left(4.15\times 10^7\right) \left(0.0031m^3/s\right) =1.29\times 10^5\)

We now use Eq. (9–5) to compute the friction factor for each pipe:

\( f_a = \frac{0.25}{\left[\log_{10} \left(\frac{1}{3.7 \left(D/\epsilon\right) } +\frac{5.74}{N_{Ra}^{0.9}} \right) \right]^2 } \)

\( f_a = \frac{0.25}{\left[\log_{10} \left(\frac{1}{3.7 \left(578\right) } +\frac{5.74}{\left(1.37\times 10^5\right) ^{0.9}} \right) \right]^2 } =0.0241\)

In a similar manner we compute \( f_b = 0.0240\) and \(f_c = 0.0242\). These values are quite close in magnitude and such precision may not be justified. However, with greater disparity among the pipes in the network, more sizable differences would occur and the accuracy of the iteration technique would depend on the accuracy of evaluating the friction factors.

Now, insert the friction factors and the assumed values for Q into Eqs. (12–18), (12–20), and (12–23) to compute \( k_a, k_b\), and \(k_c\) :

\( h_a =\left[5.32+451\left(f_a\right) \right] \left(1.64\times 10^5\right) Q_a^2=k_aQ_a^2\)

\(h_a =\left[5.32+451\left(0.0241\right) \right] \left(1.64\times 10^5\right) Q_a^2=2.655 \times 10^6Q_a^2 \)

Then, \( k_a = 2.655 \times 10^6 \).Completing the calculation gives

\(h_a = 2.655 \times 10^6 \left(0.0033\right)^2 =28.91\)

Similarly, for branch b:

\( h_b = \left[8.0+225.6\left(f_b\right) \right] \left(1.64 \times 10^5\right) Q_b^2 = k_b Q_b^2\)

\( h_b = \left[8.0+225.6\left(0.0240\right) \right] \left(1.64 \times 10^5\right) Q_b^2 = 2.20 \times 10^6 Q_b^2\)

\( h_b = 2.20 \times 10^6\left(0.0036\right)^2 = 28.53 \)

For branch c:

\( h_c = \left[13.32+451\left(f_c\right) \right] \left(1.64 \times 10^5\right) Q_c^2 = k_c Q_c^2\)

\( h_c = \left[13.32+451\left(0.0242\right) \right] \left(1.64 \times 10^5\right) Q_c^2 = 3.974 \times 10^6 Q_c^2\)

\( h_c = 3.974 \times 10^6 \left(0.0031\right)^2 = 38.19 \)

This completes Step 4. Now do Step 5.

For circuit 1,

\( \sum{ h1 }= h_a – h_b = 28.91 – 28.53 = 0.38\)

For circuit 2,

\( \sum{ h2 }= h_b – h_c = 28.53 – 38.19 = -9.66\)

Now do Step 6.

Here are the correct values for the three pipes:

\( 2k_aQ_a = \left(2\right) \left(2.655 \times 10^6\right) \left(0.0033\right) =17523\)

\( 2k_bQ_b = \left(2\right) \left(2.20 \times 10^6\right) \left(0.0036\right) =15850\)

\( 2k_cQ_c = \left(2\right) \left(3.974 \times 10^6\right) \left(0.0031\right) =24639\)

Round-off differences may occur. Now do Step 7.

For circuit 1,

\( \sum{\left(2kQ\right)_1 } =17523+15850=33373\)

For circuit 2,

\( \sum{\left(2kQ\right)_2 } =15850+24639=40489\)

Now you can calculate the adjustment for the flow rates \( \Delta Q\) for each circuit, using Step 8.

For circuit 1,

\( \Delta Q_1 = \frac{\sum{h_1} }{\sum{\left(2kQ\right)_1 } } =\frac{0.38}{33373} = 1.14 \times 10^{-5}\)

\( \Delta Q_1 = \frac{\sum{h_2} }{\sum{\left(2kQ\right)_2 } } =\frac{-9.66}{40489} = -2.39 \times 10^{-4}\)

The values for \( \Delta Q\) are estimates of the error in the originally assumed values for Q. We recommended that the process be repeated until the magnitude of \( \Delta Q\)is less than 1 percent of the assumed value of Q. Special circumstances may warrant using a different criterion for judging \( \Delta Q\).

Step 9 can now be completed. Calculate the new value for \( Q_a\) before looking at the next panel.

The calculation is as follows:

\( Q_a^{'} = Q_a – \Delta Q_1 = 0.0033 – 1.14 \times 10^{-5}\)

\( = 0.003 29 m^3/s\)

Calculate the new value for \( Q_c\) before [/latex] Q_b[/latex]. Pay careful attention to algebraic signs.

You should have

\( Q_c^{'} = Q_c – \Delta Q_2 = -0.0031-\left(-2.39\times 10^{-4}\right) \)

\( = -0.002 86 m^3/s\)

Notice that \( Q_c\) is negative because it flows in a counterclockwise direction in circuit 2. We can interpret the calculation for \( Q_c^{'}\) as indicating that the magnitude of \( Q_c\) must be decreased in absolute value.

Now calculate the new value for \( Q_b\)Qb. Remember, pipe b is in each circuit.

Both \( \Delta Q_1\) and \( \Delta Q_2\) must be applied to \( Q_b\). For circuit 1,

\( Q_b^{'} = Q_b – \Delta Q_1 = -0.0036-1.14 \times 10^{-5}\)

This would result in an increase in the absolute value of \( Q_b\). For circuit 2,

\( Q_b^{'} = Q_b – \Delta Q_2 = +0.0036-\left(-2.39 \times 10^{-4}\right) \)

This also results in increasing \( Q_b\). Then \( Q_b\) is actually increased in absolute value by the sum of \( \Delta Q_1\) and \( \Delta Q_2\). That is,

\( Q_b^{'} =0.0036+1.14 \times 10^{-5}+2.39 \times 10^-4\)

\( = 0.003 85 m^3/s\)

Remember that the sum of the absolute values of the flow rates in the three pipes must equal 0.01 \( m^3/s\), the total Q.

We can continue the iteration by using \(Q_ab^{'}, Q_b^{'}\), and \(Q_c^{'}\) as the new estimates for the flow rates and repeating Steps 4–8. The results for four iteration cycles are summarized in Table 12.1. You should carry out the calculations before looking at the table.

Notice that in Trial 4, the values of \( \Delta Q\) are below 1 percent of the respective values of Q. This is an adequate degree of precision. The results show that:

\(Q_a = 3.402 \times 10^{-3} m^3/s=0.003402m^3/s = 204.1 L/min\)

\( Q_b = 3.785 \times 10^{-3} m^3/s=0.003785m^3/s = 227.1 L/min\)

\( Q_c = 2.813 \times 10^{-3} m^3/s=0.002813m^3/s = 168.8 L/min\)

The total Q = 600 L/min. Once again, observe that the branches having the lower resistances carry the greater flow rates.
