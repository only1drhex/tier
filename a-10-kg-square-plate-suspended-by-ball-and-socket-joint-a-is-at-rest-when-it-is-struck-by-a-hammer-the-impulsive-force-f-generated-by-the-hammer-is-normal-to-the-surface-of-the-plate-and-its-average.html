Guest Question.

A 10 -kg square plate suspended by ball-and-socket joint \(A\) is at rest when it is struck by a hammer. The impulsive force \(\bar{F}\) generated by the hammer is normal to the surface of the plate, and its average value during the 4 -ms interval that it acts is \(5000 \mathrm{~N}\). Determine the angular velocity of the plate at the instant following the impact and the average reaction at the support.

Admin
Solution

This straightforward example once again highlights the fact that the behavior of bodies in spatial motion is often counterintuitive. We ignore the weight of the plate, which is much smaller than the average applied force. In contrast, the reaction exerted by the ball-and-socket joint, which consists of forces acting in each of the coordinate directions, is impulsive, because it must be as large as necessary to prevent movement of point A. The plate pivots about the stationary point A where the ball-and-socket joint is located, so we place the origin of the body-fixed xyz coordinate system there and align the axes with the edges of the plate.

We know that \(\bar{\omega}_{1}=\overline{0}\), but have no idea what angular velocity results from the action of \(\bar{F}\). If we knew \(\bar{\omega}_{2}\) we could find \(\bar{v}_{G}\) kinematically according to

\(\bar{\omega}_{2}=\omega_{x} \bar{i}+\omega_{y} \bar{j}+\omega_{z} \bar{k}, \quad\left(\bar{v}_{G}\right)_{2}=\bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{G / A}.\)

We set \(\bar{r}_{G / A}=0.18 \bar{i}-0.06 \bar{j} \mathrm{~m}\), which leads to

\(\left(\bar{v}_{G}\right)_{2}=0.06 \omega_{z} \bar{i}+0.18 \omega_{z} \bar{j}+\left(-0.06 \omega_{x}-0.18 \omega_{y}\right) \bar{k}.\)

The inertia properties relative to \(x y z\) are obtained from the Appendix and the parallel axis theorems. The coordinates of point \(A\) relative to centroidal axes parallel to \(x y z\) are \((-0.18,0.06,0) \mathrm{m}\), which leads to

\(\begin{array}{c}I_{x x}=0.144 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2}, \quad I_{y y}=0.432 \mathrm{~kg}-\mathrm{m}^{2}, \quad I_{z z}=0.576 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2}, \\\\I_{x y}=-0.108 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2}, \quad I_{x z}=I_{y z}=0.\end{array} \)

The final angular momentum about pivot A corresponding to these properties is

\(\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{2}=\left(0.144 \omega_{x}+0.108 \omega_{y}\right) \bar{i}+\left(0.432 \omega_{y}+0.108 \omega_{x}\right) \bar{j}+0.576 \omega_{z} \bar{k} .\)

The initial angular momentum is zero, so the final angular momentum must equal the moment impulse. The sole impulsive force exerting a moment about point \(A\) is \(\bar{F}\). The moment impulse is the average moment of this force multiplied by the 4-ms interval or, equivalently, the moment of the linear impulse of \(\bar{F}\) during this interval. Thus,

\(\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{2}=\bar{r}_{F / A} \times \bar{F}_{\mathrm{av}}(\Delta t)=(0.36 \bar{i}+0.12 \bar{j}) \times(5000 \bar{k})(0.004) .\)

Matching like components in the two descriptions of \(\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{2}\) gives

\(\begin{array}{c}\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{2} \cdot \bar{i}=\left(0.144 \omega_{x}+0.108 \omega_{y}\right)=2.4, \\\\\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{2} \cdot \bar{j}=\left(0.432 \omega_{y}+0.108 \omega_{y}\right)=-7.2 ,\\\\\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{2} \cdot \bar{k}=0.576 \omega_{z}=0.\end{array} \)

We solve these equations for the rotation rates, from which we obtain

\(\bar{\omega}_{2}=35.90 \bar{i}-25.64 \bar{j}~ \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}.\)

The next step is to form the linear impulse-momentum principle in order to determine the reaction. The velocity of the center of mass corresponding to \(\bar{\omega}_{2}\) is

\(\left(\bar{v}_{G}\right)_{2}=\bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{G / A}=2.462 \bar{k} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}.\)

The initial linear momentum was zero, so the final momentum must equal the impulse of all forces:

\(m\left(\bar{v}_{G}\right)_{2}=\left[A_{x} \bar{i}+A_{y} \bar{j}+\left(A_{z}+F\right) \bar{k}\right] \Delta t.\)

The solution of these equations is

\(A_{x}=A_{y}=0, \quad A_{z}=1153 \mathrm{~N}.\)

These are average values over the 4-ms interval. The maximum values exceed these. It might surprise you that the reaction is in the same sense as the impulsive force \(\bar{F}\). This result indicates that, if the ball-and-socket joint were not present, the plate would rotate about its mass center because of the moment of the force, such that point \(A\) moves in the negative \(z\) direction. It is possible to locate a curve on the plate representing the locus of points at which the force can be applied without generating a dynamic reaction at the joint. Any such point is sometimes referred to as a center of percussion.
