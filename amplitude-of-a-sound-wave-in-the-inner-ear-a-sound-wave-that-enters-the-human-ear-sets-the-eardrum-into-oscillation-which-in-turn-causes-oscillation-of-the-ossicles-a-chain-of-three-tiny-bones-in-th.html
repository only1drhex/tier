AMPLITUDE OF A SOUND WAVE IN THE INNER EAR

A sound wave that enters the human ear sets the eardrum into oscillation, which in turn causes oscillation of the ossicles, a chain of three tiny bones in the middle ear (Fig. 16.4). The ossicles transmit this oscillation to the fluid (mostly water) in the inner ear; there the fluid motion disturbs hair cells that send nerve impulses to the brain with information about the sound. The area of the moving part of the eardrum is about 43 \(mm^2\), and that of the stapes (the smallest of the ossicles) where it connects to the inner ear is about 3.2 \(mm^2\). For the sound in , determine (a) the pressure amplitude and (b) the displacement amplitude of the wave in the fluid of the inner ear, in which the speed of sound is 1500 m/s.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

Although the sound wave here travels in liquid rather than air, the same principles and relationships among the properties of the wave apply. We can ignore the mass of the tiny ossicles (about 58 mg = 5.8 × \(10^{-5}\) kg), so the force they exert on the inner-ear fluid is the same as that exerted on the eardrum and ossicles by the incident sound wave. (In Chapters 4 and 5 we used the same idea to say that the tension is the same at either end of a massless rope.) Hence the pressure amplitude in the inner ear, \(p_{\max (inner ear) }\), is greater than in the outside air, \(p_{\max (ear) }\), because the same force is exerted on a smaller area (the area of the stapes versus the area of the eardrum). Given \(p_{\max (inner ear) }\), we find the displacement amplitude \(A_{inner ear}\) from Eq. (16.5).

\(p_{\max }=B k A\) (16.5)

EXECUTE:

(a) From the area of the eardrum and the pressure amplitude in air found in , the maximum force exerted by the sound wave in air on the eardrum is \(F_{\max }=p_{\max ( air )} S_{\text {eardrum }}\).

Then

\(\begin{aligned}p_{\text {max (inner ear) }} &=\frac{F_{\max }}{S_{\text {stapes }}}=p_{\max (a i r)} \frac{S_{\text {eardrum }}}{S_{\text {stapes }}} \\&=\left(3.0 \times10^{-2} \mathrm{~Pa}\right) \frac{43 \mathrm{~mm}^{2}}{3.2 \mathrm{~mm}^{2}}=0.40\mathrm{~Pa}\end{aligned}\)

(b) To find the maximum displacement \(A_{inner ear}\), we use A = \(p_{max}\)/Bk as in . The inner-ear fluid is mostly water, which has a much greater bulk modulus B than air. From Table 11.2:

the compressibility of water (unfortunately also called K) \(45.8 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{~Pa}^{-1} \text {, so } B_{\text {fluid }}=1 /\left(45.8 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{~Pa}^{-1}\right)=\)\(2.18 \times 10^{9} \mathrm{~Pa}\).

The wave in the inner ear has the same angular frequency ω as the wave in the air because the air, eardrum, ossicles, and inner-ear fluid all oscillate together (see in Section 15.8). But because the wave speed v is greater in the inner ear than in the air (1500 m/s versus 344 m/s), the wave number k = ω/v is smaller.
Using the value of ω from ,

\(k_{\text {inner ear }}=\frac{\omega}{v_{\text {inner ear }}}=\frac{(2 \pi \mathrm{rad})(1000 \mathrm{~Hz})}{1500 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}=4.2 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{m}\)

Putting everything together, we have

\(\begin{aligned}A_{\text {inner ear }} &=\frac{p_{\max ( inner ear )}}{B_{\text {fluid }}k_{\text {inner ear }}}=\frac{0.40 \mathrm{~Pa}}{\left(2.18 \times 10^{9} \mathrm{~Pa}\right)(4.2 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{m})} \\&=\frac{0.40 \mathrm{~Pa}}{\left(2.18 \times 10^{9}\mathrm{~Pa}\right)(4.2 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{m})} \\&=4.4 \times 10^{-11} \mathrm{~m}\end{aligned}\)

 

EVALUATE: In part (a) we see that the ossicles increase the pressure amplitude by a factor of \(\left(43 \mathrm{~mm}^{2}\right) /\left(3.2 \mathrm{~mm}^{2}\right)=13 .\). This amplification helps give the human ear its great sensitivity.
The displacement amplitude in the inner ear is even smaller than in the air. But pressure variations within the inner-ear fluid are what set the hair cells into motion, so what matters is that the pressure amplitude is larger in the inner ear than in the air.
