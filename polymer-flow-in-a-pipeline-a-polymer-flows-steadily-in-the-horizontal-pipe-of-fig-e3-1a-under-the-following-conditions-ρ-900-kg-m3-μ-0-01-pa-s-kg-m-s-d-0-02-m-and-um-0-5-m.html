Guest Question.

Polymer Flow in a Pipeline

A polymer flows steadily in the horizontal pipe of Fig. E3.1(a) under the following conditions: \(\rho=900 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}, \mu=0.01 \mathrm{~Pa} \mathrm{~s}(\mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m} \mathrm{s}), D=0.02 \mathrm{~m}\), and \(u_{\mathrm{m}}=0.5 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\). Evaluate the following, clearly indicating the units:

(a) The Reynolds number.

(b) The frictional dissipation per meter per \(\mathrm{kg}\) flowing.

(c) The pressure drop per meter.

(d) The elevation decrease for every meter of length if the polymer were to flow steadily at the same rate without any pumping needed. In this case, gravity provides the necessary energy for flow (i.e., \(\Delta p=0)\), as in Fig. E3.1(b).

Admin
Solution

(a) A check on the (dimensionless) Reynolds number gives:

\(\operatorname{Re}=\frac{\rho u_{\mathrm{m}} D}{\mu}=\frac{900 \times 0.5 \times 0.02}{0.01}=900.\) (E3.1.1)

(b) Since the Reynolds number is below the critical value of 2,000 , the flow is laminar, and the frictional dissipation term per unit length \((L=1 \mathrm{~m})\) can be computed from Eqn. (3.16):

\(\mathcal{F}=-\frac{\Delta p}{\rho}=\frac{8 \mu L Q}{\pi a^{4} \rho}=\frac{8 \mu u_{\mathrm{m}} L}{\rho a^{2}}\) (3.16)

\(\mathcal{F}=\frac{8 \mu u_{\mathrm{m}} L}{\rho a^{2}}=\frac{8 \times 0.01 \times 0.5 \times 1}{900 \times(0.01)^{2}}=0.444 \frac{\mathrm{m}^{2}}{\mathrm{~s}^{2}}=0.444 \frac{\mathrm{J}}{\mathrm{kg}}.\) (E3.1.2)

(c) An energy balance between any two points in the pipeline gives:

\(\alpha \Delta\left(\frac{u^{2}}{2}\right)+g \Delta z+\frac{\Delta p}{\rho}+w+\mathcal{F}=0 .\) (E3.1.3)

However, the first, second, and fourth terms are zero (constant velocity, no elevation change for a horizontal pipe, and no work done), so the pressure drop per meter is:

\(-\Delta p=\rho \mathcal{F}=900 \times 0.444 \frac{\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}{\mathrm{~m}^{2}}=400 \mathrm{~Pa}=0.0040 \mathrm{bar}.\) (E3.1.4)

(d) To find the elevation decrease for no pumping, again apply the overall energy balance with zero kinetic energy change and no work done. Additionally, there is no change in the pressure, since the loss in potential energy is completely balanced by pipe friction, so that:

\(\Delta z=-\frac{\mathcal{F}}{g}=-\frac{0.444}{9.81}=-0.0453 \mathrm{~m}.\) (E3.1.5)

That is, the elevation must drop by \(0.0453 \mathrm{~m}\) for every meter of pipe length.
