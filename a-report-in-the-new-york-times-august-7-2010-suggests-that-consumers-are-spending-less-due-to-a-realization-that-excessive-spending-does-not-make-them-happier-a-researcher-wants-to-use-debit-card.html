Guest Question.

A report in The New York Times (August 7, 2010) suggests that consumers are spending less due to a realization that excessive spending does not make them happier. A researcher wants to use debit card data to contradict the generally held view that the average amount spent annually on a debit card is at least $8,000. She surveys 20 consumers and asks them how much they spend annually on their debit cards. The results are given below.

7960 7700 7727 7704 8543 7661 7767 8761 7530 8128
7938 7771 7272 8113 7727 7767 7690 8000 8079 7547

It is assumed that the population standard deviation is $500 and that spending on debit cards is normally distributed. Test the claim at the 1% level of significance.

Admin
Solution

The researcher would like to establish that average spending on debit
cards is less than $8,000 or, equivalently, μ < 8,000. Thus, we formulate the competing hypotheses as

\( H_0 : μ ≥ 8,000 \)

 

\( H_A : μ \lt 8,000 \)

The normality condition of \( \overline{X} \) is satisfied since spending on debit cards is assumed to be normally distributed. Also, since the population standard deviation is known, here \( σ = 500 \), the test statistic is assumed to follow the z distribution.

a. Open the Debit_Spending data file. Note that the values for spending are in cells A2 through A21.

b. We use Excel’s AVERAGE function to help in the calculation of the value of the test statistic \( z = \frac{\overline{x} - μ_0}{σ ∕ \sqrt{n}} \) We enter “=(AVERAGE(A2:A21) − 8000)/(500/SQRT(20))”, and Excel returns −1.2008, so z = −1.2008.

c. We use Excel’s NORM.S.DIST function, first discussed in Chapter 6, to find the p-value. Recall that in order to find \( P(Z ≤ z)\), we enter “=NORM.S.DIST(z, 1)” where z is the value for which we want to evaluate the cumulative probability. If we enter “=1 − NORM.S.DIST(z, 1)”, then Excel returns \( P(Z ≤ z)\). In order to find\( P(Z ≤ −1.2008)\), we enter “=NORM.S.DIST(−1.2008, 1)”. Excel returns 0.1149.

d. The hypothesis test is conducted at the 1% significance level. Thus, since the p-value of 0.1149 is not less than α = 0.01, we do not reject the null hypothesis. In other words, at the 1% significance level, the researcher cannot conclude that annual spending on debit cards is less than $8,000. Perhaps these findings can be reconciled with a report that claims that individuals are shunning their credit cards and using debit cards to avoid incurring more debt (www.businessweek.com, September 8, 2010).
