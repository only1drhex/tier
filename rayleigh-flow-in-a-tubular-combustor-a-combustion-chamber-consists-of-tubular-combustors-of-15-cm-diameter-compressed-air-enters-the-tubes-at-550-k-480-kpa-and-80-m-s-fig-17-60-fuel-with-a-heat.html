Rayleigh Flow in a Tubular Combustor

A combustion chamber consists of tubular combustors of 15-cm diameter. Compressed air enters the tubes at 550 K, 480 kPa, and 80 m/s (Fig. 17-60). Fuel with a heating value of 42,000 kJ/kg is injected into the air and is burned with an air-fuel mass ratio of 40. Approximating combustion as a heat transfer process to air, determine the temperature, pressure, velocity, and Mach number at the exit of the combustion chamber.

Admin
Solution

Fuel is burned in a tubular combustion chamber with compressed air. The exit temperature, pressure, velocity, and Mach number are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 The assumptions associated with Rayleigh flow (i.e., steady one-dimensional flow of an ideal gas with constant properties through a constant cross-sectional-area duct with negligible frictional effects) are valid. 2 Combustion is complete, and it is treated as a heat transfer process, with no change in the chemical composition of the flow. 3 The increase in mass flow rate due to fuel injection is disregarded.

Properties We take the properties of air to be k = 1.4, \(c_p\) = 1.005 kJ/kg.K, and R = 0.287 kJ/kg.K ().

Analysis The inlet density and mass flow rate of air are

 

\(\rho_{1}=\frac{P_{1}}{R T_{1}}=\frac{480 \mathrm{kPa}}{(0.287 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K})(550 \mathrm{~K})}=3.041 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3} \)

 

\(\dot{m}_{\mathrm{air}}=\rho_{1} A_{1} V_{1}=\left(3.041 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\left[\pi(0.15 \mathrm{~m})^{2} / 4\right](80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})=4.299 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

The mass flow rate of fuel and the rate of heat transfer are

\(\dot{m}_{\text {fuel }} =\frac{\dot{m}_{\text {air }}}{\mathrm{AF}}=\frac{4.299 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s}}{40}=0.1075 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s} \)

 

\(\dot{Q} =\dot{m}_{\text {fuel }} \mathrm{HV}=(0.1075 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s})(42,000 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg})=4515 \mathrm{~kW} \)

 

\(q =\frac{\dot{Q}}{\dot{m}_{\text {air }}}=\frac{4515 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{s}}{4.299 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s}}=1050 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\)

 

The stagnation temperature and Mach number at the inlet are

 

\(T_{01} =T_{1}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2 c_{p}}=550 \mathrm{~K}+\frac{(80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})^{2}}{2(1.005 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K})}\left(\frac{1 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}}{1000 \mathrm{~m}^{2} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}\right)=553.2 \mathrm{~K} \)

 

\(c_{1} =\sqrt{k R T_{1}}=\sqrt{(1.4)(0.287 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K})(550 \mathrm{~K})\left(\frac{1000 \mathrm{~m}^{2} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}{1 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}}\right)}=470.1 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s} \)

 

\(\mathrm{Ma}_{1} =\frac{V_{1}}{c_{1}}=\frac{80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}{470.1 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}=0.1702\)

 

The exit stagnation temperature is, from the energy equation \(q=c_{p}\left(T_{02}-T_{01}\right)\),

 

\(T_{02}=T_{01}+\frac{q}{c_{p}}=553.2 \mathrm{~K}+\frac{1050 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}}{1.005 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}}=1598 \mathrm{~K}\)

 

 

The maximum value of stagnation temperature \(T_{0}^{*}\) occurs at \(\mathrm{Ma}=1\), and its value can be determined from or from Eq. 17-65. At Ma\(_{1}=0.1702\) we read \(T_{0} / T_{0}^*=0.1291\). Therefore,

 

\(\frac{T_{0}}{T_{0}^{*}}=\frac{(k+1) \mathrm{Ma}^{2}\left[2+(k-1) \mathrm{Ma}^{2}\right]}{\left(1+k \mathrm{Ma}^{2}\right)^{2}}\) (17-65)

 

\(T_{0}^{*}=\frac{T_{01}}{0.1291}=\frac{553.2 \mathrm{~K}}{0.1291}=4285 \mathrm{~K}\)

 

The stagnation temperature ratio at the exit state and the Mach number corresponding to it are, from ,

 

\(\frac{T_{02}}{T_{0}^{*}}=\frac{1598 \mathrm{~K}}{4285 \mathrm{~K}}=0.3729 \rightarrow \mathrm{Ma}_{2}=0.3142\)

 

The Rayleigh flow relations corresponding to the inlet and exit Mach numbers are ():

 

\(\mathrm{Ma}_{1} =0.1702: \qquad\frac{T_{1}}{T^{*}} =0.1541 \qquad\frac{P_{1}}{P^{*}} =2.3065 \qquad \frac{V_{1}}{V^{*}} =0.0668 \)

 

\(\mathrm{Ma}_{2} =0.3142: \qquad \frac{T_{2}}{T^{*}} =0.4389 \qquad\frac{P_{2}}{P^{*}} =2.1086 \qquad \frac{V_{2}}{V^{*}} =0.2082\)

 

 

Then the exit temperature, pressure, and velocity are determined to be

 

\(\frac{T_{2}}{T_{1}}=\frac{T_{2} / T^{*}}{T_{1} / T^{*}}=\frac{0.4389}{0.1541}=2.848 \rightarrow T_{2}=2.848 T_{1}=2.848(550 \mathrm{~K})=1566 \mathrm{~K}\)

 

\(\frac{P_{2}}{P_{1}}=\frac{P_{2} / P^{*}}{P_{1} / P^{*}}=\frac{2.1086}{2.3065}=0.9142 \rightarrow P_{2}=0.9142 P_{1}=0.9142(480 \mathrm{kPa})=439 \mathrm{kPa}\)

 

\(\frac{V_{2}}{V_{1}}=\frac{V_{2} / V^{*}}{V_{1} / V^{*}}=\frac{0.2082}{0.0668}=3.117 \rightarrow V_{2}=3.117 V_{1}=3.117(80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})=249 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

 

Discussion Note that the temperature and velocity increase and pressure decreases during this subsonic Rayleigh flow with heating, as expected. This problem can also be solved using appropriate relations instead of tabulated values, which can likewise be coded for convenient computer solutions.
