Guest Question.

The system shown in Fig. 4.3 consists of a rigid mass m attached to a rod which has mass density ρ, length l, modulus of elasticity E, and cross-sectional area A. Determine the frequency equation and the mode shapes of the longitudinal vibration.

Admin
Solution

The end condition at the fixed end of the rod is given by \(u\left(0,t\right) = 0\) 0. The other end of the rod is attached to the mass m which exerts a force on the rod because of the inertia effect. From the free-body diagram shown in the figure, the second boundary condition is given by

\( m = \frac{∂ ^2 u \left(l,t\right) }{∂^2} = P\left(l,t\right) = -\sigma \left(l,t\right) A = -EA \frac{∂ u \left(l,t\right)}{∂ x}\)

Or

\(m = \frac{∂ ^2 u \left(l,t\right) }{∂ t^2} = -EA \frac{∂ u \left(l,t\right)}{∂ x}\)

The longitudinal vibration of the rod is governed by the equation

\( u \left(x,t\right) = \phi \left(x\right) q \left(t\right) \)

\( = \left(A_{1}\sin \frac{w}{c}x + A_{2}\cos \frac{w}{c}x \right) (B_{1}\sin wt +B_{2}\cos wt ) \)

Substituting the boundary condition \( u \left(0,t\right) = 0\) into this equation yields \( A_{2} = 0 \). Using this condition, one gets

\( u \left(x,t\right) = \phi \left(x\right) q \left(t\right) = A_{1}\sin \frac{wx}{c} (B_{1}\sin wt +B_{2}\cos wt )\)

which yields

\( \frac{∂ ^{2}u}{∂ t^{2}} = – w^{2} A_{1}\sin \frac{wx}{c} (B_{1}\sin wt +B_{2}\cos wt )\)

\(\frac{∂ u}{∂ x} = \frac{w}{c} A_{1}\cos \frac{wx}{c} (B_{1}\sin wt +B_{2}\cos wt )\)

Therefore, the second boundary condition at the rod end attached to the mass yields

\(-mw^2A_{1}\sin \frac{wl}{c}(B_{1}\sin wt +B_{2}\cos wt )= – EA\frac{w}{c}A_{1}\cos \frac{wl}{c} (B_{1}\sin wt +B_{2}\cos wt ) \)

This equation implies \((mwc/ EA)\sin (wl/ c) = \cos (wl/ c)\), or

\(\tan \frac{wl}{c} = \frac{EA}{mwc}\)

This is the frequency equation which, upon multiplying both of its sides by \(wl/ c\), yields

\(\frac{wl}{c} \tan \frac{wl}{c} = \frac{wlEA}{mwc^{2}} = \frac{lA\rho }{m} = \frac{M}{m}\)

where M is the mass of the rod. The preceding equation can be written as

\(\gamma \tan \gamma = \mu\) (4.41)

where \(\gamma = wl/ c\) and \( \mu = M/ m\). Note that the frequency equation is a transcendental equation which has an infinite number of roots, and therefore, its solution defines an infinite number of natural frequencies. This equation can be expressed for each root as

\(\gamma_{j} \tan \gamma_{j} = \mu\) , j=1,2,3 (4.42)

where \(\gamma_{j} = w_{j}l/ c\), and the eigenfunction associated with the natural frequency \(w_{j}\) is

\(\phi _{j} = A_{1j}\sin \frac{w_{j}x}{c}\) (4.42)
