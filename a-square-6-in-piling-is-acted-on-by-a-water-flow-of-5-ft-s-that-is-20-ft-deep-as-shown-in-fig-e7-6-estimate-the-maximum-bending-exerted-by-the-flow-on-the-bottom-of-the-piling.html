Guest Question.

A square 6-in piling is acted on by a water flow of 5 ft/s that is 20 ft deep, as shown in Fig. E7.6. Estimate the maximum bending exerted by the flow on the bottom of the piling.

Admin
Solution

Assume seawater with ρ = 1.99 slugs/\(ft^3\) and kinematic viscosity \(\nu\) = 0.000011 \(ft^2\)/s. With a piling width of 0.5 ft, we have

\(Re_h = \frac{(5 ft/s)(0.5 ft)}{0.000011 ft^2/s} = 2.3 \times 10^5\)

This is the range where Table 7.2 applies. The worst case occurs when the flow strikes the flat side of the piling, \(C_D \approx 2.1\). The frontal area is A = Lh = (20 ft)(0.5 ft) = 10 \(ft^2\). The drag is estimated by

\(F = C_D (\frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 A) \approx 2.1(\frac{1}{2})(1.99 slugs/ft^3)(5 ft/s)^2 (10 ft^2) = 522 lbf\)

If the flow is uniform, the center of this force should be at approximately middepth. Therefore the bottom bending moment is

\(M_0 \approx \frac{FL}{2} = 522(10) = 5220 ft \cdot lbf\)

According to the flexure formula from strength of materials, the bending stress at the bottom would be

\(S = \frac{M_0y}{I} = \frac{(5220 ft \cdot lb)(0.25 ft)}{\frac{1}{12}(0.5 ft)^4} = 251,000 lbf/ft^2 = 1740 lbf/in^2\)

to be multiplied, of course, by the stress concentration factor due to the built-in end conditions.
