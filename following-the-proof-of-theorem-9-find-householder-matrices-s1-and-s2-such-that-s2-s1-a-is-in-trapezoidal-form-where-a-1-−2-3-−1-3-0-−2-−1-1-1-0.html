Guest Question.

Following the proof of Theorem 9, find Householder matrices \(S_{1} \text { and } S_{2}\) such that \(S_{2} S_{1} A\) is in trapezoidal form, where

\(A=\left[\begin{array}{rc}1 & -2 / 3 \\-1 & 3 \\0 & -2 \\-1 & 1 \\1 & 0\end{array}\right]\).

Admin
Solution

Following Algorithm 2 in Section 7.5, we define a vector u by

\(u =\left[\begin{array}{r}3 \\-1 \\0 \\-1 \\1\end{array}\right]\).

The first Householder matrix \(S_{1}\) is then \(S_{1}=I-b u u ^{T}\), where \(b=2 /\left( u ^{T} u \right)=1 / 6\).
We next calculate \(S_{1} A=\left[S_{1} A _{1}, S_{1} A _{2}\right]\), where \(S_{1} A _{i}= A _{i}-\gamma_{i} u , \gamma_{i}= u ^{T} A _{i} / 6\). The scalars \(\gamma_{i} \text { are } \gamma_{1}=1 \text { and } \gamma_{2}=-1\). Thus the matrix \(S_{1} A\) has columns \(A _{1}- u\) and \(A _{2}+ u\):

\(S_{1} A=\left[\begin{array}{rr}-2 & 7 / 3 \\0 & 2 \\0 & -2 \\0 & 0 \\0 & 1\end{array}\right]\).

We now define the second Householder matrix \(S_{2}\), where \(S_{2}\) is designed so that \(S_{2}\left(S_{1} A\right)\) has zeros in positions (3,2),(4,2), and (5,2).
Following Algorithm 2, define a vector v by

\(v =\left[\begin{array}{r}0 \\5 \\-2 \\0 \\1\end{array}\right]\)

and set \(S_{2}=I-b v v ^{T}, b=2 /\left( v ^{T} v \right)=1 / 15\). Forming \(S_{2}\left(S_{1} A\right)\), we obtain

\(S_{2} S_{1} A=\left[\begin{array}{rr}-2 & 7 / 3 \\0 & -3 \\0 & 0 \\0 & 0 \\0 & 0\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l}R \\O\end{array}\right] ; \quad R=\left[\begin{array}{rr}-2 & 7 / 3 \\0 & -3\end{array}\right]\).
