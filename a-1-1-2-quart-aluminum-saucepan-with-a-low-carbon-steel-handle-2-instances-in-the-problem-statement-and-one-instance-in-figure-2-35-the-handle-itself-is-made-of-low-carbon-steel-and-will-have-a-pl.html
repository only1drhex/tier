Guest Question.

A 1-1/2 quart aluminum saucepan, with a low carbon steel handle; 2 instances in the problem statement and one instance in Figure 2.35. The handle itself is made of low carbon steel, and will have a plastic grip attached to it that is comfortable to grasp. Before selecting a plastic, it is necessary to have information on the temperature of the aluminum handle. The aluminum handle can be considered as a rod 11 mm in diameter and 45 mm long. When being used over a stove burner, the ambient temperature is \(30^{\circ}C\), and the temperature at the base of the handle reaches \(100^{\circ}C\). For a convection coefficient, \(\bar{h_{c}}=8\, W/(m^{2}\cdot K)\) determine and graph the temperature profile. Determine also the heat transferred by the handle.

Admin
Solution

The rod will be considered as a fin attached to a wall of temperature 100°C.

Assumptions

1. The convection coefficient is constant.

2. The rod gains heat by conduction from the pan and loses heat only by convection to the surroundings. Radiation loss is neglected.

3. Material properties are constant.

4. One-dimensional conduction exists along the rod.

5. The system is at steady state.

a. Th e temperature profi le can be calculated with Equation 2.62

\(\frac {\theta}{\theta_{w}}= \frac {\cosh {[mL(1-z/L)]}}{\cosh {(mL)}}\) (2.6)

 

\(\frac{T-T_{\infty}}{T_{w}-T_{\infty}}= \frac {\cosh {[m(L-z)]}}{\cosh {(mL)}}\)

where \(m = \sqrt{\bar {h}_{c} P/kA}\) . We now evaluate each term. (Note that if \(\bar {h}_{c}\) was not provided, we would use an estimated value from Table 1.2.) With D = 11 mm, we find

 

\(P=\pi D= \pi (0.011) =0.035\) m

 

\(A= \frac {\pi D^2}{4} = \frac {pi}{4} (0.011)^2 = 0.000095 m^2\)

 

From Appendix Table B.2, for low-carbon (1C) steel, we know that k = 43 W/(m · K).

Proceeding

\(m= \sqrt {8(0.035)/[43(0.000095)]}= 8.28\) / m

Th e rod length is given as

L=0.045 m

Substituting into the temperature equation, we get

\(\frac{T-30}{100-30}= \frac {\cosh {[8.28(0.045-z)]}}{\cosh {[8.28(0.045)]}}\)

 

\(T=30+65.4 \cosh {[8.28(0.045-z)]}\)

A graph of this equation is provided in Figure 2.36.

b. The heat transferred is calculated with Equation 2.63.

\(\frac {q_{z} }{kAm\theta_{w}}= \tanh {(mL)}\) (2.63)

 

\(q_{z} = kAm (T_{w}-T_{\infty}) \tanh {(mL)}\)

 

=\(43 (0.000095)(8.23)(100-30) \tanh {[(8.23)(0.045)]}\)

 

\(q_{z} = 0.833\) W

 

The heat transferred could have been determined by using the graph of \(q_{z}/kAmθ_{w}\) versus mL from Figure 2.32.

c. The fin efficiency likewise can be found either by using Figure 2.32 or by equation. From Figure 2.32, with mL = 8.23(0.0045) = 0.370, we get

\(\eta_{f} = 0.957\)

Thus, the actual heat transferred is 0.957 times what it would be if the fin were entirely at 200°F.

d. The effectiveness can be determined with Equation 2.67 as

\(\eta_{f}= \sqrt {\frac {kP}{\bar {h}_{c}A}} \tanh (mL) = \sqrt {\frac {43 (0.0346)}{8 (0.000095)}} \tanh {(0.933)}\)

Solving

\(\eta_{f} = 15.7\)

Thus, the heat flux through an 11 mm diameter spot on the wall (before adding the fin) has been increased by a factor of 15.7 by adding the fin.

e. The rod is too hot to touch. An insulating material that will not melt at 100°C could be added as a covering over the handle.
