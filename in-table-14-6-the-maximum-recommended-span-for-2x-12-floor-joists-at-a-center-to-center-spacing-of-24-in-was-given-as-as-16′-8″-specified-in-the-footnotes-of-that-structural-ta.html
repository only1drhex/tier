Guest Question.

In Table 14.6, the maximum recommended span for 2\(\times\) 12 floor joists at a center-to-center spacing of 24 in. was given as As 16′-8″. specified in the footnotes of that structural table, the joists are assumed to have a modulus of elasticity of 1.4 \(\times 10^6\) psi, support a floor whose distributed load is 40 psf, and have a maximum deflection under load of 1/360 of their span. Using superposition, find the actual midpoint deflection of these joists. (Note that the stress developed in such joists was discussed in .)

TABLE 14.6 Allowable Floor Joist Spans (40 psf Live Load)†

Nominal Joist Size (in.) Center-to-Center Joist Spacing (in.)
12 16 24
2 \(\times \) 6 10’-2” 9’-3” 8’-2”
2 \(\times \)8 13’-6” 12’-2” 10’-8”
2 \(\times \) 10 17’-2” 15’-6” 13’-6”
2 \(\times \) 12 21’-0” 19’-0” 16’-8”
†For modulus of elasticity of 1,400,000 psi; maximum deflection equal to span divided by 360; maximum bending stress of 1350 psi.
Admin
Solution

Maximum deflection for a simply supported beam carrying a distributed load is given in of the Appendix as \( \delta _{max}=\frac{5 wL^4}{384EI}\)

where w = 100 lb/ft (see )

L = 16′-8″ = 16.67 ft

E = 1.4 \(\times 10^6\) psi

I = 178 \(in.^4\) (see of the Appendix)

Then,

\( \delta _{max}=\frac{5(100 lb/ft)(16.67 ft)^4}{384(1.4 \times 10^6 psi)(178 in.^4)} \times \frac{1728 in.^3}{1 ft^3} \)

= 0.697 in.

However, the joist span is L=16′-8″ = 200 in., so

\( \frac{L}{360} =\frac{200 in.}{360}\) =0.556 in.

Note that the actual deflection exceeds L/360 by more than 25 percent. Is the tabulated value wrong? Perhaps not, because no assumed value for dead load is specified in the footnotes of the table. If we neglect that load (10 psf), then w = 80 lb/ft, and the maximum deflection becomes 0.558 in. These apparent contradictions between tabulated and computed values are not uncommon and emphasize the need (and the ability) to spot-check published data.
