Guest Question.

Suppose you were asked for the mass of Mars. How could you determine this value?

Admin
Solution

Kepler’s third law is valid for any system of objects in orbit around an object with a large mass. Mars has two moons, Phobos and Deimos. If we rewrite Equation 13.8 for these moons of Mars, we have

\( T^{2}=\left(\frac{4 \pi^{2}}{G M_{S}}\right) a^{3}=K_{S} a^{3} \) (13.8).

\( T^{2}=\left(\frac{4 \pi^{2}}{G M_{M}}\right) a^{3} \).

\( \text { where } M_{M} \text { is the mass of Mars. Solving for this mass, } \) \( M_{M}=\left(\frac{4 \pi^{2}}{G}\right) \frac{a^{3}}{T^{2}}=\left(\frac{4 \pi^{2}}{6.67 \times 10^{-11} N \cdot m ^{2} / kg ^{2}}\right) \frac{a^{3}}{T^{2}} \)

\( =\left(5.92 \times 10^{11} kg \cdot s ^{2} / m ^{3}\right) \frac{a^{3}}{T^{2}} \).

Phobos has an orbital period of 0.32 days and an almost circular orbit of radius 9 380 km. The orbit of Deimos is even more circular, with a radius of 23 460 km and an orbital period of 1.26 days. Let us calculate the mass of Mars using each of these sets of data:
Phobos:

\( M_{M}=\left(5.92 \times 10^{11} kg \cdot s ^{2} / m ^{3}\right) \)

\( \times \frac{\left(9.380 \times 10^{6} m \right)^{3}}{(0.32 d )^{2}}\left(\frac{1 d }{86400 s }\right)^{2}=6.39 \times 10^{23} kg \).

Deimos:

\( M_{M}=\left(5.92 \times 10^{11} kg \cdot s ^{2} / m ^{3}\right) \).

\( \times \frac{\left(2.346 \times 10^{7} m \right)^{3}}{(1.26 d )^{2}}\left(\frac{1 d }{86400 s }\right)^{2}=6.45 \times 10^{23} kg \).

These two calculations are within 1% of each other and both are within 0.5% of the value of the mass of Mars given in Table 13.2.
