Guest Question.

A small copper heat sink with dimensions \(W_{1} = W_{2} = 40 mm, L_{b} = 1.0 mm, S = 1.6 mm, t = 0.8 mm\), and \(L_{f}\) = 5 mm has a uniform maximum temperature of \( T_h = 50°C \) on its bottom surface. An insulating cap is placed on the top of the heat sink. Water is used as the coolant, entering the heat sink at \( T_{m,i} \)= 30°C and \(u_{m}\) = 1.75 m/s, providing an average heat transfer coefficient of \(\bar{h} = 7590 W/m^{2} .K\). Determine the heat transfer rate from the hot surface to the water.

Admin
Solution

Known: Dimensions of copper heat sink, maximum heat sink temperature and water inlet temperature, mean velocity, and average heat transfer coefficient.

Find: Heat transfer rate.

Assumptions:
1. Steady-state conditions.
2. Adiabatic tips on heat sink fins.
3. Adiabatic heat sink sides, front and back surfaces.
4. Isothermal bottom surface temperature \(T_{h}\).
5. Constant properties.
6. Negligible axial conduction in the heat sink.

Properties: , copper \((T = 300 K): k_{Cu} = 401 W/m. K\). , water (assume \( \bar{T} ≈ 310 K ): ρ = 993 kg/m^{3}, c_{p} = 4178 J/kg . K.\)

Analysis: Since the heat sink’s bottom surface temperature is spatially uniform, and axial conduction is neglected, the heat sink’s thermal behavior corresponds to a single stream heat exchanger as shown in Figure 11.9a. Specifically, the bottom surface tempera-ture does not vary in the x-direction, but the water temperature increases as it flows through the heat sink. Hence, we may use Equation 11.22 to determine the heat transfer rate,

\(q = ε C_{min} (T_{h,i} – T_{c,i})\) (1)

where \(C_{min} = C_{c} = \dot{m} c_{p,c}\) and \(C_{r}\) → 0. From Section 11.2 and the discussion surrounding Equations 8.45b\( \frac{\Delta T_{o}}{\Delta T_{i}} = \frac{T_{\infty } – T_{m,o}}{T_{\infty } – T_{m,i}} = \exp \left(\frac{-1}{\dot{m} c_{p} R_{tot} } \right) \) and 8.46b \( q = \frac{\Delta T_{lm}}{R_{tot}} \), we note that the term 1/UA used in the definition of NTU corre-sponds to the overall thermal resistance between the two fluid streams of a heat exchanger. In this example, \(UA = 1/R_{tot}\) where \(R_{tot}\) is the total thermal resistance between the bottom of the heat sink and the fluid. Therefore, Equation 11.35a\(ε = 1 – exp (-NTU)\) may be written as

\(ε = 1 – exp (-NTU) = 1 – exp \left(-\frac{UA}{C_{min}} \right) = 1 – exp \left(-\frac{1}{R_{tot} C_{min}} \right) \) (2)

Once \(C_{min}\) and \(R_{tot}\) are evaluated, the effectiveness can be found from Equation 2, and the heat rate may be determined from Equation 1.

The number of fins is equal to the number of channels and is \(N = W_{1}/S = 40 mm/1.6 mm = 25\). The minimum heat capacity rate is

\( C_{min} = \dot{m}_{C} c_{p,c} = N ρ u_{m} L_{f} (S – t)c_{p} \)

 

\(= 25 × 993 kg/m^{3}× 1.75 m/s × 0.005 m× (0.0016 m – 0.0008 m) × 4178 J/kg . K\)

 

= 726 W/K

The total thermal resistance is calculated in Comment 4 and is \(R_{tot} = 17.8 × 10^{-3} K/W\). From Equation 2,

\(ε = 1 – exp \left(-\frac{1}{R_{tot} C_{min}} \right) =1 – exp \left(-\frac{1}{17.8 × 10^{-3} K/W ×726 W/K} \right) = 0.0745 \)

and from Equation 1,

\( q = C_{min}(T_{h} – T_{c,i}) = 0.0745 × 726 W/K × (50°C – 30°C) = 1080 W\)

Comments:
1. If the water temperature is assumed to be constant as it flows through the heat sink, the heat rate is \(q = (T_{h} – T_{c,i})/R_{tot} = (50°C – 30°C)/17.8 × 10^{-3} K/W = 1120 W\). The assump-tion of constant water temperature leads to an overestimate of the actual heat rate.
2. The outlet temperature of the water is \(T_{c,o} = T_{c,i} + q/C_{min} = 30°C + (1080 W)/ (726 W/K) = 31.5°C.\)
3. From Equations 11.15 \(\Delta T_{lm} = \frac{\Delta T_{2} -\Delta T_{1} }{\ln (\Delta T_{2}/\Delta T_{1} )} = \frac{\Delta T_{1} -\Delta T_{2} }{\ln (\Delta T_{1}/\Delta T_{2} )} \) and 11.16 \(\left[\begin{matrix} \Delta T_{1} \equiv T_{h,1} – T_{c,1} = T_{h,i} – T_{c,i} \\ \Delta T_{2} \equiv T_{h,2} – T_{c,2} = T_{h,o} – T_{c,o} \end{matrix} \right]\) , \(Δ T_{lm} = [(T_{b} – T_{c,i}) – (T_{b} – T_{c,o})]/ln[(T_{b} – T_{c,i})/ (T_{b} – T_{c,o})] = [31.5°C – 30°C]/ln[(50°C – 30°C)/(50°C – 31.5°C)] = 19.2°C\) and \(q = Δ T_{lm}/ R_{tot} = 19.2°C/17.8 × 10^{-3} K/W = 1080 W\). Therefore, the appropriate mean temperature difference shown in the following thermal circuit, \(Δ T_{m}\), is the log mean temperature difference [11]. As such, this problem could have been solved using an LMTD approach, but an iterative solution would have been required.
4. The total thermal resistance corresponds to the following thermal circuit.

where \(Δ T_{m}\) is the appropriate mean temperature difference between the bottom of the heat sink base and the fluid. The thermal resistance of the base is

\( R_{t,base} = L_{b}/(k_{Cu} W_{1} W_{2}) = (0.001 m)/(401 W/m . K × 0.040 m × 0.040 m)\)

 

\( = 1.56 × 10^{-3}K/W\)

The parallel resistances in the thermal circuit represent the fins and unfinned portion of the base. The combination of these two resistances is the overall thermal resistance of the fin array, as given by Equation 3.108\(R_{tot} = \frac{\theta _{b}}{q_{t}} = \frac{1}{\eta _{o} h A_{t}} \) with Equation 11.3\(\eta _{o} = 1 – \frac{A_{f}}{A} (1-\eta _{f})\):

\( R_{t,o} = \frac{1}{\eta _{o} \bar{h} A } = \frac{1}{ \bar{h} \left[A – A_{f}(1 – \eta _{f}) \right] } \)

In this expression, \(A_{f}\) is the surface area of all the fins and \(A = A_{f} + A_{b}\), where \(A_{b}\) is the area of the unfinned portion of the base. Thus

\(A_{f} = 2 L_{f} W_{2} N = 2 × 0.005 m × 0.040 m× 25 = 0.01 m^{2}\)

and

\(A = A_{f} + (W_{1} – N_{t})W_{2} = 0.01 m^{2}+ (0.040 m – 25 × 0.0008 m) ×0.040 m \)

 

\( = 0.0108 m^{2}\)

The quantity \(\eta _{f}\) is the efficiency of a single fin, given by Equation 11.4\( \eta _{f} = \frac{tanh (mL)}{mL} \). We first calculate

\( m L_{f} = \sqrt{2 \bar{h}/k_{Cu}t }L_{f}\)

 

\( = \sqrt{2 × 7590 W/m^{2} . K/(401 W/m . K × 0.0008 m)} × 0.005 m = 1.09\)

Then

\( \eta _{f} = \frac{tanh (mL_{f})}{mL_{f}} = \frac{tanh (1.09)}{1.09} = 0.732\)

 

\( R_{t,o}= \frac{1}{ \bar{h} \left[A – A_{f}(1 – \eta _{f}) \right] } \)

 

\( = \frac{1}{ 7590 W/m^{2} . K[0.0108 m^{2} – 0.01 m^{2} (1 – 0.732)} = 0.0162 K/W\)

Therefore, the total thermal resistance is

\(R_{tot} = R_{t,base} + R_{t,o} = 1.56 × 10^{-3}K/W + 0.0162 K/W = 17.8 × 10^{-3} K/W\)
