Guest Question.

The outer surface of a vertical tube, which is 1 m long and has an outer diameter of 80 mm, is exposed to saturated steam at atmospheric pressure and is maintained at 50°C by the flow of cool water through the tube. What is the rate of heat transfer to the coolant, and what is the rate at which steam is condensed at the surface?

Admin
Solution

Known: Dimensions and temperature of a vertical tube experiencing condensation of steam at its outer surface.

Find: Heat transfer and condensation rates.

Assumptions:
1. The condensate film thickness is small relative to the cylinder diameter.
2. Negligible concentration of noncondensable gases in the steam.

Properties: , saturated vapor \((p = 1.0133 bars): T_{sat} = 100°C, ρ_{v} = 1/v_{g} = 0.596 kg/m^{3}, h_{fg} = 2257 kJ/kg\). Table A.6, saturated liquid \((T_{f} = 75°C): ρ_{l} = 1/v_{f} = 975 kg/m^{3},μ_{l} = 375 × 10^{-6} N .s/m^{2} , k_{l} = 0.668 W/m. K, c_{p,l} = 4193 J/kg . K, \nu_{l} = μ_{l} /ρ_{l} = 385 × 10^{-9} m^{2}/s.\)

Analysis: Since we assume the film thickness is small relative to the cylinder diameter, we may use the correlations of Sections 10.7 and 10.8. With

\( Ja = \frac{c_{p,l} (T_{sat} – T_{s})}{h_{fg}} = \frac{4193 J/kg . K(100 – 50) K}{2257 × 10^{3} J/kg} = 0.0929\)

it follows that

\(h^{\prime}_{fg} = h_{fg}(1 + 0.68 Ja) = 2257 kJ/kg (1.0632) = 2400 kJ/kg\)

From Equation 10.42,

\(P = \frac{k_{l} L (T_{sat} – T_{s})}{μ_{l} h^{\prime}_{fg} (\nu^{2}_{l} /g)^{1/3}} \) \(= \frac{0.668 W/m . K × 1 m ×(100 – 50) K}{375 × 10^{-6}N . s/m^{2} × 2.4 × 10^{6} J/kg \left[\frac{ (385 \times 10^{-9} m^{2}/s)^{2}}{9.8 m/s^{2}} \right]^{1/3} } =1501 \)

Therefore, Equation 10.44 applies:

\(\overset{-}{Nu} _{L} = \frac{1}{P} (0.68 P + 0.89)^{0.82} = \frac{1}{1501} (0.68 × 1501 + 0.89)^{0.82} = 0.20\)

Then

\(\overset{-}{h}_{L} = \frac{\overset{-}{Nu}_{L}k_{l} }{(\nu ^{2}_{l}/g)^{1/3}} = \frac{0.20 × 0.668 W/m . K}{\left[\frac{ (385 \times 10^{-9} m^{2}/s)^{2}}{9.8 m/s^{2}} \right]^{1/3}} = 5300 W/m^{2} . K\)

and from Equations 10.33 and 10.34

\(q= \overset{-}{h}_{L} (π DL)(T_{sat} – T_{s}) = 5300 W/m^{2} . K ×π ×0.08 m × 1 m (100 – 50) K= 66.6 kW\)

 

\(\dot{m} = \frac{q }{h^{\prime}_{fg}} = \frac{66.6 × 10^{3}W}{2.4 × 10^{6}J/kg} = 0.0276 kg/s\)

Note that using Equation 10.26, with the corrected latent heat, the film thickness at the bottom of the tube δ(L) for the wave-free laminar assumption is

\(\delta (L) = \left[\frac{4k_{l} \mu _{l} (T_{sat} – T_{s})L}{g \rho _{l}(\rho _{l} – \rho _{v}) h^{\prime}_{fg} } \right]^{1/4}\)

 

\(\delta (L) = \left[\frac{4 × 0.668 W/m . K × 375 × 10^{-6}kg/s . m (100 – 50) K × 1 m}{9.8 m/s^{2} × 975 kg /m^{3} (975 – 0.596) kg/m^{3} × 2.4 × 10^{6}J/kg} \right]^{1/4} \)

 

\(\delta (L) = 2.18 × 10^{-4} m = 0.218 mm\)

Hence δ(L) ≪ (D/2), and use of the vertical plate correlation for a vertical cylinder is justified.

Comments:
1. The condensation heat and mass rates may be increased by increasing the length of the tube. For 1 ≤ L ≤ 2 m, the calculations yield the variations shown below, for which 1000 ≤\( Re_{δ}\) ≤ 2330 or 1500 ≤ P ≤ 3010. The foregoing calculations were performed by using the wavy-laminar correlation, Equation 10.44, for P ≤ 2530 (L ≤ 1.68 m) and Equation 10.45 \(\overset{-}{Nu}_{L} = \frac{\bar{h}_{L} (\nu _{l}^{2}/g)^{1/3} }{k_{l}} = \frac{1}{P} [(0.024P – 53)Pr^{1/2}_{l} + 89]^{4/3}\), for P > 2530 (L > 1.68 m). Note, however, that the correlations do not provide equivalent results at P = 2530. In particular, Equation 10.45 \(\overset{-}{Nu}_{L} = \frac{\bar{h}_{L} (\nu _{l}^{2}/g)^{1/3} }{k_{l}} = \frac{1}{P} [(0.024P – 53)Pr^{1/2}_{l} + 89]^{4/3}\) is a func-tion of Pr, whereas Equation 10.44 \(\overset{-}{Nu} _{L} = \frac{1}{P} (0.68 P + 0.89)^{0.82} \) is not.

2. If a noncondensable gas such as air is mixed with the steam, heat transfer and conden-sation rates can be reduced significantly. This is due to multiple effects [36]. For example, q and can drop by 65% if the steam contains only 1% air by weight. Steam condensers that operate at subatmospheric pressure, such as those utilized in Rankine cycles, must be meticulously designed to prevent infiltration of air.
