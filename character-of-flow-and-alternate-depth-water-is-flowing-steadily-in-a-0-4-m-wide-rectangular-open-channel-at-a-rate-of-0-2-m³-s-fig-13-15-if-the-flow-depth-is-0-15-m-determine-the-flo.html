Character of Flow and Alternate Depth

Water is flowing steadily in a 0.4-m-wide rectangular open channel at a rate of 0.2 m³/s (Fig. 13–15). If the flow depth is 0.15 m, determine the flow velocity and if the flow is subcritical or supercritical. Also determine the alternate flow depth if the character of flow were to change.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION Water flow in a rectangular open channel is considered. The character of flow, the flow velocity, and the alternate depth are to be determined.

Assumptions The specific energy is constant.

Analysis The average flow velocity is determined from

 

\(V=\frac{\dot{V}}{A_{c}}=\frac{\dot{V}}{y b}=\frac{0.2 m ^{3} / s }{(0.15 m )(0.4 m )}=3.33 m / s\)

 

The critical depth for this flow is

 

\(y_{c}=\left(\frac{ V ^{2}}{g b^{2}}\right)^{1 / 3}=\left(\frac{\left(0.2 m ^{3} / s \right)^{2}}{\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)(0.4 m )^{2}}\right)^{1 / 3}=0.294 m\)

 

Therefore, the flow is supercritical since the actual flow depth is y = 0.15 m, and \(y<y_{c}\). Another way to determine the character of flow is to calculate the Froude number,

 

\(Fr =\frac{V}{\sqrt{g y}}=\frac{3.33 m / s }{\sqrt{\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)(0.15 m )}}=2.75\)

 

Again the flow is supercritical since Fr > 1. The specific energy for the given conditions is

 

\(E_{s 1}=y_{1}+\frac{\dot{V}^{2}}{2 g b^{2} y_{1}^{2}}=(0.15 m )+\frac{\left(0.2 m ^{3} / s \right)^{2}}{2\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)(0.4 m )^{2}(0.15 m )^{2}}=0.7163 m\)

 

Then the alternate depth is determined from \(E_{s 1}=E_{s 2} \text { to be }\)

 

\(E_{s 2}=y_{2}+\frac{\dot{V}^{2}}{2 g b^{2} y_{2}^{2}} \rightarrow \quad 0.7163 m =y_{2}+\frac{\left(0.2 m ^{3} / s \right)^{2}}{2\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)(0.4 m )^{2} y_{2}^{2}}\)

 

Solving for \(y_{2}\) gives the alternate depth to be \(y_{2}\) = 0.69 m. Therefore, if the character of flow were to change from supercritical to subcritical while holding the specific energy constant, the flow depth would rise from 0.15 to 0.69 m.

Discussion Note that if the water underwent a hydraulic jump at constant specific energy (the frictional losses being equal to the drop in elevation), the flow depth would rise to 0.69 m, assuming of course that the side walls of the channel are high enough.
