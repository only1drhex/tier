Guest Question.

Find the normalized eigenvectors of the matrix

\(A=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 0 & 1 \\1 & -1 & 0 \\3 & 0 & 4\end{array}\right]\)
Admin
Solution

The first step is to find the eigenvalues of the matrix. We begin by deriving the characteristic polynomial. We have

 

\(\begin{aligned}\operatorname{det}|A-\lambda I| &=\operatorname{det}\left|\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 0 & 1 \\1 & -1 & 0 \\3 & 0 & 4\end{array}\right]-\lambda\left[\begin{array}{lll}1 & 0 & 0 \\0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\right| \\&=\operatorname{det}\left|\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 0 & 1 \\1 & -1 & 0 \\3 & 0 & 4\end{array}\right]-\left[\begin{array}{lll}\lambda & 0 & 0 \\0 & \lambda & 0 \\0 & 0 & \lambda\end{array}\right]\right|\end{aligned}\)

 

This gives

 

\(\begin{aligned}\operatorname{det}\left|\begin{array}{ccc}2-\lambda & 0 & 1 \\1 & -1-\lambda & 0 \\3 & 0 & 4-\lambda\end{array}\right| &=(2-\lambda) \operatorname{det}\left|\begin{array}{cc}-1-\lambda & 0 \\0 & 4-\lambda\end{array}\right|+\operatorname{det}\left|\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1-\lambda \\3 & 0\end{array}\right| \\&=(2-\lambda)[(-1-\lambda)(4-\lambda)]-(3)(-1-\lambda) \\&=(-1-\lambda)[(2-\lambda)(4-\lambda)-3] \\&=(-1-\lambda)\left[\lambda^{2}-6 \lambda+5\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

We set this equal to zero to obtain the characteristic equation

 

\(\begin{aligned}(-1-\lambda)\left[\lambda^{2}-6 \lambda+5\right] &=0 \\\Rightarrow 1+\lambda=0 \text { or } \lambda &=-1 \\\lambda^{2}-6 \lambda+5 &=(\lambda-5)(\lambda-1)=0 \text { or } \lambda=5, \lambda=1\end{aligned}\)

 

Sowe have three distinct eigenvalues{ âˆ’ 1, 5, 1}.We compute each eigenvector in turn. Starting with \(\lambda_{1}=-1\) the eigenvector equation is

 

\(A v_{1}=-v_{1}\)

 

We set the eigenvector equal to

 

\(v_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{l}x \\y \\z\end{array}\right]\)

 

where x, y, z are three unknowns. Applying the matrix A gives us

 

\(A v_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 0 & 1 \\1 & -1 & 0 \\3 & 0 & 4\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l}x \\y \\z\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}2 x+z \\x-y \\3 x+4 z\end{array}\right]\)

 

Setting this equal to \(-v_{1}\) gives three equations

 

\(\begin{array}{r}2 x+z=-x \\x-y=-y \\3 x+4 z=-z\end{array}\)

 

The second equation immediately tells us that x = 0. (We add y to both
sides):

 

\(x-y=-y, \Rightarrow x=0\)

 

Using x = 0 in the third equation, we have

 

\(\begin{array}{c}3 x+4 z=-z, x=0 \\\Rightarrow 4 z=-z\end{array}\)

 

Which can be true only if z= 0 as well. This leaves us with

 

\(v_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{l}0 \\y \\0\end{array}\right]\)

 

Under general conditions, y can be any value we choose. However, in this case we require that the eigenvectors be normalized. Therefore we must have

 

\(v_{1}^{\dagger} v_{1}=1\)

 

We compute this product. We consider the most general case; therefore, we allow y to be a complex number. The Hermitian conjugate of the eigenvector is

 

\(v_{1}^{\dagger}=\left[\begin{array}{lll}0 & y^{*} & 0\end{array}\right]\)

 

Therefore the product is

 

\(v_{1}^{\dagger} v_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{lll}0 & y^{*} & 0\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l}0 \\y \\0\end{array}\right]=0+y^{*} y+0=|y|^{2}\)

 

Setting this equal to unity, we find that

 

\(\begin{array}{r}|y|^{2}=1 \\\Rightarrow y=1\end{array}\)

 

up to an undetermined phase, which we are free to discard. Therefore the first eigenvector is

 

\(v_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{l}0 \\1 \\0\end{array}\right]\)

 

Now we consider the second eigenvalue, \(\lambda_{2}=5\). This gives

 

\(\begin{aligned}A v_{2} &=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 & 0 & 1 \\1 & -1 & 0 \\3 & 0 & 4\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l}x \\y \\z\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}2 x+z \\x-y \\3 x+4 z\end{array}\right]=5 v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{c}5 x \\5 y \\5 z\end{array}\right] \\\Rightarrow 2 x+z &=5 x \\x-y &=5 y \\3 x+4 z &=5 z\end{aligned}\)

 

From these equations we obtain

 

\(\begin{array}{l}z=3 x \\x=6 y\end{array}\)

 

and so we can write the eigenvector as

 

\(v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{l}x \\y \\z\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}6 y \\y \\5 x\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}6 y \\y \\30 y\end{array}\right]\)

 

The Hermitian conjugate of the vector is

 

\(v_{2}^{\dagger}=\left[\begin{array}{lll}6 y^{*} & y^{*} & 30 y^{*}\end{array}\right]\)

 

Normalizing we find

 

\(v_{2}^{\dagger} v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{lll}6 y^{*} & y^{*} & 30 y^{*}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c}6 y \\y \\30 y\end{array}\right]=36|y|^{2}+|y|^{2}+900|y|^{2}=937|y|^{2}\)

 

\(\begin{array}{l}v_{2}^{\dagger} v_{2}=1 \\\Rightarrow|y|^{2}=\frac{1}{937} \text { or } y=\frac{1}{\sqrt{937}}\end{array}\)

 

This gives

 

\(v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{c}6 y \\y \\30 y\end{array}\right]=\frac{1}{\sqrt{937}}\left[\begin{array}{c}6 \\1 \\30\end{array}\right]\)

 

For the third eigenvalue, we have

 

\(A v_{3}=v_{3}\)

 

Which gives three equations

 

\(\begin{array}{r}2 x+z=x \\x-y=y \\3 x+4 z=z\end{array}\)

 

Therefore we obtain from the first equation

 

\(z=-x\)

 

and from the second equation

 

\(x=2 y\)

 

So we can write the eigenvector as

 

\(v_{3}=\left[\begin{array}{c}2 y \\y \\-2 y\end{array}\right]\)

 

Normalizing, we get

 

\(\begin{array}{l}1=v_{3}^{\dagger} v_{3}=\left[\begin{array}{lll}2 y^{*} & y^{*} & -2 y^{*}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c}2 y \\y \\-2 y\end{array}\right]=4|y|^{2}+|y|^{2}+4|y|^{2}=9|y|^{2} \\\Rightarrow|y|^{2}=\frac{1}{9} \text { or } y=\frac{1}{3}\end{array}\)

 

This allows us to write the third eigenvector as

 

\(v_{3}=\left[\begin{array}{c}2 y \\y \\-2 y\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}\frac{2}{3} \\\frac{1}{3} \\-\frac{2}{3}\end{array}\right]=\frac{1}{3}\left[\begin{array}{c}2 \\1 \\-2\end{array}\right]\)
