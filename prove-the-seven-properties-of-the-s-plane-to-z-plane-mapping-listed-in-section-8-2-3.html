Guest Question.

Prove the seven properties of the s-plane-to-z-plane mapping listed in Section 8.2.3.

Admin
Solution

(a) The stability boundary in s-plane is :

s = jω , for all ω between \([-\infty, \infty]\)

By \(z=e^{s T}\), this boundary is mapped to :

\(z=e^{j \omega T}=\cos \omega T+j \sin \omega T\)

 

\(\Longrightarrow|z|=|\cos \omega T+j \sin \omega T|\)

Thus, the unit circle in z-plane represents the stability boundary.

(b) In the small vicinity around s = 0 in the s-plane,

\(s=-\sigma \pm j \omega_{d}\)

where \(\sigma \ll \omega_{s}=\frac{2 \pi}{T} \text { and } \omega_{d} \ll \omega_{s}=\frac{2 \pi}{T}\).

By \(z=e^{s T}\), corresponding locations relative to 1 in the z-plane are :

\(z-1=e^{\left(-\sigma \pm j \omega_{d}\right) T}-1\)

 

\(=e^{-\sigma T}\left(\cos \omega_{d} T \pm j \sin \omega_{d} T\right)-1\)

 

\(\cong\left\{1+\frac{(-\sigma T)}{1 !}+\frac{(-\sigma T)^{2}}{2 !}+\ldots\right\}\left(1 \pm j \omega_{d} T\right)-1\)

 

\(=1-\sigma T \pm j \omega_{d} T \mp j \sigma T \omega_{d} T-1\)

 

\(\cong-\sigma T \pm j \omega_{d} T\)

Thus, is approximately mapped to \(z-1=-\sigma T \pm j \omega_{d} T\). The small vicinity around z = +1 in the z-plane is identical to the vicinity around s = 0 in the s-plane by a factor of \(T=\frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}\).

(c) An arbitrary location in the s-plane is represented by :

\(s=-\zeta \omega_{n}+j \omega_{n} \sqrt{1-\zeta^{2}}\) \(=x_{s}\left(\omega_{n}\right)+j y_{s}\left(\omega_{n}\right)\)

where \(\omega_{n}\) is in rad/sec and is nondimensional. Thus, the s-plane locations give response information in terms of frequency. By \( z=e^{s T}=e^{s \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}}\), the corresponding location in the z-plane is :

\(z=e^{-2 \pi \frac{\omega_{n}}{\omega_{s}}} \cos \left(2 \pi \sqrt{1-\zeta^{2}} \frac{\omega_{n}}{\omega_{s}}\right)+j e^{-2 \pi \frac{\omega_{n}}{\omega_{s}}} \sin \left(2 \pi \sqrt{1-\zeta^{2}} \frac{\omega_{n}}{\omega_{s}}\right)\) \(=x_{z}\left(\frac{\omega_{n}}{\omega_{s}}\right)+j y_{z}\left(\frac{\omega_{n}}{\omega_{s}}\right)\)

Since \(\frac{\omega_{n}}{\omega_{s}}\) is nondimensional, the z-plane locations give response information normalized to the sample rate.

(d) Locations in the s-plane, \(s=-\sigma \pm j \omega_{d}\), are mapped to z-plane locations :

\(z=e^{-\sigma T}\left(\cos \omega_{d} T \pm j \sin \omega_{d} T\right)\)

If z is on the negative real axis, we need :

\(\cos \omega_{d} T<0, \sin \omega_{d} T=0\)

 

\(\Longrightarrow \omega_{d} T=2 \pi n+\pi, n=0,1,2,\ldots \)

 

\(\Longrightarrow \omega_{d}=(2 n+1) \frac{\omega_{s}}{2}, n=0,1,2, \ldots\)

Indeed, if \(\omega_{d}=(2 m+1) \frac{\omega_{s}}{2}, n=0,1,2, \ldots\),

\(z=-e^{-\sigma T} \Longrightarrow\) negative real axis

Thus, the negative real z-axis represents a horizontal line with a damped frequency:

\(\omega_{d}=(2 n+1) \frac{\omega_{s}}{2}, n=0,1,2, \ldots \)

(e) An arbitrary vertical line in the left half of the s-plane is represented by :

\(s=-\sigma \pm j \omega_{d}, \sigma>0\), for all \(\omega_{d}\) between \([-\infty, \infty]\)

By \(z=e^{s T}\), the vertical line is mapped to :

\(z=-e^{-\sigma T} e^{\pm j \omega_{d} T}=-e^{-\sigma T}\left(\cos \omega_{d} T \pm j \sin \omega_{d} T\right)\)

 

\(\Longrightarrow|z|=r=\left|-e^{-\sigma T}\right|=\text {constant }<1\)

 

\(\angle z=0 \longrightarrow 2 \pi \text { as } \omega_{d}=2 n \pi \longrightarrow(2 n+1) \pi\)

Thus, vertical lines in the left half of the s-plane map into circles within the unit circle of the z-plane.

(f) An arbitrary horizontal line in the s-plane is represented by :

\(s=-\sigma \pm j \omega_{d}, \text { for } \sigma \text { between }[-\infty, \infty], \text { at a given } \omega_{d}\)

By \(z=e^{s T}\), the horizontal line is mapped to :

\(z=-e^{-\sigma T} e^{\pm j \omega_{d} T}=-e^{-\sigma T}\left(\cos \omega_{d} T \pm j \sin \omega_{d} T\right)\)

\(\Longrightarrow \angle z=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{e^{-\sigma T} \sin \omega_{d} T}{e^{-\sigma T} \cos \omega_{d} T}\right)=\omega_{d} T=\) constant

\(|z|=r=0 \longrightarrow \infty \text { as } \sigma=\infty \longrightarrow-\infty\)

Thus, a horizontal line in the s-plane maps into radial lines in the z-plane.

(g) Let s-plane locations \(s_{1}\) and \(s_{2}\) be :

\(s_{1}=-\sigma \pm j \omega_{d}\)

 

\(s_{2}=-\sigma \pm j\left(\omega_{d}+m \omega_{s}\right), \quad m=1,2,3, \ldots\)

where \(\omega_{d}\) is between \(\left[-\frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}, \frac{\omega_{s}}{2}\right]\), which is called the ”primary strip”.

By \(z=e^{s T}=e^{s \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}}\), these s-plane locations are mapped to z-plane locations :

\(z_{1}=e^{-\sigma \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}}\left(\cos\omega_{d} \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}+j \sin \omega_{d} \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}\right)\)

 

\(z_{2}=e^{-\sigma \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}}\left\{\cos \left(\omega_{d}+2 m \frac{\omega_{s}}{2}\right) \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}+j \sin \left(\omega_{d}+2 m \frac{\omega_{s}}{2}\right) \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}\right\}\)

 

\(=e^{-\sigma \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}}\left\{\cos \left(\omega_{d} \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}\right) \cos 2 m \pi-\sin \left(\omega_{d} \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}\right) \sin 2 m \pi+j \sin \left(\omega_{d} \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}\right) \cos 2 m \pi+\ldots\right\}\)

 

\(=e^{-\sigma \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}}\left\{\cos \left(\omega_{d} \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}\right)+j \sin \left(\omega_{d} \frac{2 \pi}{\omega_{s}}\right)\right\}\) \(=z_{1}\)

Thus, frequencies greater than \(\frac{\omega_{s}}{2}\) appear in the z-plane on top of corresponding lower frequencies. Physically, this means that frequencies sampled faster than \(\frac{\omega_{s}}{2}\) will appear in the samples to be at a much lower frequency. This is called ”aliasing”.
