Velocity in a Flow Composed of Three Components

An irrotational region of flow is formed by superposing a line source of strength \((\dot{V} / L)_{1}=2.00 m ^{2} / s \text { at }(x, y)=(0,-1)\), a line source of strength \((\dot{V} / L)_{2}=\) -1.00 m¬≤/s at (x, y) = (1, -1), and a line vortex of strength –ì = 1.50 m¬≤/s at (x, y) = (1, 1), where all spatial coordinates are in meters. [Source number 2 is actually a sink, since \((\dot{V} / L)_{2}=\) is negative.] The locations of the three building blocks are shown in Fig. 10‚Äì52. Calculate the fluid velocity at the point (x, y) = (1, 0).

Solution

SOLUTION For the given superposition of two line sources and a vortex, we are to calculate the velocity at the point (x, y) = (1, 0).

Assumptions 1 The region of flow being modeled is steady, incompressible, and irrotational. 2 The velocity at the location of each component is infinite (they are singularities), and the flow in the vicinity of each of these singularities is unphysical; however, these regions are ignored in the present analysis.

Analysis There are several ways to solve this problem. We could sum the three stream functions using Eqs. 10‚Äì44 and 10‚Äì47, and then take derivatives of the composite stream function to calculate the velocity components. Alternatively, we could do the same for velocity potential function. An easier approach is to recognize that velocity itself can be superposed; we simply add the velocity vectors induced by each of the three individual singularities to form the composite velocity at the given point. This is illustrated in Fig. 10‚Äì53. Since the vortex is located 1 m above the point (1, 0), the velocity induced by the vortex is to the right and has a magnitude of

 

Line source at point (a, b):

 

\(\begin{aligned}\phi &=\frac{\dot{V} / L}{2 \pi} \ln r_{1}=\frac{\dot{V} / L}{2 \pi} \ln \sqrt{(x-a)^{2}+(y-b)^{2}} \\\psi &=\frac{\dot{V} / L}{2 \pi} \theta_{1}=\frac{\dot{V} / L}{2 \pi} \arctan \frac{y-b}{x-a}\end{aligned}\) (10-44)

 

Line vortex at point (a, b):

\(\begin{aligned}&\phi=\frac{\Gamma}{2 \pi} \theta_{1}=\frac{\Gamma}{2 \pi} \arctan \frac{y-b}{x-a} \\&\psi=-\frac{\Gamma}{2 \pi} \ln r_{1}=-\frac{\Gamma}{2 \pi} \ln \sqrt{(x-a)^{2}+(y-b)^{2}}\end{aligned}\) (10-47)

 

\(V_{\text {vortex }}=\frac{\Gamma}{2 \pi r_{\text {vortex }}}=\frac{1.50 m ^{2} / s }{2 \pi(1.00 m )}=0.239 m / s\) (1)

 

Similarly, the first source induces a velocity at point (1, 0) at a 45¬∞ angle from the x-axis as shown in Fig. 10‚Äì53. Its magnitude is

 

\(V_{\text {source } 1}=\frac{\left|(\dot{V} / L)_{1}\right|}{2 \pi r_{\text {source } 1}}=\frac{2.00 m ^{2} / s }{2 \pi(\sqrt{2} m )}=0.225 m / s\) (2)

 

Finally, the second source (the sink) induces a velocity straight down with magnitude

 

\(V_{\text {source 2 }}=\frac{\left|(\dot{V} / L)_{2}\right|}{2 \pi r_{\text {source 2 }}}=\frac{\left|-1.00 m ^{2} / s \right|}{2 \pi(1.00 m )}=0.159 m / s\) (3)

 

We sum these velocities vectorially by completing the parallelograms, as illustrated in Fig. 10‚Äì54. Using Eq. 10‚Äì35, the resultant velocity is

 

Composite velocity field from superposition: \(\vec{V}=\vec{V}_{1}+\vec{V}_{2}\) (10-35)

 

\(\vec{V}=\underbrace{\vec{V}_{\text {vortex }}}_{0.239 \vec{i} m / s }+\underbrace{\vec{V}_{\text {source 1 }}}_{\left(\frac{0.225}{\sqrt{2}} \vec{i}+\frac{0.225}{\sqrt{2}} \vec{j}\right)m/s}+\underbrace{\vec{V}_{\text {source 2 }}}_{{-0.159} \vec{j}\ m/s{}{{}}{}}=(0.398 \vec{i}+0 \vec{j}) m / s\) (4)

 

The superposed velocity at point (1, 0) is 0.398 m/s to the right.

Discussion This example demonstrates that velocity can be superposed just as stream function or velocity potential function can be superposed. Superposition of velocity is valid in irrotational regions of flow because the differential equations for ùúô and ùúì are linear; the linearity extends to their derivatives as well.
