Guest Question.

Consider the system \(\ddot{y}+3 \dot{y}+2 y=\dot{u}+u\).

a) Find the state matrices \(F_{c} , G_{c} , and H_{c}\) in control canonical form that correspond to the given differential equation.

b) Sketch the eigenvectors of \(F_{c}\) in the \((x_{1} , x_{2}) \) plane, and draw vectors that correspond to the completely observable \((x_{0})\) and the completely unobservable (\(x _{\overline{0}}\)) state variables.

c) Express \((x_{0})\) and \( x _{\overline{0}}\) in terms of the observability matrix \(\mathcal O\).

d) Give the state matrices in observer canonical form and repeat parts (b) and (c) in terms of controllability instead of observability.

Admin
Solution

(a) The Laplace transform of the differential equation gives the transfer function,

\(G(s)=\frac{Y(s)}{U(s)}=\frac{s+1}{s^{2}+3 s+2}\).

Hence in controller canonical form,

\(F _{c}=\left[\begin{array}{cc} -3 & -2 \\ 1 & 0\end{array}\right], G _{c}=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 0\end{array}\right], H _{c}=\left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 1\end{array}\right]\).

(b) First, we find the eigenvectors of \(F_{c}\) or the modal directions of the system,

\(\operatorname{det}\left(s I – F _{c}\right)=0 \Longrightarrow s=-1,-2\).

\(\left( F _{c}+ I \right) v _{1}=0 \Longrightarrow v_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{c}1 \\-1\end{array}\right]\).

\(\left( F _{c}+2 I \right) v _{2}=0 \Longrightarrow v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ -1\end{array}\right]\).

Using partial-fraction expansion of G(s), we can determine which modes are unobservable and which are observable. The mode s = – 1, this mode has a pole-zero cancellation in G(s), so \(v_{1}\) is the unobservable mode shape. The mode s = – 2, does appear in the minimal transfer function, so \(v_{2}\) is the observable mode shape. Therefore, the completely unobservable direction is equal to \(v_{1}\). The completely observable direction is \(v^{\perp}_{2}\) , where \(v^{\perp}_{2}\) is the projection of \(v_{2}\) on the orthogonal direction to \(v_{1}\). These vectors are drawn in the figure below. Note from the figure that \(x_{o}\) is, in fact, the same as H. Also, the observable mode, \(v_{2}\) is observable since the projection of \(v_{2}\) onto \(x_{o}\) is not zero, i.e.,

\(x _{o}^{T} v _{2}= H _{c} v _{2} \neq 0\).

(c) Observability measures the ability to reconstruct the realization state variables given an output and its derivatives. Consider the determination of the state initial condition, x(0), given the initial output measurement and its derivatives, Y(0), where,

\(Y ( 0 )=\left[\begin{array}{c} y(0) \\ \dot{y}(0) \\ \vdots \\ y^{(n-1)}(0)\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} H _{c} \\ H _{c} F _{c} \\ \vdots \\ H _{c} F _{c}^{(n-1)}\end{array}\right] x ( 0 )= \mathcal O x ( 0 )\).

So the determination of x(0) is equivalent to the solution of \(Y( 0 )= \mathcal O x ( 0 )\). From linear algebra, the unobservable state variables are in the null space of the observability matrix, and the observable state variables are in the left-range space of \(\mathcal O\):

\(x _{\bar{o}} \in \mathcal N ( \mathcal O ), \quad x _{o} \in \mathcal R \left( \mathcal O ^{T}\right)\).

So that,

\(x _{\bar{o}}= v _{1}=\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ -1\end{array}\right], x _{o}=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1\end{array}\right]\),

(d) In observer canonical form,

\(F _{o}=\left[\begin{array}{ll} -3 & 1 \\ -2 & 0\end{array}\right], G _{o}=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1\end{array}\right], H _{o}=\left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0\end{array}\right]\).

The eigenvectors of \(F_{o}\) are,

\(\operatorname{det}\left(s I – F _{o}\right)=0 \Longrightarrow s=-1,-2\).

\(\left( F _{o}+ I \right) v _{1} \quad=\quad 0 \Longrightarrow v _{1}=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 2\end{array}\right]\).

\(\left( F _{o}+2 I \right) v _{2}=0 \Longrightarrow v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1\end{array}\right]\).

The mode s = – 1, has a pole-zero cancellation from G(s), so \(v_{1}\) is the uncontrollable mode shape. The mode s = – 2, appears in G(s), so \(v_{2}\) is the controllable mode shape. These vectors are drawn in the figure below.

Controllability measures the ability to drive the states to arbitrary values. Consider the use of u(t) to move the state vector, \(x(0-)\), to an arbitrary value, say \(x(0+)\), where,

\(u(t)=g_{1} \delta(t)+g_{2} \dot{\delta}(t)+\cdots+g_{n} \delta^{(n-1)}(t)\).

So that,

\(x (0+)- x (0-)= \mathcal C u^{*}\).

where \(u^{*}=\left[\begin{array}{ll} g_{1} & g_{2}\cdots+g_{n}\end{array}\right]^{T}\). Hence, a controllable state is one in which some vector \(u^{*}\) exists such that \(x _{c}= \mathcal C u^{*}\). From linear algebra,

\(x _{\bar{c}} \in \mathcal N \left( \mathcal C ^{T}\right), \quad x _{c} \in \mathcal R \{ \mathcal C \}\).

So that,

\(x _{\bar{c}}= v _{1}=\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ -1\end{array}\right], x _{c}=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1\end{array}\right]\).
