A Two-Region Model of a Tornado

A horizontal slice through a tornado (Fig. 10‚Äì28a) is modeled by two distinct regions. The inner or core region (0 < r < R) is modeled by solid body rotation‚Äîa rotational but inviscid region of flow as discussed earlier. The outer region (r > R) is modeled as an irrotational region of flow. The flow is two-dimensional in the rùúÉ-plane, and the components of the velocity field \(\vec{V}=\left(u_{r}, u_{\theta}\right)\) are given by

 

Velocity components: \(u_{r}=0 \quad u_{\theta}= \begin{cases}\omega r & 0<r<R \\ \frac{\omega R^{2}}{r} & r>R\end{cases}\) (1)

 

where ùúî is the magnitude of the angular velocity in the inner region. The ambient pressure (far away from the tornado) is equal to \(P_{\infty}\). Calculate the pressure field in a horizontal slice of the tornado for 0 < r < ‚àû. What is the pressure at r = 0? Plot the pressure and velocity fields.

Solution

SOLUTION We are to calculate the pressure field P(r) in a horizontal radial slice through a tornado for which the velocity components are approximated by Eq. 1. We are also to calculate the pressure in this horizontal slice at r = 0.

Assumptions 1 The flow is steady and incompressible. 2 Although R increases and ùúî decreases with increasing elevation z, R and ùúî are assumed to be constants when considering a particular horizontal slice. 3 The flow in the horizontal slice is two-dimensional in the rùúÉ-plane (no dependence on z and no w-component of velocity). 4 The effects of gravity are negligible within a particular horizontal slice (an additional hydrostatic pressure field exists in the z-direction, of course, but this does not affect the dynamics of the flow, as discussed previously).

Analysis In the inner region, the Euler equation is an appropriate approximation of the Navier‚ÄìStokes equation, and the pressure field is found by integration. In we showed that for solid body rotation,

 

Pressure field in inner region (r < R): \(P=\rho \frac{\omega^{2} r^{2}}{2}+P_{0}\) (2)

 

where \(P_{0}\) is the (unknown) pressure at r = 0 and we have neglected the gravity term. Since the outer region is a region of irrotational flow, the Bernoulli equation is appropriate and the Bernoulli constant is the same everywhere from r = R outward to r ‚Üí ‚àû. The Bernoulli constant is found by applying the boundary condition far from the tornado, namely, as \(r \rightarrow \infty, u_{\theta} \rightarrow 0 \text { and } P \rightarrow P_{\infty}\) (Fig. 10‚Äì29). Equation 10‚Äì27 yields

 

Steady incompressible Bernoulli equation in irrotational regions of flow:

 

\(\frac{P}{\rho}+\frac{V^{2}}{2}+g z=C=\text { constant everywhere }\) (10-27)

 

As r ‚Üí ‚àû: \(\underbrace{\frac{P}{\rho}}_{P_{\infty/}{\rho}}+\underbrace{\frac{V^{2}}{2}}_{V \rightarrow 0 \text { as } r \rightarrow \infty}+\underbrace{g z}_{assumption 4}=C \rightarrow C=\frac{P_{\infty}}{\rho}\) (5)

 

The pressure field anywhere in the outer region is obtained by substituting the value of constant C from Eq. 3 into the Bernoulli equation (Eq. 10‚Äì27). Neglecting gravity,

 

In outer region (r > R): \(P=\rho C-\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}=P_{\infty}-\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}\) (4)

 

We note that \(V^{2}=u_{\theta}^{2}\). After substitution of Eq. 1 for \(u_{\theta}\), Eq. 4 reduces to

 

Pressure field in outer region (r > R): \(P=P_{\infty}-\frac{\rho}{2} \frac{\omega^{2} R^{4}}{r^{2}}\) (5)

 

At r = R, the interface between the inner and outer regions, the pressure must be continuous (no sudden jumps in P ), as illustrated in Fig. 10‚Äì30. Equating Eqs. 2 and 5 at this interface yields

 

Pressure at r = R: \(P_{r=R}=\rho \frac{\omega^{2} R^{2}}{2}+P_{0}=P_{\infty}-\frac{\rho}{2} \frac{\omega^{2} R^{4}}{R^{2}}\) (6)

 

from which the pressure \(P_{0}\) at r = 0 is found,

 

Pressure at r = 0: \(P_{0}=P_{\infty}-\rho \omega^{2} R^{2}\) (7)

 

Equation 7 provides the value of pressure in the middle of the tornado‚Äîthe eye of the storm. This is the lowest pressure in the flow field. Substitution of Eq. 7 into Eq. 2 enables us to rewrite Eq. 2 in terms of the given far-field ambient pressure \(P_{infty}\),

 

In inner region (r < R): \(P=P_{\infty}-\rho \omega^{2}\left(R^{2}-\frac{r^{2}}{2}\right)\) (8)

 

Instead of plotting P as a function of r in this horizontal slice, we plot a nondimensional pressure distribution instead, so that the plot is valid for any horizontal slice. In terms of nondimensional variables,

 

Inner region (r < R): \(\frac{u_{\theta}}{\omega R}=\frac{r}{R} \quad \frac{P-P_{\infty}}{\rho \omega^{2} R^{2}}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^{2}-1\)

 

Outer region (r > R): \(\frac{u_{\theta}}{\omega R}=\frac{R}{r} \quad \frac{P-P_{\infty}}{\rho \omega^{2} R^{2}}=-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{R}{r}\right)^{2}\) (9)

 

Figure 10‚Äì31 shows both nondimensional tangential velocity and nondimensional pressure as functions of nondimensional radial location.

Discussion In the outer region, pressure increases as speed decreases‚Äî a direct result of the Bernoulli equation, which applies with the same Bernoulli constant everywhere in the outer region. You are encouraged to calculate P in the outer region by an alternate method‚Äîdirect integration of the Euler equation without use of the Bernoulli equation; you should get the same result. In the inner region, P increases parabolically with r even though speed also increases; this is because the Bernoulli constant changes from streamline to streamline (as also pointed out in ). Notice that even though there is a discontinuity in the slope of tangential velocity at r /R = 1, the pressure has a fairly smooth transition between the inner and outer regions. The pressure is lowest in the center of the tornado and rises to atmospheric pressure in the far field (Fig. 10‚Äì32). The flow in the inner region is rotational but inviscid, since viscosity plays no role in that region of the flow. In fact, the inner region can even consist of a different fluid than the outer region. For example, a tank of water draining through a hole in the bottom of a tank often forms a ‚Äúbathtub vortex‚Äù (Fig. 10‚Äì28b) that can be approximated as an inner region of air and an outer region of water. The flow in the outer region is irrotational but viscous. Note, however, that viscosity still acts on fluid particles in the outer region. (Viscosity causes the fluid particles to shear and distort, even though the net viscous force on any fluid particle in the outer region is zero.)
