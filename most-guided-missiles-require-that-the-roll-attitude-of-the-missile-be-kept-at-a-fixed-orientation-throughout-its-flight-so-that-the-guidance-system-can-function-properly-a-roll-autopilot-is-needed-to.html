Guest Question.

Most guided missiles require that the roll attitude of the missile be kept at a fixed orientation throughout its flight so that the guidance system can function properly. A roll autopilot is needed to maintain the desired roll orientation. Figure 10.5 is a sketch of a wing-controlled missile

Admin
Solution

In this example we design a feedback control system that will keep the roll orientation near \(0^\circ \) while not exceeding a given limit on the aileron deflection angle. The following equations of motion for the rolling motion of the missile were developed in Chapter 5:

\(I_x\dot{p}=\frac{\partial L}{\partial p}p+\frac{\partial L}{\partial \delta _a}\delta _a \dot{\phi }=p \)

Rewriting these equations as

\(\dot{p}=L_p p+L_{\delta _a}\delta _a \dot{\phi }=p\)

where

\(L_p=\frac{\partial L/\partial p}{I_x} and L_{\delta _a}=\frac{\partial L/\partial \delta _a}{I_x}\)

the equations can easily be written in the state variable form as follows:

\(\left[\begin{matrix} \dot{\phi } \\\dot{p} \end{matrix} \right]=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & L_p \end{bmatrix}\left[\begin{matrix} {\phi } \\{p} \end{matrix} \right] +\left[\begin{matrix} 0\\L_{\delta _a} \end{matrix} \right]{\delta _a } \)

or

\(\dot{x}=Ax+B\eta \)

where

\(A=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & L_p \end{bmatrix}\)

 

\(B=\left[\begin{matrix} 0\\L_{\delta _a} \end{matrix} \right]\)

The quadratic performance index that is to be minimized is

\(J=\int_{0}^{\infty }{\left[\left(\frac{\phi }{\phi _{max}} \right)^2+\left(\frac{p }{p _{max}} \right)^2+\left(\frac{\delta _a }{\delta _{max}} \right)^2 \right] dt } \)

where

\(\phi _{max}= \text{ the maximum desired roll angle} \\ p _{max}= \text{ the maximum desired roll rate} \\ \delta _{max}= \text{ maximum aileron deflection}.\)

Comparing the performance index given here with the general form allows us to specify the matrices Q and R:

\(Q=\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\phi ^2_{max}} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{p ^2_{max}} \end{bmatrix} \)

 

\(R=\frac{1}{\delta ^2_{max}}\)

The optimum control law is determined by solving the steady-state Riccati matrix equation:

\(A^TS + SA — SBR^{-1}B^TS + Q = 0\)

for the values of the S matrix. The optimal control law is given by

\(\eta = -k^Tx\)

where
\(k^T = R^{-1}B^TS \)

Substituting the matrices A, B, Q, and R into the Ricatti equation yields a set of nonlinear algebraic equations for the unknown elements of the S matrix *:

\( \frac{1}{\phi ^2_{max}}=S^2_{12}L^2_{\delta _a}\delta ^2_{max}=0 \\ S_{11}+S_{12}L_p – S_{12}S_{22}L^2_{\delta _a}\delta ^2_{max}=0 \\ 2S_{12}2S_{22}L_p+\frac{1}{p ^2_{max}}-S^2_{22}L^2_{\delta _a}\delta ^2_{max}=0\)

For the case in which the missile has the aerodynamic characteristics

\(L_p = -2 rad/s \\ L_{\delta _a}=9000 s^{-2} \\ \phi _{max}= 10^\circ = 0 . 174 rad \\ p _{max}= 300^\circ/s = 5.23 rad/s \\ \delta _{max} = \pm 30^\circ = \pm 0.524 rad \)

the nonlinear Ricatti equations can be solved for the elements of the Ricatti matrix S:

\(S=\begin{bmatrix} 1.135 & 0 .0012 \\ 0 .0012 & 0.00005 \end{bmatrix} \)

The control law gains can now be calculated from the equation:

\(k^T = R^{-1}B^TS \)

and the control law is found to be \(\delta _a = -3.0\phi – 0.103p.\)
