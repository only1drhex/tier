Guest Question.

In AM (amplitude modulation) radio, there are two very important waveforms‚Äîthe signal, s(t), and the carrier. All of the information we desire to transmit, voice, music, and so on, is contained in the signal waveform, which is in essence transported by the carrier. Therefore, the Fourier transform of s(t) contains frequencies from about 50 Hz to 20,000 Hz. The carrier, c(t), is a sinusoid oscillating at a frequency much greater than those in s(t). For example, the FCC (Federal Communications Commission) rules and regulations have allocated the frequency range 540 kHz to 1.7 MHz for AM radio station carrier frequencies. Even the lowest possible carrier frequency allocation of 540 kHz is much greater than the audio frequencies in s(t). In fact, when a station broadcasts its call letters and frequency, they are telling you the carrier‚Äôs frequency, which the FCC assigned to that station!

In simple cases, the signal, s(t), is modified to produce a voltage of the form

\(v(t)=[A+s(t)] \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)\)

where A is a constant and \(\omega_{c}\) is the carrier frequency in rad/s. This voltage, œÖ(t), with the signal ‚Äúcoded‚Äù within, is sent to the antenna and is broadcast to the public, whose radios ‚Äúpick up‚Äù a faint replica of the waveform œÖ (t).
Let us plot the magnitude of the Fourier transform of both s(t) and œÖ (t) given that s(t) is

\(s(t)=\cos \left(2 \pi f_{a} t\right)\)

where \(f_{a}\) is 1000 Hz, the carrier frequency is 900 kHz, and the constant A is unity.

Solution

The Fourier transform of s(t) is
\(S (\omega)= F \left[\cos \left(\omega_{a} t\right)\right]=\pi \delta\left(\omega-\omega_{a}\right)+\pi \delta\left(\omega+\omega_{a}\right)\)
and is shown in Fig. 15.20.
The voltage œÖ (t) can be expressed in the form
\(v(t)=[1+s(t)] \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)=\cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)+s(t) \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)\)
The Fourier transform for the carrier is
\(F \left[\cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)\right]=\pi \delta\left(\omega-\omega_{c}\right)+\pi \delta\left(\omega+\omega_{c}\right)\)
The term \(s(t) \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)\) can be written as
\(s(t) \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)=s(t)\left\{\frac{e^{j \omega_{c} t}+e^{-j \omega_{c} t}}{2}\right\}\)
Using the property of modulation as given in Table 15.3, we can express the Fourier transform of \(s(t) \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)\) as
\(F \left[s(t) \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)\right]=\frac{1}{2}\left\{ S \left(\omega-\omega_{c}\right)+ S \left(\omega+\omega_{c}\right)\right\}\)
Employing S(œâ), we find
\(F \left[s(t) \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)\right]= F \left[\cos \left(\omega_{a} t\right) \cos \left(\omega_{c} t\right)\right]\)
\(=\frac{\pi}{2}\left\{\delta\left(\omega-\omega_{a}-\omega_{c}\right)+\delta\left(\omega+\omega_{a}-\omega_{c}\right)\right. \left.+\delta\left(\omega-\omega_{a}+\omega_{c}\right)+\delta\left(\omega+\omega_{a}+\omega_{c}\right)\right\}\)
Finally, the Fourier transform of œÖ (t) is
\(V (\omega)=\frac{\pi}{2}\left\{2 \delta\left(\omega-\omega_{c}\right)+2 \delta\left(\omega+\omega_{c}\right)+\delta\left(\omega-\omega_{a}-\omega_{c}\right)\right. \left.+\delta\left(\omega+\omega_{a}-\omega_{c}\right)+\delta\left(\omega-\omega_{a}+\omega_{c}\right)+\delta\left(\omega+\omega_{a}+\omega_{c}\right)\right\}\)
which is shown in Fig. 15.21. Notice that S(œâ) is centered about the carrier frequency. This is the effect of modulation.
