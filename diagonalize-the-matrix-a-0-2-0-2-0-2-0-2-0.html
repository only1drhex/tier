Guest Question.

Diagonalize the matrix

\(A=\left[\begin{array}{lll}0 & 2 & 0 \\2 & 0 & 2 \\0 & 2 & 0\end{array}\right]\)
Admin
Solution

Notice that this matrix is symmetric (we will see later it is also Hermitian) and so we know ahead of time that the eigenvectors constitute a basis. Solving the characteristic equation, we find

 

\(0=\operatorname{det}\left|\left[\begin{array}{lll}0 & 2 & 0 \\2 & 0 & 2 \\0 & 2 & 0\end{array}\right]-\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\lambda & 0 & 0 \\0 & \lambda & 0 \\0 & 0 & \lambda\end{array}\right]\right|=\operatorname{det}\left|\begin{array}{ccc}-\lambda & 2 & 0 \\2 & -\lambda & 2 \\0 & 2 & -\lambda\end{array}\right|\)

 

This leads to the eigenvalues \(\{0,-2 \sqrt{2}, 2 \sqrt{2}\}\) (exercise). For the first eigenvalue we have

 

\(A v_{1}=0\)

 

This leads to the equations

 

\(\begin{array}{c}2 y=0, \Rightarrow y=0 \\2 x+2 z=0, \Rightarrow z=-x\end{array}\)

 

Therefore the eigenvector can be written as

 

\(v_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{c}x \\0 \\-x\end{array}\right]\)

 

Normalization gives

 

\(1=\left[\begin{array}{lll}x^{*} & 0 & -x^{*}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c}x \\0 \\-x\end{array}\right]=2|x|^{2}\)

 

Therefore we can take \(x=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\), and the first eigenvector is

 

\(v_{1}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\begin{array}{c}1 \\0 \\-1\end{array}\right]\)

 

For the second eigenvalue we have

 

\(A v_{2}=-2 \sqrt{2} v_{2}\)

 

This leads to the equations

 

\(\begin{aligned}2 y &=-2 \sqrt{2} x \\2 x+2 z &=-2 \sqrt{2} y \\2 y &=-2 \sqrt{2} z\end{aligned}\)

 

A little manipulation shows that

 

\(y=-\sqrt{2} x, z=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} y=x\)

 

and so we have

 

\(v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{c}x \\-\sqrt{2} x \\x\end{array}\right]\)

 

Normalization gives

 

\(\begin{aligned}1 &=v_{2}^{\dagger} v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{lll}x^{*} & -\sqrt{2} x^{*} & x^{*}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c}x \\-\sqrt{2} x \\x\end{array}\right]=|x|^{2}+2|x|^{2}+|x|^{2}=4|x|^{2} \\& \Rightarrow|x|^{2}=\frac{1}{4} \text { or } x=\frac{1}{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

and so the normalized eigenvector is

 

\(v_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{c}x \\-\sqrt{2} x \\x\end{array}\right]=\frac{1}{2}\left[\begin{array}{c}1 \\-\sqrt{2} \\1\end{array}\right]\)

 

The third eigenvalue equation is

 

\(A v_{3}=2 \sqrt{2} v_{3}\)

 

A similar procedure shows that the third eigenvector is

 

\(v_{3}=\frac{1}{2}\left[\begin{array}{c}1 \\\sqrt{2} \\1\end{array}\right]\)

 

The unitary matrix that diagonalizes A is found by setting its columns equal to the normalized eigenvectors of A

 

\(U=\left[\begin{array}{lll}v_{1} & v_{2} & v_{3}\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\0 & -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2}\end{array}\right]\)

 

The inverse of this matrix is found from \(U^{\dagger}\), which is

 

\(U^{\dagger}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\\frac{1}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{1}{2}\end{array}\right]\)

 

Now we apply the transformation to the matrix A:

 

\(U^{\dagger} A U=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\\frac{1}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{1}{2}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{lll}0 & 2 & 0 \\2 & 0 & 2 \\0 & 2 & 0\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\0 & -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2}\end{array}\right]\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}=& {\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\\frac{1}{2} & \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} & \frac{1}{2}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & -\sqrt{2} & \sqrt{2} \\0 & 2 & 2 \\0 & -\sqrt{2} & \sqrt{2}\end{array}\right] } \\=& {\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 0 & 0 \\0 & -2 \sqrt{2} & 0 \\0 & 0 & 2 \sqrt{2}\end{array}\right] }\end{aligned}\)
